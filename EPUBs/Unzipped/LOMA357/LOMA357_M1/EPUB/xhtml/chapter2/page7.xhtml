Real Estate and Mortgages

Another type of investment is real estate, which is property that consists of land and any buildings or natural resources on it. Two examples of real estate assets are your house and your employer’s office building. Like a stock, real estate is considered an equity investment because it is an ownership asset.

However, real estate can also serve as the basis for a fixed-income investment. A property owner can secure a long-term loan, called a mortgage, by pledging the real estate property as collateral. The borrower agrees to pay off the mortgage loan with regular payments of principal and interest. A mortgage is a debt for a borrower but an investment for a lender.

 

Types of Real Estate Properties

Depending on its intended use, a real estate property is generally classified as

  • Residential. This type of property is intended to be used as a place of residence for the owner. Residential real estate can be a single-family property or a multifamily property, such as a duplex.

  • Commercial. This type of property is owned for the purpose of producing income. Examples of commercial real estate properties are multifamily residential properties (such as apartments), office buildings, hotel and resort properties, industrial properties, and retail properties—such as shopping centers.

  • Agricultural. This type of property is intended for agricultural use or as undeveloped, open space—also known as raw land. Important agricultural uses for real estate include dairy farming, crop production, ranching, agribusiness, and timber production.

 

Real Estate Appraisals

The value of a real estate property depends on many factors. Location is one of the most important factors in a property’s value. For example, properties in thriving metropolitan areas are typically more valuable than properties in less desirable locations. In addition, properties in popular locations are generally more liquid—and result in higher sales prices—because popular locations attract more potential buyers than unpopular locations.

The size of a property also affects its value. For instance, a small apartment building with 15 units will be valued differently than a large apartment building with 300 units. To compare properties of different sizes, residential and commercial property values are quoted by square footage—such as $100 per square foot.

Another factor affecting value is the use of the property. A property might have one value as a home and another value when used as office space. Improving a property or converting it to a new use is also likely to change the property’s value.

To determine the value of a real estate property, a qualified professional—called an appraiser—conducts an appraisal, which is an estimate of a property’s value as of a given date. Appraisers follow professional standards in determining property values. A property’s value varies depending on the approach the appraiser uses. Different approaches include

  • Market value appraisals, in which the appraiser estimates the price a buyer would be willing to pay for a property in current market conditions. Typically, an appraiser estimates market value using the comparables method, which estimates the market value of a property by referring to recent selling prices for other properties having similar characteristics.

  • Value-in-use appraisals, also known as use value appraisals, in which the appraiser estimates a property’s value to a particular user. Sometimes a property is dedicated to a particular use or has special benefits. In such cases, a value-in-use appraisal can be lower or higher than a market value appraisal for the same property. For example, a church building has a special purpose that is not accounted for in a market value appraisal.

  • Investment value appraisals, in which the appraiser estimates the value of a property to a particular investor, with the property valued at its highest and best use. Unlike a value-in-use appraisal, the investor is assumed to be making the best use of the property. This appraisal approach usually results in higher values than market value appraisals.

  • Insurance value appraisals, also known as replacement value appraisals, in which the appraiser estimates the amount an insurance company would potentially cover under property insurance if all buildings and fixtures on a property were destroyed. An insurance value appraisal excludes the value of the land. Thus, the insurance value appraisal of a specific property is typically lower than the market value appraisal, value-in-use appraisal, or investment value appraisal.

  • Liquidation value appraisals, in which the appraiser estimates the price that a property would bring if the owners were forced to sell it quickly, under distressed circumstances. An appraised liquidation value for a given property is typically lower than its appraised market value or appraised investment value.

Real Estate Ownership

Real estate ownership provides benefits to investors. Owners can rent out properties and receive rental income. In addition, owners have the potential for capital gains if their properties appreciate in value. A capital gainis the amount by which the selling price of an investment is more than its purchase price.

Real estate also helps with portfolio management. For example, real estate returns are not strongly linked to stock and bond returns. Therefore, real estate ownership diversifies an investment portfolio.

Moreover, real estate properties are attractive assets for investors who want to hedge against inflation. Inflation is a prolonged rise in the average level of prices in an economy that decreases the purchasing power of the economy’s currency. Typically, real estate prices rise during periods of inflation, thus protecting the purchasing power of investors’ capital. You will read in Chapter 4 about the effect that inflation has on investment returns.

As with most investments, real estate also carries risks. Rental income can be unpredictable because property owners may experience vacancies in rentable properties. If an economic downturn occurs, real estate investments can be negatively affected by decreasing property valuations and increasing vacancies. In addition, real estate investments are relatively illiquid because they usually take longer to sell than other types of assets.

 

Mortgage Loans

Some institutional investors supply funds to borrowers through mortgage loans, which borrowers use to purchase properties. The level of protection provided to a lender depends upon whether the mortgage is a recourse loan or a nonrecourse loan:

  • A recourse loan is a mortgage or other secured loan that allows the lender to seek financial damages if the borrower fails to pay the loan, and the value of the property is not enough to cover the outstanding liability. The lender can obtain legal action, known as a deficiency judgment, to enforce collection of the outstanding amount owed from the borrower’s other assets.

  • A nonrecourse loan is a mortgage or other secured loan that allows the lender to seize the property if the borrower fails to pay the loan; the lender can then sell the property to satisfy the outstanding debt. If the property’s value does not entirely satisfy the debt, the contract does not permit the lender to pursue the borrower for the additional funds, and the lender must take a loss. Typically, mortgage loans that life insurance companies hold are nonrecourse loans.

Most lenders for recourse as well as nonrecourse loans expect loan balances to be amortized at a steady rate. Amortization is the reduction of a debt by regular payments of principal and interest that result in full payment of the debt by the maturity date. Mortgage lenders develop amortization schedules for borrowers to repay loans by a given date.

Like bonds, the income collected from a mortgage can provide a predictable stream of cash flows that is a good match with an insurer’s obligations for products that provide periodic income payments, such as payout annuities. However, mortgages tend to be somewhat riskier investments than bonds, and are less liquid than bonds. Moreover, credit rating agencies do not rate mortgages. Therefore, mortgage investors may find it challenging to evaluate the risk of defaults by borrowers.

The two main types of mortgages are residential mortgages and commercial mortgages.

 

Residential Mortgages

A residential mortgage is a loan secured by a single-family home. Residential mortgages can be conventional or nonconventional:

  • A conventional mortgage loan is made on the basis of the borrower’s credit rating and the value of the collateral that is the basis for the mortgage.

  • A nonconventional mortgage loan does not satisfy lender requirements for conventional loans, but does satisfy U.S. government criteria—such as those specified by the Department of Veterans Affairs (VA) or Federal Housing Administration (FHA)—and thus qualifies for government-provided mortgage insurance that guarantees the mortgage.

Residential mortgage lenders evaluate potential borrowers for their ability to repay loans. Lenders consider factors such as a borrower’s credit rating and current income to assess the borrower’s risk of defaulting on the loan.

Some borrowers repay their residential mortgage loans before they are scheduled to. When early repayment occurs, lenders receive fewer interest payments than anticipated. The risk associated with the early return of principal for a mortgage is called prepayment risk. Partly because of prepayment risk, insurers that invest in residential mortgages do not usually hold individual mortgage loans.

 

Commercial Mortgages

A commercial mortgage is a loan secured by commercial real estate. Although commercial mortgages can be structured in a variety of ways, a typical commercial mortgage is a nonrecourse, fixed-rate loan with a partial repayment of interest and principal during the loan period, and a balloon payment—a lump-sum payment of the final amount of the mortgage—on a specified date.

Most of the mortgages that insurers hold are commercial mortgages that finance retail stores, shopping centers, office buildings, factories, warehouses, hospitals, and apartment buildings.

 

Real Estate Investment Trusts

Investors can invest indirectly in real estate and mortgages through real estate investment trusts. A real estate investment trust (REIT) is a corporation or trust that uses the pooled capital of a number of investors to purchase and manage real estate property or mortgage loans. Investors sometimes consider REITs to be a separate asset class because of their unique features.

REITs often specialize in a single type of real estate—such as apartment, retail, office, residential, or industrial properties. The two primary types of REITs are

  • Equity REITs, which use capital to purchase income-producing assets, such as apartments or office buildings. The REIT owns each property in the portfolio and is responsible for managing its properties. The REIT produces revenue for investors by collecting lease and rental payments.

  • Mortgage REITs, which invest in loans to borrowers who wish to purchase real estate. A mortgage REIT can invest in individual mortgages or in investment vehicles that represent a group of mortgages. The REIT earns income from the interest on the loans.

Investors purchase shares in a REIT and earn a share of the income that the REIT generates. By law, a REIT must distribute at least 90% of its taxable income to shareholders annually in the form of dividends. Thus, REITs are attractive to investors seeking income-generating investments. In addition, REIT dividends generally offer higher yields than stock dividends.

Another benefit for investors is that REITs use professional real estate managers, who know how to acquire, finance, develop, and renovate properties, and to negotiate leases to get the most return for shareholders.

Compared to other forms of real estate investment, REITs typically offer investors less opportunity for price appreciation. However, REITs provide liquidity and diversification benefits for real estate investors. Therefore, insurers and other institutional investors view REITs favorably.

Some REITs are traded on securities exchanges. A securities exchange is an organized marketplace where members of the exchange buy and sell specific types of securities—such as common stocks and bonds. REITs traded on securities exchanges are called public REITs.

Public REITs tend to be more liquid than private REITs, which are privately traded and limited to institutional investors and other affluent investors. The majority of REITs are private REITs.

Because REITs invest in real estate and mortgages, they share similar risks. For example, a REIT that owns apartment buildings will earn less income if its apartments have to lower rental rates. In addition, REITs may be concentrated in a specific geographic area or type of property. For instance, a REIT that mostly owns regional shopping centers is at risk if such centers lose popularity with consumers.
