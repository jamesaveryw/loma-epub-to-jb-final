Stocks

Recall that an equity security is a security that represents an ownership interest in the issuing company. When most people think of equity securities, they think of stocks, which represent equity ownership through units called shares. A person or organization that owns shares of a company’s stock is a shareholder—also known as a stockholder.

In general, the historical return on stocks has been substantially higher than the historical return on bonds. Also, stocks are generally very liquid assets, and are often more liquid than bonds.

On the other hand, stocks are associated with higher risks than bonds. Stock prices are typically more volatile than bond prices. Shareholders also have fewer protections against a company’s default than bondholders do.

Stocks can earn returns through price appreciation and dividend income. Price appreciationis an increase in the market value of an invested asset. A dividend is a distribution of a company’s profits payable to owners of the company’s stock.

Depending on the institutional investor, stocks can make up a significant portion of an investment portfolio. For life insurers, stocks make up a large share of the assets held in separate account portfolios. However, stocks make up a smaller share of general account portfolios because of regulations that restrict the amount of common stock an insurer can have in its general account.

 

Common and Preferred Stocks

Companies issue two basic types of stock. A common stock is a type of stock that usually entitles the owner to (1) vote on the selection of the board of directors and on other important company matters and (2) receive dividends on the stock, if they are declared. Not all common stocks pay dividends.

In contrast, a preferred stock is a type of stock that typically does not carry the voting rights of common stock but does carry a stated dividend rate. Companies pay dividends to preferred shareholders before common shareholders.

Preferred stocks are hybrid securities because they have characteristics of both equity and debt securities. Like the fixed-interest payments of bonds, preferred stocks have fixed dividend rates. Preferred stocks also have par values, like bonds. Like equity securities, preferred stocks can appreciate in price—although preferred stock prices typically appreciate less than common stock prices.

In the event of a company’s liquidation, owners of preferred stock have a higher claim priority over company assets than common shareholders. However, a company’s bondholders have a higher claim priority than its preferred and common shareholders.

As mentioned above, common stocks typically have a stronger potential for price appreciation than preferred stocks. Therefore, many institutional investors focus on common stock over preferred stock. For example, insurance companies typically own little preferred stock.

 

Dividends

Companies are not required to declare dividend payments on common stock. If a company pays dividends to shareholders, it typically follows an established payment pattern—such as on a quarterly basis.

When a company reduces or eliminates a dividend, the company’s share price usually falls. Hence, dividend-paying companies strive to pay dividends regularly unless extraordinary circumstances force the company to reduce or eliminate them.

Companies can pay cash dividends, stock dividends, or special dividends:

  • A cash dividend is a cash payment to a shareholder for each share of stock the shareholder owns. Cash dividends, which are paid at a stated price per share, are the most frequent form of dividends for common stocks. For example, if a company declares a cash dividend of $0.25 per share, a shareholder owning 100 shares will receive $25 (100 × $0.25).

  • A stock dividend is a type of dividend in which a company distributes additional shares of its stock to shareholders. Stock dividends are expressed as a stated percentage of the shareholder’s number of shares. For example, if a company declares a 5% stock dividend, a shareholder owning 100 shares will receive an additional five shares (100 × 0.05). A stock dividend does not increase or decrease a shareholder’s proportionate share of ownership in the company. For instance, if a shareholder owns 2% of a company before the stock dividend is distributed, she will own 2% of the company after the distribution.

  • A special dividend, sometimes called an extra dividend, is a nonrecurring dividend that is usually paid in cash and is considerably larger than the normal dividend a company pays. Companies may declare special dividends to reward shareholders for onetime events, such as a period of exceptionally strong earnings.
