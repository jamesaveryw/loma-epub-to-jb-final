Capital Markets

As previously stated, capital markets—including stock markets and bond markets—exist to move capital from net suppliers to net users. Capital markets make it easier for capital suppliers and capital users to find each other.

In capital markets, some entities—called participants—play a direct role, while other entities—called influencers—affect the markets without playing a direct role. In addition, some participants play more than one role, depending on the types of transactions in which they engage. The three main participants in capital markets are

  • Suppliers. These entities inject funds into the capital markets through their securities purchases. Retail investors and businesses participate as suppliers in the capital markets. In general, retail investors are indirect suppliers of capital through the financial services products that they purchase. For instance, if you purchase mutual funds for your personal retirement savings account, the funds then purchase securities for their portfolios.

  • Users. These entities draw funds from the capital markets through their securities sales. Typically, businesses and governments are users of capital. They apply the funds raised in capital markets on endeavors expected to provide long-term value.

  • Facilitators. These entities enable buyers and sellers to complete securities transactions. Investment banks are institutions that specialize in underwriting debt and equity offerings and act as intermediaries between issuers of securities and investors. Custodians keep physical or virtual custody of securities, maintain controls, and keep inventories of securities that specific investors presently own. Settlement agents hold securities while a transaction is in progress, ensuring that ownership is formally transferred from the seller to the purchaser only after the purchaser has submitted payment for the securities.

Institutional investors are large suppliers of capital. They collect funds from households, retirement funds, and other businesses. Through their investing activities, they move money into the capital markets. For example, insurance companies collect funds to support their insurance products and insert funds into capital markets through their investment purchases.

Institutional investors can also act as capital users. For instance, stock insurers sell shares of their stock to the public to raise funds for business operations.

By supplying funds to capital markets, investors provide an important service to the global economy. They supply capital needed for productive projects that generate economic growth. Countries with growing economies enable businesses to make more profits. Increasingly profitable businesses can afford to pay employees higher wages and hire new employees. Therefore, capital suppliers contribute to rising standards of living around the world.

Capital suppliers also promote capital efficiency. Excess funds from one part of the world flow to other parts of the world that are in need of financial resources. Both parties benefit from participating in capital markets: suppliers earn better returns on their capital than they could by saving, and users gain access to funding that may not have been available through other means.
