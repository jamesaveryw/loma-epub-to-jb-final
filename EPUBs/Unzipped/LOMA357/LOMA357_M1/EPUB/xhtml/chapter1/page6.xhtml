Overview of the Investment Function

Financial services companies typically employ a variety of investment professionals to carry out the responsibilities of the investment function. A company’s internal controls can affect how responsibilities are divided.

Companies vary in the kinds of arrangements they have with their investment functions. Within the investment function, operations tend to be divided into two or three categories. An insurance company’s investment function receives support from and provides input to other functional areas in the company.

Companies may outsource investment management duties to external vendors. A vendor, also known as a service provider or a solution provider, is a third party that a company hires to perform specific tasks or provide specific services.

Companies can gain access to external data through vendors. The investment function collects and analyzes external and internal data by using specific information systems.

 

Roles of Investment Professionals

The investment function of insurers and other financial services companies typically has the following professional roles:

  • Chief Investment Officer. The chief investment officer (CIO) is a senior-level executive who manages a company’s investment function. The CIO is responsible for implementing the company’s investment policies within the investment function. In addition, the CIO advises the company’s senior executives and board of directors on investment matters and can recommend changes to the company’s investment policies.

  • Portfolio Managers. A portfolio manager is an investment professional with broad authority and accountability for fulfilling the mission and objectives of an investment portfolio. A portfolio manager is responsible for constructing the portfolio and managing the portfolio’s assets to meet the portfolio’s established risk constraints and return requirements. Within an insurance company, a portfolio manager is typically assigned to handle assets that support a particular product or business line.

  • Investment Analysts. An investment analyst, sometimes called a securities analyst or financial analyst, is an investment professional responsible for monitoring issuers of securities and making trading recommendations. Some analysts are generalists, whereas others specialize in a specific asset type or industry. Investment analysts provide research and insights that support investment decisions made by portfolio managers.

  • Traders. A trader is an investment professional responsible for executing purchases and sales of publicly traded securities under instructions from a portfolio manager. At smaller firms, a trader may handle a broad range of securities; at larger firms, a trader may specialize in trading a specific type of financial asset. Traders constantly monitor financial markets to ensure that they execute trades at the best possible prices, thus maximizing the benefits of the portfolio managers’ investment decisions.

  • Investment Operations Professionals. Investment operations professionals provide administrative support to the investment function. Trade settlement specialists monitor executed trades to ensure that the exchange of securities and cash is completed with trading partners in a timely manner. Other operations professionals maintain records of investment transactions in internally managed computer systems. Companies may hire external parties for some administration activities.

  • Investment Risk Management Professionals. Investment risk management professionals focus on identifying and measuring risks that affect a company’s investments. For example, asset-liability management risk is the risk that a company’s capital could be depleted due to a narrowing of the difference between returns earned on assets and liabilities or a mismatch in cash flows for assets and liabilities. Asset-liability managers monitor a company’s assets and investments supporting a specified product line, such as annuities. Asset-liability managers help a company manage risk for a given product line by (1) tracking cash inflows and outflows and (2) projecting mismatches between assets and liabilities. At some companies, the portfolio managers may perform some asset-liability management duties, such as ensuring that sufficient funds are available to support a product’s liabilities. In many companies, asset-liability managers and other investment risk management professionals provide risk reports to the company’s board of directors.

  • Investment Information Technology Professionals. Investment information technology professionals maintain the technology for software and information processing packages, data reporting, data communications, information feeds, and data storage. In recent years, information technology professionals have increased the resources devoted to information security—the physical, technical, and procedural steps a company takes to prevent the loss, wrongful disclosure (accidental or intentional), or theft of information.

Internal Controls

Companies split up responsibilities within the investment function as a form of internal control. According to the principle of segregation of duties, also known as the principle of dual control, an employer should design jobs so that incompatible functions are assigned to different individuals. In this context, incompatible functionsare job duties in the normal course of employment that, when combined, place an employee in a position where he could commit an illegal act or could conceal errors or irregularities.

When incompatible functions are combined, a company is also less likely to detect unintentional errors that its employees commit. Segregating—or “separating”—duties helps a company safeguard its assets by providing a framework in which the company can quickly detect and address suspicious actions, errors, and irregularities.

An effective internal control system separates the custodial, authorization, and accounting tasks associated with the company’s assets. In this context, custodial tasksinclude the physical or electronic handling of assets. Therefore, an employee who assumes physical or electronic custody of securities should not be the same employee who maintains the accounting records for those securities. Furthermore, employees with custodial or accounting tasks should not have authorization to buy, sell, or otherwise dispose of securities. Institutional investors often use an external provider for custodial tasks to comply with the need for internal controls.

For financial transactions conducted by internal employees, many financial services companies require that an employee with a sufficient authorization limit officially authorize a transaction before it can occur. An authorization limit is the maximum monetary amount that a company employee has official power to approve for disbursement without prior approval by another company employee with higher authority.

Depending on their role in the investment function, individual employees have different levels of authorization to complete financial transactions.

EXAMPLE

A junior securities trader may have to obtain authorization for all security purchases or sales in excess of $2 million. The trader has sufficient authorization to complete any transaction below that threshold.

A senior securities trader may have to obtain authorization for all security purchases or sales in excess of $10 million. If the junior securities trader is seeking authorization for a $3 million purchase, the senior securities trader can authorize that transaction.

 

Organization of the Investment Function

Financial services companies can organize their investment functions in a variety of ways. For insurance companies, examples of these arrangements are as follows:

  • The company operates the investment function in a department within the company. With this arrangement, the company may obtain specialized investment management services from external sources.

  • The company previously had an internal investment department, but established it as a separate company that retains a corporate affiliation with the insurance company. In these cases, the separate investment management firm performs most investment services for the insurance company, while also providing services to other clients—including other types of institutional investors.

  • The company does not employ many internal investment professionals in its investment department. Instead, such companies obtain most of their investment services from external providers. The external investment management firms have no past or current affiliation with the insurance company.

  • A financial services company includes an insurance company and an investment management firm. The insurer and investment management firm are subsidiaries, or companies that are owned or controlled by another company, known as a holding company. A financial holding companyconducts activities that are financial in nature or incidental to financial activities—such as insurance activities, securities activities, banking, and investment and advisory services. In this situation, the affiliated investment management firm can provide services to the financial holding company and the affiliated insurance company, each of whom is an institutional investor.

EXAMPLE

The Dexicos Financial Group, a financial holding company, has several subsidiaries. One of the subsidiaries, Dexicos Investment Management, performs the investment function for all other subsidiaries of Dexicos Financial Group. Dexicos Investment Management also provides investment management services for institutional investors outside of the Dexicos Financial Group.

Regardless of the manner in which an insurer organizes its investment function, the investment operations themselves are similar across many types of investment functions.

Within an investment function’s organizational structure, companies often divide operations activities into the following classifications:

  • Front-Office Operations. Front-office operations typically consist of investment operations activities that deal directly with the securities marketplace, trade negotiations, and trading securities. Front-office functions encompass portfolio management, asset management, asset trading, investment analysis, and investment research. Because many roles in the front-office directly interact with clients and visibly affect client accounts, they are often called “client-facing” roles.

  • Middle-Office Operations. Middle-office operations typically consist of trade-support operations, including investment forecasting, securities regulatory compliance, trade settlements, funds disbursement, investment risk management, and portfolio performance reporting.

  • Back-Office Operations. Back-office operations typically consist of official company record-keeping operations, including investment accounting, investment reporting, investment information technology, data governance and security, and management of vendor relationships.

The middle-office and back-office operations support the activities of the front office. Some financial services companies divide investment operations into just two categories—front-office and back-office operations. Such companies consider middle-office activities to be a part of back-office operations.

Interaction with Other Functions

Within an insurance company, the investment function interacts with several functional areas that provide support to or require input from investment professionals. Notably, the investment function works with the risk management, accounting, treasury operations, product development, and investment marketing functions.

 

Risk Management

While every function plays a role in managing an insurer’s risks, some insurers have a separate unit devoted to risk management and analysis. Other insurers may include risk management activities in other functions, such as finance.

The risk management function (1) measures and monitors the company’s exposures to multiple types of risks and (2) analyzes the probabilities and impacts of potential risk events. In addition, the risk management function may be responsible for overseeing the company’s capital management and asset-liability management efforts.

The risk management function monitors the financial risks associated with investment operations. Risk management professionals also review investment activities to verify that investment professionals are following company policies regarding appropriate levels of risk exposure.

Insurance regulators and securities regulators also scrutinize insurers’ investment activities. Together, an insurance company’s risk management professionals and investment professionals ensure that the company complies with regulations.

We cover the relationship between risk management and the investment function in greater detail in Chapter 6.

 

Accounting

The accounting function records, tracks, and reports on a company’s financial transactions, including investment transactions. Accountants gather financial information and distribute it to company managers. Insurers use records of transactions to generate financial statements and reports for regulators and other external stakeholders.

Accountants also ensure compliance with complex financial reporting standards related to the investments of insurance companies.

 

Treasury Operations

In insurance companies, the treasury operations function manages and maintains records and reports for all of an insurer’s cash transactions, specifically money deposited or withdrawn from accounts at a bank or other financial institution. Employees in treasury operations set up bank accounts and disburse policy proceeds via checks and electronic funds transfer.

A company’s treasury function, through its cash management activities, keeps the company’s cash reserves at needed levels. Cash management includes cash forecasting, bank relations, bank account administration, and short-term lending and borrowing activities. Cash forecasting involves tracking and predicting the movement of money into and out of the company.

Although the treasury function and the investment function are usually separate within an insurer, they must work together. Investment employees make strategic decisions and execute transactions to manage the company’s assets, while treasury employees handle the resulting cash inflows and outflows. Together, they ensure prudent management of the company’s finances.

 

Product Development

In life insurance companies, actuaries play a significant role in product development. An actuary is an expert in financial risk management and the mathematics and modeling of insurance and annuity products, as well as other financial instruments. Actuaries help insurers develop assumptions used in pricing new products.

To create a product’s financial design, actuaries and other product development team members require information from investment professionals. During product planning and development, investment professionals identify the types of assets that are available to match the characteristics of a given product. Investment professionals also evaluate any product design assumptions involving the assets backing a product and support the long-term matching of assets with a product’s liabilities.

 

Investment Marketing

A company’s investment marketing function is responsible primarily for the marketing and sales of investment services to potential institutional investment clients. When making client presentations, the company’s sales professionals rely on information that investment professionals provide.

The investment marketing function develops all of the information for use in product or service descriptions and promotional information about the company’s institutional investment management services. The marketing unit then creates, produces, and distributes the communications pieces.

In addition, a unit within client communications prepares all responses for requests for proposals received from potential clients. A request for proposal (RFP) is a document that an organizational buyer submits to possible suppliers that provides detailed information about the organization’s needs and invites the supplier to bid on satisfying those needs. Investment professionals provide details to help marketers complete RFPs submitted by possible customers of their investment services.

Employees in an investment marketing group may also support training programs for an institutional investment product. For example, an insurance company might educate its own sales staff or the client’s staff about the different subaccounts available to variable annuity customers.

Outsourcing Management Duties

Some financial services companies manage their invested assets internally. However, other companies may not have the resources—or the desire—to hire investment professionals who have expertise in each aspect of investment management. These companies may rely on external vendors to perform certain investment management duties. The practice of hiring an external vendor to perform specified operational functions is called outsourcing.

A financial services company can partially or completely outsource investment management duties. Outsourcing is particularly attractive to smaller companies and companies that do not possess the internal expertise to manage specific types of assets.

EXAMPLE

Palvica Regional Bank is a small institutional investor with a limited investment staff: three portfolio managers, one investment analyst, and one securities trader. While the company’s investment professionals are skilled in managing securities issued in North America and Europe, they want to expand their portfolio holdings by adding securities issued in Asia.

Palvica’s investment team recognizes that it does not possess the expertise to invest in Asian securities. Therefore, the company hires Ukugo Investment Group, an investment management firm that specializes in selecting and managing Asian securities for institutional investors. By outsourcing to Ukugo, Palvica is able to broaden the types of assets it invests in on behalf of its clients.

 

Systems and Data

For investment professionals, constant access to real-time financial market information is critical. Financial markets are fluid, with prices changing throughout the day, so investment professionals can face an urgency to act on current financial market pricing. As a result, financial services companies must take special care to ensure that external data feeds continue without interruptions.

Institutional investors use systems and databases to collect, organize, and analyze information. Because financial services companies collect a large amount of information, they have corporate policies to manage and protect their data.

 

Sources of Data

Investment professionals use data that come from internal and external sources. Investment operations within the company generate some internal data. For example, companies keep records of their own account balances, asset values, executed trades, transaction costs, and cash inflows and outflows.

The company’s portfolio transactions generate other internal data. For instance, a company tracks changes in the composition of a portfolio’s holdings after recent purchases increase the percentage of one asset type relative to other asset types. Companies can calculate portfolio performance over varying lengths of time, from a single day to several decades.

Although internal data are useful for internal evaluation, investment professionals work primarily with data generated externally. In addition to real-time prices, investment professionals look at data related to financial markets, companies, industries, and market economies.

External data help investment professionals assess asset values and investment opportunities. For instance, many investors examine financial statements from publicly traded companies to determine those businesses’ financial health and growth prospects. Shares of companies that are poised to outperform their competitors could be attractive investments for institutional portfolios.

External data also help investment professionals make portfolio decisions. For example, studying historical patterns can be useful in predicting the direction of asset prices in the future. Therefore, many investment professionals use historical data to anticipate market trends, which enable them to position portfolios so as to profit from their insights.

An institutional investor that employs multiple investment analysts can deploy them to gather large quantities of data. However, many companies find it more efficient to use external vendors to acquire data for their analysts. Some vendors also provide services to organize and analyze the data they collect.

Bloomberg L.P. is a popular external data vendor for institutional investors. Bloomberg provides subscribers with access to real-time financial markets data through its proprietary computer system, known as the Bloomberg Terminal.

Bloomberg also offers financial news, analytics tools, trade execution capabilities, and electronic communication between subscribers.

 

Types of Systems and Databases

The volume and complexity of investment data require financial services companies to merge information from multiple internal and external data sources. To manage all of their gathered information, companies use information systems.

The investment function uses several types of information systems:

  • Investment accounting systems support general ledger processing, reporting to clients, and compliance with internal and external controls. These systems also generate sales proposals to institutional clients.

  • Trade management systems—also known as order management systems—are associated with trading platforms and support the processes of trade initiation, trade execution, and transaction recording. These systems allow traders to monitor and execute trades electronically in real time.

  • Portfolio management systems support investment management activities. These systems display cash balances and portfolio holdings, monitor investment transactions, track asset and portfolio market values, support portfolio performance measurement and analysis, generate investment reports, and comply with internal policies and external regulations.

  • Risk management and analysis systems support the processes for risk management, asset analysis, asset-liability management, and scenario analysis. Many risk analysis systems are dedicated to a single type of asset instead of all asset types.

 

Data Analysis

Investment professionals use data to help them discover investment opportunities. To draw relevant insights from their data, they conduct data analysis, which is a process of applying a variety of analytical techniques to collected data, and using the results of the analysis to formulate recommendations concerning a research objective.

Advancements in technology have made it easier for investment professionals to conduct quantitative research, which is research designed to generate concrete information about people and their behaviors that can be analyzed, summarized in the form of numbers, and easily communicated to others. For example, many computer programs now conduct data mining, a type of analysis in which numerical data are examined to uncover trends, patterns, and relationships. The ability to identify trends faster than other investors is a competitive advantage for an institutional investor.

Technology will continue to change investment management as analytical capabilities grow increasingly sophisticated. Computer scientists are working on artificial intelligence (AI)—the development of intelligent machines that work, “learn,” and react like humans. Applying AI to investing goes beyond tasking machines with sifting through enormous volumes of data; AI will help analysts interpret data, thus turning the data into useful information.

Because of advancements like AI, investment analysts will be able to spend more time on qualitative research, which is research that uses subjective data collection methods and produces results that are difficult to summarize in numerical form. Qualitative research examines what people think, how they feel about the subject under study, and the words they use to express those thoughts and feelings.

EXAMPLE

Heidi Nilsen, an investment analyst, is performing research on a company for a possible investment. Heidi has conducted quantitative research, including studying the company’s financial reports and the company’s sales trends relative to its competitors. She wants to conduct qualitative research to develop a sense for the level of confidence that the company’s managers have in its ability to increase product sales in the future.

Heidi watches a recent television interview that the company’s chief executive officer (CEO) gave to a business news channel. In addition to the substance of the CEO’s answers, Heidi studies the CEO’s tone of voice and body language during the interview, hoping to infer clues about the executive’s true feelings regarding his company’s prospects.

Because people can track and present data about investment performance in so many ways, investment professionals use data visualization tools to put data into context. A data visualization tool is a technology-driven graphical display that conveys an intuitive understanding of data.

Data visualization tools graphically present data in easy-to-understand formats—for example, with charts, graphs, and tables—making it easier to analyze and evaluate the performance of individual assets and investment portfolios. Figure 1.2 is an example of a chart that investors might use.

 

Data Governance

Financial services companies contain many functions—including the investment function—that rely heavily on information, so companies need internal guidelines for handling data. Data governance is the process of establishing organizational ownership and accountability for data so that business processes are optimized and data are secure and protected.

Data governance provides a framework for organizational decisions about how to manage data, realize value from it, minimize its cost and complexity, manage risk, and ensure compliance with ever-growing external requirements for information.

At some companies, data governance is the responsibility of one of the standing committees that report to a company’s board of directors. A standing committee can keep the board and executives informed of data governance issues that can affect the company’s performance.

Regardless of a company’s organizational structure, successful data governance requires oversight from senior managers to support efforts at the functional level and ensure close coordination with the information technology (IT) unit.

 
Information Security

For financial institutions, the unauthorized intrusion into a company’s information systems to move money out of accounts, compromise confidentiality of data, and engage in any number of other activities that could disrupt financial markets are of great concern. Therefore, holistic data governance involves establishing information security measures.

EXAMPLE

As a matter of company policy, Dexicos Investment Management restricts user access to its trade management system. Furthermore, users can only perform certain activities in the system depending on their role in the trading process:

  • Only traders can initiate and execute trades.
  • Portfolio managers and traders can view transaction information but cannot edit records.
  • Only trade settlement specialists and other investment operations professionals can enter and edit transaction records.

Analysis: By limiting access and user capabilities within the system, Dexicos prevents unauthorized users from viewing and manipulating information pertaining to the company’s trading activities.

The investment function is a particularly attractive target for cybercriminals who wish to steal funds or information, disrupt normal business operations, and destroy public confidence in financial markets. To fend off malicious cyberattacks, companies engage in a type of information security known as cybersecurity, which encompasses measures undertaken to protect a company’s computer networks, systems, and data from unauthorized access. The following insight provides examples of some cybersecurity practices a financial services company may use.

INSIGHT

Cybersecurity Practices for Financial Services Companies

A financial services company may implement any of the following cybersecurity practices:

  • Conduct annual risk assessments to identify internal and external threats to data security.
  • Address system vulnerabilities by keeping computer software up to date, installing software “patches” for vulnerable applications, and using firewalls, which are combinations of hardware and software that create electronic barriers between the public and private areas of an insurer’s systems.
  • Protect data through encryption, a type of technology that encodes data so that only an authorized person possessing the required hardware and/or software can decode that data.
  • Regularly create data backups, which are copies of computer data that are usually stored in offsite locations. Data backups prevent the permanent loss of important information and aid in restoring critical operations if a disruptive event occurs.
  • Restrict user access to systems and enhance user authentication measures. Authenticationis the technological process of ensuring that someone requesting or sending information is who he claims to be.
  • Regularly train company employees on cybersecurity threats, their role in managing information, and the company’s cybersecurity policies and procedures.
  • Establish a testing schedule to ensure that cybersecurity policies and procedures are working as intended.
  • Set up a committee to issue cybersecurity reports to the company’s senior managers and board of directors.
  • Create incident response policies to outline how the company will respond in the event of a successful breach of security.
  • Monitor and comply with applicable government regulations regarding cybersecurity.

External vendors pose a significant risk to a company’s cybersecurity. Even if a financial services company is vigilant about protecting its data, it cannot assume that its business partners are exercising the same level of care. Therefore, a comprehensive cybersecurity policy should include the monitoring of vendors, including examining the vendor’s security practices and establishing procedures for a coordinated response to cyberattacks to contain the damage of successful breaches.
