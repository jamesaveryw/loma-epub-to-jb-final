The Risk-Return Trade-Off

Investors must balance their desire to earn returns with their desire to limit risks. In investing, the risk-return trade-off is the relationship between risk and return in which higher risks offer the possibility of higher returns and lower risks tend to produce lower returns.

To achieve higher rates of return, investors typically have to accept more risk—in the form of uncertainty and volatility. However, future investment returns are not guaranteed; chasing potentially greater returns exposes an investor to potentially greater losses. Essentially, risk-return trade-off decisions involve exchanging safety for the possibility of larger returns.

Because different asset classes have different risk and return characteristics, the risk-return trade-off is one factor in asset class selection for investment portfolios. Some characteristics that indicate higher risk for an investment include

  • Longer investment holding periods

  • Lower credit ratings for borrowers

  • Higher interest rates charged to borrowers

  • Less knowledge about an investment’s risks

  • Less transparency in reporting about investment risks

  • Investments with low liquidity or low marketability

  • Loans made in the absence of security or collateral

  • Less information about or less certainty of income streams or cash flows

Each institutional investor sets its own policy for how much risk it is willing to accept to pursue a desired rate of return. In later chapters, you will read about how these policies are set at the corporate, functional, and operational levels. You will also read about specific calculations that investors use to adjust return measurements for the amounts of risk assumed.

For insurance companies, another type of risk-return trade-off to consider is the need to balance solvency and profitability. An insurer has to hold some money to ensure solvency, but needs to invest to improve its profitability. The trade-off involves assessing the appropriate amount of (1) capital to devote to each goal and (2) investment risk to assume. If an insurer

  • Holds too much money or does not assume enough investment risk, it could exceed its solvency goal, but profitability could be at risk

  • Invests too much money or assumes too much investment risk, it could exceed its profitability goal, but solvency could be at risk
