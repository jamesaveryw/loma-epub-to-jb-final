Hypothetical Security Evaluation and Selection Scenarios

Investors can use many kinds of criteria to evaluate assets, but many of them are asset-specific. This text does not discuss every possible criterion. Instead, we present a hypothetical portfolio and discuss how one portfolio manager might pick individual assets for certain asset classes. In each section, we present some of the criteria that investment professionals may use while evaluating and selecting an asset to represent a particular asset class. Although our examples are relatively simple, the decision-making process can be much more complex.

For each asset type, Tia’s investment team has performed research and identified potential investments. Based on the information that her investment analysts provide, Tia needs to select the assets that will position the portfolio to meet the company’s risk and return objectives.

In the following sections, Tia selects one corporate bond and one common stock using information that her analysts provide. While we describe Tia’s process for selecting two assets, note that she needs to select several assets to complete the portfolio’s construction. The order in which she selects assets could affect her selection criteria for subsequent assets. However, for simplicity, assume that her corporate bond selection does not affect her common stock selection, and vice versa.

Tia wants to choose the bond with the best combination of a long term to maturity, high yield to maturity, low duration, and high credit ratings. She evaluates the three bonds against her desired characteristics.

All three bonds have similar term-to-maturity length, although Bond C has the longest. While Bond C has the highest YTM, it also has the highest duration and lowest credit ratings, so Tia eliminates Bond C as a possibility.

Selecting a Common Stock

Tia needs to select a large-cap common stock that pays a dividend. The market capitalization, or market cap, is the total market value of all of a company’s outstanding shares of stock, which is calculated by multiplying the number of outstanding shares by the price per share. Typically, investors classify stocks as small-cap, mid-cap, or large-cap, depending on the issuing company’s market capitalization.

Tia’s investment team has researched several potential large-cap common stocks to evaluate the profitability and growth prospects of the issuing companies. To aid in analysis, the team used profitability ratios, which are financial ratios that measure a company’s profitability by comparing the company’s gain from operations to the resources employed or invested to earn the gain.

One commonly used profitability ratio is earnings per share (EPS), which is the portion of a company’s net profit after taxes that is attributable to each outstanding share of the company’s common stock. Higher EPS values indicate greater profitability. Investors calculate EPS according to the following formula:

Earnings per share (EPS) = Net profit after taxes
Number of outstanding common stock shares

For each stock issuer, Tia’s team also studied the company’s leverage, which—in the context of corporate finance—is a measure of the amount of debt a company is using to support its operations. If a company has too much leverage, it may not be able to keep up with the interest payments on its debt. Therefore, investment professionals use different leverage ratios to measure financial risk. A leverage ratio is a comparison of values that represent (1) the amount of a company’s debt obligations and (2) its ability to meet those obligations.

Tia’s team narrowed the list of large-cap common stocks to three possible choices. Tia requested information on each stock’s historical returns and standard deviations over the past 10 years. In addition, she requested each stock’s dividend yield, which is the ratio of a company’s annual dividend payments to its current market price. A stock’s dividend yield indicates the annual percentage return earned by the dividends paid on a share of stock. Investors calculate dividend yield as follows:

Dividend yield = Annual dividend amount paid per share
Market price per share

When market interest rates are low, stocks that have a high dividend yield can be an attractive investment alternative to debt securities, especially for investors seeking current income. Potential investors can compare a stock’s dividend yield to the market interest rate on a debt security to help determine which investment will likely provide the higher income yield.

Tia doesn’t want to pay too much for a stock with the desired risk and return characteristics, so she also wants to review some valuation ratios. A valuation ratio compares a share’s market price to another measure of a company’s ability to generate value for shareholders. Investment professionals use several types of valuation ratios to evaluate whether a particular stock’s shares are overvalued or undervalued.

One of the most common valuation ratios used in stock analysis is the price/earnings (P/E) ratio, which compares a stock’s market price per share to its earnings per share. The formula for the P/E ratio is:

Price/earnings (P/E) ratio = Market price per share
Earnings per share (EPS)

Investment professionals use the P/E ratio to determine if the market share price is reasonable relative to the earnings of the company. A stock may be overpriced if it has a high P/E value relative to the stocks of other companies in the same industry. Conversely, a stock with a relatively low P/E value may be a good value.
