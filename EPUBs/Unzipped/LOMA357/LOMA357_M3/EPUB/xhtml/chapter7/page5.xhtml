Fund Evaluation and Selection

In the previous section, you read about some of the ways that investment professionals can select investments for portfolios that support life insurer general account products. For separate account portfolios, insurance companies do not select investments on behalf of their customers. Instead, they are responsible for offering a menu of investment fund options called subaccounts, as we defined in Chapter 1. An insurer’s investment function evaluates and selects the subaccounts offered to separate account customers.

Traditionally, insurers structured subaccounts to operate like mutual funds. In recent years, as exchange-traded fund (ETF) use has generally increased, insurance companies are offering more subaccounts that are structured like ETFs. Because of operational differences, ETFs generally have lower costs than mutual funds that hold a similar group of securities.

An insurer’s subaccount menu can include both internally managed and externally managed fund options. The fund managers of external fund options are known as subadvisors. Typically, insurance companies enter into agreements with subadvisors to increase the number of investment options available to separate account customers. In many cases, a subadvised subaccount is managed to be nearly identical to a fund that the subadvisor offers its customers.

Subaccounts: Not Exactly the Funds They Mimic

Essentially, a subaccount is a clone of another fund. For instance, suppose Dexicos Life Insurance Company offers a subaccount based on Algravita Fund Company’s VITA 60/40 Fund. The subaccount is called Dexicos VITA 60/40 Fund to signify that it is a separate offering from the fund that Algravita, the subadvisor, offers to its customers.

Because of structural aspects that are beyond the scope of this text, a subaccount may experience slight differences in fees and performance when compared to the fund it is based on. Therefore, the historical performance of a subaccount is reported separately from that of the fund it mimics. For investors, such differences can complicate efforts to evaluate a subaccount and compare it to its peers.

Insurance companies want to ensure that their current subaccount menus meet the objectives of their separate accounts. Thus, investment professionals are continually evaluating their company’s collection of subaccounts as well as other funds that are potential candidates for inclusion in their offerings. The investment team can make recommendations for adding new subaccounts and removing existing subaccounts that no longer meet the company’s needs. Typically, subaccount recommendations are supported by due diligence and must comply with regulatory standards.

Insurers set corporate policies to guide decisions about their subaccount menus. Such policies set constraints on the menu of fund options and their internal and external administration. These constraints typically address

  • The assortment of proposed fund categories

  • Number of funds permitted in each category

  • Procedures for approving and removing fund options

  • Operational controls and limitations

EXAMPLE

Sample Policy Constraint on External Fund Options

To be considered for inclusion in the subaccount menu, an externally managed fund must have existed for a minimum of three years and hold assets in excess of $100 million. In addition, the fund must maintain a consistent management philosophy, as indicated by adherence to the fund style outlined in its prospectus.

Investment professionals evaluate a variety of factors to evaluate and select fund options for their subaccount menus. Some factors, such as historical returns and return volatility, have been covered in previous chapters. Other notable factors are the fund’s classification, investment style, management team, expense ratio, ratings, and comparison to fund peers.

 

Fund Classifications and Investment Styles

Financial services companies have many options for classifying mutual funds and ETFs. In addition, some fund providers include multiple classifications in one name.

EXAMPLE

Dexicos Investment Management offers a mutual fund called Dexicos International Large-Cap Growth Fund. The fund invests internationally in equities with a large market capitalization and a growth orientation.

Investment professionals usually classify funds according to observable characteristics. Some characteristics are subjective, so classifications can be somewhat arbitrary. Notable methods of fund classification include

  • Asset class

  • Management approach

  • Investment style

  • Market capitalization

  • Asset allocation

  • Geography

  • Sector

In fund investing, style boxes graphically represent fund information. Investors can use style boxes to quickly recognize a fund’s classification. We discuss style boxes later in this chapter.

 

Asset Class

Funds can be classified by asset classes and sub-asset classes. Three common classifications by asset class are equity, fixed-income, and absolute return:

  • The assets of equity funds, also called stock funds, typically consist of common stocks, but may also include preferred stocks or real estate. Equity funds are often categorized by market cap, industry sector, and/or geographic location. The fund in the previous example, the Dexicos International Large-Cap Growth Fund, is an equity fund.

  • Fixed-income funds typically consist of specified debt instruments and are categorized by the specified type of debt instrument. Further classifications include (1) type of debt instrument held, (2) average term to maturity of held assets, (3) credit quality, and (4) geographic location. For example, a fixed-income fund may be categorized as a government bond fund, a long-term bond fund, or an investment-grade bond fund. A multisector bond fund invests in bonds from several fixed-income sectors—including U.S. government obligations, U.S. corporate bonds, foreign bonds, and high-yield U.S. debt securities.

  • Absolute return funds seek to achieve positive returns in all market conditions by investing in a variety of asset classes—including alternative investments and derivatives—and employing alternative investment management techniques.

 

Management Approach

Depending on the management approach of the fund managers, investors can broadly categorize a fund as

  • Active. In the context of fund investing, an active management strategy attempts to outperform the average market return earned by fund peers. However, an active management strategy risks underperforming the average market return.

  • Passive. In the context of fund investing, a passive management strategy attempts to earn the average market return that fund peers earn. Compared to active management, passive management typically involves longer holding periods and less frequent trading, which results in lower transaction costs. Because a passive management strategy is characterized by a low portfolio turnover rate, it is also known as a buy-and-hold strategy. The portfolio turnover rate is the rate at which the fund buys or sells securities, expressed as a percentage of average total assets.

The differences in the active and passive management approaches can be seen in the way that a fund manager treats portfolio holdings. For instance, an active manager may trade a bond before maturity if the bond’s current price is above par value. In contrast, a passive manager usually intends to hold a bond to maturity. Note that in both cases, the fund manager is constantly monitoring the bond holding; a passive management strategy does not mean that the fund manager ignores the bond until maturity.

Typically, investors judge actively and passively managed funds against market indexes. In addition, many passively managed funds are constructed to replicate indexes. Such funds, called index funds, seek to produce a pattern of performance similar to that of a specified market index.

EXAMPLE

The Dexicos S&P 500 Index Fund is a mutual fund that invests in stocks in the S&P 500 Index. The fund is constructed to closely track the index’s returns. Thus, fund investors earn roughly the same rate of return as the S&P 500 Index.

 

Investment Style

One of the most common fund classifications is by investment style. For example, investors typically divide equity funds into

  • Growth funds, which invest in stocks of companies with the potential for above-average growth, resulting in capital appreciation. Growth stocks typically have high P/E ratio values and pay little or no dividends.

  • Value funds, which invest in stocks of companies that are undervalued in relation to the overall market and that often pay significant dividends. Value stocks are generally associated with patient investors who will wait for a stock’s market price to rise to the value they believe it deserves. Compared to growth funds, value funds usually have lower price volatility. In addition, value funds tend to outperform growth funds during economic recessions. On the other hand, growth funds usually outperform value funds during periods of economic expansion.

  • Core funds, or blend funds, are a type of fund that seeks growth and value stocks. Some fund providers also use the term “core fund” to indicate funds that can invest in both stocks and bonds.

Other fund styles are designed to achieve a certain objective. For instance, income fundsare dedicated to earning a reliable income stream from investments that provide interest, dividend payments, or rent. Stable value funds are conservative funds that focus on capital preservation and offer a guaranteed minimum rate of return plus the potential for excess interest crediting. Market Capitalization

Funds, particularly equity funds, can be categorized by the size of the companies they invest in:

  • Small-cap funds invest in companies with a small market capitalization—for example, less than $2 billion.

  • Mid-cap funds invest in companies with a medium market capitalization—for example, $2 billion to $10 billion.

  • Large-cap funds invest in companies with a large market capitalization—for example, greater than $10 billion.

The exact amounts for each capitalization level are not universally recognized. In addition, some investors recognize additional market cap sizes. For instance, some consider micro-cap stocks to have capitalizations below $300 million and mega-cap stocks to have capitalizations above $100 billion.

 

Asset Allocation

Some funds are categorized according to the asset allocation they maintain. Each asset allocation category tailors the fund’s asset allocation to a certain level of risk tolerance.

A lifestyle fund, also known as an asset allocation fund or risk-based fund, has the objective of maintaining a specific level of risk at all times. The three main types of lifestyle funds are

  • Aggressive allocation funds, designed for investors with high risk tolerances

  • Moderate allocation funds, designed for investors with moderate risk tolerances

  • Conservative allocation funds, designed for investors with low risk tolerances

While lifestyle funds generally maintain a specific risk level, other funds adjust their allocations in anticipation of steady changes in an investor’s risk tolerance. Target-date funds, also known as lifecycle funds, invest in various asset classes and automatically change the asset allocation over a period of time. Typically, a target-date fund is designed with an end date in mind. The main objective of a target-date fund is to support an investor’s goal of accumulating a certain amount of money by the specified date.

Initially, a target-date fund has a relatively aggressive allocation—for example, the fund might hold a high percentage of equity investments. As the fund’s target date approaches, the fund’s allocation gradually becomes more conservative and its risk level gradually decreases. Because of the feature that automatically shifts allocations over time, target-date funds are popular in the retirement services industry.

 

Geography

Funds that invest in securities issued by companies with international operations are classified according to the country or region in which those companies are located. For instance,

  • Global funds, also known as world funds, invest in companies located both in the investor’s home country and beyond; for a U.S. investor, global funds can include companies located in the United States and outside of the United States

  • International funds, also known as foreign funds, invest exclusively in companies located outside of the investor’s home country; for a U.S. investor, international funds exclude companies located in the United States but can include companies located anywhere else

  • Regional funds invest in companies located in only a particular geographic region—such as Europe, Asia, or Latin America

  • Country funds invest in companies located in one particular country

  • Emerging market funds invest in companies located in developing countries, which are typically countries with low levels of income per citizen and little industrialization

 

Sector

Sector funds invest in companies in a particular sector or industry. Common types of sector funds include communications funds, energy funds, financial services funds, health care funds, and technology funds. Sector funds provide less diversification than other types of funds but can generate higher returns when the particular sector performs well.

Morningstar, a company that provides investment research services, popularized the use of style boxes in its fund ratings. The Morningstar Style Box is a nine-square grid that provides a graphical representation of a fund’s investment style. The appropriate box on the grid is blackened or checked to provide a quick visual cue as to the fund’s basic style classification. Management Team

Investment professionals look at a fund’s management team to evaluate whether its managers can achieve the fund’s objectives. Examples of questions that investment professionals may ask during fund analysis include

  • Does the management team have a well-developed, consistent investment process?

  • How closely does the management team adhere to the fund’s stated style?

  • Does the management team’s style result in excessive portfolio turnover?

  • Do the managers invest their personal money in this fund?

  • How many years has the current management team managed this fund?

  • Has the fund changed managers at any point in the last several years?

Investment professionals may have the opportunity to interview key management team members. In this case, the investment professionals hope to learn more about the management team’s decision-making process. They also investigate a fund manager’s administrative support systems, operations, procedures, and personnel. In particular, they examine the fund’s

  • Methods of pricing securities and creating daily fund values

  • Trading systems and practices

  • Accounting, recordkeeping, and financial reporting systems

  • Internal risk management framework

  • Ethics policy

  • Regulatory compliance function

 

Expense Ratios

Investment professionals also look at a fund’s expense ratio, which expresses the fund’s total annual operating expenses during a given period as a percentage of the fund’s average net assets during the same period. Fund operating expenses can include expenses related to auditing, accounting and other recordkeeping, taxes, custody services, paying the fund managers, and marketing.

Because expenses reduce the potential returns for fund investors, investment professionals generally prefer funds with low expense ratios. Investment professionals often compare a particular fund’s expense ratio to those of other funds in its peer group. A peer group is a collection of funds that have the same style, share similar risk characteristics, and are competitors within their market niche.

In general, actively managed funds have higher expense ratios than passively managed funds. Actively managed funds charge higher management fees to compensate for the additional resources used in pursuit of market outperformance. In contrast, passively managed funds generally charge lower management fees because they simply attempt to earn the average market return.

 

Fund Ratings

Institutions may not rely solely on their own investment team’s findings for fund analysis. External fund advisory services provide ratings, research, and commentary for fund investors. Thus, investment professionals can review external fund ratings to supplement their own research and analysis.

For some institutional investors—such as insurance companies—incorporating external ratings is part of responsible due diligence when offering funds as investment options for customers. In addition, using external fund ratings helps institutions avoid the appearance of conflict of interest in analyzing the funds that they offer to customers. For instance, an insurance company that also serves as a fund provider could appear to favor internally managed funds in its subaccount menu. However, by using independent fund analysis to support its subaccount selections, the insurer can maintain the appearance of impartiality.

 

Fund Comparisons

Investment professionals typically compare a fund’s performance to the performance of a benchmark index, such as a market index. They also compare the total returns of a particular fund to the total returns of other funds in the same peer group.

Through these comparisons, investment professionals can evaluate a fund’s relative performance. Investment professionals generally prefer funds that consistently outperform the benchmark and most of the peer group over a period long enough to represent varying market conditions—such as a 10-year period.

One tool that investment professionals use to support fund comparisons is a scorecard, which is a table or worksheet used for analyzing or comparing various funds. Investment professionals use a scorecard as part of identifying fund option candidates, as well as for ongoing monitoring of a company’s menu of fund options. Figure 7.6 presents an example of a scorecard.

Scorecards grade a group of funds on a set of measures, such as the expense ratio or 5-year return. For each criterion, funds are ranked from best to worst based on their score. Some scorecards assign funds a numerical ranking, while others assign a letter grade or multiple stars.

Scoring and ranking of funds can indicate the quality of a particular fund compared to its peer group and benchmark. In addition, some scorecards calculate the average score for all funds in a peer group, and then group them into percentiles or quartiles.

Investment professionals can use scorecards to screen new funds for possible inclusion in a company’s menu of fund options. The relative rankings can quickly identify funds that are unsuitable for inclusion.
