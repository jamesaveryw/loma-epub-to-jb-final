Strategies for Managing Specific Risks

In Chapter 4, we discussed the risks associated with investing and specifically about the two types of risks:

  • Systematic risks, which are nondiversifiable and can affect every investment in a given market

  • Specific risks, which are diversifiable and only affect certain investments in a given market

For investors, systematic risks are unavoidable. To have the opportunity to earn investment returns, investors have to accept exposure to certain market risks, such as interest-rate changes or inflation. Thus, portfolio managers focus their risk management efforts on the types of risks they can control—the specific risks within a particular portfolio.

A portfolio manager can apply diversification and hedging strategies to reduce a portfolio’s overall risk. As you learned in Chapter 2,

  • Diversification spreads out risk by investing in a variety of assets with different risk characteristics

  • Hedging balances one risk with a complementary risk that will ideally offset the original risk

 

Diversification

Typically, the primary way to diversify a portfolio is by including multiple asset classes—ideally, asset classes with negative correlations to the other classes in the portfolio. In the last chapter, we explained how asset allocation incorporates diversification among asset classes. We also mentioned that investment professionals can further diversify a portfolio by investing among various sub-asset classes within an asset class.

In addition to asset class diversification, investment professionals can diversify portfolios in many other ways. For instance, they can diversify a portfolio’s investments through different

  • Investment vehicles—such as splitting a bond allocation between individual bonds and bond exchange traded funds (ETFs)

  • Investment styles—such as holding both growth and value stocks

  • Sectors or industries—such as holding corporate bonds from the industrial, financial, and health care sectors

  • Income streams—such as holding assets that generate interest income, dividend income, and rental income

  • Market caps—such as holding small-cap and large-cap stocks

  • Maturities—such as holding short-term, intermediate-term, and long-term bonds

  • Geographic regions—such as holding securities issued in North America, Europe, Africa, and Asia

Regardless of the manner of diversifying, the goal is the same: to include investments that reduce a portfolio’s exposure to the specific risks related to its existing investments. You’ve heard the adage “Don’t put all your eggs in one basket.” Diversification places portfolio investments in multiple “baskets,” or asset categories, so the portfolio is less dependent on the success of any one basket. In other words, diversification reduces the portfolio’s asset concentration risk, which is the risk that results when investments are concentrated excessively in any single category.

EXAMPLE

Laurie Bordeaux is a portfolio manager for an institutional investor that is building an investment portfolio of Canadian real estate properties. The portfolio currently owns one property, a multifamily residential property in Vancouver. Because the real estate portfolio consists of one property, it is entirely dependent on that property’s value. Thus, the portfolio’s risk is concentrated.

Analysis: The portfolio’s real estate property could lose value for a variety of reasons—damage from a fire or flood, increased competition from a new apartment complex built nearby, or a broad downturn in the Vancouver economy. To diversify the portfolio, Laurie could purchase properties with different characteristics—such as residential properties in different sizes, styles, and locations. To further diversify, Laurie could purchase nonresidential properties outside of Vancouver. For example, she could purchase an office building in Montreal or an agricultural property near Winnipeg.

Diversification is a long-term portfolio management strategy. Over time, proper diversification reduces specific risks so that the majority of the portfolio’s remaining risk is made up of systematic risk. Figure 8.1 illustrates this concept.

 

Hedging

An investor can use a hedge to protect against an adverse outcome that results in a financial loss. An effective hedge balances out an investment loss with an increase in the hedge’s value. By counterbalancing a potential loss, a hedge works to reduce a portfolio’s overall risk.

In practice, however, an investment professional may have a difficult time creating a hedge that perfectly offsets a potential loss. Therefore, hedging is generally an investment risk reduction strategy, not a risk elimination strategy.

In contrast to diversification, hedging is a short-term portfolio management strategy. Investment professionals use hedges to offset adverse price movements in their investments within given periods. After a period of time, a hedge may lose its effectiveness, requiring additional action.

Investment professionals use hedges to protect against specific risks—such as currency risk or interest-rate risk. As introduced in Chapter 4, currency risk arises from changes in currency exchange rates, and interest-rate risk arises from changes in market interest rates. Such changes could cause particular investments in a portfolio to lose value. Thus, hedging those investments reduces the potential loss for the investor.

Derivatives are one of the popular investment vehicles for creating hedges. As we mentioned in Chapter 3, derivatives derive their values from underlying assets. For example, the value of a particular put option on a common stock depends on the fluctuating market price of the stock’s shares. Because of the relationship between a derivative and its underlying asset, an investment professional can position a derivative to gain in value as the underlying asset loses value, thus reducing the effect of the underlying asset’s lost value.

EXAMPLE

An investor, Helen Flynn, holds shares of a stock and a put option on the same stock. The put option gives Helen the right to sell shares at the strike price until the option expires.

Analysis: If the price of Helen’s stock shares falls below the strike price, then exercising the option will limit her loss. Note that as the stock price falls further below the strike price, the put option becomes more valuable to the option holder—which, in this case, is Helen.

Derivative values can also be based on other financial market values, such as exchange rates or interest rates. For instance, an interest-rate swap between two parties exchanges the cash flows based on a fixed interest rate with the cash flows based on a variable, or floating, interest rate. Because the assets and liabilities of life insurance companies are sensitive to interest-rate fluctuations, many insurers use interest-rate swaps to hedge interest-rate risk.

EXAMPLE

Dexicos Life Insurance Company wants to hedge against the risk of falling market interest rates. Specifically, Dexicos wants to ensure that it can receive a fixed stream of cash flows even as it reinvests money from maturing bonds into bonds with lower coupon rates. Therefore, Dexicos enters into an interest-rate swap agreement with Palance Bank based on a specified principal amount.

Analysis: Dexicos holds a bond with a floating coupon rate that is pegged to changes in the Treasury bill rate. Dexicos pays Palance the interest payments that Dexicos receives from this bond. Meanwhile, Palance pays Dexicos the interest payments it receives from a long-term bond it holds that has a fixed coupon rate. Because Palance’s long-term bond pays a consistent interest rate, the interest payments are a steady stream of cash that Palance is paying to Dexicos. Dexicos uses the payments to ensure that it can match its asset and liability cash flows, thus hedging against the risk of falling interest rates.

Traditionally, derivatives were the primary instruments for hedging a portfolio’s investments. However, as ETF offerings grow more diverse, some sophisticated ETFs allow investors to hedge against specific risks, such as interest-rate risk or currency risk. Because ETFs are generally cheaper and more liquid than derivatives, ETFs are appealing options for institutional investors looking to create hedges in their portfolios.
