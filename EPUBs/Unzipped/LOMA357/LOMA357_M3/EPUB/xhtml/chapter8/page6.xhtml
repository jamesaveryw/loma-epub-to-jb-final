Operational Controls

Generally, internal controls are the steps a company takes to encourage adherence to its management policies, promote operational efficiency, and safeguard the organization’s assets. Within the investment function of an institutional investor, internal controls ensure that investment activities are handled consistently, accurately, and appropriately. Thus, portfolio managers, traders, and other investment professionals should understand and follow the internal controls that apply to investment operations. Examples of these operational controls include the following:

  • Investment procedures should be clearly documented

  • Investment professionals should use appropriate research and analysis to support investment transaction decisions

  • Traders should obtain proper authorization for asset purchases and sales

  • Transaction costs for trades should be consistent with company policies for best execution and expense management

  • Investment transactions should be recorded accurately and promptly

  • Documentation pertaining to investment activities should be retained for potential audits and other purposes

In Chapter 1, you learned that financial services companies segregate duties as a form of internal control. As a result, in the context of investment operations, investment professionals making transaction decisions cannot carry out those transactions or maintain the transaction records. Different individuals must perform all three activities to properly segregate the responsibilities.

EXAMPLE

Rita, a portfolio manager for an institutional investor, evaluates the bond market and decides to purchase a particular bond that meets the objectives of a given portfolio. She instructs Geno, the team’s trader, to purchase the bond for the portfolio. After Geno has executed the trade, he communicates the details to Veronica, a trade settlement specialist. Veronica works with operations professionals to ensure that the trade is completed and properly recorded in the company’s portfolio management and investment accounting systems. Later, operations professionals compare the internal record to the external statement generated by the broker whom Geno used to execute the trade.

Analysis: No one individual performs multiple roles in this transaction. For instance, Geno cannot decide to purchase any bond he likes for the portfolio. He receives instructions from Rita, the portfolio manager, who is responsible for assessing whether the bond is suitable for the portfolio. Likewise, Geno cannot record the trade with a different price than he actually paid. Veronica and the other specialists are responsible for recording the transaction as it occurred and verifying the accuracy of the company’s records.

Some operational controls apply to particular investment vehicles. For example, an insurance company develops internal standards for engaging in derivatives transactions as part of its risk management framework. In the remainder of this chapter, we briefly discuss notable operational controls for private placements, dividends, and real estate holdings.

 

Controls for Private Placements

Life insurance companies and other institutional investors establish specific operational controls for private placement bonds in their portfolios. One important control is monitoring the credit quality of such bonds. Without public rating agencies to provide supporting information, investors must perform extensive credit analysis on private placement bond issuers.

Examples of the types of controls that institutions use for their private placement bond holdings include

  • Calculating the percentage of private placement bonds in the total bond portfolio and comparing the result to the authorized maximum percentage of such bonds

  • Ensuring the initial and ongoing creditworthiness of issuers

  • Regularly monitoring the issuers for adherence to any bond covenants

  • Performing credit analysis of private placement bonds to uncover any evidence of deteriorating investment quality

  • Regularly reporting the results of bond and issuer analysis to an internal committee that oversees investments

  • Periodically submitting up-to-date private placement bond information to appropriate regulatory authorities

 

Controls for Dividends

To the extent possible, institutional stockholders use automation to track and record dividend collections. Operational controls associated with dividend collection include showing that dividend income is

  • Received and collected as it was declared

  • Collected in a timely fashion

  • Recorded properly and accurately in the company’s systems

  • Properly valued, in the case of stock dividends

  • Accurately reported in all required financial reporting

 

Controls for Real Estate Properties

Institutional investors with large real estate holdings may have several internal departments for property management or may hire external parties for management. In this section, we present control procedures for arrangements in which an investor hires an external property management company.

Institutional investors design controls for real estate properties to ensure that

  • Properties are properly safeguarded

  • Rental income payments are promptly and accurately applied

  • Expenditures are legitimate

  • Property managers follow approved procedures

  • Cash flows to investors are timely and correct

  • Property upkeep is adequate

The institutional investor and the property management company usually establish a system for paying management and maintenance expenses and depositing the income that a property generates. Typically, a property manager does one of the following:

  • Deducts expenses from rental income. In most instances, the property manager pays the maintenance expenses from the monthly rental income it collects, and then remits—or sends as payment—the remaining income to the investor.

  • Receives an expense allowance. At the beginning of each month, the investor sends the property manager an allowance for expenses. In this case, all income from the property is deposited into the investor’s remittance account.

Institutional investors conduct audits that examine the property management company’s financial records and compare them to the institution’s records. Auditors also (1) review the property manager’s operating procedures to ensure that it has complied with the management agreement and (2) examine the physical condition of properties under management.
