Monitoring Investment Portfolios

Investment professionals monitor institutional investment portfolios to ensure that the investments are being managed responsibly. They monitor portfolios for investment style drift, allocations that require rebalancing, asset-liability management considerations, and compliance to policies and regulations.

 

Style Drift

Many investment portfolios designate an investment style in an investment policy statement (IPS) or other type of planning document. For example, many mutual funds designate an investment style in the fund’s prospectus. Thus, potential and current investors in a given fund typically expect the fund’s managers to pursue investments that fit the fund’s stated style.

Over time, a portfolio manager may attempt to improve the portfolio’s returns by selecting investments that do not align with the portfolio’s designated investment style. The result is style drift, which is the divergence of a portfolio from its stated investment objective or style. Although many investors think of style drift as a concern primarily for investment funds, any portfolio with an established style can experience style drift. Investment professionals analyze portfolio holdings to determine the magnitude of a particular portfolio’s style shift.

Investors need to know about style drift because it could indicate that a portfolio manager is not following a portfolio’s management guidelines. Eventually, style drift could result in a portfolio that does not meet an investor’s objectives. For instance, current investors in a fund experiencing style drift may find that the fund is no longer suitable as an investment.

EXAMPLE

As stated in its prospectus, the Dabbledus Equity Value Fund selects value stocks that fit certain criteria for inclusion in the fund’s investment portfolio. Over the past three years, the Dabbledus Equity Value Fund has consistently underperformed a market index that serves as the fund’s benchmark. As a result, the fund’s manager is feeling pressure from disappointed investors to provide strong performance in the near future. Thus, the manager starts to invest in a small number of stocks that fit some, but not all, of the fund’s criteria, in order to benefit from those stocks’ favorable recent performance. In addition, the manager is experimenting with adding growth stocks in an attempt to enhance the portfolio’s overall returns.

Analysis: The manager of the Dabbledus Equity Value Fund is gradually investing in stocks that exhibit different characteristics than the portfolio indicated it would hold. Over time, the fund’s actual style is drifting from its intended style. Once investors notice the style drift, some may discontinue investing in the fund because the fund’s actual style is not appropriate for their investment objective.

Style drift can occur for a number of reasons. In some cases, a portfolio’s management team may change and the new management’s interpretation of the stated objectives or style differs from that of the former managers. In other cases, such as the above example, portfolio managers intentionally extend beyond established style parameters in an effort to improve portfolio returns.

If style drift occurs because portfolio managers are chasing better performance, the portfolio’s actual risk-return profile may not match the intended profile described in the IPS. This inconsistency can hurt the investor because the portfolio’s investments are not properly aligned with its risk and return objectives.

Style drift is particularly relevant to investment funds, such as mutual funds or exchange traded funds (ETFs). For example, many fund analysts look for how closely a fund’s actual style is aligned with the style stated in its prospectus. Funds with more consistent management styles are evaluated more favorably. External fund rating agencies also evaluate investment styles when determining fund ratings.

Actively managed investment funds are generally more susceptible to style drift than passively managed funds. Actively managed funds typically trade more frequently, which provides them with more opportunities to deviate from their stated investment styles. However, passively managed funds can also drift over time. For instance, a passively managed small-cap stock fund may hold stocks that gradually increase in value enough to become classified as mid-cap stocks. If the fund continues to hold such stocks, its portfolio will drift from its small-cap focus.

For an institutional portfolio, the investment style is derived from the objectives and constraints described in the IPS. An inconsistent style could indicate that the portfolio is not properly focused on achieving its objectives. Therefore, investment professionals monitor portfolios for signs of style drift.

EXAMPLE

An insurance company’s investment portfolio has an IPS that states the portfolio’s bond investment style. Although portfolio managers have some discretion, the portfolio’s bond investments are generally held until maturity.

Analysis: If the portfolio managers are too aggressive in trading bonds before maturity, then they are at risk of producing style drift. Therefore, they may track the average holding period for their bond investments to ensure that the majority are managed according to the stated style.

 

Rebalancing

Investment professionals monitor a portfolio’s asset allocations. As mentioned in Chapter 5, when a portfolio’s allocations deviate too far from the strategic asset allocation, investment professionals can rebalance the portfolio to bring its asset weightings back to the intended proportions.

Most institutional portfolios include instructions for periodic rebalancing in their IPS. Some portfolios may rebalance on a quarterly basis, while others may only rebalance once per year. In addition, portfolio policies may describe asset allocation criteria, including designated allocation limits. If a portfolio’s allocations exceed its limits, investment professionals may be required to rebalance it immediately, regardless of its next scheduled rebalancing date.

 

ALM Analysis

Some life insurance companies monitor their investment portfolios as part of their asset-liability management (ALM) efforts. For insurers, ALM-related investment monitoring could include

  • Reviewing the investment strategy for the general account and each product segment to ensure that each strategy appropriately addresses investment risk

  • Reviewing investment income allocations to ensure proper allocations to each product segment based on the designated allocation method

  • Verifying, for a sample of investment transactions, that each transaction was assigned to the appropriate product segment

Some aspects of investment-related ALM analysis are prompted by regulatory review. For example, regulators generally conduct cash-flow testing and scenario testing. Note that while regulators may test cash flows annually, an insurer’s actuaries may test more frequently. If necessary, actuaries can work with portfolio managers to adjust investment strategies to support a product’s cash flow needs.

 

Compliance

For an institutional portfolio, investment professionals monitor the portfolio to ensure that it complies with the portfolio’s investment policy, the institution’s internal policies, and any applicable industry regulations.

 

Investment Policy Statement

In Chapter 5, we introduced the IPS as a written document that describes a portfolio’s guidelines regarding risk and return objectives, investment constraints, asset allocation, and performance evaluation. The IPS outlines the responsibilities of the portfolio management team. Thus, the team needs to evaluate its activities to ensure that its actions are appropriate as described in the IPS. For example, the portfolio management team monitors the portfolio’s levels of risk against the risk appetite, risk tolerance, and risk limits established in the IPS.

 

Internal Policies

Investment professionals also monitor a portfolio to comply with an institution’s internal policies, such as its risk policy and investment policy. As described in Chapter 5, the risk policy generally outlines the process for managing risk, and the investment policy generally outlines a set of formal rules and broad guidelines for investing. These policies may be more or less restrictive than a particular portfolio’s IPS.

For financial services companies, corporate oversight of risk management includes ALM. For instance, an insurance company’s risk committee reviews the results of cash-flow testing. This committee also reviews internal reports, such as the duration gap report. Therefore, investment professionals monitor investments for their potential impact on ALM efforts and may have to justify certain investment decisions to oversight committees.

Some institutional investors have policies regarding due diligence for investments. For example, investment professionals conduct ongoing due diligence on investment funds for insurance companies that select externally managed options for their subaccount menus. The investment professionals carefully and thoroughly investigate whether a fund manager is adhering to the objectives described in the fund’s prospectus. They also research any turnover in the fund management team and analyze the fund’s operations.

The insurer’s investment professionals also monitor the performance of all subaccounts—whether they are managed internally or externally—to ensure that the offerings are meeting the objectives of the corresponding separate account products. We cover evaluation of fund performance later in this chapter.

If, over several reporting periods, an externally managed fund’s investment performance fails to meet minimum performance standards for returns, risk, and risk-adjusted returns, investment professionals investigate the reasons for the subpar performance. Insurers need to replace funds that no longer meet the objectives of their separate accounts. Prior to removing a fund from their subaccount offerings, an insurer typically places the fund on a watch list, which is a list of funds that require heightened monitoring and must subsequently meet specified performance requirements.

EXAMPLE

At Dexicos Life Insurance Company, any fund that underperforms its benchmark for 10 consecutive quarters is placed on a watch list. Dexicos offers the Dabbledus Equity Value Fund as a subaccount option for its variable annuity customers. The Dabbledus Equity Value Fund consistently underperforms its benchmark for 10 quarters and is placed on the company’s watch list. The fund is closely monitored and will be replaced if it fails to meet performance requirements in the next two quarters.

Life insurance companies that offer fund options for separate account products proactively identify and monitor at least one candidate with the potential to replace each fund currently on their menu. Therefore, companies always have a suitable replacement fund ready if an offered fund underperforms. If a fund on a watch list is later removed, the replacement fund takes its place on the menu.

Typically, companies have procedures for placing a fund on a watch list and for removing it as an offering. For insurers, such a procedure usually requires notifying all parties affected by the action, including fund managers and separate account customers. When replacing a fund option, an insurer typically moves all of the money invested in the removed fund to the fund replacing it.

 

Regulations

Finally, investment professionals monitor portfolios to ensure compliance with regulations, which we cover in depth in the next chapter. For example, insurance company portfolios in the United States are subject to federal securities regulations and state and federal insurance industry regulations.

Regulators usually want to see documentation regarding a portfolio management team’s investment decisions or decision-making process. In addition, regulators typically review records of executed trades and examine investment operations practices. Therefore, investment professionals typically document portfolio activities to demonstrate their regulatory compliance.
