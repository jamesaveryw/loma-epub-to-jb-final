Ethics for Investment Professionals

For companies in any industry, ethical behavior is essential to gaining and keeping the trust of customers. In general, poor ethical standards can result in

  • Customer dissatisfaction with a company’s products or services

  • Distrust of a company and its employees, which may result in a tarnished reputation and loss of business

  • Loss of licensing or barring of a professional from industry participation

  • Customer lawsuits and increased regulatory scrutiny

  • Fines or jail time, depending on whether the unethical behavior also breached any laws

Following ethical standards does not limit a company’s competitiveness. In fact, within the financial services industry, a company’s reputation for high ethical standards can be a competitive advantage. Building a stellar reputation is critical for financial services companies to stand out as their products become more commoditized in the minds of consumers. Commoditizationis the process by which a product reaches a point in its development where it has no features that differentiate it from competitive products other than price. Because many financial services customers don’t recognize the differences between similar products, a company can distinguish itself based on its reputation for quality and fairness. Thus, ethical standards can help companies attract and retain customers.

Regulations and ethical standards both protect the interests of financial services customers. In general, regulations are rules that instruct you on what you must or must not do, and ethical standards are guidelines that advise you on what you should or should not do.

EXAMPLE

A regulation requires an investment management firm to send portfolio performance reports to its clients at least annually. If the company does not meet the requirement in a given year, it is in violation of the regulation and is subject to regulatory punishment.

Ethical standards obligate the company to provide information that helps clients understand their performance reports, even though it is not required to do so. For instance, the company discloses that its performance data include transaction costs; this disclosure provides clients with additional context for assessing their investment returns.

At times, regulations and ethical guidelines overlap to affect the behavior of investment professionals. For instance, investment professionals that act as fiduciaries have regulatory and ethical considerations.

Clients entrust investment professionals with their money and expect those professionals to apply care and skill in handling their investments. Across the industry, many investment professionals adhere to codes of conduct to maintain customer trust. In addition, ethical standards often include guidelines regarding conflicts of interest, trading practices, and responsibilities to clients.

 

Codes of Conduct

To promote consistent ethical behaviors among particular groups of professionals, some organizations designate sets of shared ethical standards. For example, a company’s code of conduct, also known as a code of business conduct or a code of ethics, formally states its values and its expectations for how its employees should behave in the course of business.

A corporate code of conduct typically illustrates situations that employees might encounter in their work environment and helps employees evaluate the appropriateness of various responses to a given situation. For instance, a company can place a limit on the maximum monetary value of gifts that its employees are allowed to accept from customers and business partners to prevent inappropriate influence.

Sample Code of Conduct Statement

An employee may accept gifts during the course of employment only if the gift is valued at $50 or less. In addition, an employee may never accept gifts during a bidding process or contract negotiation.

Financial services companies usually have several codes of conduct. For instance, an insurance company may have (1) a general code for all employees and (2) supplemental codes tailored to employees working in specific functions, such as the investment function.

In addition to corporate codes of conduct, investment professionals typically follow professional codes of conduct. Such codes apply to members of the investment profession who agree to support the standards as part of maintaining their professional standing. The insight below presents one of the most prominent codes for investment professionals, which comes from the CFA Institute. The CFA Institute is an international organization that promotes a global set of investment industry standards for ethics and professional conduct. In addition, the CFA Institute offers education programs, such as the Chartered Financial Analyst (CFA®) designation.

INSIGHT

The CFA Institute Code of Ethics

Members of the CFA Institute (including CFA charterholders) and candidates for the CFA designation (“Members and Candidates”) must:

  • Act with integrity, competence, diligence, respect, and in an ethical manner with the public, clients, prospective clients, employers, employees, colleagues in the investment profession, and other participants in the global capital markets.
  • Place the integrity of the investment profession and the interests of clients above their own personal interests.
  • Use reasonable care and exercise independent professional judgment when conducting investment analysis, making investment recommendations, taking investment actions, and engaging in other professional activities.
  • Practice and encourage others to practice in a professional and ethical manner that will reflect credit on themselves and the profession.
  • Promote the integrity and viability of the global capital markets for the ultimate benefit of society.
  • Maintain and improve their professional competence and strive to maintain and improve the competence of other investment professionals.

Conflicts of Interest

Many ethical guidelines for investment professionals describe how to avoid or address conflicts of interest. A conflict of interest is a situation in which the interests or actions of one entity are incompatible with the interests or actions of another entity. As the previous chapter described, some investment professionals have a fiduciary duty to act in the best interest of their clients. Therefore, professionals should make every effort to avoid conflicts that tempt them to put their interests ahead of their clients’ interests.

Corporate codes of conduct help investment professionals navigate situations that could affect their judgment. For example, a company policy may require a portfolio manager to disclose any potential conflicts of interest before managing a client’s assets.

In addition, professional code of conduct requirements address conflicts of interest. For example, the CFA Institute instructs investment professionals on the types of conflicts that investment professionals should avoid to maintain their independence and objectivity and recommends controls to reduce the potential for conflicts.

Even the appearance of a potential conflict could compromise a client’s trust. Thus, investment management teams generally have policies to reduce the opportunities for conflicts of interest when managing client portfolios.

EXAMPLE

An investment management team wants to avoid the appearance of favoritism when it selects brokers to place trades involving client assets. Therefore, the team has a policy of declining all gifts from brokers. In addition, the team maintains transparency by providing clients with an explanation of their process for selecting brokers.

Analysis: The investment team knows that the company’s permissible gift policy could create a conflict of interest. Also, the team is aware that inappropriate acceptance of gifts or benefits can cause the team to lose their objectivity, which can become the basis for client lawsuits or regulatory action. Therefore, they have removed the potential conflict for their team by disallowing gifts from brokers.

Investment professionals must be diligent in managing situations in which conflicts of interest may occur because their actions typically have a meaningful effect on the performance of client portfolios. Three types of behaviors that represent conflicts of interest are (1) receiving particular forms of compensation, (2) self-dealing, and (3) engaging in soft-dollar arrangements.

 

Forms of Compensation

Compensation creates a conflict of interest when an investment professional’s financial incentives are not aligned with his client’s best interest. For example, investment advisors who receive sales commissions for selling investment products may have an incentive to sell products that earn them the highest commissions, even if such products are not appropriate for a particular client. This conflict of interest can affect which products the advisor recommends to the client.

EXAMPLE

Antonio LaMotta, an investment advisor, is selecting a mutual fund for the investment portfolio of Lacy Sullivan. Based on research and analysis, he narrows his choices to three similar small-cap equity funds. All three would be suitable for Lacy’s portfolio, but each has a different fee structure, which affects the amount of commission Antonio receives.

Analysis: The choice between the three mutual funds represents a conflict of interest for Antonio. He has an incentive to choose the fund that generates the biggest commission for him. However, selecting such a fund likely will result in higher fees for Lacy than the other fund choices. Therefore, Antonio needs to recognize the effect of fund fees on Lacy’s investment returns. In addition, he should prioritize fee management over commission generation in his fund selection process.

Another downside to commission-based compensation is that it can encourage excessive trading. Because commissions represent a cost to the client, excessive trading results in the client paying higher transaction costs than necessary. Part of a fiduciary’s responsibility is managing portfolio costs; thus, excessive trading conflicts with this responsibility.

To reduce the incentive to recommend unnecessary investment transactions, some investment advisors receive fee-based compensation instead of being compensated for product sales. Typically, a fee-based advisor is paid based on services performed for a client, the amount of client assets under management, or both.

Investment professionals whose primary responsibility is managing client investments are usually paid based on performance incentives. For instance, a large percentage of a portfolio manager’s compensation is generally tied to the performance of her assets under management. The purpose of such a compensation structure is to incentivize the portfolio manager to grow the values of client portfolios through skillful investing.

EXAMPLE

Terri Martinez, a portfolio manager, receives a base salary plus an annual performance bonus. She receives her bonus if the assets under her management outperform a designated benchmark over the course of the year. The amount of her bonus is based on the magnitude of her outperformance, with a higher margin of outperformance resulting in a larger bonus.

Analysis: Because of Terri’s compensation structure, she has an incentive to grow the assets she manages. By investing wisely, she will earn more in compensation and generate more value for her clients’ portfolios.

If an investment professional is compensated based on a portfolio’s assets under management, such compensation is often disclosed to investors. Disclosures regarding compensation structures can prevent conflicts of interest by enabling an investor to ensure that a portfolio manager’s compensation is aligned with the investor’s interests.

 

Self-Dealing

Another conflict of interest for investment professionals is self-dealing. In the context of corporate investment management, a self-dealing transaction occurs when an investment professional’s purchase of an investment for a client portfolio directly or indirectly benefits the investment professional’s company rather than the client. The potential for self-dealing increases when the company has an undisclosed financial interest in the investment being made.

EXAMPLE

Dexicos Life Insurance Company’s primary business is offering life insurance and annuity products. However, Dexicos also has an asset management business that internally manages several mutual funds. When selecting fund options for its variable annuity products, Dexicos includes some of its internally managed funds instead of similar funds that are managed externally.

Analysis: The inclusion of internally managed funds could indicate self-dealing. Because such funds are internally managed, Dexicos earns fund management revenue when its variable annuity customers select those fund options. Thus, Dexicos’s investment professionals need to ensure that adding internally managed funds to their clients’ variable annuity fund options is in the clients’ best interest. In addition, Dexicos should disclose its conflict of interest to its variable annuity customers.

Self-dealing practices can adversely affect a client’s investments. The investments selected by self-dealing could underperform relative to comparable investments managed by other companies. In addition, the self-dealt investments may have higher fees than comparable investments, resulting in added costs that reduce the client’s portfolio returns.

 

Soft-Dollar Arrangements

Investment professionals often purchase research and other services from brokerage firms. If they pay for such services directly, they are said to use hard dollars. However, they can also pay for brokerage services through soft dollars, which are considerations an investment professional or company earns with a particular broker-dealer as a result of executing commission-generating trades through that broker-dealer. Thus, a soft-dollar arrangement is a situation in which an investment professional directs client trades to a broker-dealer in exchange for receiving soft dollars, which the professional uses to obtain products or services—often research services—from the broker-dealer.

In effect, soft-dollar arrangements establish an ongoing relationship between an investment professional and a broker-dealer. The investment professional has an incentive to direct trades to the broker-dealer, who benefits from the commission revenue. Once enough soft dollars are earned, the investment professional uses soft dollars as a form of currency to purchase research reports, which can be beneficial in managing client portfolios.

Soft-dollar arrangements can raise ethical issues about whether clients whose trades are generating commissions for a particular broker-dealer are benefitting from the brokerage services that the investment professional receives by using soft dollars. In other words, a soft-dollar arrangement can create a conflict of interest in which an investment professional benefits from his use of soft dollars but his clients do not.

EXAMPLE

Example 1: An investment professional uses soft dollars to purchase a broker-dealer’s research report about corporate bonds. The report helps the investment professional improve her management of corporate bonds in her clients’ portfolios. In this case, the investment professional’s use of soft dollars is appropriate because the brokerage research assists her in making investment decisions for her clients’ portfolios.

Example 2: An investment professional uses soft dollars to purchase a new desk and other office equipment. In this case, the investment professional is using soft dollars to pay for business expenses. This use of soft dollars is inappropriate because the expenditures benefit the professional without improving his management of his clients’ portfolios.

To determine whether a soft-dollar arrangement is appropriate, an investment professional might consider the following questions:

  • Do the brokerage services that the investment professional purchased with soft dollars assist in her investment decision-making process?

  • Has the investment professional disclosed his soft-dollar practices to his clients prior to participating in the soft-dollar arrangement?

One of the main problems with soft-dollar arrangements is the lack of transparency for clients. Because soft dollars are incorporated into trade expenses, clients may not be aware of their true transaction costs. As you know, higher costs reduce the returns a portfolio earns from its investments.

Although soft-dollar arrangements are legal in many jurisdictions, the CFA Institute and securities regulators have various guidelines regarding the use and disclosure of soft-dollar arrangements. Some jurisdictions even consider a soft-dollar arrangement a breach of fiduciary duty when the clients involved pay more than the lowest available commission rate. In the United States, for example, the Securities and Exchange Commission (SEC) generally considers brokerage services as ineligible for soft-dollar arrangements unless the situation meets certain requirements. In such a situation, an investment professional should be able to demonstrate to the SEC that soft dollars paid for services that ultimately benefitted the clients whose transactions generated those dollars. Otherwise, the SEC could take regulatory action against the investment professional.

Some investment professionals prefer to use hard dollars to pay for brokerage services because of the regulatory and ethical complexities regarding soft-dollar use. In addition, some prefer to use hard dollars because of customers’ negative perception of soft-dollar arrangements.

Trading Practices

Many ethical guidelines for investment professionals cover trading practices. In general, an investment professional is supposed to act in the client’s best interest when executing trades for the client’s portfolio. However, as mentioned earlier, trading practices can result in numerous ethical issues. Some notable unethical practices are insider trading, trading ahead of research, front running, late trading, and collusion. As you will see, some of these trading practices are also illegal, depending on the jurisdiction.

 

Insider Trading

Insider trading is the prohibited act of buying or selling a company’s securities based on knowledge of material nonpublic information, also called inside information or insider information. Such information—examples include a company’s financial performance, business plans, or legal struggles—can significantly affect the market value of a company’s securities. Therefore, insider trading enables individuals to unfairly take advantage of and profit from possessing material nonpublic information.

Insider trading is both unethical and illegal in many jurisdictions, including the United States. For example, the SEC prioritizes the detection and prosecution of insider trading violations because “insider trading undermines investor confidence in the fairness and integrity of the securities markets.” 1 For one famous example of insider trading detected by the SEC, see the insight below.

INSIGHT

Insider Trading of ImClone Stock

ImClone Systems was a publicly traded biopharmaceutical company that developed medicines for cancer prevention and treatment. In 2001, the company’s CEO, Samuel Waksal, learned that regulators were going to deny approval of a new cancer drug. The drug was expected to be a major source of revenue for ImClone, so Waksal knew the negative news would send the company’s share price down.

Before the news went public, Waksal sold thousands of his shares in the company. Through her broker, Peter Bacanovic, American businesswoman Martha Stewart learned of the pending denial of ImClone’s drug and also sold thousands of company shares before the news went public. After the public learned that regulators had denied approval of the drug, ImClone shares dropped significantly. Thus, through their stock sales, Waksal and Stewart avoided financial losses.

The SEC investigated the suspiciously timed ImClone stock sales and eventually arrested Waksal, Stewart, Bacanovic, and others who had personal connections to Waksal. As a result of her arrest, Stewart was forced to resign as CEO of her company, Martha Stewart Living Omnimedia.

Waksal, Stewart, and Bacanovic were charged with insider trading, received prison sentences, and were required to pay fines. The SEC banned Waksal for life from serving as an officer to a publicly traded company. Stewart was not allowed to serve in an officer role for a publicly traded company for five years. The SEC also barred Bacanovic from working as a broker or investment advisor.

Source: Based on summary information from the SEC and other sources.

An individual does not have to benefit directly from material nonpublic information to commit an insider trading violation. Sharing such information can also qualify as a violation of insider trading rules.

EXAMPLE

Omar works at Rampford Consolidated, a publicly traded company. At work, Omar overhears some coworkers discussing Rampford’s negotiations to purchase a rival company. The pending acquisition, which is not public knowledge, will significantly improve Rampford’s product line and increase its profitability.

Omar mentions the pending acquisition to his friend Jeremy because Jeremy frequently expresses interest in buying Rampford stock. Based on this knowledge, Jeremy realizes that Rampford’s stock price will rise dramatically and buys a sizeable amount of company shares.

Although the share price of the acquiring company usually falls in the short term after an acquisition is announced, the public immediately recognizes the long-term implications of Rampford’s acquisition. Once the acquisition is publicly announced, the price of Rampford shares increases rapidly, as Jeremy anticipated. As a result, Jeremy makes a significant profit on his investment.

Analysis: Jeremy acted on material nonpublic information to purchase shares of Rampford before the information became public. Even though Omar did not trade on the material nonpublic information, he helped his friend engage in insider trading. Therefore, both men violated insider trading rules. Instead of helping his friend gain an unfair advantage, Omar should have kept the acquisition information to himself or warned Jeremy not to act on the information until it became public. If he learns later that Jeremy acted anyway, Omar should report Jeremy to securities regulators.

In the course of their work, investment professionals may encounter material nonpublic information. For instance, investment professionals working at an investment bank could have access to undisclosed details regarding an upcoming corporate merger that their team is pursuing. They can only share such information with people who have a legitimate business need to know, such as lawyers involved in the merger proceedings. When employees have access to sensitive information, companies must establish procedures that prevent information from being circulated outside the company or to employees who do not have a business need to know.

One control procedure that companies can follow is a review of employee and proprietary trading activities. Such activities are checked against a prohibited securities list—also known as a restricted securities list—which is a list of securities that specified company employees, due to their job responsibilities, are prohibited from buying or selling for either their personal accounts or any accounts they control. By monitoring the securities transactions of certain employees involved in investment activities, companies can ensure that their employees are adhering to trading policies and procedures.

 

Trading Ahead of Research

To comply with regulatory standards, investment management firms must establish policies and procedures that restrict or limit the information flow between the research department—or others with reasonable knowledge of the information contained in research reports—and the trading department. These controls are designed to prevent trading ahead of research reports, which is an unethical activity that occurs when, prior to the release of a research report, the trading operation of an investment management firm uses nonpublic information in a research report as a basis for increasing or decreasing its inventory position in a particular security.

EXAMPLE

Traders at UDJ Financial, a broker-dealer, know that customer demand for shares of Hattime Corporation will increase after UDJ’s research department releases its positive assessment of Hattime’s stock. UDJ’s traders purchase extra shares of Hattime for their inventory so that they can meet the expected increase in customer order volume after the research report is released.

Analysis: UDJ Financial’s traders are unethically using their knowledge of nonpublic information by trading ahead of the release of their research department’s report. The traders should wait until the report is publicly released before acting on its information.

 

Front Running

Front running is a specific type of insider trading that involves the unethical trade of a security by an investment professional based on advance knowledge of large pending client orders of the same security. Front running usually involves pending trades for amounts that can move markets—such as a purchase of 10,000 shares of a particular security. Through front running, the investment professional is able to personally profit from the security’s likely price movement.

EXAMPLE

Josephine Radha, a trader, received notice of a group of client accounts that will be purchasing a total of 50,000 shares of Lauticoal Company stock. Before executing the client trades, she purchases 1,000 shares of Lauticoal for her personal account. Then she purchases 50,000 shares on behalf of the client accounts. The large purchase order raises Lauticoal’s share price.

Analysis: Josephine used her advance knowledge to purchase Lauticoal shares before her clients did. Thus, she unethically earned a personal gain by front running her clients’ trades. Josephine should have put her clients’ interests first and completed the clients’ purchase before making her own purchase of Lauticoal shares.

 

Late Trading

Late trading is a type of illegal preferential treatment given to some investors who are allowed to trade securities—typically open-end mutual funds—after a stock market closes using that day’s closing prices. Late trading enables certain investors to use the day’s closing prices to conduct transactions, which are, in turn, based on information that became available only after the market’s close.

Recall from Chapter 3 that (1) an open-end mutual fund’s share price is determined by calculating the fund’s net asset value (NAV), and (2) a fund’s NAV is calculated once per trading day, after the market closes. An investor who wants to receive a particular day’s NAV on her trade has a limited window of time to place her trade to qualify for that NAV. However, late trading grants an investor a particular day’s NAV even if the trade is placed after the qualifying window.

In the United States, late trading is illegal because the investor is using information that was not available when markets were open to gain an unfair advantage over other participants in the market.

EXAMPLE

The Pamelton Bond Fund is a mutual fund that invests in long-term bonds. At the market close on Monday, the fund’s NAV was calculated as $40, meaning that any fund orders received between last Friday’s close and Monday’s close would receive a price of $40 per share.

After the market close on Monday, surprising economic data was released, leading investors to conclude that market interest rates would rise faster than expected in the future. Goran Huxtable, an investor, realized that the value of his Pamelton Bond Fund shares would fall on Tuesday due to the new data. He called a friend who worked at Pamelton Fund Advisors, placed an order to sell his shares in the fund, and asked for his trade to be given Monday’s trade date so that he could sell at $40 per share. His friend agreed and processed his request.

As expected, the share value of the Pamelton Bond Fund fell on Tuesday. The NAV was calculated at $35 per share.

Analysis: Because of his friendship, Goran’s trade received special treatment. Other investors who placed orders on Monday after the market closed received the next day’s price of $35 per share, while Goran received $40 per share. Thus, Goran’s late trading enabled him to unfairly profit from Monday night’s new information. The employees at Pamelton Fund Advisors should have treated Goran’s trade like all of the other trades received after Monday’s close.

 

Collusion

In general, collusion is an agreement between two or more people to defraud another person of his rights. In the context of investing, collusion generally gives market participants an unfair advantage by discouraging competition.

EXAMPLE

Woxway Capital wants to sell a private placement bond and receives bids from five qualified buyers, including Palance Bank. However, before receiving any bids, a trader at Woxway secretly agreed to sell the bond to Palance at a specific price. In exchange, Palance agreed to funnel future business to Woxway. Thus, even though other buyers submit higher bids, Woxway sells the bond to Palance.

Analysis: Woxway Capital’s trader and Palance Bank coordinated the price of the winning bid. Their collusion unfairly obstructed the other buyers’ efforts to purchase the private placement bond. Woxway should have evaluated the interested buyers based on the quality of their bids.

Some types of collusive practices in financial markets are

  • Coordinating prices, trades, or trade reports with other institutions

  • Directing or requesting other institutions to alter a price

  • Threatening, harassing, coercing, intimidating, or otherwise attempting to improperly influence the activities of other institutions

Responsibilities to Clients

Typically, ethical guidelines outline the responsibilities that investment professionals have toward their clients. For instance, investment professionals should have an established process for making prudent, informed investment decisions.

EXAMPLE

An investment team has a process for identifying and selecting investments for client portfolios. For a particular portfolio, the team’s analysts conduct research and analysis on potential investments. The portfolio manager meets with the analysts, who recommend specific securities. Based on her professional experience and her understanding of the portfolio’s objectives and constraints, the portfolio manager determines which investments are appropriate for the portfolio. Then the portfolio manager makes the final purchase decision.

Analysis: In this case, the investment team has a specific process that it has developed to make reasonable purchase decisions. They consistently purchase securities for client portfolios according to this process and can articulate the reasoning behind each decision. Thus, the team’s client knows that his portfolio’s investments have been thoroughly researched and carefully selected.

Investment professionals are responsible for ensuring that investment actions taken on behalf of a particular client’s portfolio are appropriate for that client. As you read in Chapter 5, an investment policy statement (IPS) describes the objectives and constraints for a particular portfolio. Therefore, investment professionals need to understand a portfolio’s IPS before making decisions that affect its investments.

Other responsibilities that investment professionals have to clients include

  • Treating clients and client portfolios fairly

  • Regularly disclosing relevant information to clients

  • Protecting the privacy and confidentiality of their clients

  • Properly handling client funds and safeguarding client assets

 

Fair Dealing

Investment professionals are responsible for treating client portfolios fairly, which is a practice known as fair dealing. In other words, an investment professional shouldn’t favor one client over another—regardless of the (1) size of a client’s portfolio, (2) amount of revenue the client’s portfolio generates, or (3) quality of the investment professional’s personal relationship with the client.

EXAMPLE

An investment professional buys several thousand shares of a stock at the time of its initial public offering. The professional allocates shares to several client portfolios. The portfolios that pay the most in management fees receive the most shares.

Analysis: The investment professional is not engaging in fair dealing with the share allocations. Certain client portfolios are receiving preferential treatment based on the fee revenue that they generate for the investment professional. Instead, the professional could allocate the shares proportionally based on a specific factor, such as the portfolio’s total asset size. Thus, the portfolios with the largest amounts of total assets would receive the most shares, and so forth.

In general, fair dealing means that an investment professional does not discriminate against or disadvantage a particular client relative to the professional’s other clients. However, fair dealing does not require treating all client accounts equally. For example, investment professionals can offer premium services—such as access to specialized alternative investment strategies—to a client as long as all clients are aware of the different service levels and have an opportunity to purchase the additional services offered.

 

Disclosures

Investment professionals have a responsibility to keep clients informed of matters that are relevant to the performance of their portfolios. Thus, investment professionals regularly disclose important information to clients. Examples of information disclosed by investment professionals include

  • A general description of the investment professional’s decision-making process

  • Details regarding specific portfolio management activities, such as trade execution and proxy voting

  • Explanations for deviations from a portfolio’s strategic asset allocation, such as instances of tactical asset allocation decisions

  • Methodologies for asset valuation and performance reporting

  • Breakdowns of management fees and other portfolio costs

  • Significant changes in personnel, such as a change in portfolio managers

  • Potential conflicts of interest, such as any soft-dollar arrangements

  • Existence of pending lawsuits or regulatory actions

Disclosures to clients should be regular and timely. For instance, many investment professionals disclose portfolio performance on a quarterly and annual basis. In the case of an unusual event, clients are likely to be notified once the event occurs. For example, if a mutual fund’s portfolio manager resigns, fund investors should know immediately because the change has a material effect on the management of the fund.

Disclosures to clients should be easy to locate, accurate, and clearly written. For instance, investment fees and portfolio manager compensation should be explained so that clients understand what services they are paying for and the financial incentives for their portfolio’s manager.

In addition, disclosures should give clients a complete understanding of how their assets are being managed and how their portfolios are performing. Misleading performance reporting can give investors an incomplete sense of portfolio performance.

EXAMPLE

An investment professional preparing a performance report for a client selects date ranges in which the professional’s investment decisions outperformed the portfolio’s benchmark. The professional does not include periods in which the portfolio underperformed.

Analysis: In this case, the client is not getting all of the information necessary to assess the portfolio’s performance. Selectively picking periods of performance to portray investment decisions in the most flattering manner is unethical. For this reason, many investment professionals adhere to a set of reporting standards, such as the CFA Institute’s Global Investment Performance Standards (GIPS®).

Certain investments are required to provide specific disclosures. For instance, a mutual fund’s prospectus describes certain aspects of the fund—such as a fund’s goals, style, managers, expenses, fees, and past performance. The purpose of a prospectus is to provide transparency so that potential and current investors can make informed decisions regarding their participation in the fund.

 

Privacy and Confidentiality

Through their work with clients, investment professionals often become privy to personal information. Therefore, they have a responsibility to maintain the privacy of their clients and to protect confidential materials. Investment professionals should have a process for collecting, storing, and using client information.

In addition, privacy protection regulations limit the sharing of customers’ nonpublic personal information. However, such regulations generally do not limit sharing of publicly available information—such as information present in telephone directories or published government records.

The investment industry is an attractive target for cybercriminals who wish to steal financial information. Thus, investment professionals have a responsibility to keep client data secure and protected from unauthorized access.

 

Theft, Fraud, and Misappropriation of Funds

Investment operations provide investment professionals with opportunities for theft and fraud. Broadly, theft is the unlawful taking of money or property, while fraud involves obtaining an unauthorized benefit—such as money or property—by deception. For instance, misappropriation of funds is a fraud that occurs when an individual authorized to handle a customer’s funds illegally alters documents or electronic files to divert funds for personal use, even if the use is on a temporary basis.

EXAMPLE

William Fleischer, an investment advisor, wants to buy a new car but does not have enough money available to complete his purchase. He borrows cash from one of his client accounts to pay for his new car. To cover his tracks, he manipulates records of the account’s cash balance. Within a month, William has enough money to cover the amount borrowed and returns it to the client’s account.

Analysis: William misappropriated funds because he used his access to client funds to obtain his new car. He also used his position to alter the client’s records, thus deceiving anyone who needed to see how much cash the account held.

Stealing from and defrauding clients are practices that are unethical as well as illegal. Besides laws and regulations, strong internal controls are necessary to prevent investment professionals from committing such actions. For instance, a company’s controls regarding the segregation of duties exist to avoid placing employees in circumstances in which they could abuse access to client funds.
