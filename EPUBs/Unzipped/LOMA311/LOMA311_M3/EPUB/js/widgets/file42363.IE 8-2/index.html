Claims Practices: Fair or Unfair?

Let’s review some examples of claims practices. For each example, click on if you think the practice is fair; click on if you think the practice is unfair.

When the insurer denies a claim it doesn’t always indicate the policy provision that relates to the claim.

Unfair. Knowingly misrepresenting to claimants or insureds relevant facts or policy provisions that relate to coverages at issue is an unfair claims practice.

The insurer consistently settles life insurance claims for substantially less than the amount actually due. If a claimant files a lawsuit to challenge the decision, the insurer then pays the full amount due under the policy.

Unfair. Insurers are prohibited from compelling claimants to sue to recover amounts due by offering substantially less than the amounts those claimants ultimately recovered in lawsuits.

When the insurer receives a request for a claim form, it provides the form within 10-15 calendar days of the request.

Fair. Insurers are required to provide forms necessary to present claims within 15 calendar days of a request for such forms.

An insurer adheres to documented policies and procedures for settling claims.

Fair. Insurers are required to adopt and implement reasonable standards for prompt investigation and settlement of claims.

Because of staffing shortages, an insurer experiences long delays in acknowledging the receipt of communications related to claims.

Unfair. Failing to acknowledge with reasonable promptness the receipt of communications that are pertinent to claims is an unfair claims practice.
