appellate courts
Courts that are authorized to review the decisions of lower courts. See Module 1, Chapter 1.
arbitration
An alternative dispute resolution method in which impartial third parties, known as arbitrators, evaluate the facts in dispute and render a decision that usually is binding on the parties. See Module 1, Chapter 2.
arbitrators
Impartial third parties who evaluate the facts in a civil dispute and render a decision that is binding on the parties. See Module 1, Chapter 2.

assignee

assignor

An affirmative defense that totally bars a plaintiff’s claim when the plaintiff understood the nature of the risk presented by the defendant’s conduct and voluntarily incurred that risk. See Module 1, Chapter 2.

The life insurance policy nonforfeiture benefit that will become effective automatically when a renewal premium is not paid by the end of the grace period and the policyowner has not elected another nonforfeiture option. Return to text.

A life insurance policy nonforfeiture benefit under which the insurer will automatically pay an overdue premium by making a loan against the policy’s cash value if the cash value equals or exceeds the amount of the premium due. Return to text.
aviation exclusion provision
A life insurance policy provision that states that an insurer will deny payment of policy proceeds if the insured’s death is caused by certain aviation-related activities.

backdating
A practice by which an insurer makes the effective date of an insurance policy earlier than the date of the application. See Module 3, Chapter 8.
bad faith
Knowingly committing a wrong or, with a dishonest motive, failing to fulfill a legal duty. See Module 1, Chapter 3.
bargaining contract
A contract created when both parties, as equals, set the terms and conditions of the contract. See Module 2, Chapter 4.
bearer paper
A negotiable instrument that is payable to bearer and thus can be negotiated by the holder without having to be signed by a specific payee. See Module 2, Chapter 6.
bid rigging
A way that competitors raise prices in situations in which purchasers obtain goods or services by soliciting competing bids; the competitors agree in advance who will submit the winning bid. See Module 1, Chapter 3.

A contract under which both parties make legally enforceable promises. See Module 2, Chapter 4.

binding premium receipt
A premium receipt that provides temporary life insurance coverage that becomes effective on the date specified in the receipt. Return to text.

The indorsement of a negotiable instrument by signature only, with no additional wording; a blank indorsement makes the instrument bearer paper. See Module 2, Chapter 6.
breach of contract
The failure of a party to a contract to perform a contractual obligation. See Module 1, Chapter 2.
broker-dealer
A term often used to refer to individuals and firms that transact securities businesses. See Module 3, Chapter 7.
burden of proof
(1) The duty to present evidence at trial to prove a given fact or set of facts. (2) The duty to persuade the trier of fact in a trial that an alleged fact is true. See Module 1,
Chapter 2.

A rule generally applied by U.S. courts when corporate directors are alleged to have breached a fiduciary duty. According to the rule, directors will not be held personally liable for making business decisions if there is a reasonable basis to believe that the directors acted in good faith and with due care. See Module 1, Chapter 3.
bylaws
A corporate document that contains the basic rules under which a corporation operates. See Module 1, Chapter 3.
case law
The body of law that consists of the written decisions of a common law jurisdiction’s courts. Contrast with statutory law. See Module 1, Chapter 1.

A life insurance policy nonforfeiture option that permits a policyowner to discontinue premium payments, surrender the policy, and receive the policy’s cash surrender value in a lump-sum payment. Also known as the cash payment nonforfeiture option. Return to text.

A set of facts that gives a person the right to judicial relief. See Module 1, Chapter 2.

A document issued by a state agency that grants a corporation its legal existence and its right to operate as a corporation. Sometimes called a corporate charter.

An individual life insurance policy provision that permits a policyowner to transfer all ownership rights by notifying the insurer, in writing, of the change, without having to enter into a separate assignment agreement. When the insurer records the ownership change in its records, the change generally becomes effective as of the date the policyowner signed the written notification Return to text.

The person in an insurance company who is responsible for overseeing and managing the company’s compliance with regulatory requirements and applicable internal policies and procedures. See Module 1, Chapter 1.

A contract provision that allows the parties to a contract to mutually decide which jurisdiction’s laws will govern their contract. See Module 2, Chapter 4.

In a civil law jurisdiction, a code that contains the general principles of law that apply to relationships between people that arise through birth, adoption, marriage, death, contracts, and personal liability. The civil code usually also regulates property rights. See Module 1, Chapter 1.
civil laws
Laws that are concerned with private—that is, nongovernmental—rights and remedies. See Module 1, Chapter 2.

A legal system based on the Roman legal system in which the laws are codified into written codes enacted by the legislature. See Module 1, Chapter 1.
civil litigation
A judicial process by which private parties go to court to enforce a legal right or to obtain a remedy for a civil wrong. See Module 1, Chapter 2.

A beneficiary designation that identifies a certain group of people, rather than naming each person individually. Return to text.

A degree of proof in which evidence shows that the truth of the facts asserted is highly probable and leaves no reasonable doubt as to the truth of those facts. Return to text.

A contract for which only those terms and conditions that are printed in—or attached to—the contract are considered to be part of the contract. Return to text.

A testamentary document that supplements a will. See Module 2, Chapter 6.

The transfer of some of a policyowner’s ownership rights in a life insurance policy to provide security for a debt. Return to text.

A legal system based on the common law of England. See Module 1, Chapter 1.

A bilateral mistake that occurs when both parties to a contract make the same mistake. Return to text.

In some states, certain property owned by a married couple in which each spouse has an undivided half interest. See Module 2, Chapter 6.
commutative contract
An agreement under which the parties specify in advance the values that they will exchange, and the parties generally exchange items or services that they think are of relatively equal value. See Module 2, Chapter 4.
comparative negligence
A system of apportioning losses between plaintiffs and defendants when both parties’ negligence contributed to a plaintiff’s loss. See Module 1, Chapter 2.
compensatory damages
Damages intended to compensate an injured party for the amount of the monetary losses that resulted from a defendant’s wrongful conduct. See Module 1, Chapter 2.

A written document that sets out the cause of action on which a plaintiff in a civil lawsuit bases her claim against the defendant. See Module 1, Chapter 2.
