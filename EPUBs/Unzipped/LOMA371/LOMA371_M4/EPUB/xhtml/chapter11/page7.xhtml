Annuity Periodic Income Payments

Another potential benefit of an annuity is the regular income that an insurance company pays during the contract’s payout period. In general, insurers calculate the present values of their future payments and sum the present values to estimate the total cost of periodic income payments.

Insurers make periodic income payments according to a payout option, as selected by annuity contract owners. Two types of payout options are guaranteed annuity payout options and life annuity payout options.

 

Guaranteed Annuity Payout Options

Not every annuity is structured as a life annuity. Guaranteed annuity payout options provide regular payments of either a designated amount or for a designated period and are not linked to any life expectancy or mortality risk. Thus, a guaranteed annuity payout option guarantees that the insurance company will distribute the entire accumulated value of an annuity contract. The two types of guaranteed annuity payout options are

  • The fixed amount option, which requires the contract owner to choose the amount of income that the insurer will pay periodically. With this option, the insurer pays the same amount on a regular basis until the contract value is exhausted.

  • The fixed period option, which requires the annuity owner to choose the length of time the periodic income payments will continue. With this option, the insurer makes payments for a specified period of time regardless of whether the annuitant is living. If the annuitant dies during the payout period, then the contract’s beneficiary receives the remaining payments.

There is an interrelationship between the fixed amount option and the fixed period option. For instance, if a contract owner chooses a fixed amount option, then the amount chosen will influence the number of payments that are made before the contract value is exhausted.

For guaranteed annuity payout options, the insurer’s cost of future payments at the start of the payout period equals the contract’s accumulated value at the maturity date, when the contract is annuitized.

 

Life Annuity Payout Options

For life annuity payout options, insurers promise to make regular income payments for a period related to the life expectancy of one or more annuitants. Life annuity payout options apply to both immediate annuities and deferred annuities. For all of the life annuity payout options, the amount that the insurer pays periodically is based in part on the contract’s accumulated value or the single premium used to purchase the option.

The typical kinds of life annuity payout options are as follows:

  • A single life annuity, also known as a life only annuity or straight life annuity, is a payout option that provides periodic income payments in an amount based on both the single premium and the annuitant’s life expectancy. Under this option, the number of periodic payments is based on the lifetime of the annuitant. The insurer makes regular payments from the beginning of the payout period until the annuitant’s death, after which the company ceases payments. Thus, a single life annuity is the most basic form of a life annuity. For a single life annuity, an insurer calculates the cost of annuity payments over a period extending to the end of the mortality table—for example, to age 120—rather than for a specified number of years.

  • A single life annuity with period certain is a payout option that guarantees periodic income payments throughout the lifetime of the annuitant and also guarantees that the payments will continue for at least a specified period. If the annuitant dies before the end of the specified period, the insurer provides the periodic income payments to the beneficiary until the end of the period. If the annuitant dies after the end of the specified period, the beneficiary does not receive any periodic income payments.

  • A single life with refund annuity is a payout option that guarantees specified periodic income payments throughout the lifetime of the annuitant and also guarantees that the insurer will provide a refund if the annuitant dies before the total of the periodic income payments already made equals the amount paid for the annuity. In such a scenario, the contract’s beneficiary receives the refund, which is the difference between the amount the contract owner paid for the annuity and the total amount of periodic income payments paid to the annuitant prior to her death. The refund of the remaining premium can be paid to the beneficiary as a lump sum (cash refund) or in payments (installment refund).

  • A joint life annuity is a payout option that typically covers more than one life and guarantees periodic income payments until one of the annuitants dies, and then pays nothing more.

  • A joint and survivor (J&S) annuity, also known as a joint and last survivorship annuity, is a payout option that guarantees a series of periodic income payments to two or more annuitants until both or all of the annuitants die. The insurer bases periodic income payments on the life expectancies of the annuitants. Depending on the terms of the annuity, payments may decrease after the first annuitant dies.

Life annuity payout options expose insurers to mortality risk because calculations for life annuities rely on the mortality experience of a population of annuitants. In other words, insurers must estimate the cost of the lifetime guarantees based on the life expectancies of the annuitants. If the actual mortality rate among the product’s annuitants is

  • higher than expected, then the insurer will generally make fewer periodic income payments than projected (meaning the cost to the insurer will be lower than expected)

  • lower than expected, then the insurer will generally make more periodic income payments than projected (meaning the cost to the insurer will be higher than expected)

To calculate the cost of one year’s periodic payments under a life annuity, insurers first find the total number of annuitants living at the beginning of the year. (If calculating for non-annuitized deferred annuities, this step includes subtracting the number of surrenders from the total number of annuitants.) Then, insurers multiply the result by the appropriate mortality factor to determine the number of annuitants still living at the end of the year. Next, they multiply the amount of each periodic payment by the number of surviving annuitants. The sum of the resulting values is the cost for one year for a block of contracts.

As described in Chapter 9, an insurer designing an annuity product can use a lower mortality rate than it expects to experience so as to provide a mortality margin into the design. The mortality margin introduces an element of conservatism into the design by increasing the assumed survivorship rates, thereby increasing the estimated number of payments the insurer will make. If the actual mortality rate among the product’s annuitants is higher than expected, the product is likely to be more profitable than projected because of the lower cost of periodic income payments. (Profitability may also be affected by the length of the accumulation period.)
