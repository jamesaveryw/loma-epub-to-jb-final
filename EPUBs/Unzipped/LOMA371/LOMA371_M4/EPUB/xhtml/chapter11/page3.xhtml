Types of Annuities

An annuity is a contract between an insurer and a contract owner, who applies for and funds the annuity. In most annuity contracts, the contract owner is also the annuitant, whose lifetime the insurer uses to determine the amount and duration of periodic income payments payable under the contract. (Unless stated otherwise, assume that this chapter is discussing life annuities, which provide periodic income payments for the lifetime of the annuitant.)

Typically, the annuitant is the person who receives periodic income payments from the insurer. However, the contract owner could designate a payee to receive the payments instead of the annuitant. For simplicity, in this chapter, we assume that the contract owner is also the annuitant, and that the annuitant is also the payee and therefore receiving the insurer’s periodic income payments.

During the annuity contract’s payout period, also known as the liquidation period, the insurer makes periodic income payments to the annuitant. The annuitant may want payments to begin right away, or may prefer to wait for the contract to build more value before receiving payments. To accommodate either preference, insurers provide two basic types of annuity products:

  • Immediate annuities, under which periodic income payments begin no more than one year after the contract is purchased. For example, a person who is about to retire might use a lump-sum amount to buy an immediate annuity that begins making monthly payments within 30 days.

  • Deferred annuities, under which periodic income payments are scheduled to begin at least one year after the contract’s purchase date. Deferred annuities provide the opportunity for invested premiums to grow during the accumulation period, which begins on the contract’s purchase date and runs until either the beginning of the payout period or the date when the owner terminates the contract. Typically, the contract’s accumulated value fluctuates during the accumulation period. At some point in the future, a deferred annuity contract owner could choose to annuitize the contract. The process of annuitization converts the contract’s accumulated value into periodic income payments. In practice, many deferred annuity contracts are never annuitized.

For an immediate annuity, the issue date marks the beginning of the payout period. If a deferred annuity is annuitized, the beginning of the payout period is known as the maturity date or the conversion date. Annuity products also vary based on investment risks and principal guarantees. Thus, annuities can generally be categorized as fixed annuities or variable annuities.

 

Fixed Annuities

With a fixed annuity, the insurer guarantees (1) the minimum interest rate that it will apply to any accumulated value and (2) the minimum amount of the annuity payments that it will pay. Depending on the contract owner’s choices, a fixed annuity is also either an immediate annuity or a deferred annuity.

For fixed annuities, the insurer decides how to invest premiums and manages its general account assets so as to support the contractual guarantees. Thus, for fixed annuities, the insurer bears the investment risk.

A fixed indexed annuity (FIA), also called an indexed annuity, is a type of fixed deferred annuity that offers principal and interest rate guarantees, as well as the possibility of additional earnings based on changes in an index. An index is a statistical measurement system that tracks the changes in a group of similar values. We will describe an FIA’s index-crediting mechanism later in this chapter.

 

Variable Annuities

With a variable annuity, the amount of the accumulated value and the amount of the annuity payments fluctuate in accordance with the performance of one or more subaccounts. Premiums allocated to subaccounts are invested with an insurer’s separate accounts.

Compared to fixed annuities, variable annuities offer customers the possibility of greater investment growth in exchange for more investment risk and fewer guarantees. Because a variable annuity’s contract owner selects subaccounts, the owner—not the insurer—directly bears the investment risk associated with these subaccounts.

To mitigate risk, a variable annuity contract owner can usually place a portion of premiums in a fixed fund option, which guarantees a fixed rate of interest for a specified period of time. Premiums allocated to a fixed fund option are invested with the insurer’s general account. A fixed fund option has virtually the same characteristics as a fixed deferred annuity.
