Indexed Crediting for Fixed Indexed Annuities

Because fixed indexed annuities (FIAs) are a special type of fixed deferred annuity, they have an accumulated value, a surrender value, a death benefit, and payout options. Although FIAs permit flexible premiums, most are purchased with a single premium.

The main difference between FIAs and nonindexed fixed deferred annuities is the FIA crediting mechanism, known as indexed crediting. With indexed crediting, the FIA contract guarantees that the insurance company will periodically award monetary credits to the contract’s accumulated value on a specified basis; the credits are related to the growth rate in a specified external index of stock prices. The monetary credits awarded to the contract’s value are known as index credits. The specified external index is known as the reference index or the benchmark index. In many FIAs, the reference index is the Standard & Poor’s 500 (S&P 500) index.

FIAs have some features not found in other annuity products. For instance, the contract owner typically must leave the money in the chosen index-crediting method for a specified minimum contract term. The contract term is the length of time over which the insurer will calculate interest for the contract’s accumulated value.

As another example, nonforfeiture laws that apply to all individual deferred annuities set a minimum surrender value typically equal to 87.5 percent of premiums paid, with the surrender value accumulated at 1 to 3 percent interest for the term the contract has been in force. An FIA could, in theory, permit the contract owner to lose some of the premium upon surrender after a prolonged market downturn. However, this outcome is unlikely if the FIA contract contains provisions protecting the value of the premium.

In general, the index-crediting formula calculates index credits by multiplying an FIA contract’s accumulated value by the crediting rate, which is based on the reference index’s growth rate but is affected by provisions. Some indexed-crediting provisions limit how much the insurer contributes to the accumulated value. Other provisions serve to protect the contract owner if the reference index fails to achieve growth. In addition, insurers have several options for calculating index credits.

 

Limiting Provisions

Index-crediting formulas do not typically award the full growth rate of the index to the contract. Rather, these formulas have provisions designed to limit the insurer’s obligation for indexed crediting. The limits are based on economic factors, such as interest rates and credit spreads, as well as the cost of the options that the insurer buys to hedge the contract.

The limiting provisions may include a participation percentage, a cap, or a yield spread:

  • A participation percentage provision stipulates the rate at which the insurer shares the reference index’s growth with the contract owner. For example, for a period in which the reference index grows in value by 10 percent, a participation percentage of 60 percent reduces the growth rate credited to the contract’s accumulated value to 6 percent (found as 0.10 × 0.60 = 0.06).

  • A cap is an upper limit, expressed as a percent, on the rate of a reference index’s gain in value that the insurer credits to the annuity contract as interest. For instance, suppose an FIA contract has a provision that sets the cap at 10 percent. For a period in which the value of the reference index grows by 12 percent, the insurer limits the rate of interest it credits to the contract to the capped rate of 10 percent. An index-crediting formula may combine a participation percentage provision and an interest-rate cap; if combined, the insurer typically applies the participation percentage provision first, then the cap.

  • A yield spread provision states that the insurer always deducts a specified percentage from the growth rate in the reference index. For example, for a period in which the value of the reference index grows by 12 percent, a yield spread of 3 percent reduces the growth rate the insurer credits to the contract’s accumulated value to 9 percent (found as 12% - 3%). For another period, if the reference index grows by 5 percent, then the rate credited to the contract is 2 percent (found as 5% - 3%). The yield spread provision is less common than the interest-rate cap and the participation percentage provision.

 

Protecting Provisions

While some provisions limit the insurer’s sharing of the reference index growth with FIA contracts, other provisions protect contract owners by guaranteeing that they will not lose any portion of their premiums in the contract. Two examples of such provisions are the floor provision and the return of premium provision:

  • A floor provision specifies that the contract’s values are not reduced if the linked index decreases in value during a measurement period. Floor provisions typically guarantee that the index credit for the contract’s term cannot be less than zero. However, the index credit for a single period within the contract term can be zero.

  • Laws and regulations may permit the surrender value to fall below 100 percent of premiums. However, FIA contracts sometimes include a return of premium provision, under which the insurer promises to pay the contract owner the full value of premiums—minus any partial withdrawals—upon surrender if the contract has not built an accumulated value greater than the value promised in the provision.

 

Index Credit Calculation Methods

An insurance company’s first step in determining the amount of index credits to award to an FIA contract is to establish the contract term. Most FIAs have terms that range from one year to ten years, with six or seven years being the most common.

Insurers can calculate the index credits an FIA receives in a variety of ways. Rather than attempt to describe all the variations, we present three basic methods: the annual reset method, the point-to-point method, and the high water mark method.

 

Annual Reset Method

The annual reset method, also known as the ratchet method, involves comparing the value of the reference index at the start of each contract year with the value at the end of the year. For the next year, the starting value is “reset” to the ending value from the previous year. Under this method, the index values are reset at the beginning of each contract year during the term of the contract.

The annual reset method allows the contract owner to lock in gains each year and avoid losses. When the market goes up, the contract owner earns excess interest. When the market remains stable or goes down, the contract neither gains nor loses value. The following insight provides an example of applying the annual reset method.

Point-to-Point Method

The point-to-point method compares the value of the reference index at the start of the annuity contract term to the value at the end of the term to determine what, if any, interest has accrued because of a change in the index. If the value of the index at the end of the term is higher than the value at the beginning of the term, the contract’s accumulated value will earn index credits. If the index shows negative growth or no growth, the accumulated value will not be credited.

Because earnings depend only on the value of the index at the end of the term, they are insulated from the effects of short-term fluctuations during the term. As long as the index values rise overall, the insulating effect can be an advantage. On the other hand, if values over the term stagnate or decline, it can be a disadvantage because contract owners cannot lock in short-term gains.

High Water Mark Method

The high water mark method compares the value of the reference index at the beginning of the contract term with the highest value—or high water mark—that the index reaches on any contract anniversary date during the term. If the index has gained in value on the high water mark compared to the value at the beginning of the term, the contract’s accumulated value earns index credits for the contract term. However, if the index value at the high water mark is the same as or less than the index value at the start of the contract term, the accumulated value does not earn index credits. As a result, this method—like the annual reset method and the point-to-point method—allows the contract owner to take advantage of index gains without being subject to index losses.

The advantage of using high water marks rather than year-end or term-end values is that earnings are not dependent on index growth at the end of each contract year or at the end of the term. According to this method, the index need only show growth on any contract anniversary date in order to earn index credits.
