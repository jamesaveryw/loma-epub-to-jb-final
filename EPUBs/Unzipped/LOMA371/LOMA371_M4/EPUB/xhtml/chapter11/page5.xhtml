Annuity Death Benefits

For many types of annuities, the contract owner selects a death benefit or death benefit rider for the contract. (We will cover riders later in the chapter.) In doing so, the owner designates the contract’s beneficiary, which is the person or entity who may receive benefits accrued or values remaining in the annuity contract upon the death of the annuitant.

Depending on which payout option an immediate annuity contract owner selects at the time of purchase, the insurer may or may not make payments to a beneficiary upon the annuitant’s death. For example, one type of payout option guarantees periodic income payments for a certain period and pays the beneficiary if the annuitant dies during that period. Another type of payout option provides the beneficiary with a refund of the unused portion of principal if the annuitant’s death occurs before the insurer has paid out the principal’s full value in periodic income. Such payout options are widely utilized in practice. We describe payout options in greater detail later in this chapter.

For a deferred annuity, the beneficiary receives a standard death benefit if the annuitant dies before the contract has been annuitized. Many deferred annuities also offer optional death benefits for an additional fee that will provide greater death benefits than the standard. The amount of a deferred annuity’s death benefit generally depends on the accumulated value of the contract at the time of the annuitant’s death rather than on the amount of premiums paid. However, certain contracts offer a return of premium death benefit as part of the contract, allowing for the greater of the accumulation value or premiums paid less withdrawals.

The process insurance companies use to estimate the cost of future deferred annuity death benefits is similar to the process they use to estimate the cost of life insurance death benefits, as discussed in the previous chapter. Typically, an insurer

  • Subtracts the expected number of surrenders from the number of in-force contracts at the beginning of the contract year.

  • Applies an appropriate mortality rate to the remaining number of contracts to determine the number of death benefits payable for the year. Because annuitants tend to live longer than life insureds, insurers don’t use the same sets of mortality data for annuity products as they do for life insurance products.

  • Repeats the process for each year of the accumulation period. The total of the future death benefits equals the sum of the projected death benefits payable for each year in the accumulation period.
