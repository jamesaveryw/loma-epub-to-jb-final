Cost of Benefits for Multiyear Term Life Insurance

Like the cost of benefits calculations for one-year term insurance, the cost of benefits calculations for multiyear term life insurance products involve estimating the present value of the cost of the death benefits. However, cost of benefits calculations for multiyear term life insurance products must address two issues that are not generally involved in annual premium calculations for one-year term life products:

  • Level annual premiums

  • Lapse rates (if a premium calculation is done on a monthly basis, some one-year term policies may account for lapse rates)

Policyowners generally purchase multiyear term life insurance products with a series of level annual premiums. A level annual premium (LAP) is any set of equal annual payments having a present value equal to a given single premium. Level annual premiums allow policyowners to pay the same premium amount for the stipulated duration of years from issue. For example, a policy with a 10-year LAP would state the premium as level for ten years.

To cover the insurer’s cost of benefits—and the other premium components, such as operating expenses—actuaries must find a LAP amount for which the sum of the present values of the premium payments is equivalent to the present value of a hypothetical single premium. We’ll provide an example of such a calculation later in the chapter. (For simplicity, the mortality cost will be the only component we’ll calculate.)

For a block of multiyear term life insurance policies, the number of insureds decreases each year because some insureds die and others allow their policies to lapse. Year-by-year decreases in the number of insured lives in the block affect the cost of benefits for the block in the following ways:

  • The number of premium payments the insurer collects from the block of policies decreases each year.

  • Lapses that occur in the early policy years may prevent the insurer from fully recovering the acquisition expenses associated with the block of policies.

We’ll use the following steps to illustrate the process of estimating the mortality cost for a block of multiyear term life insurance policies:

  • Project the number of benefit payments for the term’s duration

  • Determine the average amount of death benefit paid within the block of policies

  • Estimate the future value of death benefits for the entire block

  • Find the present value of the block’s future death benefits

  • Calculate the monetary amount of the level annual premium (LAP)

 

Projecting the Number of Benefit Payments

To calculate the total number of death benefit payments for a block of multiyear term insurance policies, an insurer must (1) calculate the number of benefit payments for each year in the term, and (2) sum the numbers to find the total number of payments. In other words, the insurer must project the total number of deaths for the duration of the term.

In the first year of the block’s term, the insurer multiplies the number of insureds at the beginning of the year by the appropriate mortality rate to project the number of deaths expected during the year.

After the first year, the insurer must account for both policy lapses and insured deaths. To calculate the projected number of deaths in the block for a particular year—and the number of resulting death benefit payments—the insurer first takes the number of insureds remaining at the beginning of that year and subtracts the number of insureds withdrawing (as estimated by applying the lapse rate). The result is the number of surviving insureds associated with in-force policies in the block. Next, the insurer applies the appropriate mortality rate to this number to find the number of insureds in the block who are expected to die by the end of that particular year.

The total number of deaths represents the number of benefit payments the insurer expects to make during the entire term.

 

Determining the Average Death Benefit Amount

Next, the insurer needs to determine the average amount of the death benefit paid within the block. To find this amount, the insurer sums the face amounts for all the policies in the block of business and divides by the number of policies in the block.

 

Estimating the Future Death Benefits

Then, the insurer multiplies the average death benefit amount by the number of deaths in a particular year to find the value of the death benefits for that year. The insurer performs these calculations for every year and sums the death benefit values to find the total value for the term. Finding the Present Value of Future Death Benefits

To calculate the present value of the death benefits for a multiyear term life insurance product, the insurer must calculate the present value of the death benefits for each year in the policy’s term rather than for a single year. Thus, each year’s estimated future death benefits are multiplied by the appropriate PVIF.

Note that we are assuming that all death benefit payments are occurring at the midpoint of the year. We made the same assumption in our discussion of one-year term life insurance. If we were to assume that payments occurred at the end of the year, the difference in timing would change the PVIFs used.

Once the insurer has calculated the present value of the death benefits for each year in the term, it can sum the present values to find the present value of the block’s total death benefits. This number, which represents the single premium, is important in determining the level annual premium. Calculating the Level Annual Premium

Recall that multiyear term life insurance products are generally purchased with a series of level annual premiums (LAPs). To calculate the LAP amount for a block of multiyear term life policies, the insurer must find a set of equal annual premium payments for which the sum of the present values is equivalent to the single premium.

In the previous section, the insurer applied PVIFs to find the single premium. Now, it must apply PVIFs to find the LAP. However, this calculation uses a different set of PVIFs than those used in the previous insight. Because we’re assuming that premium payments will occur at the beginning of each year, we must use PVIFs for a period of one year (n – 1) instead of one-half year (n = ½).

The sum of the present values of the LAP* must be equivalent to the single premium of $2,653,647, as calculated in the previous insight. To solve for the monetary amount of the LAP, we must solve the following equation:

35,822.79 × LAP = $2,653,647

OR

LAP = $2,653,647 ÷ 35,822.79

LAP = $74.077, which we can round to $74.08

The LAP of $74.08 is the premium payment that each policyowner in the block pays each year to keep the policy in force.

*Note: We have simplified the LAP calculation to highlight the portion of the premium attributable to the mortality cost. Remember that, in reality, the LAP must also factor in the cost of other benefits, operating expenses, and other premium components. In addition, periodic adjustments—such as factoring in the timing of premium payments—affect LAP calculations.
