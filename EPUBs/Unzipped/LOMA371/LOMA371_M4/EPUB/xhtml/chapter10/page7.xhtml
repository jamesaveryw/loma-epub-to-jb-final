Cost of Benefits for Whole Life Insurance

When discussing the cost of benefits for one-year and multiyear term life insurance products, we were primarily focused on death benefits, as mortality cost was a significant factor in setting premiums for those products. Setting premiums for whole life insurance is similar to setting premiums for term life insurance and consists of calculating premiums and setting margins. Again, the goal of the process is to calculate the present value of the cost of benefits.

However, whole life insurance has two features that are usually not present for one-year and most multiyear term life insurance products:

  • Death benefit coverage while the policy remains in force during the lifetime of the insured (rather than for a limited number of years, in the case of term life products without continuation provisions)

  • Cash value and surrender benefits

To estimate future benefit payments for whole life insurance, actuaries must estimate the timing and average amount of

  • Death benefits for each year, using an appropriate mortality table

  • Lapses and surrender benefits, based on the number of insureds at the beginning of each year, the expected lapse or surrender rates for each year, and the surrender value per unit, as follows:

Annual surrender = Number of insureds at beginning of year × Lapse or surrender rate × Surrender value per unit

In calculating the total future benefits for most whole life insurance products, the following assumptions generally apply:

  • Level annual premiums are collected at the beginning of each year.

  • Death benefits are paid at the midpoint of each year.

  • Surrender benefits do not begin until the policy has been in force for several years. (Most products do not build a cash value until the policy has been in force for several years.) When surrender benefits become payable, we assume they are paid at the beginning of each year.

  • The final benefit payments for this plan of insurance will be made when the insured group reaches an advanced age, such as age 120.

To finalize the premium calculations for a whole life insurance product, the insurer would first calculate a single premium and then set level periodic premium amounts to derive a LAP, as previously shown.
