Cost of Benefits for One-Year Term Life Insurance

As stated earlier, a single premium purchases a one-year term life insurance policy. One-year term life insurance—which is the simplest life insurance product—pays a death benefit only if the insured person dies during the year that the policy is in force. Because term life insurance policies usually do not accumulate cash values, actuaries do not need to calculate nonforfeiture values. Therefore, to determine the cost of benefits component of a one-year term life policy’s single premium, actuaries are primarily focused on estimating the mortality cost. The general steps for finding the mortality cost for a one-year term life policy include

  • Projecting the number of insureds who will die while the product is in force

  • Determining the average amount of the death benefit paid within a block of policies

  • Estimating the future value of the death benefits for the entire block

  • Finding the present value of the block’s future death benefits

 

Projecting the Number of Death Benefits

The insurer’s first step in calculating the mortality cost for a block of one-year term life insurance policies is to project the number of death benefits it expects to pay. The number of death benefits for the block equals the number of insureds who die during the year that the policies are in force. Insurers use the mortality rates provided in mortality tables to project the number of deaths for the block.

 

Determining the Average Death Benefit Amount

Then, the insurer needs to determine the average amount of the death benefit paid during the period. Because an insurer typically calculates the cost of benefits for a particular block of business, the average amount of a death benefit is equal to the average face amount of the policies in the block.

 

Estimating the Future Death Benefits

The formula for calculating the future death benefit payments for a block of term life policies is

Estimate of future death benefits = Total number of insured deaths during the period × Average amount of a death benefit

As you can see, an insurer can estimate a value for the block’s future death benefits payable by multiplying the number of death benefits it expects to pay during the period by the average amount of a death benefit. Let’s see an example.

EXAMPLE

Cardmore Insurance Company is estimating the mortality cost for a block of 10,000 one-year term life insurance policies. The block of policies is issued to men, age 35. For this estimate, the company consults the chosen mortality table and applies a mortality rate of 0.65 per 1,000 lives. The mortality rate for one life equals the tabular mortality rate divided by the number of lives—1,000—as shown:

0.65 ÷ 1,000 = 0.00065

Using the mortality rate for one life, Cardmore can project an average of how many insureds out of 10,000 will die during the year. The company multiplies the mortality rate for one life by the number of insureds in the block:

0.00065 × 10,000 = 6.5 deaths

Thus, Cardmore expects to pay an average of 6.5 death benefits per 10,000 insureds during the one-year term of this product. Each policy in the block has a $100,000 face amount. Therefore, we can use our future death benefits formula to project the total amount of expected future death benefits for this block:

Estimate of future death benefits = Total number of insured deaths during the period × Average amount of a death benefit
  = 6.5 deaths × $100,000 face amount per policy
  = $650,000

Analysis: Based on its actuarial estimate of 6.5 deaths for this particular block of policies, Cardmore Insurance Company should expect to pay $650,000 in death benefits. Obviously, 6.5 people cannot die in a given year. However, for pricing purposes, the actuarial estimate of 6.5 is more precise than using 6.0 or 7.0 as the number of projected deaths in the block during the year.

 

Finding the Present Value of Future Death Benefits

The present value of the future death benefits is the amount the insurer would need to invest at an assumed interest rate at the product’s inception to precisely cover the product’s estimated future death benefits. This assumed interest rate represents the insurer’s projected rate of return on the invested premiums.

Mortality tables indicate the estimated number of deaths that are likely to occur within a given year, but they do not indicate the actual dates during the year when deaths will occur. Although deaths and death benefit payments are likely to occur throughout the year, insurers often simplify calculations by assuming that all death benefits will be paid at a specific point during the year.

In our discussion, we assume that all death benefits are paid at the exact midpoint of the year. This assumption requires discounting death benefits for a specified period, n, of one-half year. Thus, an insurer would multiply the total amount of death benefits for a block of policies by the present value interest factor (PVIF) for a specified interest rate, i, and period of n = ½.

EXAMPLE

The insurance company from the previous example, Cardmore, wants to find the present value of the future death benefits it estimated: $650,000. Assume the interest rate is 7%; the corresponding PVIF for a 7% interest rate and a ½-year period equals 0.967. Cardmore finds the present value of the future death benefits as follows:

Present value of future death benefits for block of policies = Estimated future death benefits × PVIF
  = $650,000 × 0.967
  = $628,550

Analysis: $628,550 is the present value of the future death benefits for this block of 10,000 policies. Therefore, if Cardmore invests $628,550 today, it should earn enough on its investments to pay out $650,000 by the end of the one-year term.
