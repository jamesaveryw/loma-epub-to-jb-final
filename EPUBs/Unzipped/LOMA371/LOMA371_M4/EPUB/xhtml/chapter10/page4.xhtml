Types of Pricing Premiums

To cover the costs of providing life insurance policy benefits, actuaries must determine the proper premium rates to charge. In addition, the premium rates must enable the insurer to cover the costs of developing and administering products, as well as to generate a reasonable profit.

Insurance companies use premiums for both reserves and pricing. The portion of premiums used for reserves are set aside to pay future contract obligations and meet mandated reserve requirements. Every type of premium we discuss in this chapter is a type of pricing premium. A pricing premium is the monetary amount per unit of coverage that an insurer must collect from customers to cover any portion of the product’s cost of benefits or the insurer’s costs for supporting the product that is not covered by investment earnings.

For our purposes, pricing premiums can take the following forms:

  • A single premium, which is a form of premium payment in which one lump sum covers all of the financial considerations—such as operating expenses and cost of benefits—for the life of the contract. Single premiums typically purchase one-year term life insurance policies.

  • Level premiums, which are periodic premium payments that are equal in amount and are generally paid monthly, quarterly, or annually. Typically, level premiums purchase multiyear term life insurance and whole life insurance policies.

  • Flexible premiums, which allow policyowners to make payments to the insurer at various times so as to increase the savings element in a contract that was established with a single initial premium. Flexible premiums typically purchase universal life insurance policies.

In establishing a premium rate for a particular product, actuaries don’t calculate specific rates for individual policies; instead, they develop a rate for a block of policies. Thus, in this chapter’s examples, we will describe the process for calculating the cost of benefits for a sample block of policies. (For educational purposes, we have simplified the discussion. Because product design is complex, actuaries generally use product development software to perform necessary calculations.)

Throughout this chapter, as we calculate the cost of benefits, remember that insurers must also account for expenses such as commissions and taxes, along with other cash outflows. So, for a given product, the associated charges will be greater than the cost of benefits charge alone.

Because the death benefit is the primary policy benefit payable on a life insurance policy, we dedicate a significant portion of this chapter to describing mortality cost calculations for one-year term life, multiyear term life, whole life, and universal life insurance products.
