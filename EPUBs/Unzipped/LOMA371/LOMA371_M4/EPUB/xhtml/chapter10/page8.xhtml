Cost of Benefits for Universal Life Insurance

So far, we have discussed the cost of benefit calculations for term life insurance products and whole life insurance, a type of permanent life insurance product. Another form of permanent life product is universal life (UL) insurance, which is characterized by flexible premiums, a flexible face amount, a flexible death benefit amount, and separation of the three primary policy elements—mortality charges, interest rate, and expense charges.

In some forms of UL policies, the cash value amount is reduced by significant expense charges, called surrender charges, if the policyowner chooses to surrender the policy. For these types of UL policies, regulators may require insurance companies to use another term—such as account value or accumulation value—to describe the cash value that accumulates in the policy. In addition, a second term—either net cash value or cash surrender value—describes the amount available to policyowners. For simplicity, we will use the term cash value in connection with universal life policies.

In this section, you’ll learn more about the characteristics of UL insurance. Then, you’ll read about considerations when calculating the cost of benefits for a block of UL policies.

 

Universal Life Insurance Overview

UL insurance is available in a number of varieties. In this chapter, we limit our discussion to basic fixed universal life (UL) insurance, indexed universal life (IUL) insurance, and variable universal life (VUL) insurance. Regardless of the form of UL insurance, UL policies are subject to different kinds of charges, which insurers deduct from cash values.

 

Types of Universal Life Insurance

Basic fixed UL insurance pays at least a stated guaranteed minimum interest-crediting rate on a policy’s cash value. The insurer also provides for a higher current interest-crediting rate if economic and competitive conditions warrant.

Indexed universal life (IUL) insurance, also called equity indexed universal life insurance, is a type of universal life insurance that offers certain principal value and earnings guarantees, but also offers the possibility of additional earnings by linking the cash value to a published index, such as the S&P 500. An IUL policy’s cash value is backed by the insurer’s general account assets and is generally divided into two policy accounts:

  • A fixed account, in which the crediting rate is a guaranteed minimum interest-crediting rate, similar to basic fixed UL insurance products.

  • An index account, in which the crediting rate is determined by changes in an index. The index-crediting rate is subject to a guaranteed minimum crediting rate—typically called the floor—and a maximum crediting rate—typically called the cap.

The main attraction of IUL is that it offers additional gains in the cash value when the market is strong, with relatively little risk because of the guarantees from the fixed account’s crediting rate and the index account’s floor. The floor limits the product’s investment risk, whereas the cap limits the product’s growth potential. Thus, IUL has a fairly conservative risk-reward structure.

Variable universal life (VUL) insurance is a version of UL insurance that combines the premium and death benefit flexibility of universal life insurance with the investment flexibility and risk of variable life insurance. VUL owners have the option to allocate the cash value to the insurer’s general account or to invest in various subaccounts (or fund options) of the insurer’s separate account. Typically, the VUL owner may change his allocations at least annually.

VUL policies do not guarantee minimum investment earnings or cash values for premiums allocated to subaccounts. Instead, the insurer’s credits to the cash value directly reflect the net investment earnings of the subaccounts. Thus, VUL insurance provides higher potential rewards than basic fixed UL insurance and IUL insurance, but with higher risks.

 

Deductions from Universal Life Policy Components

As with other life insurance products, UL product designs must cover the insurer’s costs of supporting the product. Actuaries determine the policy charges that apply to the insurer’s expenses associated with UL insurance and then add a margin to (1) cover the risk that the actual expenses will exceed expected expenses and (2) provide a profit on the product. Each expected expense and the margin added to that expense constitute the amount of that particular policy charge.

A UL policy specifies separate deductions from the cash value for various policy elements—such as expense charges, mortality charges, and, in some situations, surrender charges. You read about some of these charges in previous chapters.

UL policy charges are typically deducted from policy values at various times while the policy is in force, depending on policy provisions. For instance, the insurer may deduct

  • Charges from the premium payment before the premium amount is added to the policy’s cash value

  • Charges from the policy’s cash value on a monthly basis

  • A surrender charge from the policy’s cash value at the time of a surrender or partial withdrawal

For VUL insurance, charges may be deducted from the policy’s investment returns, typically whenever the cash value is calculated.

  • The mortality and expense (M&E) charge is typically a percentage of the cash value.

  • The investment management charge is typically deducted from the subaccount value.

Insurers have different ways of calculating UL mortality charges, depending on the form of death benefit that the policy specifies.

Calculating the Net Amount at Risk

Although definitions vary from one contract to another, a UL policy’s net amount at risk generally represents the amount of the insurer’s funds that would be required at any given time to pay the policy death benefit. As we have previously stated, the insurer manages its general account assets so it can cover policy cash values, among other things. Therefore, at any given time, a UL policy’s net amount at risk equals the difference between the death benefit and the cash value.

Net Amount at Risk = Death Benefit – Cash Value

The net amount at risk is the remaining amount of money that the insurer would need to pay the policy’s death claim. Thus, for a UL policy, the net amount at risk is a better measure of the insurer’s cost of benefits than the death benefit. The differences between the UL death benefit options are easier to recognize when we enter values into the net amount at risk formulas. First, let’s see an example of the net amount at risk calculation for a policy with an Option A plan.

EXAMPLE

Justin Morton, age 45, is the policyowner-insured of a UL insurance policy issued by Lowfive Insurance Company. His policy has a face amount of $200,000 and a cash value of $50,000. Because Justin’s policy has an Option A plan, Lowfive’s net amount at risk on his policy is found as follows:

Net Amount at Risk = Face Amount – Cash Value
Net Amount at Risk = $200,000 – $50,000
Net Amount at Risk = $150,000

After one year, the policy’s face amount remains $200,000 and its cash value has grown to $75,000. Thus, the insurer’s net amount at risk decreases to $125,000, found as follows:

Net Amount at Risk = Face Amount – Cash Value
Net Amount at Risk = $200,000 – $75,000
Net Amount at Risk = $125,000

Analysis: In both scenarios, the policy’s death benefit was equal to the face amount, or $200,000. However, Lowfive’s net amount at risk decreased from the first scenario to the second. Therefore, we see that for a UL policy with an Option A plan—assuming the face amount remains the same—as the policy’s cash value grows, the insurer’s net amount at risk decreases.

Now, let’s examine the same policy with an Option B death benefit plan.

EXAMPLE

As before, Justin’s policy has a face amount of $200,000 and a cash value of $50,000. Because Justin’s policy has an Option B plan, his policy’s death benefit is $250,000, calculated as follows:

Death Benefit = Face Amount + Cash Value
Death Benefit = $200,000 + $50,000
Death Benefit = $250,000

In addition, Lowfive’s net amount at risk is as follows:

Net Amount at Risk = (Face Amount + Cash Value) – Cash Value
Net Amount at Risk = ($200,000 + $50,000) – $50,000
Net Amount at Risk = $200,000

Again, after one year, the policy’s face amount remains $200,000 and its cash value has grown to $75,000. The death benefit and net amount at risk are calculated as follows:

Death Benefit = Face Amount + Cash Value
Death Benefit = $200,000 + $75,000
Death Benefit = $275,000
Net Amount at Risk = (Face Amount + Cash Value) - Cash Value
Net Amount at Risk = ($200,000 + $75,000) – $75,000
Net Amount at Risk = $200,000

Analysis: In one year, the policy’s death benefit grew from $250,000 to $275,000. However, Lowfive’s net amount at risk—$200,000—remained the same. Therefore, for a UL policy with an Option B plan, as the policy’s cash value grows, the death benefit grows. Assuming the face amount doesn’t change, the insurer’s net amount at risk stays constant.

Lastly, we have an example of the net amount at risk and death benefit calculations if Justin’s policy has an Option C plan.

EXAMPLE

Justin’s policy has a face amount of $200,000 and a cash value of $50,000. To date, Justin has paid $7,000 in premiums. The policy’s death benefit is calculated as follows:

Death Benefit = Face Amount + Premiums Paid
Death Benefit = $200,000 + $7,000
Death Benefit = $207,000

Lowfive’s net amount at risk is calculated as follows:

Net Amount at Risk = (Face Amount + Premiums Paid) – Cash Value
Net Amount at Risk = ($200,000 + $7,000) – $50,000
Net Amount at Risk = $157,000

Analysis: Under the Option C plan, the policy’s death benefit changes when Justin pays more premiums—or adjusts the policy’s face amount—but not when the cash value changes. Lowfive’s net amount at risk will fluctuate depending on the amount of premiums that Justin pays and any changes to the policy’s cash value.

 

Calculating the Monthly Mortality Charge

For UL insurance, two estimates are required to find the cost of benefits: (1) the number of death benefits in a month—estimated by applying the mortality rate—and (2) the average death benefit amount. Typically, the monthly mortality rate applied varies by gender and underwriting risk class and increases as the insureds age.

Two monthly mortality rates apply to UL products:

  • The guaranteed maximum mortality rate is specified in the contract and sets an upper limit on the mortality charge. The insurer guarantees not to charge a rate higher than the guaranteed maximum mortality rate.

  • The current mortality rate is the monthly mortality rate actually used to calculate the monthly mortality charge and is generally substantially lower than the guaranteed maximum mortality rate. The current mortality rate represents the expected mortality rate plus a mortality margin. (Think of the mortality margin as a type of provision for adverse deviation that specifically applies to mortality costs and mortality charges.) The formula to calculate the current mortality rate is

Current mortality rate = Expected mortality rate + Mortality margin

When calculating a UL product’s monthly mortality charge, the insurer makes a reasonable assumption as to a death benefit amount and uses that amount to set a rate that can be applied to contracts of any size. Generally, the benefit amount for a UL policy is considered to be the insurer’s net amount at risk. Thus, for UL products, the insurer develops a monthly mortality charge in accordance with a certain level of net amount at risk.

The insurer could develop a mortality charge per $1,000 of net amount at risk, based on the assumption that the average death benefit amount is $1,000. Then, for a UL policy with a net amount at risk of $10,000, the insurer could charge ten times the mortality charge developed for a policy with a net amount of risk of $1,000. Some insurers may “band” the mortality rate so that the higher the net amount at risk, the lower the mortality rate per $1,000.

To finalize an estimate of the cost of benefits, we must multiply the average benefit cost by the average benefit amount—in this case, the net amount at risk. For each UL contract, the insurer applies a selected mortality rate to the net amount at risk to calculate a monthly mortality charge. Monthly mortality rates indicate to the insurer how many insureds are likely to die within each month.

EXAMPLE

Hannah Kendall is insured under a UL policy issued by Charion Insurance Company. The insurer has determined that its net amount at risk for her policy is $100,000—which represents 100 units of $1,000 of insurance (found as $100,000 ÷ $1,000). In addition, Charion’s calculated monthly mortality charge for Hannah’s policy is $0.57 per $1,000 of insurance. Thus, the insurer calculates the total monthly mortality charge for Hannah’s policy as follows:

Monthly mortality charge (per $1,000 of net amount at risk) × units per $1,000 of net amount at risk = Total monthly mortality charge
$0.57 × 100 units = $57.00

Now, suppose that Charion’s net amount at risk for Hannah’s policy changes to $200,000—which represents 200 units per $1,000 of net amount at risk. In this case, the total monthly mortality charge would be $114.00 ($0.57 × 200).

Analysis: Because Charion’s net amount at risk increased in the second scenario, the total monthly mortality charge increased. Likewise, if the net amount at risk had decreased, the amount of the total monthly mortality charge would have been lower.

The monthly mortality charge is often combined with a separate monthly expense charge, and the resulting combination is called a monthly policy charge. For UL products, the insurer uses expense analysis techniques to set a monthly expense charge to cover its expenses. The formula to calculate the monthly policy charge is

Monthly policy charge = Monthly expense charge + Monthly mortality charge
