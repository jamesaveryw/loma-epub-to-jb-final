Cost of Benefits for Riders

Throughout this chapter, you have seen how life insurance companies use mortality rates to project the mortality cost for a block of similar policies. Insurers generally model the cost of benefits for each policy rider in a similar way.

Recall from Chapter 6 that the projected cost of benefits generally equals the sum of each potential benefit payment to a policyowner multiplied by the expected probability that the benefit will be payable, as seen in in the following formula:

Projected cost of a single benefit for a given policy year = Potential benefit amount payable × Probability that the benefit will be payable

In general, riders present antiselection risks to insurers. Remember that antiselection risk is the risk of loss or adverse product performance due to the tendency of individuals who believe they have a greater-than-average likelihood of loss to seek insurance protection to a greater extent than do those who believe they have an average or a less-than-average likelihood of loss. For instance, a proposed insured who is convinced she will eventually have a critical illness is more likely than the average proposed insured to seek attachment of a critical illness benefit rider to her policy. Therefore, actuaries take antiselection risk into consideration when assessing the probability that a particular rider benefit will be payable.
