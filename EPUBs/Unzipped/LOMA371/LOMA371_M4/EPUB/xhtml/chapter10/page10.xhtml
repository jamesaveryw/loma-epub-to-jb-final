Setting Pricing Premium Rates

While this chapter has gone into depth about estimating the cost of benefits, setting a product’s pricing premiums involves other financial components. Figure 10.3 illustrates the relationships between the premium components that we have covered in this text.

You read about one such component, investment earnings, in Chapter 7. An insurer invests a portion of the premiums it receives and expects to achieve investment earnings. Investment earnings enable the insurer to set a product’s base premium lower than the sum of its expected cost of benefits and operating expenses. In other words, investment earnings should offset some of the charges necessary to support the product.

In Chapter 9, we discussed another component, operating expenses. When designing products, actuaries develop conservative estimates for both operating expenses and cost of benefits. For instance, an insurer may project a mortality rate that is higher than expected to ensure that the product’s premium rate will be more than adequate.

Conservatism in developing actuarial assumptions provides a buffer that allows for adverse deviation. Thus, each product has a built-in margin for error. If deviations are positive—that is, a product’s actual expenses are less than its expected expenses—then the result is extra profit for the insurer. Stated differently, positive deviations become contributions to the company’s earnings.

As you saw in Figure 10.3, actuaries use the premium components to calculate the premium for a block of policies. Then, they establish the premium rate for the block by dividing the single premium by the number of policies in the block. The premium rate is expressed as a basic amount of insurance coverage called the unit of coverage. For life insurance, a unit of coverage usually is $1,000 of coverage. To find the premium amount to charge for a particular policy, the insurer multiplies the premium rate by the number of coverage units.

EXAMPLE

Whyzee Insurance Company’s actuaries determine that the annual premium rate for a block of whole life insurance policies is $4.50 per $1,000 of coverage. The insurer wants to calculate the annual premium amount for one of the block’s policyowners, Darren Schneider. The face amount of Darren’s policy is $500,000.

Analysis: Darren’s policy has 500 units of coverage, found as $500,000 ÷ $1,000. Whyzee multiplies the annual premium rate by the number of coverage units to find the amount of Darren’s annual premium payment:

$4.50 × 500 = $2,250

The product’s premium rate represents the base rate that the insurer charges to policyowners in the block. In addition, other factors affect the premium rate that the insurer actually charges a particular policyowner. For instance, an underwriter may assess a proposed insured as a substandard risk. In that case, the insurer would charge the policyowner a higher premium rate than owners of policies with insureds that are classified as standard risks.
