Glossary

accelerated death benefit
A supplemental life insurance policy benefit that typically allows the policyowner to elect to receive all or part of the policy’s death benefit before the insured’s death if certain conditions are met. Also known as a living benefit. See Module 4, Chapter 10.
accidental death benefit
A supplemental life insurance policy benefit that requires the insurer to pay a specified amount of money in addition to the policy’s basic death benefit if an insured dies as a result of an accident. See Module 4, Chapter 10.
accumulated value
For a deferred annuity contract, the total amount of premiums and earnings, less the amount of any withdrawals or charges assessed by the insurer. Also known as account value. See Module 3, Chapter 7.
accumulation period
For a deferred annuity, the period that begins on the contract’s purchase date and runs until either the beginning of the payout period or the date when the owner terminates the contract. See Module 4, Chapter 11.
accumulation unit
An ownership share in a selected subaccount of a separate account held during the accumulation period. See Module 3, Chapter 7.
actuarial assumption
An estimated value used in financial modeling, typically for the purposes of risk management or technical product design. See Module 1, Chapter 1.
actuarial control cycle
A process of actuarial practices expressed as a process of three repeated steps: define the need, design the solution, and monitor the results. See Module 1, Chapter 1.
actuarial memorandum
The document in an Actuarial Opinion and Memorandum (AOM) consisting of a formal report containing documentation supporting the conclusion expressed in the actuarial opinion. See Module 1, Chapter 2.
actuarial opinion
The document in an Actuarial Opinion and Memorandum (AOM) which states that the company’s reserves are adequate for the anticipated cash flows arising from the insurer’s contractual obligations, given the assets supporting them. See Module 1, Chapter 2.
Actuarial Opinion and Memorandum (AOM)
A formal asset-liability management (ALM) report that consists of two documents: an actuarial opinion and an actuarial memorandum. See Module 1, Chapter 2.
actuary
A technical expert in insurance, annuities, and financial instruments who applies mathematical knowledge to industry and company statistics to calculate various financial values. See Module 1, Chapter 1.
administrative charge
A charge an insurer levies to cover the cost of issuing certain types of life insurance or annuity contracts, making administrative changes to such contracts, preparing contract owner statements, and performing general maintenance activities. Also known as administrative expense charge, administrative fee, contract fee, or service fee. See Module 3, Chapter 9.
administrative supervision
A legal condition under which an insurer may be required to obtain the insurance commissioner’s permission before taking any of a variety of specified actions. See Module 1, Chapter 2.
adverse deviation
In product operations, a deviation that produces a decrease in actual product profitability compared to assumed product profitability. Return to text.
annual reset method
For fixed indexed annuities, an index-crediting mechanism that involves comparing the value of the reference index at the start of each contract year with the value at the end of the year. For the next year, the starting value is reset to the ending value from the previous year. Also known as ratchet method. See Module 4, Chapter 11.
Annual Statement
A financial report that every insurer in the United States must file annually, with interim quarterly reports, with the National Association of Insurance Commissioners (NAIC) and the insurance regulatory organization in each state in which the insurer conducts business to assist regulators with monitoring insurer solvency. See Module 1, Chapter 2.
annuitant
The person whose lifetime the insurance company uses to determine the amount and duration of periodic income payments payable under an annuity contract. See Module 4, Chapter 11.
annuitization
The process of converting the accumulated value of a deferred annuity into the annuity’s periodic income payments. See Module 4, Chapter 11.
annuity due
A series of equal periodic payments for which the payment occurs at the beginning of each payment period. See Module 3, Chapter 7.
annuity mortality table
A type of mortality table that shows the projected mortality rates and survival rates for a population of annuitants only. See Module 3, Chapter 8.
antiselection risk
The risk of loss or adverse product performance due to the tendency of individuals who believe they have a greater-than-average likelihood of loss to seek insurance protection to a greater extent than do those who believe they have an average or less-than-average likelihood of loss. Also known as adverse selection risk. See Module 1, Chapter 2.
asset adequacy analysis
A broad actuarial practice undertaken to ensure that the assets backing reserves meet established standards. See Module 1, Chapter 4.
asset-based commission schedule
For annuity sales, a commission schedule that calculates commissions as a percentage of the accumulated value of a deferred annuity contract’s funds. In addition, commissions are payable as long as the contract remains in force, even if the contract owner stops making premium payments. See Module 3, Chapter 9.
asset-liability management (ALM)
In an actuarial context, the practice of coordinating the administration of an insurer’s asset portfolio (its investments) with the administration of its liability portfolio (its obligations to customers) so as to achieve the best possible effects. See Module 1, Chapter 1.
asset portfolio
In asset-liability management (ALM), the portfolio in which the insurer holds securities and other invested assets. See Module 1, Chapter 3.
asset risk (C-1 risk)
The contingency risk that the insurer will lose money—other than from movements in market interest rates—on its investments in stocks, bonds, mortgages, and real estate. See Module 1, Chapter 2.
assumed mortality
The hypothetical or assumed number or rate of deaths in a given group of people. See Module 3, Chapter 8.
back-end sales charge
A sales assessed when an investor sells a security. See Module 3, Chapter 9.
Basel Committee on Banking Supervision (BCBS)
A membership organization comprising representatives of bank supervisory authorities and central banks from countries with prominent economies whose mission is to provide a forum for discussing bank supervision matters worldwide. See Module 1, Chapter 2.
Basel III
A comprehensive set of financial supervision reform measures, developed by the Basel Committee on Banking Supervision (BCBS), and designed to strengthen the regulation, supervision, and risk management of the banking, insurance, and securities sectors globally. See Module 1, Chapter 2.
basic mortality table
A type of mortality table that has no margin built into the rates, is used for technical product design, and provides realistic mortality rates so that an insurer can best estimate future mortality costs. See Module 3, Chapter 8.
beneficiary
The person or legal entity who may receive benefits accrued or values remaining in an annuity contract upon the death of the contract owner or annuitant. See Module 4, Chapter 11.
big data
A large amount of information gathered from various sources, in various formats, and at a rapid speed. See Module 1, Chapter 1.
business process risk
The risk of a loss related to the processes used by an organization to create, sell, and service a product. See Module 1, Chapter 2.
business risk
The risk that changes in a company’s external environment will affect its operations. See Module 1, Chapter 2.
cap
For fixed indexed annuities, an upper limit, expressed as a percent, on the rate of a reference index’s gain in value that the insurer credits to the annuity contract as interest. See Module 4, Chapter 11.
cash-flow matching
A risk management strategy that involves identifying the patterns of cash outflows for insurance products and matching those cash outflows with a selection of assets that will produce a similar pattern of cash inflows. See Module 1, Chapter 3.
cash flow testing
The use of simulation modeling to project the cash flows associated with an insurance company’s assets and liabilities as of a given valuation data and to compare the timing and amounts of asset and liability cash flows at various times after the valuation date. See Module 1, Chapter 4.
cash surrender value
The amount of the cash value that a policyowner is entitled to receive from the insurer upon surrender of the policy. Also known as the surrender value or surrender benefit. See Module 4, Chapter 10.
cohort
A defined group of people. See Module 3, Chapter 8.
competition risk
The risk that an insurer’s relative market position will be adversely affected by the actions of its direct competitors, changes in industry structure, or changes in standards for the use of technology. See Module 1, Chapter 2.
competitive intelligence
The systematic collection and analysis of publicly available information about competitors and ongoing developments in the marketing environment. Return to text.
compounding period
Each of the interest periods used in future value calculations. See Module 3, Chapter 7.
comprehensive business analysis
An evaluation of all the factors that are likely to affect the design, production, pricing, marketing, and sales potential of a new product. Return to text.
conditionally vested commission
A commission that becomes vested only after an agent reaches a certain age or number of years with the company. See Module 3, Chapter 9.
conditional tail expectation (CTE)
A statistical measure of the severity of tail risk, calculated as the average of all the values within a specified range of a probability distribution. See Module 1, Chapter 4.
Conference of Sponsoring Organizations of the Treadway Commission (COSO)
A consortium of self-regulatory member organizations that provides frameworks and guidance on enterprise risk management, internal control, and fraud defense. See Module 1, Chapter 2.
confidence limit
An expression of the probability that a specific outcome will fall within a stated range. See Module 1, Chapter 4.
contingency pricing structure
A variation in premium rate structures for individual life insurance products that gives an insurer mechanisms for adjusting the company’s charges for an in-force product to reflect the company’s financial results for the product. Return to text.
contingency risks (C-risks)
In the United States, four officially recognized categories of risk that regulators have identified as being crucial to an insurer’s cash flows and that directly affect an insurer’s capital adequacy. See Module 1, Chapter 2.
continuation coverage
The coverage provided to the customers of a failed insurance company under a specific kind of reinsurance arrangement. See Module 1, Chapter 2.
contract maintenance charge
For variable (unit-linked) life insurance and annuity products, a periodic charge assessed to cover general maintenance costs, such as preparation of account and other statements. See Module 3, Chapter 9.
contract owner
The person or other entity who owns and exercises all the rights and privileges of an annuity contract. See Module 4, Chapter 11.
contractual reserves
The amount that, together with future premiums and investment income, an insurer estimates it will need to pay policy benefits as they come due. See Module 1, Chapter 1.
cost accounting systeym
An accounting subsystem that accumulates expense data for the dual purposes of effective cost control and accurate product design activities. See Module 3, Chapter 9.
cost accumulation
The process of capturing all company costs and classifying them in meaningful ways. See Module 3, Chapter 9.
cost allocation
The accounting process of assigning an indirect cost to a particular cost object according to a formal procedure. Also known as expense allocation. See Module 3, Chapter 9.
cost allocation method
The formal procedure used to assign indirect costs. See Module 3, Chapter 9.
costing
The process used during technical product design to set a product’s expense projections. See Module 3, Chapter 9.
cost object
Any purpose for which a business measures costs. See Module 3, Chapter 9.
cost of benefits
For an insurance or annuity product, the value of the contractually required benefits that the product promises to pay; the projected cost of benefits generally equals the sum of each potential benefit payment to a customer multiplied by the expected probability that the benefit will be payable. Return to text.
cost of capital
The overall percentage cost that an insurer pays for the funds it employs. See Module 3, Chapter 7.
crediting-rate resolution
A formal declaration made by the board of directors of the rate of interest the insurer will credit on customers’ money held in interest-bearing products. See Module 1, Chapter 3.
credit risk
The risk that (1) a borrower defaults on debt obligations by failing to make payments of principal or interest on time, or (2) a lender sustains a financial loss based on an adverse change in a borrower’s creditworthiness. See Module 1, Chapter 2.
critical illness benefit
An accelerated death benefit under which the insurer agrees to pay a portion of the policy’s face amount to the policyowner if the insured suffers from one of a number of specified diseases. Also known as a dread disease (DD) benefit. See Module 4, Chapter 10.
currency risk
The risk arising from changes in currency exchange rates. Also known as foreign exchange risk. See Module 1, Chapter 2.
current interest-crediting rate
The interest rate an insurer declares and pays if a fixed-rate contract remains in force for a specified period of time. See Module 3, Chapter 7.
current mortality rate
In universal life (UL) products, the monthly mortality rate actually used to calculate the monthly mortality charge; is generally substantially lower than the guaranteed maximum mortality rate. See Module 4, Chapter 10.
cyber risk
The risk of a financial loss, business disruption, theft of proprietary information, or damage to the reputation of an organization caused by failure in a process, procedure, or technology involving interconnected systems. See Module 1, Chapter 2.
data scientist
An individual who uses techniques from statistics and computer programming to (1) identify patterns and trends in data and (2) create mathematical models that forecast business outcomes. See Module 1, Chapter 1.
Day 1 functionality
Product administration and IT elements that a company must have in place and functioning when a product is first made available for sale. Return to text.
Day 2 functionality
Product administration and IT elements that are necessary at some future date to service and administer an insurance product, but which can be implemented after the product has been launched. Return to text.
default risk
In the context of investing, the type of credit risk that involves the risk that an insurer will not receive the cash flows to which it is entitled because a party with which the insurer has a financial arrangement is late with payments or entirely fails to fulfill its debt obligations. See Module 1, Chapter 2.
deferred annuity
An annuity under which periodic income payments are scheduled to begin more than one annuity period after the date on which the annuity was purchased. See Module 4, Chapter 11.
dependent variable
A variable that reacts to changes in other variables. By convention, dependent variables are labeled y. See Module 1, Chapter 4.
deposit-based commission schedule
For annuity sales, a commission schedule that pays commissions only on premium payments made by annuity owners. See Module 3, Chapter 9.
derivative security
A financial security, such as a stock option, that derives its value from another security. Also known as a derivative. Return to text.
deterministic modeling
A form of quantitative modeling that simulates the real-world interactions of a stated set of input variables and produces a single set of output variables. See Module 1, Chapter 4.
direct expense
A product expense incurred for or physically traceable to a specified life insurance or annuity product. Also known as traceable cost. See Module 3, Chapter 9.
discounting period
Each of the interest periods used in present value calculations. See Module 3, Chapter 7.
distribution risk
The risk of financial losses resulting from activities and resources involved in making products available to customers. See Module 1, Chapter 2.
diversifiable risk
Risk that is specific to an individual asset or issuer. Also known as nonsystematic risk and specific risk. See Module 1, Chapter 3.
diversification
A risk management strategy for spreading risk by investing in different assets having different risk profiles. See Module 1, Chapter 3.
downgrade risk
In the context of investing, the type of credit risk that involves the risk of deterioration in a credit rating assigned to an issuer of a debt security. See Module 1, Chapter 2.
duration
A statistic that measures the price sensitivity of an asset to changes in interest rates; can be defined as the time-weighted value of expected cash inflows, expressed in years. See Module 1, Chapter 3.
duration gap report
An asset-liability management (ALM) report that provides a snapshot of the insurer’s asset-liability match at the time of the report. See Module 1, Chapter 3.
duration matching
A risk management strategy that involves matching the duration statistics for fixed-income assets such as bonds with the duration statistics for the insurance company liabilities that the assets support. See Module 1, Chapter 3.
dynamic assumptions
In financial modeling, variables that change over time, but in a way that is predictable and formula driven. See Module 1, Chapter 4.
dynamic financial analysis (DFA)
The use of simulation modeling and multiple scenario testing to project an insurer’s assets, liabilities, and owners’ equity as of a given valuation date and to compare the values of those variables at various times after the valuation date. See Module 1, Chapter 4.
dynamic solvency testing (DST)
An application of dynamic financial analysis (DFA) that involves projecting an insurer’s capital as of a given valuation date and comparing the projected amounts of capital at various times after the valuation date. See Module 1, Chapter 4.
earnings
For an insurance or annuity product, the amount that a product adds to the insurance company’s capital in a given period; takes into account noncash adjustments, such as a product’s contribution to capital. See Module 3, Chapter 7.
economic capital
An estimate of the amount of capital that a financial institution calculates to internally manage its own risks. Also known as internal capital. See Module 1, Chapter 2.
enterprise risk management (ERM)
A system that identifies and quantifies risks from both potential threats and potential opportunities and manages these risks in a coordinated approach that supports the organization’s strategic objectives. See Module 1, Chapter 1.
equity risk
The risk of a loss arising from movements in the direction of the stock, or equity, markets. See Module 1, Chapter 2.
ethical behavior
Behavior that meets accepted standards of moral conduct. See Module 1, Chapter 3.
event risk
The risk that a single, unforeseen occurrence will negatively affect a company’s financial position. See Module 1, Chapter 2.
expected mortality
The number or rate of deaths statistically likely to occur in a group of people at a given age. See Module 3, Chapter 8.
expense budget
A financial plan of action that identifies the types and amounts of expenses a company expects to incur during an accounting period. See Module 1, Chapter 2.
expense loading
The amount charged to cover each policy’s share of the expenses of operation. See Module 1, Chapter 2.
expense risk
The risk that actual expenses will differ from the amounts assumed during product design. See Module 1, Chapter 2.
experience data
Internal, proprietary data about the past behavior of certain variables such as mortality rates and expenses. See Module 1, Chapter 1.
experience mortality rate
The historical rate of death in a given group of people. See Module 3, Chapter 8.
experience study
A compilation of data representing company or industry-wide historical experience with a specified modeling variable. See Module 1, Chapter 4.
extrapolation
In financial modeling, the process of estimating values outside of a known range on the basis of other values derived from direct observation. See Module 1, Chapter 4.
favorable deviation
In product operations, a difference between actual and assumed product values that produces an increase in actual product profitability relative to assumed product profitability. Return to text.
feasibility study
An evaluation of whether a company has the capability to take a new product to market. Return to text.
Financial Assistance and Solvency Tracking (FAST) System
In the United States, a collection of analytical solvency tools used to evaluate the solvency of insurers that includes a ratio-based evaluation system established by the National Association of Insurance Commissioners (NAIC); based on (1) ratio analysis of an insurer’s most recent financial statements and (2) analysis of a five-year history of specific aspects of the insurer’s financial statements. See Module 1, Chapter 2.
financial condition examination
In the United States, the part of an onsite formal regulatory audit that investigates two broad aspects of an insurer’s operations: (1) whether the insurer’s accounting records are accurate and the insurer is being operated on a sound and lawful basis, and (2) whether the insurer’s financial and business profiles contain any apparent hazards to the insurer’s solvency. See Module 1, Chapter 2.
financial model
A computer-based mathematical model that approximates the operation of real-world financial processes. See Module 1, Chapter 1.
first-year commission
A commission on life insurance policies that is payable when a contract is purchased and is equal to a stated percentage of the premium the insurer receives during the first policy year. See Module 3, Chapter 9.
fixed amount option
A guaranteed annuity payout option in which the contract owner chooses the amount of income that the insurer will pay periodically; the insurer pays the same amount on a regular basis until the contract value is exhausted. See Module 4, Chapter 11.
fixed annuity
An annuity contract under which the insurer guarantees (1) the minimum interest rate that it will apply to any accumulated value and (2) the minimum amount of the annuity payments that it will pay. See Module 4, Chapter 11.
fixed assumptions
In financial modeling, variables for which actuaries assign a specific value or proportional relationship, and the value or proportion remains unchanged throughout multiple iterations of a model. See Module 1, Chapter 4.
fixed expense
An expense that remains relatively constant regardless of the number of policies or contracts sold or the level of operating activity. See Module 3, Chapter 9.
fixed fund option
For variable life insurance and annuity products, a fund option which guarantees a fixed rate of interest for a specified period of time; premiums allocated to a fixed fund option are invested with the general account. See Module 4, Chapter 11.
fixed indexed annuity (FIA)
A type of annuity that offers the contract owner specified principal and interest rate guarantees, as well as the possibility of additional earnings based on changes in an index by linking crediting on the accumulation value to a published index of stock market prices. Also known as an indexed annuity. See Module 4, Chapter 11.
fixed period option
A guaranteed annuity payout option under which the insurer makes annuity payments for a specified period of time regardless of whether the annuitant is living. See Module 4, Chapter 11.
flexible premium
For life insurance products, a form of premium payment in which a customer can make payments to the insurer at various times so as to increase the savings element in a contract that was established with a single initial premium; such premiums are used with universal life insurance. See Module 4, Chapter 10.
floor provision
For fixed indexed annuities, a provision which specifies that the contract’s values are not reduced if the linked index decreases in value during a measurement period. See Module 4, Chapter 11.
foreign investment risk
The risk of loss when investing in foreign companies if the government takes actions such as nationalization or taxation. See Module 1, Chapter 2.
fraud risk
The risk of a loss when one party intentionally deceives another party to induce that party to part with something of value, usually confidential information or money. See Module 1, Chapter 2.
front-end sales charge
A one-time transaction charge imposed when an investor purchases shares in subaccount investment funds. See Module 3, Chapter 9.
full costing
An accounting method for estimating product-related expenses in which direct and indirect expenses are both counted. Also known as absorption costing. See Module 3, Chapter 9.
fund operating expense charge
A periodic charge that a fund manager assesses to cover its costs of managing variable subaccount assets, buying and selling securities, and administering trades. See Module 3, Chapter 9.
futures studies
Studies that identify possible developments that could disrupt trends—thereby changing the extrapolation of the values from the past—and then produce an array of possibilities for future values based on these developments. See Module 1, Chapter 4.
future value (FV)
The amount that an original sum is expected to be worth at a specified future date, given a specified interest rate. See Module 3, Chapter 7.
future value interest factor (FVIF)
The future value of $1.00 at a given interest rate for a stated number of periods. See Module 3, Chapter 7.
future value interest factors (FVIF) table
A table that lists future value interest factors. See Module 3, Chapter 7.
general account
For an insurer, a portfolio of assets that supports the company’s contractual obligations to owners of the company’s guaranteed products, including whole life insurance, fixed-rate annuities, and other nonvariable products. Also known as general account portfolio. See Module 3, Chapter 7.
general management risk (C-4 risk)
The contingency risk of losses resulting from the insurer’s ineffective general business practices or from the need to pay a special assessment to cover another insurer’s unsound business practices. See Module 1, Chapter 2.
generational mortality table
A type of mortality table in which mortality rates are dependent on both the person’s age and the year the person attains that age. Also known as a dynamic mortality table. See Module 3, Chapter 8.
geopolitical risk
The risk of loss due to unexpected changes in international relationships, political alignments, socioeconomics, or cultural shifts. See Module 1, Chapter 2.
governance
The combination of people, rules, controls, and policies put in place to provide oversight and direction for the organization’s operations and actions so that the organization can achieve its strategic objectives. See Module 1, Chapter 3.
guaranteed annuity payout option
A type of annuity payout option that provides regular payments of either a designated amount or for a designated period and are not linked to any life expectancy or mortality risk. See Module 4, Chapter 11.
guaranteed interest-crediting rate
The minimum interest rate an insurer must pay on a contract’s accumulated value. See Module 3, Chapter 7.
guaranteed lifetime withdrawal benefit (GLWB)
An annuity living benefit rider that guarantees minimum withdrawals to a variable annuity owner for life without being required to annuitize the contract. See Module 4, Chapter 11.
guaranteed maximum mortality rate
For universal life (UL) products, a monetary amount specified in the contract which sets an upper limit on the mortality charge. See Module 4, Chapter 10.
guaranteed minimum accumulation benefit (GMAB)
A variable annuity living benefit rider that guarantees a minimum protected value for a variable annuity contract even if its accumulated value declines because of poor investment performance. Usually, the contract owner must wait for a specified period before the guaranteed value becomes available. See Module 4, Chapter 11.
guaranteed minimum death benefit (GMDB)
A variable annuity contract feature which generally guarantees that the contract’s death benefit will at least equal a specified minimum amount. See Module 4, Chapter 11.
guaranteed minimum income benefit (GMIB)
An annuity living benefit rider that guarantees a minimum protected value that can be converted into periodic income payments—usually after a waiting period of seven to ten years—even if the annuity’s accumulated value declines because of poor investment performance. See Module 4, Chapter 11.
guaranteed minimum withdrawal benefit (GMWB)
An annuity living benefit rider that guarantees a minimum protected value against which a variable annuity contract owner may make annual withdrawals of a specified amount, without charge, even if the contract’s accumulated value falls below the protected value as a result of poor investment performance. See Module 4, Chapter 11.
guaranty association
An organization that operates under the supervision of the state insurance commissioner to protect policyowners, insureds, beneficiaries, annuitants, payees, and assignees against losses that might result from the impairment or insolvency of an insurer that operates in that state. See Module 1, Chapter 2.
heaped commission schedule
For life insurance sales, a commission schedule that features relatively high first-year commissions and lower renewal commissions. See Module 3, Chapter 9.
hedge cost
An expense for trading and holding derivative securities. Return to text.
hedging
A risk management strategy that involves holding an asset with characteristics that counterbalance one or more of the risks in the investor’s risk array. See Module 1, Chapter 3.
high water mark method
For fixed indexed annuities, an index-crediting mechanism that compares the value of the reference index at the beginning of the contract term with the highest value—or high water mark—that the index reaches on any contract anniversary date during the term. See Module 4, Chapter 11.
human resources risk
The risk that a loss will occur as a result of a company’s failure to properly train or evaluate prospective and current employees. See Module 1, Chapter 2.
hurdle rate
The minimum percentage rate of return on capital that an insurer must achieve to cover its cost of capital. Also known as required return. See Module 3, Chapter 7.
idea generation
The process of searching for new product ideas that are consistent with the company’s overall product development strategy and the needs of its target markets. Return to text.
idea screening
An activity in which a team quickly and inexpensively evaluates new product ideas to separate workable ideas from those that have little or no potential. Return to text.
immediate annuity
An annuity contract under which periodic annuity payments begin no more than one year after the contract is purchased. See Module 4, Chapter 11.
independent variable
A variable that influences the behavior of another variable. By convention, independent variables are labeled x. See Module 1, Chapter 4.
indeterminate premium structure
A variation in premium rate structures for individual life insurance products that allows the company to periodically reset premium rates on products while guaranteeing customers that premiums will not exceed a maximum specified level. Return to text.
index
A statistical measurement system that tracks the performance of a group of similar investments. See Module 4, Chapter 11.
index credits
For fixed indexed annuities, the monetary credits that are awarded to the contract’s value. See Module 4, Chapter 11.
indexed crediting
For fixed indexed annuities, guarantees that the insurance company will periodically award monetary credits, known as index credits, to the contract’s accumulation value on a specified basis related to a specified external index of stock market price movements. See Module 4, Chapter 11.
indexed universal life (IUL) insurance
A type of universal life insurance that offers certain principal value and earnings guarantees, but also offers the possibility of additional earnings by linking the cash value to a published index. Also called equity indexed universal life insurance. See Module 4, Chapter 10.
indirect expense
An expense that cannot be traced to or that is not incurred for one specific product. Also called common cost. See Module 3, Chapter 9.
initial investment
For a new product, the amount of capital that an insurer must invest to establish the product. See Module 3, Chapter 7.
inflation risk
The risk that the price of goods and services will increase over time, causing a decline in the value of investments and returns. See Module 1, Chapter 2.
insolvency
An insurer’s inability to meet its financial obligations on time. See Module 1, Chapter 1.
Insurance Regulatory Information System (IRIS)
In the United States, a tool within the FAST system that monitors the financial condition of insurers with a view toward preventing insolvencies which uses 12 standardized financial ratios to identify insurance companies that are likely to experience financial difficulties. See Module 1, Chapter 2.
interest-crediting rate
The interest rate credited to products on behalf of customers. Also known as crediting rate. See Module 3, Chapter 7.
interest-crediting strategies
The formulas and criteria a company uses to set the interest rates it will credit for interest-sensitive products. See Module 1, Chapter 3.
interest-rate assumption
For an insurance product, the assumed rate of investment earnings on the product in a specified period after investment expenses have been covered. Also known as assumed interest rate. See Module 3, Chapter 7.
interest-rate risk (C-3 risk)
The contingency risk that movements in market interest rates will cause an insurer’s assets to lose value or its liabilities to gain value. See Module 1, Chapter 2.
interest-sensitive cash flow testing
A type of cash flow testing in which an insurer analyzes the effects of various interest-rate scenarios on cash flows. See Module 1, Chapter 4.
interest spread
The difference between the rate of return an insurer earns on its investments and the current interest-crediting rate. See Module 3, Chapter 7.
internal audit
An examination of the company’s records, policies, and procedures conducted by a person who works for the organization, typically in the compliance area. See Module 1, Chapter 3.
internal control
The process a company takes to encourage adherence to its policies, promote operating efficiency, and safeguard the organization’s assets. See Module 1, Chapter 3.
internal rate of return (IRR)
For a life insurance or an annuity product, the interest rate at which the product’s net cash flows must be discounted, using present value techniques, in order to exactly repay the insurer’s initial investment in the product. See Module 3, Chapter 7.
International Association of Insurance Supervisors (IAIS)
An association that works globally to (1) improve regulatory supervision of the insurance industry, (2) maintain efficient, fair, safe, and stable insurance markets, (3) protect policyowners, (4) promote well-regulated insurance markets, and (5) support global financial stability. See Module 1, Chapter 2.
International Organization for Standardization (ISO)
An independent, non-governmental international standards-setting organization that consists of national standard bodies of 164 countries See Module 1, Chapter 2.
in the money (ITM)
A situation in which the benefits available from ownership options or riders are more favorable for the customer than the benefits available under the contract itself. See Module 3, Chapter 9.
investment
Any use of a company’s resources that is intended to generate a profit or positive return of some type. See Module 1, Chapter 1.
investment activity report
An asset-liability management (ALM) report that specifies the details of all investment portfolio transactions, including all asset acquisitions and all dispositions of assets from the investment portfolio through sales, prepayments (redemptions), or repayment at maturity. See Module 1, Chapter 3.
investment policy
A policy that actuaries and investment experts set to manage the investment risk associated with new products; specifies limits on the asset types and the proportions of the assets the company will use to support a product, as well as any strategies needed to balance a new product’s unmanaged liability risks. Return to text.
investment portfolio performance review
A quarterly asset-liability management (ALM) report that summarizes the company’s investment performance for the board of directors and the ALM committee. See Module 1, Chapter 3.
investment risk
The possibility that an investment may fail to generate some or all of an expected profit or that the insurer will lose all or part of the original investment. See Module 1, Chapter 2.
joint and survivor (J&S) annuity
A life annuity payout option that provides periodic income payments to two or more annuitants. The periodic income payments are based on the life expectancies of the annuitants and continue until both or all of the annuitants have died. Also known as joint and last survivor annuity. See Module 4, Chapter 11.
joint life annuity
A life annuity payout option that typically covers more than one life and guarantees periodic payments until one of the covered individuals dies, and then pays nothing more. See Module 4, Chapter 11.
