Product Monitoring, Evaluation, and Feedback

Insurers are constantly introducing new product ideas into the product development cycle. As a result, they must monitor and evaluate the new products that emerge. In particular, a product’s evaluation continues after a company has introduced it for sale. This activity creates an ongoing feedback process. For example, companies accumulate the actual expense levels the new products generate early in their operation and communicate the emerging data to staff in administrative areas of the company.

The ongoing monitoring, evaluation, and feedback process may result in minor refinements or major innovations. Insurers must track and evaluate the performance of existing products in terms of their impact on the company’s financial performance, the performance of agents and other product distributors, and the marketplace reactions of customers and competitors.

To evaluate a product’s success, companies can monitor the following results:

  • Value and number of contracts sold

  • Actual compared to assumed product profitability

  • Number of new customers

  • Demographic characteristics of purchasers

  • Surrender or withdrawal rates

  • Mortality rates

  • Rates of policy changes such as conversions or reinstatements

  • Sales through various distribution channels or methods

  • Impact of the product on other products—for example, whether product sales augment or replace sales of existing products

Some factors that can contribute to poor sales of a new product include

  • An inadequate compensation structure for sales agents

  • Incorrect interpretation of the needs of the target market

  • Inadequate training of sales agents or customer service representatives about the product

  • Competition from another product offered by the same company or by a competitor

If the results from a product are unsatisfactory, the company has a range of options to explore, from slightly adjusting the product to discontinuing future sales of the product. When modifying a product, an insurer must determine the reasons for the failure of the product. When sales of a new product are poor, a company can take the following corrective actions:

  • Redesigning the product to meet a specific need for customers or sales agents

  • Changing the product’s underwriting guidelines

  • Using a different distribution system

  • Pulling the product from the market while determining how best to service the products already issued

  • Providing additional training for sales agents

Most of these actions require the insurer to rerun financial models to ensure that the changes made are likely to produce the needed results.

EXAMPLE

Example. Traffic Life Insurance released a universal life insurance product that is not performing to the company’s satisfaction. The company considers several possible modifications, such as adding a desirable feature or raising the interest-crediting rate. Traffic decides to offer a new rider and modifies the product accordingly. If the modification does not attract enough additional customers to produce satisfactory results, Traffic will consider another corrective action, such as increasing the interest-crediting rate or discontinuing new sales of the product entirely.

After a company accumulates experience with a product, the company can compare the actual values from experience against the expected (or assumed) values used in product design. The company creates tables of actual and expected values and then conducts actual-to-expected (A/E) analysis for each of many different financial variables—such as the cost of benefits, company expenses, internal rate of return, investment earnings for fixed-rate products, and premium collections.

Note that expected values and assumed values can be different concepts. We discuss the difference between expected mortality values and assumed mortality values in Chapter 8.

Actuarial software can access a company’s product experience data and calculate A/E values for many combinations of data. For example, software can report actual lapses and surrenders according to the reasons for the terminations, and swiftly organize this feedback so that decision-makers can quickly see which reasons for lapses and surrenders are most common.

In comparisons of actual and expected values, a difference between the values is a deviation. Deviations can be favorable or adverse. In product operations, an adverse deviation produces a decrease in actual product profitability compared to assumed product profitability. Any amount included in the assumption values to provide an allowance for unexpected negative outcomes is known as a provision for adverse deviation (PAD). Adverse deviations are the reason for including PADs in a deterministic product design. An adverse deviation arises whenever a specific product result differs from the modeled assumptions so that

  • Product revenue is lower than assumed

  • Benefits payments are greater than assumed

  • Product expenses are greater than assumed

If large adverse deviations develop, then the insurer must try to identify the reasons for them and, if possible, take corrective action, such as cutting operating expenses. If an insurer is able to regularly update the assumptions used to estimate its future policy benefits, there is less need for the estimate to include a PAD.

In product operations, a favorable deviation is a difference between actual and assumed product values that produces an increase in actual product profitability relative to assumed product profitability. A favorable deviation occurs whenever a specific product result differs from the assumptions used in product design so that

  • Product revenue is greater than assumed

  • Benefits payments are lower than assumed

  • Product expenses are lower than assumed

As the term suggests, a favorable deviation requires no corrective action, though further study may indicate ways that future product design can capitalize on such deviations. Note that a reliable A/E analysis requires years of data, which can pose difficulties for insurers; a company may be selling a product for years before reliably learning whether the deviations are favorable or not.
