Technical Product Design

For life insurance products, technical product design begins with setting a product’s financial design. Once the design has been established and tested, the development team creates a budget and schedule for the implementation process. Figure 6.1 outlines the technical product design stage.

When designing new life insurance or annuity products, insurers focus on two key objectives: ensuring the timely delivery of benefits to customers and generating profit for the company. Other important objectives of product design include

  • Establishing a level of future benefits that the company can be certain to deliver under unknown future economic and demographic conditions

  • Covering the company’s expenses of supporting the product

  • Providing adequate future investment income to support the company’s financial obligations for products

  • Understanding specific product risks in terms of the company’s risk standards

  • Identifying premium and commission structures that will succeed in the marketplace

  • Providing an adequate return to the company’s owners

  • Enhancing competitive positioning in the marketplace

  • Addressing untapped or under-targeted consumer needs

In terms of managing risk, technical product design is the most important stage of product development. During technical product design, the company fully develops the preliminary plans created during product planning and comprehensive business analysis. The technical product design stage requires the contributions of employees from several areas, such as marketing, finance, and underwriting. However, this chapter focuses largely on product design activities generally performed by the actuarial department. Companies may use staff actuaries or consulting actuaries to develop and design new products.

When possible, the company uses experience data from existing products during technical product design. By comparing experience data from similar products, insurers are more likely to understand customers’ purchase habits and expectations, which increases the odds of creating a financially successful new product. Product design poses a risk to a company because the profitability of a new product depends on using correct product design assumptions. For each new product, technical product design gives the company the opportunity to

  • Improve sales by adding a product that satisfies a customer need

  • Manage risk by appropriately balancing expenses and income and accurately categorizing and pricing risk

  • Increase earnings with a product that generates profit

  • Manage capital needs by creating a cost-efficient product that requires less financing

In the previous chapter, we discussed new product risk and the challenges posed by product complexity and innovation. During the technical product design phase, the company decides how to maximize benefits by fine-tuning specific product features or processes that pose these risks or challenges. For example, initiatives such as using a new distribution channel or significantly relaxing underwriting standards might improve operating efficiency, but they might also increase the level of risk. Effective product design is a matter of deciding what features or processes to include and how best to implement them.

 

Establishing a Financial Design

A product’s financial design begins by building on the product design objectives and other specifications created during the earlier stages of product development. With these specifications in mind, product development actuaries typically model new product designs for life insurance or annuities using product development software. This software uses mathematical modeling to simulate the financial processes and conditions that occur over the time that contracts are in force for a given product. Insurers use product development software to implement deterministic and stochastic approaches to product modeling.

  • Companies typically use deterministic modeling in product design for relatively simple products such as term life insurance, traditional whole life insurance, and fixed immediate annuities.

  • For more complex products such as variable products, companies use stochastic modeling in product design. Stochastic modeling provides detailed information about the volatile risks associated with secondary guarantees and other complicated risks. Many insurers are shifting to primarily stochastic modeling for all products, though regulatory standards may require insurers to apply deterministic modeling in some instances.

Companies can also conduct sensitivity analysis of extreme values to better understand how projected profits may be sensitive to variations in unknown future results. Sensitivity testing involves many model runs, ranging from fewer than a hundred runs for deterministic models to thousands of runs for stochastic models. Sensitivity analysis for a given product may involve both types of model.

Recall that each estimated value used in a life insurance or an annuity product design is known as an actuarial assumption. A typical product design includes actuarial assumptions for investment earnings, the costs of providing benefits, operating expenses, cash flow on existing and new business, expected loss scenarios, and unexpected financial results. Where applicable, insurers compare actuarial assumptions to relevant experience data from similar products.

 

Components of the Financial Design

In determining a product’s financial design, an insurer incorporates several product components, such as expected impacts on the company’s reserves and surplus created by the new product’s sales, as well as a charge for company operating expenses, a charge for the cost of benefits, a provision for investment results, premiums, and a contribution to company earnings. Each of these components must meet the valuation standards specific to the actuarial profession, such as the Actuarial Standards of Practice (ASOPs) set by the Actuarial Standards Board (ASB).

In addition to premiums, considerations from customers may include annuity considerations, premiums for riders, and separate expense charges. Companies generally model the cost of benefits for each rider similarly to how they would model the costs for any other benefit.

In undertaking product modeling, companies can use different approaches. 1 Figure 6.2 illustrates one such approach, an equation that calculates a premium amount.

After the product design team estimates a premium amount, the team can then evaluate this amount to see if it meets product design goals. For instance, if the insurer wishes to create a product with a lower price, the team can lower the required premium amount and then rerun the models to determine if the product is likely to achieve its goals at the new price. Once the team has determined a target amount, it decides how best to set the product elements to reach this goal.

 

Risk and Corporate Risk Statements

Every product design must meet the company’s formal risk guidelines. Risks that can produce negative financial outcomes for products include

  • Unfavorable swings or stagnation in market interest rates.

  • Unfavorable regulatory decisions, unfavorable court decisions, and unfavorable changes in the law, including tax law.

  • Unfavorable deviations from values assumed in a deterministically modeled product design.

  • Unfavorable accounting rules.

  • Negative tail scenarios, which are highly unfavorable scenarios identified in a stochastically modeled product design as potential outcomes. Recall from Chapter 4’s discussion on probability distributions that results more than three standard deviations away from the mean are called tails. A negative tail scenario represents a highly unlikely event on the unfavorable side.

To manage investment risk, actuaries and investment professionals set an investment policy for the assets that will back the new product. In the context of product development, an investment policy limits the asset types—by security, issuer, credit rating, admitted/non-admitted status, and many other features—and the proportions of the assets the company will use to support a product. An investment policy also specifies appropriate strategies for balancing a new product’s liability risks, including mortality risk. When setting investment policies, insurers must also be sure that the policies meet applicable insurance regulatory rules and statutes.

To manage mortality risk, companies develop underwriting guidelines and premium rate tables. Underwriting guidelines establish a structure for risk classes and premium rate structures to ensure that the company charges the correct premiums for the risks it accepts. For life insurance products, actuaries also must state a reinsurance strategy and a retention limit.

Companies use a variety of techniques for addressing risks in product outcomes. In general, approaches vary based on the form of modeling used during product design.

  • For a deterministically modeled product design, any allowances for negative outcomes can consist of a stated amount of money or a degree of conservatism in setting assumed values.

  • For a stochastically modeled product design, companies focus on the probability that the product will meet or exceed the company’s profitability target. The company’s risk processes generally determine, in advance, the minimum acceptable profitability for a product. The resulting product design is acceptable if the percentage of stochastic scenarios that meet or exceed the company’s profitability target is greater than or equal to the minimum percentage.

EXAMPLE

For new products to be approved, Marmalade Life Insurance applies a minimum probability of 80% of achieving the company’s profit target. Upon testing the initial product design for a new product, 77% of the stochastic scenarios met or exceeded the company’s profit target. The product design team then adjusted the product features and tested the design again. This time, 83% of the stochastic scenarios met or exceeded the profit target, and Marmalade approved the product.

 

Charges for Operating Expenses in Product Design

How do actuaries calculate the amount to charge for company expenses? The actuaries estimate the costs—other than the cost of benefits—the company will incur in supporting the product, with allowances for future inflation.

To develop cost estimates, actuaries incorporate information from records of the company’s past expenses—called expense experience—as well as expenses of similar companies offering similar products. The company’s accounting area often develops the expense assumptions used in product design. The accounting area also works with the administrative areas to determine costs for expected transactions such as applications or policy loans. Then actuaries use these expense assumptions as a basis for estimating actual expenses.

The credibility of experience data refers to the predictive value of the data set for a given purpose. For example, past trends have limited credibility because over a long future period, a trend is likely to cease or be supplanted by another trend. Therefore, in making long-term expense projections, actuaries make allowances for inflation and business cycles, or for the possibility that such cycles might be disrupted. For even greater accuracy in estimating future expenses, actuaries can analyze qualitative data such as customer feedback to help decision makers imagine how future events could diverge from established trends.

We discuss incorporating operating expenses into technical product design further in Chapter 9.

 

Cost of Benefits in Product Design

The cost of benefits, sometimes known as the cost of insurance, is the value of the contractually required benefits that a product promises to pay. While benefit expenses are included with other expenses in general accounting, the product design team and actuarial department often need increased granularity, or a finer, point-by-point level of detail. The projected cost of benefits generally equals the sum of each potential benefit payment to a customer multiplied by the expected probability that the benefit will be payable. We can express the projected cost of a single insurance benefit as follows:

EXAMPLE

Projected cost of a single benefit for a given policy year = Potential benefit amount payable × Probability that the benefit will be payable

The death benefit for a specific block of policies is $10,000,000, and the estimated mortality rate for the group of insureds is 5%. Therefore, the projected cost of the block’s death benefits for that year is $10,000,000 x .05 = $500,000.

A single product may offer numerous benefits, and the product’s design must anticipate the costs of each of these benefits throughout the life of the product. The projected cost of all benefits is the sum of the individual benefit costs.

The combinations of benefits differ for various product types, as follows:

  • For life insurance products, the insurer’s benefit obligations for a given policy year are equal to the death benefit amount multiplied by the mortality rate—the rate at which people in a group of insureds die—plus any nonforfeiture benefit and estimated costs associated with available riders.

  • For annuity contracts in the accumulation phase, the main benefit is a contract’s accumulation value. Annuity contracts in the accumulation phase may include surrender charges as well as the costs for any riders elected by the contract owner. For these products, the element of mortality applies to secondary death benefits, such as those provided under a guaranteed minimum death benefit (GMDB) provision.

  • For straight life annuity contracts in the distribution phase, in simple terms, annuity benefits for a given period are equal to the periodic annuity benefit payment multiplied by the annuitant’s probability of living for that period. Other payout options require different approaches for finding the cost of benefits.

Policyholder behavior is a key design consideration for life insurance and annuity products. Recall from Chapter 2 that policyholder behavior refers to the benefit utilization choices of customers who own life insurance or annuities, and policyholder behavior risk refers to the risk that policyholder actions or decisions will adversely affect product performance. For example, more policyholders may stop paying their premiums than the project design team had anticipated for the year. Because circumstances can vary widely from customer to customer and even from month to month, policyholder behavior risks can be difficult to incorporate into deterministic models. However, stochastic product modeling can allow for effective modeling of policyholder behavior when deterministic product modeling is not sufficient.

Some product design features create more policyholder behavior risk than others.

  • Flexible premiums, which allow customers to select the timing and amount of premium payments, can make it harder to predict the timing and amount of premium income.

  • Provisions for surrenders, withdrawals, and lapses may enable customers to terminate or reduce the value of contracts before the insurer has recovered its initial acquisition costs.

  • Options for reinstatement and conversion of life insurance may change the amount of risk the insurer has accepted, and may also change the product mix in force.

  • Secondary guarantees attached to variable annuities generally increase in value as the investments underlying the product decrease in value. We’ll discuss this particular product design risk further in Chapter 11, and general policyholder behavior risk further in Chapter 9.

 

Investment Returns and Interest in Product Design

For fixed and variable products, an insurance company invests premiums in many different assets, such as corporate bonds, government bonds, mortgages, and corporate stocks. The value of these investments can grow substantially over time. Insurers credit investment returns and interest to customer accounts differently, depending on the product, as shown below. During technical product design, companies must evaluate what the optimal investment policy for a new product is, and whether that policy is a good fit with the company’s existing investment patterns, currently offered products, and existing investment expertise. If the investment policy is not a good fit, and if the company still wishes to pursue the new product, the company must acquire new competencies, expertise, consulting services, or support software. An insurer must apply particular scrutiny to products that are supported by the general account, for which the insurer has assumed all risk.

For example, new complex design features may require an insurer to use support from hedging programs using investments in derivative securities. As you recall, hedging is an investment strategy that combines different offsetting types of securities in a given investment portfolio in order to reduce the overall riskiness, or volatility, of the portfolio’s asset mix. A derivative security, or derivative, is a financial security, such as a stock option, that derives its value from another security. A hedging program involves hedge costs, which are expenses for trading and holding derivative securities. Hedge costs can magnify the potential profits or losses from a product. For example, a hedge program might have a number of measures in place to control volatility, but in a sufficiently volatile market, the program may make adjustments so frequently that the hedge costs quickly increase. Effective hedging, however, reduces the risks of taking overall losses on products.

 

Premium Structure

Premium structure is a key component of life insurance products’ financial design. A premium structure defines the conditions under which the premium rates for a product may vary from customer to customer, and sets a range for those rates. For example, a premium structure typically requires higher premiums for life insurance policies that have extra benefits, or that are more likely to be paid out sooner. Many companies offer the following variations in premium structures for individual life insurance products:

  • Premium structures may apply different premium rates based on the insured’s risk characteristics. For example, premium rates may vary depending on an insured’s sex, age, and tobacco use behavior. Insurers usually divide these premium rate structures into standard, substandard, and preferred risk classes.

  • An insurer may grade premium rate structures by the policy face amount. In this case, a customer who purchases a high face amount in one policy may pay less in total premiums than would a customer who purchases the same total face amount in multiple policies. In effect, purchasing a high face amount in a single policy is a form of volume discount. This variation is most applicable for term life policies.

  • A contingency pricing structure gives the company mechanisms for adjusting the company’s charges for an in-force product to reflect the company’s financial results for the product. Some insurers use contingency pricing for interest-sensitive whole life products, participating products, and universal life products, as well as for the post-level-term phase in level-premium term life products. Not all insurers use contingency pricing structures.

  • An indeterminate premium structure allows the company to periodically reset premium rates on individual life insurance products while guaranteeing customers that premiums will not exceed a maximum specified level. For these products, the contract language includes a scale of guaranteed maximum premiums. This premium structure allows the company to adjust product charges to reflect the company’s anticipated future interest and mortality experience.

EXAMPLE

Collaboration Life Insurance designed a whole life insurance product with an indeterminate premium structure, and as such charges a “current” premium based on its current assumptions. When Collaboration discovers that the product is underperforming and at risk of not being able to pay out benefits, the company may raise this product’s premium rate. However, Collaboration must adhere to the scale of guaranteed maximum premiums included in the policy contracts.

 

Testing a Product Design

Once an insurer has created a product design, it must test and validate the design. From an actuarial perspective, a product’s design consists of modeling the financial results for one or multiple sets of estimated values and comparing the resulting values to established objectives for the product. The tests typically examine whether the product’s profitability, assets, reserves, and cash flows are adequate. Tests can also examine interest-rate sensitivity as well as other concerns such as surplus strain, which is the decrease in an insurer’s surplus caused by the first-year costs and reserve requirements associated with a new product.

Some product development software packages automatically adjust a product design to meet the stated product objectives. If initial testing of a product design produces unsatisfactory results, actuaries can change some of the assumed values entered into the model—provided the new values are realistic and actuarially sound.

Examples of changes that would ordinarily improve a product model’s profitability, if all other factors remain the same, would be to

  • Decrease benefit costs

  • Increase charges to customers

  • Decrease product expenses

  • Increase assumed investment earnings

The company’s real-world operations must support any design adjustment that an actuary makes to the product design.

EXAMPLE

Countermeasure Life Insurance has two new product designs undergoing adjustment. For the first product, actuaries propose lowering the expense assumptions in the model. Countermeasure’s real-world operations must support this change by finding new, more efficient ways to do business so that actual expenses are reduced.

For the second product, actuaries propose increasing an investment income assumption. Countermeasure must support this assumption by adopting new investment strategies that are likely to produce greater investment income.

As noted above, the adjustments must be realistic in scope. If, for example, a proposal requires a product to lower its expense assumptions beyond what the company’s real-world operations can achieve, the product does not have a realistic probability of earning a profit.

A company may also test a product design using non-actuarial methods, such as marketing the product to a sample group of people to evaluate the possible level of market demand.

 

Budgeting and Scheduling

The budgeting and scheduling process for new products typically consists of (1) preparing a budget for the human, financial, and computer resources needed to implement the product and (2) developing a project schedule.

  • A product development budget is a project planning document that shows the costs, such as staffing requirements, associated with each planned work activity and the anticipated revenue from the new product. The insurer also uses this budget later for monitoring and control.

  • A project schedule is a project planning timetable that shows the times required for the completion of planned tasks and the relationships among these tasks. Creating a project schedule includes identifying which tasks must be completed sequentially and which may be completed concurrently. A product development schedule typically includes very specific technical product design activities, such as drafting initial product specifications, policy forms, policy riders, and any financial exhibits required for initial filing. Certain forms of product management, such as agile product methodology, may not require a fixed project schedule with the same level of detail.

Once the budget and project schedule are complete, the product development team presents the completed product design to decision-makers in the appropriate line of business. This presentation to decision-makers focuses on demonstrating the product design’s capability to meet various company objectives. For example, the team must prove that the proposed pricing structure adequately supports the guarantees the product offers, or that the product will achieve a certain internal rate of return given its capital requirements. The team also must project the capital requirements for the new product. The decision-makers on the executive committee and the heads of the functional areas for the line of business must review and approve further development of the product. The team should demonstrate to the corporate risk committee that the proposed product design fits the company’s risk-return profile and employs appropriate risk management techniques.
