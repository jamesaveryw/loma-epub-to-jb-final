Product Development Activities

Development of individual life insurance and annuity products usually begins with product planning and comprehensive business analysis. These activities are designed to identify possible new products and evaluate their potential.

Figure 5.5 depicts the typical process of product development. Note that the actual process differs in details from one company to another, and companies may use their own terms for product development activities.

 

Product Planning

Product planning consists of two main activities: idea generation and idea screening, as seen in Figure 5.6.

 

Idea Generation

Idea generation involves searching for new product ideas that are consistent with the needs of the company’s target markets and the company’s overall product development strategy. The company searches continuously for new ideas by conducting market research, engaging in special projects, monitoring its own products, and monitoring competing products. The company considers totally new product ideas as well as product revisions or product rate changes. Most companies have a formalized process for identifying and considering new product ideas. Creativity is a paramount consideration during idea generation. Many company cultures encourage employees to suggest ideas without spending too much time evaluating them beforehand, as the team can evaluate a proposed product’s practical aspects in the next phase of product development.

Important sources of ideas for new products or enhancements to existing products are the product development team, the sales department, affiliated sales agents, and actuaries. New product ideas also come from research performed by the company’s competitive intelligence unit or units, which usually take the form of a subdivision of a company’s product development team. Competitive intelligence is the systematic collection and analysis of publicly available information about competitors and ongoing developments in the marketing environment. Some companies employ multiple competitive intelligence units to specialize in information about different markets or product types.

 

Idea Screening

During idea screening, a team quickly and inexpensively evaluates new product ideas to separate workable ideas from those that have little or no potential. Many companies apply a predetermined set of screening criteria. Insurance companies use the following criteria for screening new product concepts:

  • Ability to meet customer needs

  • Compatibility with the regulatory environment

  • Marketability to target markets

  • Alignment with the company’s corporate goals and strategies

  • Potential profitability

  • Compatibility with the company’s image

  • Alignment with the company’s current market position

  • Potential for generating additional new sales without displacing or replacing sales of existing products

  • Compatibility with the company’s existing or proposed distribution channels

  • Compatibility with the company’s current actuarial, financial/investment, administrative, accounting, and other systems

  • Understandability for target customers

 

Comprehensive Business Analysis

After quickly eliminating the least promising product ideas, a company subjects the remaining product ideas to a comprehensive business analysis, which is an evaluation of all the factors that are likely to affect the design, production, pricing, marketing, and sales potential of a new product. The comprehensive business analysis evaluates a product’s feasibility: Given the factors potentially affecting the product, is this product a viable option for the company?

In comprehensive business analysis, the product development team identifies the investment strategy that optimizes a proposed product’s performance—such as choosing investments by projected rate of return—and evaluates whether the proposed products can meet all legal and regulatory requirements. Comprehensive business analysis creates a foundation for the following product development activity of technical product design.

During this stage of product development, the product development team must also produce documentation to convince company decision makers that a product idea is worth developing. To support a commitment of company resources, decision makers like to see documentation demonstrating that the product

  • Will satisfy the company’s risk-return requirements and will pay back the company’s investment within a specified time

  • Will have the competitive features required to meet a specified level of sales within the targeted demographic

  • Is likely to be accepted by a sufficient number of captive and independent sales agents who will put their efforts into selling it

  • Is a good fit with the company’s existing expertise and administrative operations

  • Is a good fit with the company’s existing product mix and marketing strategies

  • Can be reliably supported by the company’s existing or planned technological system

Figure 5.7 identifies the elements that are included in a comprehensive business analysis, such as a market analysis, product design objectives, a feasibility study, a marketing plan, marketing projections, and a product proposal.

A market analysis documents an evaluation of all environmental factors that might affect sales of a proposed product. In essence, it represents the company’s effort to demonstrate that market demand exists to support a proposed product. In many cases, an insurer can derive a market analysis for a new product from information already gathered during ongoing market research activities for similar new product ideas. For a product type that is totally new to the company, however, staff must undertake a complete research process.

As a product idea progresses through the product development process, the product development team makes progressively more detailed decisions about the product design. Although the team conceptualizes many aspects of a new product before performing a market analysis, the market analysis provides a foundation that helps in setting product design objectives.

Product design objectives for a life insurance or an annuity product document the product’s basic characteristics, such as its benefits, any limits on the contract amount or the applicant’s age at issue, any special features, the commission range, and any settlement options. These specifications serve as guidelines during technical design of the product. The following insight shows examples of product design objectives.

Product Design Objectives—Life Insurance and Annuities

Underwriting Specifications

  • Distribution system, marketing channels
  • Commission structures and bonus rates
  • Marketing overhead expense rate
  • Promotion and advertising expense allocation
  • Competitive positioning of product
  • Chargeback schedules
  • Field expenses, if any

  • Death benefits, settlement options
  • Renewability
  • Convertibility
  • Minimum and maximum face amounts
  • Cash values, surrender benefits, dividend formulas, settlement options
  • Transaction charges, surrender charge schedule
  • Subaccounts for variable products
  • Assumptions as to investment earnings, persistency, average contract size, and expense charges
  • Range of premiums, minimum/maximum
  • Levels of graded premiums
  • Guaranteed or nonguaranteed rates
  • Modes and methods of payment
  • Age bands
  • Policy fees

  • Asset-based fees

  • Interest guarantees
  • Withdrawal and surrender benefits

  • Omnichannel compatibility
  • Expected system requirements

A feasibility study evaluates whether the company has the capability to take a new product to market. To evaluate a new product’s operational and technical feasibility, the company studies whether the product

  • Can be structured to be competitive and profitable

  • Can be accommodated by the insurer’s operating and distribution systems

  • Can be supported by the insurer’s current resources, employees, and technological capacity

  • Complies with the company’s risk tolerance, underwriting policies, and reinsurance retention strategy

A feasibility study can include a review of the results of another company’s introduction of a similar product. A market analysis can also inform a feasibility study, as the level of potential market demand influences how much resources a company must likely commit to promoting the product. A feasibility study should evaluate and demonstrate how product implementation will compete for resources with other company initiatives and how this interaction might affect the product’s speed to market.

Similar to a feasibility study, a legal and tax review evaluates any legal or tax concerns associated with a new product. The company studies whether a product would comply with all relevant regulations and statutes, and whether the product is likely to cause tax issues for customers.

A marketing plan defines a company’s product, price, distribution, and promotion strategies for reaching potential customers and meeting their needs. The marketing plan typically

  • Outlines the specific, action-oriented tactics the company has developed to support its marketing goals and strategies for the proposed new product

  • Specifies the steps required for accomplishing day-to-day marketing activities, as well as marketing objectives, budgets, and a plan for evaluating progress toward objectives

  • Relies greatly on the information collected during market analysis

A plan developed for a new product is similar to the marketing plans developed for existing products and covers the financial design, promotion, and distribution for the proposed product.

Marketing projections are preliminary sales and financial forecasts that include estimates of potential unit sales, revenues, costs, and profits for a proposed product. A projection specifies an expected or a most likely value—rather than a best-case value or a worst-case value—for an unknown future value, to provide a realistic expectation for a product’s performance. At a preliminary stage of product development, marketers create rough projections of future sales volume, sales revenue, expenses, and profit, which marketers define as follows:

  • Sales volume is the number of units of product sold.

  • Sales revenue is the total dollar volume of sales. Sales revenue is calculated by multiplying the sales volume by the average price per unit.

To project important sales figures, marketers define expenses as the administrative costs the company incurs to support a product, plus the periodic income or benefit payments, cash surrenders, and other benefits the company will pay out.

Projections of these values at an early stage of product development may provide useful data, but data from early projections contain a great deal of uncertainty. As product development progresses, some of this uncertainty disappears, and actuaries are able to revise the projections as they obtain clearer information.

If the comprehensive business analysis indicates strong potential for the proposed product, the product development team prepares a product proposal and presents it to top management for a go/no-go decision on the idea. A product proposal, or business plan, is a document used to present a product idea to an internal team that decides whether to allocate company resources toward developing the product idea. A product proposal describes the following aspects of a product:

  • The product’s purpose

  • Key product features

  • The target market for the product and projected sales volume

  • Differentiation from other products

  • Plans for promotion, advertising, distribution, and sales

  • Requirements for administrative resources, including technology

  • Probability projections and capital needs such as investment changes

  • Regulatory considerations

A product proposal that receives full management approval serves as an overall guide for technical product design and product introduction. We discuss these remaining stages of the product development process, along with product monitoring, in the next chapter.
