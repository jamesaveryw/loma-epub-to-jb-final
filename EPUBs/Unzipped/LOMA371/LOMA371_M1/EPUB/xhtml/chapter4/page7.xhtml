Model Analysis

When insurers run multiple iterations of a financial model, they are testing multiple scenarios that consist of projections of actuarial and economic values. To understand the implications of a model’s results, insurers must be able to determine the amount and timing of changes in output variables created by changes in input variables. For example, model analysis of a proposed life insurance product can measure changes in the output of projected profits based on changes in input variables such as market interest rates, lapse rates, mortality, and so on. Insurers generally obtain this information through scenario testing and sensitivity analysis.

 

Scenario Testing

Scenario testing for financial modeling is a method of evaluating modeling results that involves entering different sets of data into a model and then determining how changes in the input data affect the model’s output data. Generally, the scenarios being tested include a mix of optimistic, moderate, and pessimistic scenarios with at least one worst-case scenario. For example, the annual OASDI Trustees Report on Social Security uses scenario testing to project potential shortfalls in the U.S. Social Security program as various scenarios take place.

One common practice among insurers using deterministic models is to create optimistic, pessimistic, and most likely scenarios using input data derived from historical experience. By making changes to input variables and then running separate iterations of the model for each new value, insurers can capture varying degrees of optimism or pessimism and better determine the likelihood of each of these outcomes.

One important application of scenario testing is dynamic financial analysis. Dynamic financial analysis (DFA) is the use of simulation modeling and multiple scenario testing to project an insurer’s assets, liabilities, and owners’ equity as of a given valuation date and to compare the values of those variables at various times after the valuation date. In general, DFA holistically analyzes risks and challenges for the entire organization’s operations. DFA produces decision-support information that an insurer can use in narrowly focused or broad-ranging risk analysis and risk management.

At a strategic level, DFA often takes the form of dynamic solvency testing. Dynamic solvency testing (DST) is an application of DFA that involves projecting an insurer’s capital as of a given valuation date and comparing the projected amounts of capital at various times after the valuation date. Because DST illustrates the effects of various plausible scenarios on the adequacy of an insurer’s forecasted capital, it is often called capital adequacy testing. Capital adequacy refers to the minimum amount of capital that an insurer must hold to meet a specified standard for capital.

Another specific application of scenario testing is to determine the adequacy of the assets backing an insurance company’s reserves. Asset adequacy analysis is a broad actuarial practice undertaken to ensure that the assets backing reserves meet established standards. Actuaries can use deterministic or stochastic scenarios to test asset adequacy; in recent years, stochastic scenarios have become increasingly popular with insurers. On a more specific level, insurers typically measure the adequacy of assets and liabilities for particular products through cash-flow testing. Cash flow testing (CFT) is the use of simulation modeling to project the cash flows associated with an insurance company’s assets and liabilities as of a given valuation date and to compare the timing and amounts of asset and liability cash flows at various times after the valuation date.

Cash-flow testing requires that an insurer model assets and liabilities under a variety of economic scenarios.

  • Modeling assets. Asset cash-flow models use a variation of the company’s asset valuation and reporting system to estimate future asset values. The values produced by the models for asset prepayment rates, asset default rates, and reinvestment rates vary by scenario.

  • Modeling liabilities. Liability cash-flow models use a variation of the company’s liability reporting system to estimate future liability cash flows. The values produced by the models for market interest rates and interest-crediting rates vary by scenario. Insurers commonly use liability cash-flow modeling to evaluate liabilities associated with deferred annuities.

Most insurers use modeling software that projects asset and liability cash flows simultaneously.

 

Sensitivity Analysis

Insurers use sensitivity analysis to assess the level of the change in input needed to create a change in the outputs. Sensitivity analysis is a method of measuring the responsiveness of the outputs produced by a financial model to changes in the values of the model’s input variables.

Like scenario testing, sensitivity analysis requires multiple scenarios and multiple processing runs. However, whereas scenario testing measures the amount of the change in a dependent variable created by a change in an independent variable, sensitivity analysis measures how much change in the independent variable is necessary to create a change in the dependent variable. In essence, scenario testing asks “What will the outcome of these scenarios be?”, while sensitivity testing asks “What effect does this variable have on another variable?” Sensitivity analysis can also be useful in identifying a range of values for independent variables over which the changes in dependent variables are acceptable.

EXAMPLE

Example. Chevalier Life Insurance is considering both scenario testing and sensitivity analysis to analyze potential interest rate risk to the company. When using scenario testing, Chevalier would define and test scenarios to answer questions such as, “How are our assets and liabilities likely to change if the market interest rate remains steady at 2%, falls to 1.5%, or rises to 2.5%?”

When using sensitivity analysis, Chevalier would define and test scenarios to answer a question such as, “How much can the market interest rate change before it has a significant effect on company cash flows?”

Insurance companies often use sensitivity analysis to identify the sensitivity of the company’s assets and liabilities to changes in market interest rates. Insurers can then use the results of this analysis to construct investment portfolios and to provide input into technical product design. Sensitivity analysis can also be used to measure the interest sensitivity of product cash flows. Analysis of the effects of various interest-rate scenarios on cash flows is known as interest-sensitive cash-flow testing. Insurers can perform interest-sensitive cash-flow testing on a single product, a block of products, or an entire product line. Insurers often use interest-sensitive cash-flow testing to support various actuarial opinions and memoranda that regulators require.

In addition to scenario testing and sensitivity analysis, actuaries must further analyze and validate the results of the model to ensure the results are actuarially sound and reasonable. The following insight lists some questions actuaries may ask when validating the results of a financial model.

INSIGHT

Validation of Model Results

After running a financial model, actuaries are responsible for validating the results to ensure the outcomes are reasonable in light of the inputs. Actuaries may ask the following questions:

  • Are outputs consistent with inputs?
  • How many errors were generated and what amount was involved?
  • Are the intended assumptions the ones used in the model?
  • Are model assumptions unchanged unless they were meant to be changed?
  • Are results as expected, both in direction and magnitude?
  • If there are several model runs at different dates, are the latest results consistent with the trend?
  • Are the results consistent with the impacts obtained from any sensitivity analysis that was conducted?
  • How do the results for the first projection period compare to the most recent period of actual results for a comparable product?
