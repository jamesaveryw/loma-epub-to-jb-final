Model Outputs

Outputs represent the results of a financial process. A model’s outputs can be single values or a listing of all the possible values in a data set, depending on the particular modeling approach used. The estimates of future financial values generated by models usually take one of three forms: point estimates, range estimates, and probability distributions.

 

Point Estimates and Range Estimates

Deterministic models, which assign single values to input variables, produce results in the form of single-value estimates. Optimization models, which generate optimum results, also produce single-value estimates. An estimate that is assigned a single value is referred to as a point estimate.

Insurers sometimes use point estimates to calculate product profitability based on interest earnings. Insurers also use point estimates to identify projected lapse rates.

The Fairview Insurance Company used a deterministic model to project the first-year lapse rate of a new life insurance product. Using single-value input variables derived from Fairview’s historical lapse experience data, the model produced an estimated first-year lapse rate of 10% for the product.

As an alternative to assigning single values to estimates, decision makers can acknowledge the uncertainty of forecasted values by specifying a range of values for a model’s outcomes. An estimate that provides a range of possible outcome values is known as a range estimate. An estimated first-year lapse rate of 5 to 15 percent is a range estimate. By analyzing a range of values, decision makers can take steps to protect against potential unfavorable situations and take advantage of potential favorable situations.

With range estimates, financial models sometimes also produce a confidence limit. A confidence limit expresses the probability that a specific outcome will fall within a stated range. The more confident the forecaster is that a projected value will be correct, the higher the value of the confidence limit assigned to that projected value. Only stochastic models generate outputs with probabilities attached.

Fairview then used a stochastic model to project the product’s first-year lapse rate, with the outputs expressed as range estimates. The model also produced confidence limits to project the probability of the first-year lapse rate falling in each range estimate.

Lapse Rate Confidence Limit
Between 4% and 6% 68.0%
Between 3% and 8% 95.0%
Between 2% and 10% 99.0%

Based on these results, Fairview can see that the first-year lapse rate is highly likely to fall between 2% and 10%.

 

Probability Distributions

Stochastic modeling assigns multiple values to input variables and produces results in the form of probability distributions. A probability distribution is a data distribution that includes both data values and the probability of observing each value. So, the output of a stochastic model indicates how likely certain results are to occur, which allows an insurer to better analyze risk. Because the output variables produced by deterministic modeling do not involve randomness—output variables can be predicted with 100 percent certainty based only on the input variables—they are not associated with a probability distribution.

Sometimes the results of stochastic modeling follow a normal distribution, which is the most common form of probability distribution. A normal distribution, also known as a normal probability distribution, is one in which the number of values that are less than the mean is the same as the number of values greater than the mean, and in which the values are dispersed in a specific proportion. In other words, there are as many values greater than the average as there are values less than the average, which is what we normally expect when we find the average value on a set. When plotted on a graph, the data values in a normal distribution form a bell-shaped curve called a normal curve, also known as a normal distribution curve. A normal distribution typically results when the modeled variable is the sum of a large number of independent, identically distributed variables.

Normal distributions are symmetrical. Thus, if you were to divide a normal curve down the middle, using a vertical line, the left and right sides of the curve would match exactly. The midpoint of the curve is the mean, or arithmetic average of the values in the data set, calculated by summing all of the values in a specified group and dividing by the number of values in that group. In a normal distribution, the mean is also equal to the median—the middle value in the data set—and the mode—the most common value in the data set. The standard deviation is a measure of the dispersion of values in a data set around the mean of the data set.

Although all normal distributions are symmetrical, not all symmetrical distributions are normal distributions. The exact differences between non-normal symmetrical distributions and normal distributions are beyond the scope of this course. In general, these differences stem from the relationship between data values and the distribution’s mean. Individual data values in a normal distribution are dispersed around the mean so that approximately

  • 68.27 percent of the data fall within one standard deviation on either side of the mean

  • 95.45 percent of the data fall within two standard deviations on either side of the mean

  • 99.73 percent of the data fall within three standard deviations on either side of the mean

The two portions of the normal curve that extend beyond three standard deviations on either side are called tails. If the data being analyzed is normally distributed, the probability of a value being in one of the tails is extremely small—approximately 0.003, or 0.3%. Figure 4.2 provides an illustration of a normal distribution. In this normal curve, with a standard deviation of 4, the mean is located at “0,” and the standard deviations are located at +/- 4, +/- 8, +/- 12.

Because real-world observations are rarely distributed normally, the probability distributions produced by stochastic modeling are often non-normal distributions. A non-normal distribution is generally an asymmetric distribution in which the number of values on one side of the mean is greater than the number of values on the other side of the mean. In a non-normal distribution, the mean is not generally equal to the median and the mode. An example of a non-normal curve is shown in Figure 4.3.

Financial values, such as stock values, are often distributed non-normally because they are affected by a variety of market factors. Many variables used in the financial design of insurance products are also distributed non-normally, including

  • Investment earnings, which depend on market conditions

  • Mortality costs and other costs of benefits

  • Product shelf life

  • Lapse rates, withdrawal rates, changes in premium allocations, and benefit utilization choices, all of which depend on policyholder behavior

  • Product expenses

Analysts often use skewness and kurtosis measures in addition to the mean, median, and mode to describe probability distributions.

Skewness is a statistical parameter that describes the lack of symmetry in a probability distribution. A normal distribution has a skewness of 0. A distribution is positively skewed if the peak of the distribution is shifted to the left of the mean value. A distribution is negatively skewed if the peak of the distribution is shifted to the right of the mean value. The non-normal curve shown in Figure 4.3 is positively skewed.

Kurtosis is a statistical parameter that describes the shape of a probability distribution. Distributions with high kurtosis tend to have “fat” or heavy tails. A tail is considered “fat” if the probability of values occurring in the tail and the risk associated with tail values—known as tail risk—are higher than predicted by the normal distribution. Some distributions, like the one in Figure 4.3, may have a “fat” tail at only one end. Note also that a distribution might have low kurtosis, meaning that relatively fewer values appear in the tails. Kurtosis may appear in both symmetrical and asymmetrical probability distributions.

The low probability/high severity risks in the tail are important for insurers because they indicate the potential for catastrophic losses or windfall gains. Insurers typically measure the severity of tail risk by calculating a statistic known as the conditional tail expectation. The conditional tail expectation (CTE) is the average of all the values within a specified range of the probability distribution.

To properly focus on values within a distribution’s tails, an actuary measures CTE in terms of the scenarios it excludes, rather than the scenarios it includes. The average for all the scenarios in a distribution is referred to as CTE(0) because it excludes none of the scenarios in the distribution. The average of the worst 10 percent of scenarios, which excludes 90 percent of the scenarios in the distribution, is referred to as CTE(90).

To assess the severity of the tail risk, insurers often compare the CTE for a specified range with some measure of profitability, such as internal rate of return (IRR) or return on assets (ROA). Insurers can adjust for risks above the acceptable level by establishing a higher reserve amount for the product or by modifying the product’s design.
