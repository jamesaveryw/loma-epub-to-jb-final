Video 1.1

Risk Appetite and Risk Tolerance

Risk appetite and risk tolerance both measure the amount of risk that a company will accept. But there are a couple of differences between the two.

[A cafeteria counter; there’s a cook behind the counter. A sign on the counter says “TODAY’S SPECIAL: RISK”. A man walks up to the counter, and looks thoughtful.]

Risk appetite is a qualitative measure for the amount and type of risk that a company is willing to seek out. It describes the company’s attitude toward risk in general terms.

Man: I’ll have the special, please.

[The cook nods and lifts up, from behind the counter, a huge platter with a roast turkey and trimmings. The man starts back, a little surprised.]

Man: I can’t eat all that!

For example, a typical insurer has a conservative attitude toward risk, focusing on solvency over profitability. In other words, it has a low appetite for most risks.

[The cook puts away the big platter of turkey, and hands the man a plate with a more modest lunch serving of turkey and fixings. The man smiles and walks away happy.]

[The same man in a grocery store, by a meat counter with a butcher behind the counter. (A sign, sticker, or label on the meat counter says “RISK”.) He’s looking thoughtful. A thought balloon appears over his head, with an image of that plate of turkey he had earlier in it.]

Risk tolerance is different in two ways. For one, it defines how much risk a company will accept as opposed to how much it will seek out.

[The man raises a hand to get the grocer’s attention.]

Man: One kilogram, please.

Risk tolerance is also quantitative. It describes risk in more specific, quantifiable terms.

[Close-up on a digital grocery scale, with slices of “turkey” (representing the risk) being piled on. As slices are added, the digital number on the scale goes up until it hits 1.0.]

Man: That’s enough, thank you!

[The man accepts the wrapped package from the grocer. He looks at the package. In a popup a nutrition information label is displayed. Instead of a “calories” at the top of the label, instead we see Risk: 1.0. The rest of the label can be blurred and illegible. ]

For instance, an insurer might issue specific risk tolerance statements, such as:

  • “Respite Life Insurance will not risk more than 5 percent of its economic capital in any given year”
  • “Respite’s earnings will not vary by more than 10 percent from one year to the next”
  • “Respite seeks a 95 percent probability of maintaining its current AAA rating”

So while risk appetite generally describes how much risk that an insurer will seek out, risk tolerance quantifies how much risk the insurer will take on.

Contingency Risks

Asset risk (C-1) includes:

Losses in market values of assets (for reasons other than interest-rate movements)

A borrower defaults by failing to make timely payments of interest or principal

Pricing risk (C-2) includes:

Adverse mortality experiences relative to actuarial assumptions: for life insurance products, higher mortality rates than expected; for annuity products, higher life expectancy rates than expected

Increases in administrative costs

Interest-rate risk (C-3) includes:

Losses in market values of assets, as caused by interest-rate movements

A loss on a forced sale of assets, as caused by rising market interest rates

An increase in customer withdrawals, as caused by rising market interest rates

Losses on bond prepayments and mortgage prepayments, as caused by falling market interest rates

Losses due to asset-liability mismatches

General management risk (C-4) includes:

Losses due to an expansion into new lines of business or geographic expansion

Losses due to changes in laws that adversely affected the business

Adverse judgments from class-action lawsuits

Reputational loss

Ratings downgrades

Losses caused by mismanagement of any sort

Figure 2.2

Remedial Actions Available to Regulators in Cases of Financial Distress

The Model Hazardous Condition Regulation authorizes regulators to require the financially impaired insurer to take corrective actions, such as any of the following:

  • Use reinsurance to reduce its liabilities and obtain surplus relief
  • Limit its new or renewal business
  • Reduce its general and commission expenses by specified means
  • Increase its capital and surplus
  • Suspend or limit dividend payments
  • Limit or divest investments in specified assets
  • File reports stating the value of invested assets
  • Document the adequacy of its premium rates

The Model Supervision Act authorizes regulators to require the financially impaired insurer to obtain permission from regulators before it can take any of the following actions:

  • Sell or transfer assets or in-force business or use assets as collateral for loans
  • Withdraw funds, lend assets, or invest
  • Incur debt
  • Accept new initial premiums
  • Renew policies that are not guaranteed renewable
  • Merge with another company
  • Enter into a new reinsurance treaty
  • Pay specified surrender values to customers
  • Increase the compensation to its officers or directors

The Three Lines of Defense

[A castle on one side of the screen. From the opposite side of the screen, an army approaches. The castle doors open and a line of soldiers with shields walk out toward the army. The soldiers stop with their backs to the castle, holding the front line.]

An insurance company’s first line of defense against risks is its operational management. As the front line, they own and manage risks.

[The castle doors open again, this time with soldiers on horses coming out. The soldiers on horses stop behind the first line.]

The second line of defense consists of oversight functions, such as the risk committee and the compliance function. This line ensures that the first line is properly managing risks.

[Zoom in on archers from the castle window.]

The third line of defense, which consists of internal auditors, monitors the effectiveness of the second line. To ensure its objectivity, the third line acts independently of the other lines.

Together, the three lines of defense form a comprehensive risk management approach.

Video 3.2

Responding to KRI Values

[A large traffic light, with all of the lights off.]

Key risk indicators (KRIs) quantify the level of exposure to different risks. Depending on the level of risk that a KRI illustrates, management can respond in different ways.

One KRI for mortality risk could be the mortality rate among insureds for a given product.

If the value of this KRI is within acceptable limits and at a normal level, the company is likely not exposed to a significant level of risk.

[The bottom light of the traffic light flashes.]

No managerial action is required because the indicator and its corresponding risks are under adequate control.

[Bottom light stops flashing green.]

One KRI for interest-rate risk could be the duration gap between the asset and liability portfolios.

If the value of this KRI is within acceptable limits but at an elevated level, the company may be exposed to an increased and potentially significant level of risk.

[The middle light of the traffic light flashes amber.]

Management attention is required to determine if action needs to be taken in the near future.

[Bottom light stops flashing amber.]

One KRI for fraud risk could be the amount of financial losses due to fraudulent claims.

If the value of this KRI is past acceptable limits, the company may be exposed to a significantly high level of risk.

[The top light of the traffic light flashes red.]

Immediate action is required by senior management to manage the risk exposure.

Sample Challenges of Acquiring Quality Data
