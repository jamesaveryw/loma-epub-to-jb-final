Asset-Liability Management

In addition to the basic strategies for managing risk, life insurers practice asset-liability management (ALM) to address specific financial risks. Recall that ALM consists of a portfolio approach to managing the risks associated with a company’s assets and liabilities. A portfolio is a collection of assets with differing degrees and kinds of risk, usually assembled for meeting a defined set of goals. The process in which portfolios are evaluated for their risk-return potential is known as portfolio management.

Insurers group their risk exposures into two types of portfolios:

  • An asset portfolio, which holds the insurer’s securities and other invested assets.

  • A liability portfolio, or, within the context of ALM, product portfolio, which represents the insurer’s obligations to customers.

ALM addresses financial risks including, but not limited to, interest-rate risk, currency risk, equity risk, and policyholder behavior risk. The ALM process is designed to help insurers (1) manage risks at an acceptable level and (2) take advantage of opportunities to earn a return.

 

Risks and Returns

Recall that insurers need to balance risks incurred with potential returns. For some purposes, risks can be divided into two categories:

  • Diversifiable risk—also known as nonsystematic risk or specific risk—is risk that is specific to an individual asset or issuer. Credit risk is an example of diversifiable risk. Most financial risks are diversifiable risks.

  • Nondiversifiable risk—also known as systematic risk—is risk that affects all assets in an economic system and is therefore not specific to an individual asset or issuer. The risk of an economic recession is an example of nondiversifiable risk.

An insurance company’s asset and liability portfolios generate cash flows. In general, over the long run, asset portfolios generate net cash inflows, whereas liability portfolios generate net cash outflows.

When assets are assembled in a portfolio, their combined risks can have desirable or undesirable interactions:

  • A risk augmentation effect is present if the combination of specific risks is greater than the sum of those risks.

  • A risk offset effect is present if the combination of specific risks is less than the sum of those risks.

Portfolio risk management approaches support the safety in a portfolio of risks by avoiding a condition of high positive correlation among the portfolio’s risks. Correlation among the returns of portfolio assets means that the returns tend to move together. When returns move in the same direction, correlation is positive; when returns move in opposite directions, correlation is negative. High positive correlation occurs when the amount and direction of the movement in returns are nearly the same for all assets in the portfolio.

ALM involves four primary portfolio risk management approaches:

  • Diversification is a risk management strategy for spreading risk by investing in different assets having different risk profiles. To diversify their investments, insurers carefully spread their investment selections among the securities of different issuers in different business categories. Diversification is effective in controlling diversifiable risk, especially asset concentration risk. Asset concentration risk is the risk of an excessive concentration of assets in any single category, such as a portfolio that consists only of corporate bonds.

  • Hedging is a risk management strategy that involves holding an asset with characteristics that counterbalance one or more of the risks in the investor’s risk array. Insurers often hedge, or counterbalance, risks in some assets by holding other assets with offsetting risk characteristics.

  • Cash-flow matching is a risk management strategy that involves identifying the patterns of cash outflows for insurance products and matching those cash outflows with a selection of assets that will produce a similar pattern of cash inflows. In other words, portfolio assets are selected for their ability to generate cash inflows that offset the estimated cash outflows related to an insurer’s liabilities. In general, cash-flow matching works well for life insurance companies because their liabilities are fairly predictable and occur over relatively long time periods. Life insurers typically buy fixed-income investments to ensure predictable cash inflows.

  • Duration matching is a risk management strategy that involves matching the duration statistics for fixed-income assets such as bonds with the duration statistics for the insurance company liabilities that the assets support. In an actuarial context, we define duration as a statistic that measures the price sensitivity of an asset or liability to changes in interest rates. Duration is the time-weighted value of expected cash inflows, expressed in years. However, the actual duration statistics used by insurers vary widely from one company to another.

EXAMPLE

Example 1: Leed Insurance’s portfolio of bonds has duration of 15, which means that for a 0.1 percent increase in interest rates, the value of the bond portfolio declines by 1.5 percent. If there is a 0.1 percent decrease in interest rates, the value of the bond portfolio increases by 1.5 percent.

Example 2. Leed Insurance uses duration matching for liabilities that have highly predictable cash flows and whose value is very sensitive to interest rates. Leed structures its fixed-income asset portfolio so that the values of the asset and liability portfolios move in sync in response to interest-rate changes. In other words, the duration-matched portfolios have the same sensitivity to interest-rate changes.

These portfolio risk management strategies are not exclusive to one another; for instance, an insurer uses duration matching in addition to other strategies to achieve proper ALM. The success of these strategies depends on the insurer’s ability to balance assets and liabilities so that

  • Total assets match or exceed total liabilities

  • Total cash inflows over any time period exceed, match, or are reasonably close to total cash outflows over the same period

  • The interactions between the asset and liability portfolios are likely to reduce or eliminate some risks through risk offsets or hedges

  • The interactions between the asset and liability portfolios produce residual risks—the lesser risks that remain after balancing—that insurers can actively manage

To properly execute these strategies, insurers use specialized modeling techniques such as cash-flow testing and asset adequacy analysis. We discuss these and other modeling techniques in the next chapter. Figure 3.1 illustrates the four portfolio risk management approaches described above.

 

ALM Reporting Systems

ALM processes establish lines of communication that cross the formal boundaries of the traditional organizational hierarchy. In day-to-day operations, investment professionals typically handle the risks inherent in the assets portfolio, and product professionals typically handle the risks inherent in the liabilities portfolio.

However, for effective ALM, a company must coordinate its financial expertise in the areas of

  • Investment policy and operations

  • Product design and pricing

  • Insurance risk management, including underwriting, diversification, claims management, fraud, and reinsurance programs

  • Operating expenses, including information systems costs, legal costs, and employee compensation costs

Because ALM processes require that technical information be communicated to a variety of financial professionals and top-level decision makers, most financial institutions maintain a formal reporting system for ALM information. This system communicates the results of technical ALM analysis to an internal ALM steering committee and to regulators. The system also provides documentation and communication support to help technical experts and nonexperts work together to implement ALM strategies. ALM reporting systems produce internal ALM reports and external ALM reports.

 

Internal ALM Reports

Insurance companies use a variety of internal reports for ALM. Some reports provide details concerning the company’s liabilities, while other reports provide details about investments in the asset portfolio. Internal ALM reports support sound ALM within a company. Because of the company-specific nature of these reports, they vary from one company to another. Some of the most common internal ALM reports are the investment activity report, investment portfolio performance review, and duration gap report.

An investment activity report specifies the details of all investment portfolio transactions, including all asset acquisitions and all dispositions of assets from the investment portfolio through sales, prepayments (redemptions), or repayment at maturity. Although narrow in scope, this report is typically the most detailed and the most frequently produced of any report in a formal ALM reporting system.

EXAMPLE

For each newly acquired debt security, the investment activity report typically gives the asset’s face amount, coupon rate, yield, term to maturity, duration, and quality rating.

An investment portfolio performance review is a quarterly ALM report that summarizes the company’s investment performance for the board of directors and the ALM committee. This report is usually the broadest in scope of any ALM report, but is not the most detailed, because it covers much of the information in summary form.

The investment portfolio performance review presents quarterly and year-to-date information about the following aspects of investment performance:

  • Overall growth in the portfolio’s monetary value, specified in terms of price appreciation and income growth; an analysis of capital gains and losses; and an analysis of changes in market values

  • Acquisitions and dispositions by asset type, and an analysis of the effect on yields of all purchases and sales

  • A narrative discussion of the portfolio composition, known as the asset mix, and the suitability of the asset mix

  • Narrative explanations of key events in the external environment and the company’s strategic responses to those events

  • Summary reports on special topics such as problem assets, real estate investments, and international investments

A duration gap report provides a snapshot of the insurer’s asset-liability match at the time of the report. This report describes the results of a duration analysis of the company’s asset and liability portfolios and discusses the insurer’s current exposure to portfolio cash flow mismatches caused by changes in market interest rates. Information in a duration gap report is time-sensitive because even small changes in market interest rates and other economic factors can produce dramatic changes in the asset-liability gap.

 

External ALM Reports

Insurers commonly use two formal ALM reports for external reporting: (1) an Actuarial Opinion and Memorandum (AOM), with supporting documentation, and (2) a crediting-rate resolution. Recall that an AOM consists of two separate documents—the actuarial opinion and the actuarial memorandum—that insurers are required to submit annually to regulators.

A crediting-rate resolution is a formal declaration by the board of directors of the rate of interest the insurer will credit on customers’ money held in interest-bearing products.

Crediting rates vary widely by product and by company. An annual crediting-rate resolution typically presents details about the company’s interest-crediting strategies. Interest-crediting strategies are the formulas and criteria a company uses to set the interest rates it will credit for interest-sensitive products.

EXAMPLE

An interest-crediting strategy for a given fixed deferred annuity product would be a rule to generally credit that product with interest at a rate equal to the asset portfolio earnings rate minus 1.5 percent.
