Measuring and Managing Operational Risk

Many companies consider operational risk to be the most dangerous type of risk that they face. Recall that operational risk is the risk of financial losses resulting from inadequate or failed internal processes, controls, systems, and people. The challenge of measuring and managing operational risk can be overwhelming. Ideally, companies take a comprehensive approach to operational risk measurement and management that includes a variety of tools. These tools range from key risk indicators, risk maps, loss-incident databases, and control self-assessments to the methods of scenario testing and simulation modeling, which we discuss in the next chapter.

 

Key Risk Indicators

A key risk indicator (KRI) is a quantitative metric that indicates the level of operational risk exposures at a given point of time in order to help companies forecast future occurrences. KRIs can play an important role in managing risk by identifying potential high risk exposures and enabling the company to take timely action.

KRIs can be considered leading—identifying risk trends and issues that may need to be addressed in the future—or lagging—identifying events that have occurred in the past and that may occur again. Effective KRIs should be relevant, measurable, predictive, auditable, easy to monitor, comparable, and timely.

KRIs help to support effective governance, risk taxonomies, and internal controls by providing a consistent and repeatable way for tracking both risk exposures and management activity. By monitoring KRIs and checking them against agreed limits and thresholds, a company is able to see if its operational risk exposures are within its stated risk tolerance. Limits and thresholds may be set to help show whether an indicator is red, amber, or green. The amber zone is given more priority than green, and red is given even greater priority. Risk Maps

A risk map, also called a risk heat map, categorizes each risk identified for a particular operational or business process along two dimensions—(1) impact or significance and (2) likelihood. Risk maps typically classify individual risks as minor (green), moderate (yellow), major (orange), or severe (red), based on the impact or significance of the risk and the likelihood that the risk will occur. Severe or high risks usually require immediate attention to address the risk at its source. Major and moderate risks usually require regular monitoring, but may not demand immediate attention. Minor or low risks may require monitoring, but usually don’t require significant control. Figure 3.2 shows an example of a risk map presented in table form.

 

Loss-Incident Databases

As its name indicates, a loss-incident database is simply a database that records every loss that an insurer has experienced. It allows insurers to review operational activities from a single system and then identify incidents or activities that create or have the potential to create losses, analyze the company’s performance during those incidents, create summaries, and make recommendations for improvements.

 

Control Self-Assessments

A control self-assessment is a detailed report, typically compiled by each line manager, capturing each operational area’s key risks, controls, and management implications. The assessment can help identify potential areas of vulnerability based on the ratings received. For example, an insurance company might use a control self-assessment to (1) collect data—from underwriting, claims, and sales divisions—related to risks, inefficient or failed control processes, and performance gaps, and (2) propose actions to improve performance.
