Risk Management Culture and Product Development

Like any business, a life insurance company must create products that appeal to its customers. Life insurers rely on the revenues generated by new products to satisfy their needs for solvency and profitability. However, insurers don’t always know which new products will have the most appeal, or be the most widely demanded. Launching a new product is a risky proposition, and some new products will be riskier than others.

Risk management is important at all stages of product development, especially during product design. In this course, we define product development as the process companies use to create new products or modify existing products. Technical product design, or product design, is the phase in the product development process in which a product’s financial structure is created.

While insurers tend to avoid high-risk investments, a particular insurer, in certain cases, might be more amenable to developing a high-risk product.

EXAMPLE

Basalt Life Insurance begins development on a highly innovative new product. This innovative product is risky, because potential customers could reject it—they might decide that the new offering is too different from other products on the market, or they might not understand the new product’s advantages. But an innovative product might also attract customers who find that the product serves their needs better than any competing product would. In that case, the innovative product might have strong sales because it has no strong competition. Basalt would also gain the benefits of being perceived as understanding its customers’ needs.

Note that in some cases, a product that sells well can be risky, if the product’s pricing or underwriting is insufficiently well designed. An underpriced product that sells well is a high-risk product, because the increase in potential claims is comparatively larger than the increase in premiums. Examples such as this are why insurers must be particularly careful during the technical product design process.

Because taking on risk directly affects an insurance company’s potential solvency, and because every company is exposed to many risks unrelated to product development, an insurer’s upper management cannot make risk-related decisions arbitrarily or in haste. To ensure a consistent approach to risk management, most companies set standards for the amount of risk that the company finds acceptable, and then communicate these standards to all employees. In this way, a company establishes a risk management culture. The advantage of a strong risk management culture is that every employee is aware of his or her role in the company’s risk management efforts, and, therefore, can work more effectively toward the company’s goals. A company’s risk management culture is reflected in everything from the products the company develops and the markets the company targets to the public image the company seeks to project.

One key method of establishing a risk management culture is by instituting a company-wide practice of enterprise risk management. Enterprise risk management (ERM) is a system that identifies and quantifies risks from both potential threats and potential opportunities and manages these risks in a coordinated approach that supports the organization’s strategic objectives. ERM focuses on operations throughout the organization, to the point that ERM becomes part of company culture. By implementing ERM, a company makes better-informed decisions regarding the level of risk that the company is willing to take on. We discuss ERM further in Chapter 3.

Another part of establishing a risk management culture is defining a company’s risk appetite and risk tolerance. Due to the individualized nature of company culture, specific definitions of these terms may vary from company to company. In this course, we define risk appetite as the amount and type of risk that an organization is prepared to seek in the pursuit of profits or other strategic objectives. Risk appetite defines a company’s long-term capacity for risk-taking. Conversely, risk tolerance is an organization’s willingness and readiness to bear a risk to achieve its objectives. This tolerance is usually expressed as a company’s stated limits on risk-taking in specified categories. Under this two-tiered approach, risk appetite is a qualitative concept describing the company’s attitude toward risk, while risk tolerance becomes the quantitative expression of the company’s risk appetite. A strong risk management culture informs the process of product development. For example, a company can adopt either a first mover strategy or a fast follower strategy. With a

  • First mover strategy, a company attempts to be the first to enter a new market or the first to enter a current market with an innovative product

  • Fast follower strategy, a company quickly imitates and improves a competitor’s innovation, or is the first to introduce a similar product into an expanded market

Both strategies are subject to risk, but thorough understanding of the risks—and a company’s strengths relative to those risks—allows a company to develop products with a greater chance of success. Because life insurance products are contracts that typically bind a company for years or decades, insurers generally adopt a first mover or fast follower strategy to introduce innovations such as digital options for customers. Among insurers, “first movers” that introduce entirely new types of products, such as the introduction of universal life or long-term care products, are comparatively rare.

As noted earlier, a company’s risk management culture affects all the areas involved in product development. However, the core of insurance product development requires a highly scientific understanding of product risk, which is the responsibility of the company’s actuarial department.
