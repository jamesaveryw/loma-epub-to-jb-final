In what ways do regulators ensure the solvency of an insurance company?

  1. Impose minimum capital requirements as a prerequisite to an insurer’s obtaining a license to conduct insurance business
  2. Monitor and assess the solvency of all licensed insurers on an ongoing basis
  3. Impose minimum capital and surplus requirements for insurers and for each product line
  4. Place restrictions on insurers’ investment holdings

Regulators expect insurers to maintain solvency standards so that insurers can meet their obligations to policyholders, which is regulators’ primary concern. Regulatory monitoring also indirectly helps other various stakeholders:

  • Individuals and businesses that buy insurance policies, as well as their policy beneficiaries, expect the companies that issue those policies to remain solvent over the long term so that they will be able to pay benefits when due.

  • Stockholders expect public insurers to stay in business and grow so that they can benefit from their investment in the company.

  • Company employees expect the company to remain solvent so that they’ll continue to have jobs and earn paychecks.

  • Creditors expect insurers to remain solvent so that they will cover their debts.

  • Vendors, including reinsurers, expect insurers to remain solvent in order to avoid business interruption to their companies.

  • Governments—and society in general—expect insurers to remain solvent to support needs that the state would have to potentially provide otherwise.

The best way to meet these expectations is to identify the risks that can affect company solvency and manage the company’s exposure to those risks. In this chapter, we’ll discuss the standards and requirements insurers must meet in order to be considered solvent by regulators. Then, we’ll describe various risks insurers must manage to maintain that solvency. In Chapter 3, we’ll discuss strategies insurers use to manage those risks.
