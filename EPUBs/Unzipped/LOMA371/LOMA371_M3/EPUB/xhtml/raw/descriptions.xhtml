Market Interest-Rate Increases

A list of changes that take place during market interest-rate increases.

General Changes: Rates are up and prices are down.

Changes in Bond Markets: Downward pressure on prices of existing bonds.

Reaction in General Account: Decreases in the market value of existing interest-bearing assets, and increases in the durations of some interest-bearing assets.

Reaction in Products: Potential increases in withdrawals, lapses, and surrenders of existing interest-bearing products.

Reaction in Liabilities: Fixed-rate product liabilities may shorten.

Rules for Understanding the Time Value of Money

[A graph with two bars representing present value (left) and future value (right). A line appears from the present value bar, about a quarter way up, to the future value bar, about two-thirds up.]

Principal Sum:

A principal sum invested at interest grows over time. Therefore, the principal’s

  • Future value is greater than its present value

[A small arrow runs along the line between the bars from left to right.]

  • Present value is less than its future value

[The arrowing running along the line shifts direction, from right to left.]

Interest Rate

Because an interest rate is a rate of growth, for money earning a stated rate of positive interest for a specified period:

[The point where the line hits the future value graph is circled in red]

  • An increase in the interest rate results in an increase in the future value.

[The circled point and line move up to 80% of the future value bar.]

Interest rate increase.

  • A decrease in the interest rate results in a decrease in the future value.

[The circled point and line move down to 50% of the future value bar.]

Interest rate decrease.

[The line is reset to its original position.]

[The point where the line hits the present value graph is circled in red]

When calculating the present value of a future sum, given a stated rate of positive interest for a specified period:

  • An increase in the interest rate results in a decrease in the present value.

[The circled point and line move down to 10% of the present value bar.]

Interest rate increase.

  • A decrease in the interest rate results in an increase in the present value

[The circled point and line move up to 50% of the present value bar.]

Interest rate decrease.

Number of Periods:

[The straight line becomes a series of steps between the two points on the present value and future value bars.]

Because interest is paid periodically and reinvested for compounding, for money earning a stated rate of interest for a specified period, an increase in the number of periods will result in

[The point where the line hits the future value graph is circled in red]

  • An increase in the future value of a present sum

[The circled point and line move up to 85% of the future value bar.]

  • A decrease in the present value of a future sum

[The stepped line goes back to original values. And the circle disappears. The point where the line hits the present value graph is circled in red. The circled point and line move down to 10% of the present value bar.]

Conversely, a decrease in the number of periods will result in

[The point where the line hits the future value graph is circled in red]

  • A decrease in the future value of a present sum

[The circled point and line move down to 50% of the future value bar.]

  • An increase in the present value of a future sum

[The stepped line goes back to original values. And the circle disappears. The point where the line hits the present value graph is circled in red. The circled point and line move up to 40% of the present value bar.]

Video 9.1

Using Margins to Reduce Risk

Spirea Financial estimated that its new whole life product would sell 15,000 policies in the first year.

A temperature gauge marked “Policies” fills up. As it reaches the top, it’s labeled 15,000 policies

Spirea determined that if all 15,000 policies remained in force, the company would recover $1 million of its $1.2 million first-year expenses by the end of the second year.

This outcome would require the product to set a total expense charge of $66.67.

$1,000,000 ÷ 15,000 policies = $66.67.

Modeling indicated that the product could expect a 85-90% persistency rate (or 10-15% lapse rate) during its first year.

[The temperature gauge lowers to 90%.]

First-year policies × persistency rate

15,000 × 90%

90% persistency: Estimated 13,500 policies at end of second year”. Move this text to the top of the screen, out of the way.

But to provide a margin in case actual persistency was lower, Spirea increased its expense projections by assuming a lower 80% persistency rate (20% lapse rate).

[The temperature gauge lowers to 80%.]

First-year policies × persistency rate

15,000 × 80%

80% persistency: Projected 12,000 policies at end of second year

Because Spirea set a margin for 80% persistency, it must charge each policy $83.33 to recover $1 million of first-year expenses.

If the product performs below estimation and earns only 80% persistency, the product will still meet its goals.

$1,000,000 ÷ 12,000 policies = $83.33

$83.33 expense charge x 12,000 policies = $1,000,000

[A green checkmark appears over 12,000 policies in the calculation.]

The product’s margin mitigates the risk of lower persistency.

But the margin does not eliminate the risk entirely, as persistency could still drop below 80%.

15,000 × 70% = 10,500 policies at end of second year

$83.33 expense charge × 10,500 policies = $874,965

[$83.33 expense charge is highlighted.]

[A red “X” appears over “$874,965”.]

However, what if the product performs as originally modeled, and 90% of policies remain in force at the end of the second year?

[Temperature gauge rises back to 90%.]

Spirea would recover $1,124,955 of first-year expenses.

$83.33 × 13,500 customers = $1,124,955

[Three green check marks above “$1,124,955”.]

Because Spirea built a margin into the product, the product is more likely to perform at or above the level that Spirea prepared for.
