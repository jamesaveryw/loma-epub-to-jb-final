Describing Operating Expenses

A company incurs many of its operating expenses as a result of developing, administering, and maintaining its products. The first step in determining operating expenses in product design is identifying which operating expenses are directly relevant to the insurer’s products. The company then gathers and analyzes information about relevant expenses to determine how these expenses are likely to affect profitability over time.

 

Identifying Relevant Operating Expenses

The operating expenses that are relevant to product design can be divided into four broad categories:

  • Development expenses, also known as research and development expenses, which the insurer incurs in starting a new product or product line.

  • Acquisition expenses, which the insurer incurs to obtain and issue new life insurance policies and annuity contracts.

  • Maintenance expenses, or renewal expenses, which the insurer incurs while a contract is in force.

  • Overhead expenses, which the insurer incurs during normal business operations. Overhead expenses are not directly connected to a specific product.

For expense control purposes, companies further divide operating expenses into controllable and noncontrollable expenses, direct and indirect expenses, and variable and fixed expenses.

  • A controllable expense is a cost over which a specified manager or organizational unit has power and influence. Examples of controllable expenses include the cost of advertising, technology purchases, overtime compensation, accounting services, and various other administrative services.

  • A noncontrollable expense is a cost over which no specified manager or organizational unit has power or influence. Examples of noncontrollable expenses include depreciation on equipment, taxes, and the costs of business licenses.

  • Direct expenses, also known as traceable costs, are product expenses incurred for or physically traceable to a specified life insurance or annuity product. Examples of direct expenses include expenses for sales commissions, transaction processing, premium taxes for most products, and product implementation.

  • Indirect expenses, also called common costs, are expenses that cannot be traced to or that are not incurred for one specific product. Indirect expenses result from activities that relate to several or all of an insurer’s products. An insurer incurs the same amount of indirect expenses whether it offers a specific product. Examples of indirect expenses include expenses for accounting systems, rent, executive salaries, property taxes, and some information systems.

  • A variable expense is an expense amount that varies in direct proportion to some variation in a specified level of operating activity. Examples of variable expenses include expenses for sales commissions, premium accounting, policy issue costs, transaction accounting, and premium taxes for most products.

  • A fixed expense is an expense that remains relatively constant regardless of the number of policies or contracts sold or the level of operating activity. Examples of fixed expenses include expenses for rent, executive salaries, property taxes, marketing research, and office equipment.

A typical expense falls into multiple categories. For example, the cost of product advertising is an acquisition expense, a controllable expense, a direct expense, and a variable expense. Direct expenses often are also variable expenses and indirect expenses often are also fixed expenses. Keep in mind that over time, all expenses tend to become variable, and thus the distinction between fixed and variable expenses is generally useful only in the short term, such as within a typical reporting period. Nonetheless, the process of categorizing expenses in this fashion helps companies perform the more in-depth expense analysis necessary for product design.

 

Gathering Expense Data

To understand how known expenses are likely to behave and therefore affect profitability, insurers must collect and analyze internal and external expense experience data. Experience data describe real-world observations of specified phenomena. Internal experience data reflect the characteristics and patterns of a company’s own historical expense experience, while external experience data reflect the characteristics and patterns of other companies’ historical expense experience.

To gather internal expense data, a company uses its own accounting information systems, which gather and store historical, company-specific accounting information. For example, an insurer’s systems contain historical records of premiums paid, lapses, claim costs, the cost of various underwriting reports, and many other forms of information that comprise the costs associated with a product. One particularly important accounting subsystem is the cost accounting system, which accumulates expense data for the dual purposes of effective cost control and accurate product design activities. The cost accounting system enables cost accumulation, which is the process of capturing all company costs and classifying them in meaningful ways.

A company may have limited expense experience with a particular type of product. To bolster its internal expense data, a company acquires and analyzes external experience data to learn from other companies’ historical information. A company can gain external expense experience data from experience studies, benchmarking studies, and research conducted by independent consultants and by industry organizations such as the MIB Group Inc. (MIB), LIMRA International, and the Society of Actuaries (SOA).

 

Developing Expense Projections

The foundation for estimating future expenses is historical expense data. But future expense patterns do not always reliably follow historical expense data. The factors surrounding a company’s business are constantly changing, and expenses change with them. To develop expense projections that are more likely to match currently unknown future expense patterns, companies require expense analysis. For product design, expense analysis is a process in which insurers study relevant costs and project the characteristics and behaviors of those costs over a proposed product’s lifetime.

Financial modeling is a vital part of expense analysis. As we described in Chapter 4, insurers use financial modeling—both deterministic modeling and, increasingly, stochastic modeling—to estimate future financial performance by projecting potential income and potential costs. Stochastic modeling is increasingly useful because it can address the wider range of potential outcomes associated with the variability of policyholder behavior.

Although insurers must include expense projections in all new product designs, not all of the expenses that affect a company’s new products require extensive expense analysis and modeling. If a particular operating activity is the same for all products or if a new product is sufficiently similar to one of the insurer’s existing products, the insurer may base its expense assumptions on standard costs. Standard costs are cost estimates representing the average amount of a given type of expenditure for normal business operations. Standard costs can efficiently estimate certain costs for product design, such as the cost of mailing policy documents. Standard costs may be direct, such as the cost of mailings, or indirect, such as rent and office equipment. However, insurers must review standard costs periodically to ensure that the costs continue to accurately reflect actual conditions.
