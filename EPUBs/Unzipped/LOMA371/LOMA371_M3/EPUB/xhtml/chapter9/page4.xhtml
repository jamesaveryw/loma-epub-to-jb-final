Assigning Expenses to Products or Product Lines

Once the insurer has identified the specific expenses that are relevant to product design and projected the monetary amounts of those expenses, the insurer calculates unit costs and assigns the expenses both fairly and efficiently to particular products or product lines.

 

Calculating Unit Costs

To determine the amount of expenses to assign to a particular product, an insurer must translate total costs into unit costs. A unit cost is the amount of incurred expense attributable to a single measured amount of product. A unit cost is found by dividing the total relevant cost by the number of designated units of products, as shown below.

Unit cost = Total cost
Number of units

Unit costs can then be incorporated into the product’s financial design. For example, if an insurer knows the unit cost for an individual term life insurance policy, then the term life product must be designed so that each individual policy recoups its unit cost. For a new product, insurers generally base unit costs on projections of the product’s expected volume of business.

Unit costs for most fixed expenses are expressed as a flat amount per contract. For example, an insurer’s costs for preparing and mailing account statements to customers are fixed; the costs are the same for all contracts of the same type. Therefore, to determine a unit cost for statement preparation costs, the insurer would divide the total cost of preparing and mailing statements to customers by the number of contracts. For variable expenses, insurers generally calculate unit costs as a percentage of an important contractual amount, such as the

  • Death benefit amount

  • Initial premium amount

  • Account value

  • Amount of account activity

 

Costing Methods

The process used during technical product design to set a product’s expense projections is called costing. Insurance companies can use one of two methods for costing life insurance and annuity products:

  • Full costing, or absorption costing, is an accounting method for estimating product-related expenses in which direct and indirect expenses are both counted. Thus, full costing requires an insurer to allocate all operating expenses to each product line and potentially to each product.

  • Marginal costing, or direct costing, is an accounting method for estimating product-related expenses in which only direct expenses are counted. Under marginal costing, indirect expenses are not allocated to products.

Marginal costing generally produces a smaller operating expense estimate than full costing produces. To offset this difference, insurers typically set margins that are designed to cover contingencies. For the sake of simplicity, this example assumes that all of the product’s expenses are incurred and all premiums are paid at the same time—a highly unlikely scenario for actual insurance products.

Assigning Direct and Indirect Costs

Because direct costs are associated with a particular project, assigning unit costs for direct expenses to a particular product is usually a straightforward process. The process of assigning unit costs for indirect costs is less straightforward. An insurer usually incurs the same amount of indirect expenses regardless of whether or not it offers a specific product. For example, the costs associated with an insurer’s accounting system apply to the entire company, and would be a key company expense even if the insurer decided not to offer a given product. An insurer must therefore allocate unit costs for indirect expenses along several products and product lines.

Cost allocation, or expense allocation, is the accounting process of assigning an indirect cost to a particular cost object according to a formal procedure. A cost object is any purpose for which a business measures costs. For an insurance company, a cost object may be a product line, specific product, contract, monetary amount of contract value, customer, or any other specified business unit. The formal procedure used to assign indirect costs is known as a cost allocation method.

Managing Commission Expenses

A large portion of an insurance company’s operating expenses is the payment of commissions to financial professionals. In 2018, commissions to agents made up almost 40 percent of an insurer’s operating expenses. A product design’s commission expense is generally a direct expense, as commission payments are tied directly to sales of a specific product. Incorporating commission expenses into product design requires understanding the type of commission, which determines the timing and size of commission payments.

 

Managing Commissions for Life Insurance Sales

Insurers generally divide commissions for individual life insurance into two categories:

  • First-year commissions are payable when a contract is purchased and are equal to a stated percentage of the premium the insurer receives during the first policy year. Most insurers pay relatively large first-year commissions as a way to encourage sales. First-year commission rates for life insurance products typically range from 40 to 90 percent of the first-year premium.

  • Renewal commissions are payable while a policy is in force and are equal to a stated percentage of each premium paid for a specified number of years after the first policy year. Renewal commissions are designed to encourage persistency by providing agents with a continuing stream of income from existing policies. Agents benefit from finding and selling to customers who are less likely to lapse or surrender their policies, and therefore the insurer benefits from steady premium income and less need to make surrender payments. Renewal commissions are usually between 2 and 5 percent of the premiums received after the first policy year.

We see from this comparison that first-year commission payments increase the operating expenses for a life insurance product during the first year, while renewal commission payments increase a life insurance product’s operating expenses for multiple years. The duration of renewal commission payments depends on whether the commission is vested, nonvested, or conditionally vested:

  • A vested commission is guaranteed payable to an agent whether or not the agent represents the company when the commission becomes due.

  • A nonvested commission is payable only if the agent still represents the company when the commission becomes due.

  • A conditionally vested commission becomes vested only after an agent reaches a certain age or number of years with the company.

Most agent compensation schedules for life insurance sales include both first-year and renewal commissions. A common arrangement is the heaped commission schedule, which features relatively high first-year commissions and lower renewal commissions. While expensive for the insurer, a heaped commission schedule encourages agents to focus on generating new business. To stress the importance of persistency, insurers may increase or decrease production bonuses paid to agents, depending on whether the agent’s sales show higher or lower persistency, or offer agents a separate persistency bonus for favorable persistency results.

 

Managing Commissions for Annuity Sales

Annuity commission schedules generally do not distinguish between first-year and renewal commissions, given that annuities usually feature large initial premiums and much smaller, if any, subsequent premiums. The simplest form of commission schedule for annuities is the deposit-based commission schedule, which pays commissions only on premium payments. A deposit-based commission schedule usually pays a sum calculated as a percentage of the annuity’s premium, such as 5 percent.

By contrast, increasingly more insurers offer asset-based commission schedules. An asset-based commission schedule calculates commissions as a percentage of the accumulated value of a deferred annuity contract’s funds. In addition, commissions are payable as long as the contract remains in force, even if the contract owner stops making premium payments. At regular intervals—usually yearly—after premium payments stop, the agent receives a commission payment known as a trail commission.

Asset-based commission schedules are designed to improve persistency, conserve annuity assets, and encourage agents to provide excellent ongoing service to annuity contract owners. The insight below compares deposit-based and asset-based commission schedules.
