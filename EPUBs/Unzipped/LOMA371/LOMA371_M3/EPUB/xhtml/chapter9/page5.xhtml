Setting Margins for Profits, Risks, and Policyholder Behavior

Once the insurer has identified and allocated the expected operating expenses for a new product, it should have an idea of how much revenue the product will need to earn to generate a profit. Of course, modeling can only anticipate potential results. When actual patterns do not match projected patterns, or when expenses arise that were not included in the insurer’s projections, these events can cause adverse deviations from an insurer’s expense projections.

 

Assumptions for Policyholder Behavior

In Chapter 2, we described policyholder behavior risk as the risk that policyholder actions or decisions will adversely affect product performance. The most common examples of policyholder transactions that terminate or reduce a product’s value are lapses, surrenders, and withdrawals.

  • Recall that a lapse is the termination of a life insurance policy for nonpayment of premium. Insurance products with cash value usually continue coverage until the policy’s cash value is depleted, but without certain guarantees, will also lapse eventually if no premiums are paid. Typically, a lapse rate that is higher than projected is a potential risk to a product’s profitability. While lapses are generally considered negative occurrences because they result in a loss of revenue, lapse-supported products are designed to be profitable in the face of higher lapse rates. For such products, a lapse rate that is lower than projected would be a potential risk to profitability.

  • Recall that a surrender is a transaction in which the owner of a cash value life insurance contract or deferred annuity contract chooses to receive the contract’s monetary value before the contract matures. With a surrender of an annuity, the contract terminates and the company makes no further payments.

  • A withdrawal is a transaction in which (1) the owner of a life insurance policy elects to receive a portion of the policy’s cash value while the policy is in force, or (2) the owner of a deferred annuity contract elects to receive a portion of the contract’s accumulated value during the accumulation period. A withdrawal is also known as a partial surrender or policy withdrawal. Not all cash value life insurance policies permit withdrawals of cash value. Insurers typically allow customers to withdraw a certain percentage of the cash value or accumulated value each year without charge, such as 7 to 10 percent. Withdrawals in excess of the specified percentages are subject to penalty charges.

Insurers usually assess the persistency of life insurance business by calculating the lapse rate and surrender rate for blocks of similar policies issued to individuals who are the same age, the same sex, and in the same risk category.

  • The lapse rate for a block of life insurance policies is the ratio of business in force that terminates for nonpayment of premium during a given period to the total business in force at the beginning of that period.

  • The surrender rate for a block of insurance or annuity contracts is the ratio of the number of contracts surrendered during a given period to the total number of contracts in force at the beginning of that period.

Farthing Life Insurance has a block of 10,000 term life insurance policies in force at the beginning of the year. 1,000 of those policies lapse during the year, and policyowners surrender another 500 policies during the year. The lapse rate for the block of policies during that year is the ratio of lapses during the year to the total business in force at the beginning of the year, or:

1,000 lapses ÷ 10,000 life insurance policies = 10% lapse rate

The surrender rate for the block of policies during that year is the ratio of surrenders during the year to the total contracts in force at the beginning of the year, or:

500 surrenders ÷ 10,000 life insurance policies = 5% surrender rate

Note that many insurers count surrenders as part of the overall lapse rate instead of differentiating between lapses and surrenders.

Lapses, surrenders, and withdrawals are some of the policyholder behaviors that can affect a product’s performance. Other examples of policyholder behavior include

  • Paying flexible premiums after the initial premium

  • Determining the timing of premium payments

  • Taking out policy loans

  • Changing the allocations of premiums between accounts

  • Transferring funds between fixed and separate accounts or between various subaccounts

  • Utilizing specific benefits

To properly model a product’s performance, insurers consider all of these behaviors when determining policyholder behavior assumptions. The task of projecting these behaviors is clearly challenging. To further complicate product modeling for policyholder behavior, companies typically have little or no internal experience data for new products and features. In addition, policyholder behavior constantly evolves, particularly with respect to new products and product features. External forces such as volatile financial markets and increased access to financial information may influence a policyholder’s decisions.

One factor affecting product profitability is whether an option is “in the money” for the policyholder. In a financial context, a benefit available from an ownership option or rider is in the money (ITM) for the policyholder if the benefit is more favorable for the customer than are the benefits available under the contract itself. In other words, if exercising an option would be more favorable to the policyholder than not exercising the option, the option is ITM. A common example of an ITM benefit is a policy element that retains value during stock market declines or similar downturns.

Carla Infante purchased an annuity with an interest-crediting rate of 2.5% from Farthing Life Insurance. Five years later, the market interest rate was 2.0%. Carla chooses not to surrender her annuity contract in favor of another product offering a lower interest rate. This choice is beneficial for Carla, and therefore ITM. However, Carla’s choice is less beneficial for Farthing, which may be experiencing spread compression.

Policyholders tend to exercise ownership options in products when financial market conditions are unfavorable for the company and favorable for the policyholder. However, this generalization does not always apply. A policyholder might not exercise her options even when the options are ITM, or she might exercise the options even when the transaction would produce a loss for the policyholder. For example, the immediate needs of a policyholder’s household may take priority over the optimal exercise of the policyholder’s ownership options. Unexpected expenses in a policyholder’s life may make it more attractive for the policyholder to exercise an option for a smaller amount of money, payable immediately, than to receive a larger amount of money in the future.

An insurer must estimate how efficient its policyholders will be for a given product—that is to say, how likely a policyholder is to exercise ownership options when market conditions produce the greatest gains for the policyholder. If the insurer underestimates policyholder efficiency, the insurer is at risk of underpricing the product’s riders or other benefits, and the company might not be able to meet its profit targets. If the insurer overestimates policyholder efficiency, it may price its products too high for the market.

 

Modeling Applications for Policyholder Behavior

Recall from Chapter 4 that actuarial assumptions may be classified as fixed, dynamic, or stochastic. Historically, when modeling the potential future behavior of policyholders, insurers primarily tend to use fixed and dynamic assumptions. Insurers may use stochastic assumptions if the risk being managed has specific characteristics, as described below.

In practice, insurers differ in how they model assumptions. One company might model withdrawals as a fixed assumption, while another company chooses to model withdrawals as a dynamic assumption. A single insurer may even model an assumption as either fixed or dynamic, depending on the function for which the modeling is used. Most commonly, insurers use

  • A fixed structure for assumptions used in pricing, valuation, and forecasting

  • A dynamic structure for assumptions used in asset/liability management and risk management 2

In addition, the type of product affects the choice of modeling assumption. For example, dynamic assumptions are most common when modeling policyholder behavior for universal life products.

In theory, any assumption can be modeled using a stochastic structure: lapse rates, flexible premium payments, surrender rates, and so on. Traditionally, stochastic modeling is preferable if the underlying risks have one or more of the following characteristics:

  • The volatility of the outcomes is unknown or high.

  • A given risk is nondiversifiable.

  • The company lacks an appropriate hedge for a given product risk.

  • The future outcomes from a given risk are likely to have a skewed distribution or a “fat tail,” as described in Chapter 4.

In the context of policyholder behavior, stochastic assumptions are particularly useful when modeling products that contain nonguaranteed elements. Nonguaranteed elements (NGEs) in life insurance and annuities are product features that allow an insurer to reward customers when the insurer has experienced or anticipates it will experience favorable deviations from its assumptions, or, conversely, allow the insurer to charge customers more when the insurer has experienced or anticipates it will experience unfavorable deviations from its assumptions. For example, the cash value on some universal life insurance products earns interest based on current market interest rates, which influences policyholder behavior when market interest rates rise, fall, or remain stagnant. To take these variables into consideration, insurers increasingly use stochastic assumptions when modeling possible future policyholder behavior for products with nonguaranteed elements.

 

Setting Margins

Using internal and external experience data, insurers typically build provisions for policyholder behavior into their expense projections. If actual lapse and surrender rates are lower than expected, the expense charge in the product design is typically sufficient to cover the company’s costs. If actual lapse and surrender rates are higher than expected the insurer may not be able to fully recover its costs.

To help protect against potential losses from adverse deviations from expense projections, insurers may base their expense charges on the results of pessimistic scenarios. If an expense charge is built on a pessimistic scenario—for instance, the expectation that lapse rates will be five percent higher than initially projected—then it is more likely that any deviation from that expectation will be favorable. Basing an assumption on a non-ideal outcome amounts to creating a margin.

The relative size of a margin can be affected by a variety of product characteristics, such as

  • Competition. A competitive market often acts to force prices down. If a product is competing with a number of other products, the product’s margin is likely to be smaller to keep the price competitive. Conversely, if a product has little or no competition with other products, it is likely to have a larger margin.

  • The amount of investment risk assumed by the insurer. Because insurers explicitly use margins to manage risk, a product that represents a larger amount of risk to the insurer is likely to have a larger margin. The risk-return tradeoff applies here, as the greater the risk the insurer assumes, the greater the potential return must be. Similarly, the lower the amount of risk the product represents to the insurer, the smaller the margin is likely to be.

  • The amount of investment risk assumed by the customer. Customers who purchase variable products assume greater risks than do those who purchase products with interest-rate guarantees, such as fixed-rate deferred annuities. In general, the greater the investment risk assumed by the customer, the lower the investment risk assumed by the insurer—and therefore, as described above, the smaller the margin is likely to be. Similarly, a product that presents a lower investment risk to the customer also presents a higher investment risk to the insurer, and therefore is likely to require a higher margin—which also makes the product vulnerable to interest rate compression.

  • The length of interest-rate guarantees. A product’s interest-rate guarantees affect the insurer’s risk in issuing the product. The insurer’s ability to revise a product’s crediting rate after the product has been issued reduces the risk associated with the product. The more frequently an insurer can revise a product’s crediting rate while the product is in force, the smaller the product’s margin is likely to be. Conversely, if the insurer is locked into longer-term interest-rate guarantees, the product represents a greater risk to the insurer and is likely to have a higher margin.
