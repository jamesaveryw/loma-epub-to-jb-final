Types of Mortality Tables

An insurer chooses the type of mortality table to use depending on the product type, the purpose of the calculation, the characteristics of the population being studied, and the availability of appropriate statistics based on the insurance company’s own experience. Mortality tables can be very specific: a single table might represent a population of non-smoking male annuitants, for instance. Types of mortality tables also frequently overlap. For example, a single table might be classified as a select mortality table, a sex-distinct mortality table, and a valuation mortality table. In this section, we discuss some of the general categories and criteria that insurers use to select and build mortality tables that best meet the needs of product development.

 

Group-Specific Mortality Tables

A population mortality table shows mortality statistics for all members of a given population. Population mortality differs by country, so a mortality table valid in one country may not be valid in another country. Population mortality tables may provide a variety of data, including life expectancy, or the average number of years of life remaining for a group of people. Product-Differentiated Mortality Tables

Insurers don’t use the same sets of mortality data in life insurance product design as they do in annuity product design. Annuitants tend to live longer than do life insureds, so insurers use lower mortality rates for annuity benefit design than for death benefit pricing. A life insurance mortality table shows the projected mortality rates and survival rates for a population of life insureds only. An annuity mortality table shows the projected mortality rates and survival rates for a population of annuitants only.

When life expectancy increases for the general population, or for a subset of the population, insurers experience a decrease in benefit costs for associated life insurance policies, but an increase in benefit costs for associated life annuities. The risk this trend poses for issuers of life annuities is sometimes called longevity risk.

Individuals may also face longevity risk. When we discuss the longevity risk of an individual, we are referring to the risk that he or she will outlive his or her assets.

The techniques used to manage mortality risk do not necessarily apply to longevity risk. A few effective techniques for managing longevity risk include

  • Monitoring external and internal sources of longevity data and incorporating estimated future longevity increases into the pricing for life annuities

  • Monitoring the company’s own sales of life annuities and evaluating the popularity of these products to determine whether the products are reasonably priced relative to those of competitors

  • Updating prices regularly, where applicable, to reflect the company’s latest understanding of longevity trends

  • Setting a maximum premium volume of life annuity business that the company will issue in any particular time period

Finally, insurers sometimes use different sets of mortality data for different products within a line of business and for different classes of underwriting risk within a product. For example, insurers often use lower mortality rates for individual annuities than they do for group annuities, because annuitants of individual annuities tend to live longer than do annuitants of group annuities.

Why do annuitants of individual annuities tend to live longer than annuitants of group annuities—or, for that matter, why do annuitants tend to live longer than life insureds? It may depend partly on lifestyle, income level, or the type of job the annuitant had during his or her working years. Annuitants of individual annuities tend to be in higher income or wealth brackets, which generally contribute to longevity.

In addition, customers purchase individual annuities to plan for the future—a decision that is more likely if a person has reason to believe that she will live long enough to require additional financial security. An annuitant may expect to live longer because of family medical history, personal health regimen, or other factors. People who are less optimistic about their life expectancy are less likely to purchase annuities.

Note also that life insureds tend to live longer than uninsured people. This difference could be partly due to the typical underwriting requirement for medical examinations: the healthier a person is, the more likely that the insurance company will approve her application for life insurance.

 

Basic and Valuation Mortality Tables

Insurers create some mortality tables to fulfill specific functions, in particular estimating the cost of a product’s benefit payments and calculating the amount of required reserves. The difference between the tables designed for these two purposes is the presence of a safety margin. This margin is the difference between the mortality rate that the insurer expects and the mortality rate that the insurer wishes to prepare for. In other words, an insurer using a safety margin is better prepared (or “safer”) if actual mortality experience varies from the expected mortality rate.

  • Primarily used for technical product design, basic mortality tables are mortality tables with no safety margin built into the rates. Basic mortality tables provide realistic mortality rates so that an insurer can best estimate future mortality costs.

  • Primarily used to calculate policy reserves, valuation mortality tables are mortality tables with a margin built into the rates. Because they have a safety margin built into their rates, valuation mortality tables are inherently more conservative than are basic mortality tables. Many of the values in valuation mortality tables are either determined or approved by regulators. This practice helps an insurer prepare sufficient reserves in the event that actual mortality varies from the insurer’s expectations:

  • For life annuities, the mortality margin reduces the assumed mortality rates and increases the assumed survivorship.

  • For life insurance, the mortality margin increases the assumed mortality rates and decreases the assumed survivorship.

 

Select and Ultimate Mortality Tables

Actuaries can also construct mortality tables to reflect the effects of recent underwriting evaluations on mortality. Insurers use these tables for pricing and reserve valuation. Tables that reflect the effects of underwriting are select mortality tables, ultimate mortality tables, and select and ultimate mortality tables.

  • A select mortality table shows the expected mortality rates of people who have recently been underwritten for insurance policies. People whose risk characteristics have been evaluated recently are likely to have better mortality risk characteristics during a specified select mortality period following the underwriting evaluation than do people who have not recently undergone an underwriting selection process. (Better mortality risk characteristics for life insurance are risk characteristics that indicate lower mortality.) For mortality tables used in pricing, the select mortality period can range from three to up to 25 years.

  • An ultimate mortality table shows the expected mortality rates of people who have not recently been underwritten for insurance policies. Several years after a person has been examined in an underwriting process, the person could develop new risk characteristics. After this time, the risk inherent in the mortality estimate increases. In this context, the ultimate mortality period is the period of time after the select mortality period has ended. The ultimate mortality period has no formal length, as it reflects the remaining years after the effects of underwriting have diminished.

  • A select and ultimate mortality table, also called an aggregate mortality table, combines coordinated sets of select mortality rates and ultimate mortality rates. For periods in the next few years, the select and ultimate table shows select rates. For longer periods, the select and ultimate table shows a blend of select and ultimate rates. For periods after the effects of underwriting have diminished, the select and ultimate table shows ultimate mortality rates.

 

Sex-Distinct and Unisex Mortality Tables

Actuaries can construct mortality tables on a sex-distinct or unisex basis.

  • Sex-distinct mortality tables are mortality tables that show different mortality rates for males and females.

  • Unisex mortality tables are mortality tables that show a single set of mortality rates to be used for both males and females. When an insurer uses unisex mortality tables, men and women of the same age are charged the same rates for the same type and amount of coverage. In the United States, most group insurance products are based on unisex mortality rates because courts have generally held that the use of sex-distinct mortality tables constitutes unlawful discrimination in employment. The EU, and some states in the United States, require the use of unisex mortality tables.

Actual mortality rates and life expectancies differ by sex; on average, women live longer than do men. Thus, insurers must generally pay annuity benefits to women as a group for a longer period than to men as a group. For the same reason, insurers must pay death benefits on insured men at earlier ages on the average than for insured women. The mortality rate of women at any given age—up to the end of a mortality table—is generally lower than that for men.

Life annuity premium rates based on sex-distinct mortality tables are generally higher for women than for men having otherwise similar characteristics. Insurers sometimes compensate for female annuitants’ lower mortality rates—and longer life expectancy—by charging female annuitants higher premiums than they charge male annuitants having otherwise similar characteristics. Similarly, insurers generally compensate for the higher mortality rates among men by charging higher life insurance premiums for men than they charge for women having otherwise similar characteristics.

 

Proprietary and Published Mortality Tables

Insurers use both proprietary and published mortality tables.

  • Proprietary mortality tables are the mortality tables a single insurance company develops for internal use, based largely on its experience with its own customers. Larger insurance companies are more likely to develop proprietary mortality tables than are smaller insurers.

  • Published mortality tables, also called public mortality tables, are mortality tables that are made available to the general public through printed documents and electronic sources. Published mortality tables are based on the mortality of the public at large or a broad cross-section of the population. The mortality tables compiled and published by the Society of Actuaries are examples of published mortality tables.

Large and small insurers use published mortality tables for calculating net premiums, reserves, and cash values. Insurance companies can also use published industry mortality tables for benchmarking purposes. In addition, if a company has too little business in a given segment or is otherwise too small to have credible data from its own experience, it can supplement its experience with published mortality tables. However, recall from Chapter 2 that insurers have been moving toward principles-based reserving (PBR), in which actuarial assumptions and reserve levels change as risk factors change. The use of PBR favors proprietary mortality tables, as they better capture the risks specific to a company.
