Developing Mortality Assumptions

Actuaries develop mortality assumptions for a variety of purposes, including pricing, reserving, and product modeling, The basic process of developing mortality assumptions for a life insurance or annuity product is to take known mortality experience and adjust it to make it relevant to a given product. In general, the process follows four steps, which we describe below.

 

Calculate Experience Mortality Rates

An actuary begins the process of developing mortality assumptions for a product by identifying appropriate mortality experience and calculating experience mortality rates. Mortality experience refers to the number of deaths that actually occur in a given group of people. Accordingly, experience mortality rates are the historical rates of death in a given cohort. When developing mortality assumptions for a product, the most relevant mortality experience comes from cohorts that match the cohorts of potential buyers of the new product. An actuary derives relevant mortality experience from internal data, industry data, or any other appropriate source. The actuary may then adjust the mortality experience to suit a specific product.

 

Develop Basic Mortality Rates

If necessary, the actuary then creates a table of basic mortality rates that provide a best fit with the experience mortality rates relevant to the proposed product. These basic mortality rates reflect a smoothing of the experience mortality rates to minimize any extreme values.

 

Create a Curve of Expected Mortality Rates

Extrapolating from the basic mortality rates, the actuary creates a curve of expected mortality rates. Expected mortality refers to the number or rate of deaths statistically likely to occur in a group of people at a given age.

 

Adjust Expected Mortality

The actuary may be able to use the curve of expected mortality rates without further adjustment. However, adjustment may be necessary to ensure that the data is credible enough for the insurer to rely on it. For example, a company’s internal experience mortality data might be insufficient as a basis for extrapolation if the company has experienced relatively few deaths at a given age. The insurer might not be able to tell how well internal mortality experience fits with industry mortality experience.

By adjusting expected mortality, the actuary creates useful mortality assumptions in the form of assumed mortality rates. Assumed mortality refers to the hypothetical or assumed number or rate of deaths in a given group of people. An assumed mortality rate is the rate entered into an actuarial model of a product. When calculating assumed mortality rates, the actuary may need to adjust the expected mortality curve to address any of the following considerations:

  • To acknowledge any reasonable expectation that policy design, underwriting, the risk selection process, or policyholder behavior will likely lead to future mortality results that vary from mortality experience, including any uncertainty in the projections attached to the specified business segment under consideration

  • To acknowledge any future trends in population mortality

  • To acknowledge any desired specific allowance for adverse deviation in mortality

Most U.S. companies create mortality tables for pricing by modifying basic tables to reflect their research with respect to risk class, smoking status, policy duration, age, sex, and policy size. Watch Video 8.1 for an illustration of the process of developing mortality assumptions.
