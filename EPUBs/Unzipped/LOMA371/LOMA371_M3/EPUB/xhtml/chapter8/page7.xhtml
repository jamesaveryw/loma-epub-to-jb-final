Cost of Benefits in Technical Product Design

As noted earlier, mortality rates affect a product’s cost of benefits if the benefit involves mortality risk for life insurance products or longevity risk for life annuity products. For some products such as term life insurance, insurers directly incorporate the mortality cost into the product’s overall pricing. Other products, such as variable universal life, assess an explicit charge—usually called a mortality and expense charge, or a cost of insurance charge—to cover the product’s mortality cost. A separate charge for mortality cost is necessary for products with cash values, so that the customer can clearly see what portion of the premium covers the cost of insurance and what portion of the premium increases the product’s cash value.

To build mortality cost into a product’s pricing, an insurer typically follows two steps:

  • Estimate base mortality costs (for life insurance) or base annuity benefit costs (for life annuities)

  • Apply a mortality margin (discussed later in this chapter)

To find the base mortality cost/annuity benefit cost, we estimate the number and the amount of the average benefit payment expected in a given period. Then we multiply these amounts, as shown:

Benefit cost = Expected number of benefit payments × Amount of the average benefit payment

The calculations to apply this approach vary between a life insurance product and a life annuity.

 

Mortality Cost for Life Insurance

For a one-year term life insurance policy, the potential claim is equal to the policy’s face amount. We can estimate the number of claims—or, in the case of one policy, the probability of a claim—by using the mortality rate. So, the estimated mortality cost of a $100,000 one-year term policy issued to a male age 35 is the mortality rate for a 35-year-old male (0.002125), multiplied by the $100,000 face amount, as shown below:

Mortality cost = Probability of dying × Average face amount
Mortality cost = 0.002125 × $100,000
Mortality cost = $212.50

This calculation shows that the mortality cost of life insurance benefits is $212.50 for a $100,000 one-year term life insurance policy issued to a male age 35. A simplified mortality cost calculation can arrive at the same result by multiplying the number of deaths per thousand (for 35-year-old males, 2.125) by the average face amount measured in units of $1,000, or:

Mortality cost = Deaths per thousand × Average face amount (in $1,000s)
Mortality cost = 2.125 × $100
Mortality cost = $212.50

Note that this calculation produces a statistically weighted expectation of loss per policy in a block of policies. If actual mortality is higher than expected, then the mortality charges in the product’s design may not be sufficient to pay for the product’s actual mortality cost. To protect the insurer against adverse experience, the product design typically includes a mortality margin, which we will discuss momentarily.

Insurers build mortality cost into a product’s pricing in different ways depending on whether the product is a term life, whole life, or universal life product. We discuss life insurance technical product design in more detail in Chapter 10.

 

Annuity Benefit Cost for a Life Annuity

For a life annuity, the annuity benefit cost equals the value of the benefits promised under the contract. For one year of a single life annuity contract, the insurer’s probability of having to pay the yearly benefit amount is equal to the annuitant’s probability of surviving that year. Remember that an annuitant’s probability of surviving each year is equal to 1 minus the appropriate tabular mortality rate.

Using a product-differentiated mortality table for annuitants, the calculation of the one-year annuity benefit cost for a $1,000 annual income payment on a life annuity issued to a male age 65 is shown below:

Annuity benefit cost = Probability of living × Average annual income payment
Annuity benefit cost = (1 – Mortality rate, male age 65) × $1,000
Annuity benefit cost = (1 – 0.0132) × $1,000
Annuity benefit cost = 0.9868 × $1,000
Annuity benefit cost = $986.80

The cost of benefits—in this case, the annuity benefit cost—is $986.80 for this particular year of a life annuity with a yearly income payment of $1,000. As with life insurance products, the total mortality charge typically includes a mortality margin to protect against adverse experience. Note that some life insurance products allow the option to receive benefit payments as a stream of income payments, which are effectively annuity payments and influence the product’s pricing accordingly.

When pricing an annuity product, the payout schedule is also a factor. With a deferred annuity, which begins making payments at least one annuity period—in this context, one year—after the contract is purchased, the insurer can invest the purchase premium and build any expected gains on the investments into the product’s pricing. In the case of an immediate annuity, which begins making payments within a year after the contract is purchased, the insurer has less opportunity for investments to gain value. Insurers therefore hedge their risk with immediate annuities by using mortality credits. A mortality credit is a monetary value that an insurer (1) allotted to payments for an annuity, and (2) did not pay in full due to the death of the annuitant. When an annuitant dies before the insurer has paid all the potential payments from his annuity, the insurer can use the resulting mortality credits to fund payments for other annuity contracts.

We discuss deferred annuities and annuity product design in more detail in Chapter 11.

 

The Mortality Margin

For insurance companies, a mortality margin is a provision for conservatism in mortality risk projections. Building an expected mortality margin into a life insurance or an annuity product’s technical design may help the insurer meet its benefit obligations if actual mortality rates turn out to be different from what was expected. A positive mortality margin can be a source of company profits. Mortality margins differ for life insurance products and life annuities.

  • Life Insurance: A positive mortality margin for a death benefit results from applying a higher mortality rate than would be found in a basic mortality table. By assuming that more insureds will die, the insurer prepares for potential high-mortality scenarios where it would have to pay more claims. If actual mortality turns out to be lower than the margin-adjusted mortality rates, the product’s profits will be higher.

  • Annuities: A positive mortality margin for a life annuity payout results from applying a lower mortality rate than would be found in a basic mortality table. By assuming that more annuitants will survive to collect benefits, the insurer prepares for potential low-mortality scenarios where the insurer’s cost of benefits is high. If actual mortality rates are higher than the margin-adjusted mortality rates, the product’s profits will be higher.

Once the insurer has determined the appropriate mortality cost/annuity benefit cost and set a mortality margin, the insurer can then incorporate the total mortality cost into the product’s pricing or set an appropriate mortality charge.
