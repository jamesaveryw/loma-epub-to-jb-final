Adjusting Tabular Mortality Rates

As noted earlier, when developing mortality assumptions, actuaries may adjust mortality rates to acknowledge trends or other factors that may cause future mortality to differ from actual mortality experience. For example, demographers have noted a long-term trend of small decreases in mortality over time. Because of this trend, mortality rates and tables based on experience alone generally may not reliably indicate future mortality.

Mortality tables that have not been adjusted to account for future changes in mortality are called static mortality tables. By contrast, a common form of adjusted mortality table is the generational mortality table , or dynamic mortality table, in which mortality rates are dependent on both the person’s age and the year the person attains that age. For example, a static mortality table assumes that a 65-year-old in 2040 will have the same life expectancy as a 65-year-old in 2020. In contrast, a generational mortality table generally assumes that a group of 65-year-olds in 2040 will have a lower mortality rate than 65-year-olds in 2020, or in some cases, a higher mortality rate. Generational mortality tables are more complicated to create and use than are static mortality tables, but offer more precise estimations of how longevity may change between generations. Many insurers use a blend of the two approaches by taking a static mortality table and applying a mortality improvement scale, which is a published set of recommended adjustments to mortality tables. The recommended adjustments in a mortality improvement scale are based on recent projections of mortality trends. Industry organizations such as the SOA are the most common source of mortality improvement scales.

Insurers can use a number of statistical approaches for estimating future changes in mortality. Two simple methods for modifying tabular mortality rates to reflect mortality changes are the projection method and the setback method. The projection method is more complicated than the setback method, but also generally produces more accurate and subtle results than does the setback method.

 

Projection Method

The projection method for adjusting tabular mortality involves multiplying the tabular mortality rates by a chosen percentage. Tables that have been adjusted using the projection method to account for future changes in mortality are called mortality tables with projection.

The projection method requires the insurer to choose a percentage that matches the insurer’s expectations for future mortality. For example,

  • If the insurer expects people in the specified group to die sooner than the tabular rates indicate, the insurer would multiply the tabular rates by a factor larger than 100 percent.

  • If the insurer expects people in the specified group to live longer than the tabular mortality rates indicate, the insurer would multiply the tabular rates by a factor smaller than 100 percent.

When designing a new annuity product, Comet Life Insurance decides to use the projection method to reflect longevity trends that are likely to affect the specified group of potential annuitants. Comet multiplies the tabular rates by 80%, which indicates that Comet expects the group to live longer than expected.

Figure 8.1 compares a portion of a mortality curve based on industry mortality tables with a portion of a mortality curve that is also based on the same tables, but that has also been modified with projection. The curve with projection has been multiplied by a factor of 30 percent, indicating that the insurer is expecting people in the group to live longer. Note that, near age 100, when the unmodified mortality curve has a steep upward slope, the curve with projection has a more gradual slope. Most of the difference at the higher ages reflects a distortion of the curve caused by the projection.

 

Setback Method

The setback method of adjusting tabular mortality rates consists of showing future decreases in mortality for people of a given age by using the tabular mortality rate for a younger age. When applying an age setback, insurers typically use values of one to five years. Generally, insurers avoid using a setback of greater than five years, as a large setback could distort the mortality curve.

When adjusting a mortality table for a new life insurance product, Comet Life Insurance decides it should use a reduced mortality rate relative to tabular rates for age 35. Comet settles on a two-year age setback for age 35, which uses an age two years younger than the attained age. Comet therefore applies the tabular mortality rate for the group at age 33 to the group at age 35.
