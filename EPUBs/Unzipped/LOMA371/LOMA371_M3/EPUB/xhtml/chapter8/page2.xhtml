The cost of benefits is a vital element of product design for both life insurance and annuities. To estimate the amount and timing of future benefit payments as accurately as possible, an insurer must make assumptions regarding mortality. Mortality affects the timing and amount of benefit payments in a variety of ways:

  • For life insurance policies, mortality determines when an insurer must make benefit payments. Insurers project death benefits using historical mortality data to estimate the pattern of deaths in an insured group. They also project life insurance terminations for causes other than death using historical lapse data.

  • For annuities in the accumulation phase, contract terminations from all causes are an important influence on the cost of benefits. In general, the effect of mortality on the cost of benefits is limited to any account values payable on the death of the contract owner. Mortality estimates affect the payout factors guaranteed in the contract and also affect the pricing of death benefit options under optional rider benefits and guaranteed death benefits.

  • For life annuities in the payout phase, calculating mortality helps an insurer determine its cost to provide periodic payments, establish annuity reserves, and set annuity payout factors, which are used to determine the periodic payment.

To calculate the mortality associated with a product, and therefore price the product accordingly, insurers use mortality rates. A mortality rate is the rate at which death occurs among a defined group, or cohort, of people during a specified period, usually one year. Cohorts may be classified by age, gender, tobacco use (smoking) status, or other characteristics. Actuaries organize mortality rates in mortality tables. Recall from Chapter One that a mortality table, or life table, shows the mortality rate for each age of a given group of people—that is, how many deaths may be expected within the group at each age. Actuaries project the rate at which people in a given cohort are expected to die. The mortality rates that are shown in mortality tables are known as tabular mortality rates. Insurers have access to their own mortality research as well as the mortality research supplied by industry organizations. However, developing mortality assumptions for a life insurance or annuity product is not as simple as selecting a pre-existing mortality table and using the provided values.
