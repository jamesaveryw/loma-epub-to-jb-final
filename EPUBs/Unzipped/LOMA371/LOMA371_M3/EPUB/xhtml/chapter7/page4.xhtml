Crediting Rates and Interest Spreads

As noted earlier, an insurer gains much of its investment earnings in the form of interest. In addition to calculating how much interest they will earn on their investments, insurers also need to determine how much interest to credit to customers who own certain products.

Therefore, interest rates affect both how much money an insurer expects to receive from its customers and how much it expects to pay to its customers. The relationship between the amount of interest received and the amount of interest paid out is critical to a profitable product design, as we’ll explore.

The interest rate credited to products on behalf of customers is called the interest-crediting rate, or crediting rate. For certain products, insurers may also declare more specific interest-crediting rates:

  • A minimum guaranteed interest-crediting rate is the minimum rate the insurer must pay on the contract’s accumulated value. This rate commonly ranges from 1-3%.

  • Most contracts also provide for a higher interest rate known as a current interest-crediting rate, which is the rate the insurer declares and pays. In general, companies offer higher current interest rates for longer interest-crediting periods, and they set a new current interest rate at the end of the current interest-crediting period based on current market conditions. The new current rate can be higher or lower than the previous current rate, but it can never be lower than the guaranteed interest-crediting rate.

When market interest rates change, their relationship to a product’s crediting rate affects the product’s interest spread. The interest spread, also known as the interest margin or interest-rate margin, is the difference between the rate of return an insurer earns on its investments and the interest-crediting rate. The interest spread is one measure of a product’s potential rate of return; the higher the interest spread, the more profitable the product is likely to be. The formula for the interest spread is as follows:

Interest spread = Interest rate earned – Interest-crediting rate

So how do insurers determine the interest-crediting rate for a product? Generally speaking, the interest rate used in the product design is known as the interest-rate assumption or the assumed interest rate. The insurer projects an expected interest rate on the product’s associated investments and sets a lower assumed interest rate in order to earn an expected level of profit on the product. We can express this relationship as follows:

Expected profit margin = Expected interest rate – Assumed interest rate

During product design, the insurer must set a premium amount that, along with investment earnings, will produce enough money to cover the cost of promised benefits when due. Present value calculations are vital to this process, as is the insurer’s understanding of customer needs. If the premium amount is too low, the investment earnings could be inadequate to pay the product’s benefits, or they could provide an insufficient profit. If the premium amount is too high, it may be difficult to sell the product.

For some products, such as fixed universal life insurance, insurers periodically declare a crediting rate and apply it to the product’s cash value. For example, an insurer may credit the cash value of a fixed universal life policy with interest once each month, and declare a new crediting rate, if appropriate, once a year. To provide for an acceptable overall level of profit, insurers can use a product design that provides for a small interest spread but possibly a larger spread for expenses or mortality.

 

Life Insurance Products and Cash Values

Permanent life insurance products are often referred to as cash value insurance products because they offer the additional feature of equity in the form of a cash value, which contract owners can access in the form of policy loans, withdrawal and surrender benefits, or nonforfeiture benefits. The general account generally funds a product’s cash value if the product is a fixed contract—which typically guarantees a minimum interest rate and the contract’s investment principal. If the product is a variable contract, the insurer’s separate account funds the cash value, as we’ll discuss later in this chapter.

 

Fixed Life Insurance Products

Insurers set a cash value for fixed life insurance products based on the amount of premiums paid on the policy, the face amount of the policy, and the amount of time the policy has been in force. Insurers can use either prospective or retrospective valuation methods to calculate this cash value. The great majority of insurers use the prospective valuation method, which looks at a product’s future cash flows—that is, its future benefits and future premiums. The prospective valuation formula for determining the cash value of a fixed life insurance product is as follows:

Cash value = Present value of future benefits – Present value of future premiums

In effect, the prospective valuation method depends on deriving the present value of future sums. By contrast, a retrospective valuation method looks at a product’s past cash flows. This method uses future value, or accumulated value, techniques to base cash value on past performance. Though prospective and retrospective valuation both arrive at the same cash values, regulations require most insurers to use prospective valuation for new products.

 

Other Product Forms

The formulas for fixed products do not readily apply to flexible-premium life products, universal life products, or variable life products. For these forms of life insurance, an insurer calculates cash value by subtracting the remaining unamortized first-year expenses from the policy’s account value, as shown:

Cash value = Account value – Remaining unamortized first-year expenses

In effect, the cash value is essentially the value of the account once all first-year expenses have been paid.

 

Annuity Products and Investment Earnings

A deferred annuity—whether fixed or variable—generally has an accumulation phase during which the insurer collects and invests premiums paid by the contract owner, and the invested premiums accumulate earnings. (Fixed indexed annuities operate differently; we will discuss the design of these annuities further in Chapter Eleven.) The total amount of premiums and earnings under the deferred annuity contract, less the amount of any withdrawals and charges assessed by the insurer, is the accumulated value, or account value. Like the cash value of a life insurance policy, the accumulated value represents the owner’s equity in the contract. However, while a life insurance policy’s cash value is generally separate from the policy’s face amount, the accumulated value represents the current value of the annuity contract. How an insurer incorporates investment earnings into an annuity contract depends on whether the contract is fixed or variable.

 

Fixed-Rate Deferred Annuities

Fixed-rate deferred annuities accumulate value through interest. The insurer applies the current interest-crediting rate to annuity contract values to determine the accumulated value.

 

Variable Deferred Annuities

When contract owners pay premiums into a variable deferred annuity, they specify how they wish their premiums to be invested. Most insurers offer both fixed accounts, which are guaranteed, and variable subaccounts, which are nonguaranteed but have a higher potential for gain.

Fixed Accounts in Variable Annuities

The fixed account directs premium payments to the insurer’s general account. If the contract specifies a current interest-crediting rate, the insurer credits interest to the contract using that rate. Otherwise the insurer credits the contract with the guaranteed interest-crediting rate. As with other general account investments, the insurer bears all the investment risk for money in the fixed account, and is responsible for any losses that occur if market rates fall below the interest rate needed to support the credited rate. In general, customers place a relatively small portion of their premium into a fixed fund.

Subaccounts in Variable Annuities

The insurer uses premiums allocated to subaccounts to purchase accumulation units, which represent ownership shares in the selected subaccounts. The amount of return each subaccount earns is equal to the market return on the subaccount investments, less investment management fees, mortality and expense (M&E) risk charges, administrative expense charges, and any other charges that the insurer assesses. The value of a contract owner’s accumulation units is not guaranteed. Accumulation units increase or decrease in value based on the performance of subaccount investments. Regardless of market conditions, the number of accumulation units still increases as the contract owner pays more premiums, which are used to purchase more units.

The accumulated value of a variable annuity contract is determined by the current value and number of accumulation units the contract owner has in each subaccount investment. Interest-Rate Risk

Recall from Chapter 2 that changes in market interest rates cause the value of investments to change. These changes also affect the interest spread on products that offer interest-crediting rates. Here we’ll examine the risks that accompany increases or decreases in market interest rates, as well as the risks associated with market interest rates remaining flat over an extended period of time.

One factor that recently affected insurers’ vulnerability to interest-rate risk is the environmental shift away from predictable rise-and-fall cycles for market interest rates. When market interest rates fell, many insurers assumed that they would rise again in the near future, and priced products accordingly. When market interest rates failed to rise as expected, or fell even further, many long-term life insurance and annuity products performed far below the insurers’ expectations.

 

Increases in Market Interest Rates

Recall that when market interest rates increase, insurers can be adversely affected. For instance, an insurer would have difficulty selling its existing bonds, which have lower interest rates than the market, unless the insurer was willing to accept a loss by selling the bonds at a discount. These potential negative effects are a result of various market risks such as interest-rate risk and reinvestment-rate risk.

One significant impact that rising market interest rates may have on an insurer is that the insurer’s interest spread may narrow. When an insurer’s interest spread narrows, we refer to the phenomenon as spread compression. Spread compression typically occurs in an increasing interest-rate environment when customers immediately demand the higher market interest rate. At the same time, the average rate of return on the insurer’s existing invested assets rises only slowly, because these assets continue to earn the lower interest rates that were available in the financial markets when the insurer purchased the assets.

Spread compression does not occur automatically when market rates are increasing, because an insurer can offer lower interest rates to keep its spread constant. However, if an insurer chooses to keep its spread constant by refusing to offer customers the higher market interest rate, existing customers are less likely to continue business with the insurer, and new customers may be harder to attract. Insurers may have to choose between (1) increasing their current interest-crediting rates and accepting a lower interest spread in an effort to retain their customers’ business, (2) leaving their current interest-crediting rates at lower levels so as to avoid a lower-than-acceptable interest spread, or (3) finding a course of action somewhere between the two. A product design can include elements such as surrender charges to offset some of this risk; we discuss these elements further in Chapter 9.

When the Fluorite Life Insurance Company priced its fixed deferred annuity product, it determined that it needed a 1.5% interest spread in order to meet its goals. When Fluorite issued the contracts, it was earning 4.5% interest on the investments supporting this product. Therefore, Fluorite set the initial interest-crediting rate at 3% (4.5% earned rate – 1.5% interest spread).

Two years later, market interest rates had increased by 1%. Some of Fluorite’s competitors were now crediting 3.5% interest on their fixed deferred annuities. Fluorite had to decide whether to increase its rates to match its competitors. Fluorite was still earning 4.5% on the investments supporting its fixed deferred annuity product. If it raised its interest-crediting rate to 3.5%, the company would face a reduced interest spread of 1% (4.5% earned rate – 3.5% credited rate). This reduced spread would be lower than the spread Fluorite needed to meet its profit target.

In addition, when market interest rates increase, new interest-bearing investments in the general account earn the higher market rate. An insurer with capital available to invest in new interest-bearing securities has the opportunity to make more profitable investments in a high-interest environment.

If Fluorite had the capital to invest in new securities after market rates had risen by 1%, the company could purchase investments bearing a 5.5% interest rate for prices comparable to what the company paid two years ago for its investments bearing a 4.5% interest rate.

Decreases in Market Interest Rates

When market interest rates decrease, the price of existing interest-bearing investments increases. If an insurer purchased bonds during a period of higher interest rates, the insurer can sell these bonds before maturity for a higher price, and such bonds held in the insurer’s general account increase in market value.

Low interest rates strongly benefit borrowers. In a low interest rate environment, borrowers pay less overall interest on new loans. However, this means that lenders have more difficulty in a low interest rate environment. In such an environment, interest-bearing investments have a much lower rate of return over time. Thus, insurers cannot rely as heavily on investments to ensure their solvency, and they must consider raising prices or fees, adjusting rates, reducing operating expenses, or other such measures to compensate for lost investment income.

For these reasons, when market interest rates are decreasing, one potential negative outcome for insurers is that their liabilities may lengthen. Liabilities lengthen when fixed-rate products, such as fixed deferred annuities, remain in force for longer than the insurer assumed, because the company’s current interest-crediting rate is higher than market interest rates. When a liability is in force longer than the insurer had anticipated, the insurer’s assets are less likely to match the liability throughout the increased period.

Fluorite’s fixed deferred annuity products provide a current interest-crediting rate of 3%. When market interest rates fell to 2%, customers held on to these annuity products because they were receiving a return greater than the market was currently yielding. Therefore, Fluorite may have more liabilities than the company had anticipated, and may not have sufficient assets to adequately cover these liabilities over time.

When market interest rates are decreasing, companies can experience spread compression or spread expansion. The results depend on the company’s actions and its existing investments; an insurer might experience spread compression on some products and spread expansion on others.

Spread expansion is the widening of an insurer’s interest spread. Spread expansion typically occurs when market interest rates fall, but the insurer has investments that still earn interest at a higher rate. The insurer therefore can choose to reduce its interest-crediting rates to match the market interest rates.

One of Fluorite’s products is supported by invested assets earning 4.5% interest. Previously, Fluorite had set the product’s interest-crediting rate at 3%, in order to earn a 1.5% interest spread. When market interest rates fell by 1%, Fluorite chose to lower the product’s interest-crediting rate to 2%. The interest spread on that product then rose to 2.5% (4.5% interest earned – 2% interest-crediting rate = 2.5% interest spread).

While decreasing interest rates create the opportunity for spread expansion, they do not guarantee it. Borrowers who are paying older and higher interest rates may choose to repay their debts in full before repayment is due. Those same borrowers can usually refinance—that is, make new borrowing arrangements at the new lower prevailing interest rates. In such a situation, the insurer would lose the higher-yielding older assets. This circumstance would cause spread compression on life insurance and annuity products with long-term guarantees. To avoid spread compression during periods of decreasing market interest rates, insurers typically match their products having long-term guarantees with assets that are unlikely to be refinanced. For example, the issuer of a noncallable bond cannot redeem the bond early without paying a penalty, which makes noncallable bonds a prudent match for products with long-term guarantees.

Another of Fluorite’s products is supported by invested assets that provided a return of 4% interest. Fluorite had set the interest-crediting rate on this product to 2% to earn a 2% interest spread. When market interest rates fell by 1%, the borrower chose to repay Fluorite in full. Without the additional income from steady interest, Fluorite was required to make a new investment to support the product. Because market interest rates were generally lower, the best investment for the product that Fluorite could find paid 2.5% interest. This product’s spread therefore fell to 0.5% (2.5% interest earned – 2% interest-crediting rate = 0.5% interest spread).

Sustained Low Market Interest Rates

While many risks take the form of unexpected changes, in some cases the risk is that an environmental factor will remain the same when change is expected. One such risk is that market interest rates will remain level at a low rate rather than increasing as expected. This risk has proven very relevant to financial services companies in recent years. Much of the global economy has seen a sustained low interest rate environment since the financial crisis of 2008. Such an environment causes significant challenges for insurance companies.

The risks inherent in an environment where market interest rates remain low are similar to the risks inherent in decreasing market interest rates. In particular, spread compression is a common occurrence when market interest rates fail to rise as anticipated.

A critical risk associated with ongoing low market interest rates is that a company does not successfully plan for the possibility that interest rates do not rise. Companies that rely too strongly on historical data to guide their interest rate assumptions face the greatest challenge when interest rates fail to rise according to past precedent. In such a case, an insurer may have invested resources in developing a product that is not priced to be profitable in the current market, even though it would have been profitable in the market the insurer had anticipated.

The long-term nature of life insurance and annuity products means that even the most prudent insurer is at some risk during sustained periods of low market interest rates. An insurer may still be contractually bound by products designed and sold ten, twenty, thirty, or more years ago. In addition, insurers traditionally hold bonds and other debt securities to maturity. This practice is more conservative than selling before maturity in the assumption that market interest rates will fall, and therefore mitigates some risk. However, this practice also prevents the insurer from mitigating potential losses if market interest rates are low at the time the security matures.

 

Strategies for Managing Interest-Rate Risk

To manage interest-rate risk associated with the general account, life insurance companies rely on a simple control cycle. The typical control cycle consists of three specific types of controls:

  • Steering controls are established in advance and describe the company’s expectations of performance. Examples include policies, procedures, risk tolerances, and codes of conduct. A typical steering control for interest-rate risk is the initial interest-crediting rate on a product.

  • Concurrent controls address a company’s current activities by continuously monitoring them. A typical example of a concurrent control is the automatic checks in a system.

  • Feedback controls compare actual performance or output with established standards, typically at the end of a measurement period. A typical example of a feedback control is an internal audit.

As with other control cycles used in a company, a robust risk management cycle begins with appropriate policies, procedures, and standards. Some methods of managing interest-rate risk can be implemented during product design. Examples include

  • Employing floating interest rates for policy loans

  • Incorporating nonguaranteed elements in product design, such as policy dividends on participating fixed premium life insurance policies

  • Designing a lower minimum guaranteed interest-crediting rate, where regulations permit

  • Shortening the guarantee period for minimum guaranteed interest-crediting rates

Concurrent and feedback controls ensure adherence to the policies, procedures, and standards set as steering controls. External and internal audits are valuable tools for this purpose. ALM programs such as those described in Chapter 3 are also essential for managing interest-rate risk. The following insight discusses one way that insurers can manage risk by anticipating changes in interest rates.

INSIGHT

Economic Scenario Generators

A common tool for modeling changes in interest rates is an economic scenario generator (ESG), which is a software program that models economic environments based on the behavior of financial market values and economic variables. Life insurers primarily use economic scenario generators to model

  • Potential interactions between interest rate changes and policyowner behavior such as lapses and policy loans
  • The interaction of investment performance on assets built up from the collection of premiums
  • The payout of liabilities based on mortality or policy surrender

The Society of Actuaries and the American Academy of Actuaries manage ESGs used in regulatory reserve and capital calculations. These generators, such as the Academy Interest Rate Generator (AIRG), are available to insurers for their own use, although many insurers prefer to use these tools to supplement their own proprietary models. By anticipating potential long-term changes—or lack of changes—in market interest rates, insurers can be better prepared to manage the risks inherent in increases, decreases, or stagnation in market interest rates.

Companies can take a number of corrective actions to address potential deviations from the company’s interest-rate risk standards. These corrective actions include

  • Specifying the interest-rate characteristics of new purchases of bonds and mortgages

  • Decreasing current interest-crediting rates within allowable limits

  • Directing changes in nonguaranteed elements or other product features that render a given interest-rate condition risky

  • Offsetting specified interest-rate risks with purchases of derivative securities

Ultimately, insurers must use a wide variety of controls to manage interest-rate risk, with the understanding that market interest rates are beyond an insurer’s control. Similar controls are useful in managing mortality risk, as we’ll explore in a later chapter.
