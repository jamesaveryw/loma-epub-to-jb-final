Investment Earnings

Most of an insurance company’s investable capital comes from the premiums that the company receives. An insurer typically divides its assets into two main types of accounts: a general account and one or more separate accounts.

All insurers have a general account, also known as a general account portfolio, for assets that support the insurer’s contractual obligations to policyholders and contract owners of the company’s guaranteed products, including whole life insurance, term life insurance, fixed-rate annuities, and other nonvariable products. General account assets also support any guarantees found in variable products. Insurance companies, not policyholders and contract owners, bear the investment risk associated with the general account. To manage this risk and to comply with various regulatory and rating agency requirements, life insurers generally invest the general account in high-quality bonds and other conservative, highly liquid assets. An insurer may manage its general account assets internally, employ a third party to do so, or use a combination of the two approaches. Insurers may also purchase reinsurance on blocks of policies supported by the general account in order to reduce the company’s risk of insolvency.

Insurers that offer variable products maintain one or more separate accounts, also known as separate account portfolios or segregated funds portfolios, for assets that support such products as variable life insurance and variable annuities. A company typically divides separate accounts into smaller subaccounts, each of which consists of a pool of investments with a distinct risk-return profile, usually known as a fund style. Unlike the general account, separate account subaccounts may contain more equity securities, such as stocks, than debt securities, such as bonds and mortgages.

The customer, rather than the insurer, determines how premiums are applied to subaccounts. The customer also bears the investment risks associated with a variable product that is funded by a separate account. While some of the fund styles available to customers are conservative, insurers design separate accounts with an overall higher risk appetite than the risk appetite they assign to the general account. The insurer manages the purchase and sale of assets in the subaccounts according to the customer’s allocation choices and covers the expenses incurred for designing, distributing, and administering the variable products through explicit fees and charges.

 

Price Appreciation

Changes in the value of invested assets are the result of gains or losses. An investor realizes a gain when an asset’s selling price is higher than the asset’s price at purchase. Gains generally result from price appreciation, which is an increase in the market value of an invested asset. Conversely, losses occur when an asset’s selling price is lower than the asset’s price when originally purchased. These losses generally result from price depreciation, which is a decrease in the market value of an invested asset.

 

Income Payments

Insurers receive payments of interest, dividends, or rental income from certain investments.

  • Interest is a fee that an institution, such as a bank or government, pays for the use of money. An investment that pays simple interest pays interest only on the principal. Many bonds pay simple interest. In many situations, the interest on loans and other investments is compound interest, which occurs when an investment pays interest on both the principal and on the interest earnings that the investment accumulates. When an insurer reinvests the interest payments it receives from an investment that pays simple interest, the insurer can compound the interest. Because interest is a steady and reliable source of income, insurers invest heavily in interest-bearing investments such as bonds and mortgages. We’ll discuss factors specific to interest earnings in more detail later in this chapter.

  • An insurer typically receives dividends when the insurer holds stock in a company and the company pays a share of its profits to its stockholders. The insurer receives dividend income for holding on to stock, as opposed to capital gains, which the insurer receives for selling stock for a price higher than it paid for the stock.

  • An insurer may receive rental income for the use of any real estate the insurer owns. Compared to most stocks and bonds, real estate is relatively illiquid. So, although an insurer may have investments in commercial real estate, especially as part of the general account, these investments are usually a smaller portion of the insurer’s invested assets.

When investment earnings are expressed as a percentage of the principal, they are called the rate of return on the investment. If an asset earns more than one form of earnings, the total rate of return is determined by summing all of the earnings and dividing by the principal. Note that the total return on an investment is the sum of all the investment’s earnings.

Oceanic Life Insurance purchases $100,000 worth of stock in the Puffin Corporation. Oceanic holds the stock for a year, over which time the company earns $5,000 in dividends from the stock. At the end of the year, Oceanic sells the stock for $110,000. Because the sales price is higher than the purchase price, Oceanic earns a gain. The total return on the investment, therefore, is the sum of Oceanic’s dividend earnings of $5,000 and its $10,000 gain upon the sale of the stock ($110,000 – $100,000). The investment’s total rate of return is the total earnings of $15,000 expressed as a percentage of the stock’s purchase price of $100,000, or 15% ($15,000 ÷ $100,000 = 0.15).

 

The Time Value of Money

Investment growth is based on a concept known as the time value of money, which states that the value of a sum of money changes over time as a result of the effects of interest. This chapter’s discussion of the time value of money refers specifically to interest earnings. The time value of money concept also assumes that interest earnings are positive. According to this concept, a sum of money received in the present is worth more than the same sum of money received in the future—because the sum of money received in the present will be invested and earn interest.

Oceanic Life Insurance was given the option of receiving $100,000 now, or receiving $100,000 one year in the future. As we’ve seen, when Oceanic invested $100,000 for one year, it earned an additional $5,000 in dividends. The $100,000 sum of money, properly invested, was worth $105,000 after one year even before Oceanic sold the investment and received capital gains. Therefore, the $100,000 sum, received now, is worth more than the $100,000 sum received one year from now, because a sum received now can potentially gain an additional year of earnings.

We generally express the effects of the time value of money by calculating one of two values:

  • The future value (FV) of a sum of money is the amount that the original sum is expected to be worth at a specified future date, given a specified interest rate. Future value calculations are useful when an insurer knows the specific sum it intends to invest and needs to know how much the investment is likely to earn. When calculating future value, each period that interest is paid is known as a compounding period.

  • The present value (PV) of a sum of money is the amount that, if invested at a specified interest rate on a specified date, would grow to equal a specified future amount. Present value calculations are useful when an insurer has identified a specific sum as a future goal and needs to know how much to invest to achieve that goal. Each period used in present value calculations is known as a discounting period.

In simple terms, the present value is the principal of an investment, and the future value is the principal plus accumulated earnings. Because we are assuming that interest is always positive, the future value of a principal sum invested at interest is always greater than the sum’s present value, because future value takes into account the interest earned. Present value and future value calculations also assume compound interest, which is why compounding periods and discounting periods are relevant. While actuaries are capable of calculating future value and present value by hand, in practice they, like most people, typically use financial calculators and computers to speed up the calculations. During product design, an insurer must test numerous variables and combinations of variables for every potential product: possible interest rates, the number of compounding or discounting periods, regular and irregular streams of payments, and so on. An in-depth presentation of the various calculations used is beyond the scope of this course. However, the basic principles behind these calculations are helpful for understanding how quickly or slowly a given investment’s earnings are likely to grow. Future Value

The most basic future value calculation is determining the future value of a single amount for one period, which is equal to the principal plus the interest earned for one period. Analysts typically substitute present value for principal because, like principal, present value represents a sum of money before it earns interest.

Future Value (FV) = Present Value (PV) + Interest earned

For a one-year period, the amount of interest earned is equal to the present value multiplied by the interest rate, i.

Interest earned = PV × i

Incorporating the formula for interest into the basic future value formula results in the following equation:

FV = PV + Interest Earned
FV = PV + (PV × i)
FV = PV × (1 + i)

So, to determine the future value of a single amount for one period, we simply multiply the principal by (1 + i).

Amara plans to invest $1,000 at a 5% interest rate for one year. To determine the future value of her investment, she performs the following calculation:

FV = PV × (1 + i)
$1,050 = $1,000 × 1.05
$1,050 = $1,000 × 1.05

Insurers often calculate the future value of a single amount over multiple periods—as with a single-premium life insurance or annuity product. In this situation, the investment will pay interest on both the principal and the accumulated interest each period, thus increasing the amount of the interest paid. The present value is multiplied by (1 + i) one time for each interest period (n) in which interest is applied.

FV = PV × (1 + i) n

To simplify these calculations, analysts use future value interest factors and future value interest factor tables. A future value interest factor (FVIF) is the future value of $1.00 at a given interest rate for a stated number of periods. We express the FVIF for a specified interest period, n, and a specified interest rate, i, as FVIF(n, i). A table that lists future value interest factors is called a future value interest factors (FVIF) table. By using software or dedicated financial calculators, we can calculate a future value quickly by inputting the amount of principal, the interest rate per period, and the number of compounding periods. In recent years, many websites and mobile applications have made specialized financial calculators freely available to the general public as well.

For some situations, insurers must also calculate the future value of a series of payments. Financial analysts refer to any series of equal payments made at regular intervals over a specified time period as an annuity, a definition that includes both the payments an insurer makes to contract owners and the premiums contract owners pay to the insurer. The future value of an annuity at any given future date is the amount that a series of equal payments earning compound interest will accumulate by that date.

The future value of an annuity is a more complicated calculation than calculating the future value of a single amount because each payment in the series will earn a different amount of interest. In addition, the amount of payments that earn interest varies between an ordinary annuity and an annuity due, because of the difference in the timing of the payments.

  • An ordinary annuity is a series of equal periodic payments for which the payment occurs at the end of each specified payment period. Two examples of an ordinary annuity are an employee’s paycheck paid at the end of each work period and a bond interest payment received one period after the bond is issued. When calculating the future value of an ordinary annuity, the final payment earns no interest because it is paid at the end of the last compounding period.

  • An annuity due is a series of equal periodic payments for which the payment occurs at the beginning of each specified payment period. Two examples of an annuity due are a series of rent payments and a level series of whole life insurance premiums. When calculating the future value of an annuity due, all payments, including the final payment, earn interest for at least one compounding period.

Figure 7.1 illustrates an ordinary annuity and an annuity due, and the effects of interest on each.

Analysts can use compound value interest factors to simplify these calculations, just as they use FVIFs to simplify other future value calculations. These interest factors, called future value interest factors for an annuity (FVIFAs), represent the future value of a $1.00 annuity at a given rate of interest for a certain number of periods. A future value interest factors for an annuity (FVIFA) table lists values of FVIFAs for varying interest rates and interest compounding periods.

Calculating the future value of a sum of money is a critical part of designing annuities, cash value life insurance, and other such products. Insurer applications for calculating the future value of a sum of money include

  • Matching projected earnings with projected liabilities. The most basic use of future values is estimating the amount of earnings from investments a company will have as its liabilities come due. Because life insurance contracts can be in force for decades, insurers in particular must plan their investments over the long term. This practice is critical for ALM.

  • Projecting benefits for products that credit an interest rate to customers. If an insurer offers products that allow customers to earn interest, future value calculations help estimate the total amount of benefits the insurer is likely to pay on these products.

  • Calculating cash values. As we’ll discuss later in this chapter, one way to determine the cash value of a product involves calculating the future value of past premiums and benefits.

  • Tracking investment performance. Calculating future values creates a performance target, which can be compared to actual performance over time. For example, calculating the future value of an investment at regular one-year intervals can help an insurer review the investment’s performance each year and determine if it is still likely to meet its long-term goals.

  • Projecting the future value of premium payments. An insurer can use future value calculations to determine what the total value of a series of premium payments would be when invested at interest.

 

Present Value

Future value calculations help determine the likely result of investing a known principal, or present value, in a specific investment. By comparison, present value calculations are useful for determining the likely amount of principal needed for an investment to reach a specific goal. In other words, future value calculations start with a stated principal and work forward to the result, whereas present value calculations start with a stated goal and work backwards to the principal.

Vigorous Life Insurance intends to repay a $500,000 loan at the end of three years, and it has found an investment to help with this repayment. By inputting the investment’s interest rates and discounting periods, Vigorous can calculate the present value of the $500,000 sum—the future value—to determine how much to invest to reach the goal on schedule.

The basic calculation for present value involves multiplying the future value (or desired goal) by 1 divided by the sum of 1 + the interest rate (often represented as ). We record the basic calculation for present value as follows:

Insurers can use this calculation as the basis for determining present values for a single amount over one or more periods or for an annuity. As with future values, actuaries use financial calculators or software to find the values quickly.

Actuaries also make use of present value interest factors for annuities (PVIFAs) and present value interest factor for annuities tables (PVIFA tables). Possible applications for calculating the present value of a sum of money include the following:

  • Setting premium rates. If a product such as an annuity offers to make specific payments to the contract owner in the future, calculating the present value of those payments is essential in determining how much the contract owner must pay in premiums, which provide the sum to be invested.

  • Developing an investment budget. By calculating the most likely sum of money to invest to reach a target goal, a company can avoid investing an inadequate amount to reach the target, or investing more than is necessary.

  • Calculating cash values. As we’ll discuss later in this chapter, another way to determine a product’s cash value is by calculating the present value of a product’s future benefits and premiums.

  • Measuring profitability. As we’ll explore later in this chapter, present value calculations are fundamental to measuring profitability using a product’s net present value or profit margin.
