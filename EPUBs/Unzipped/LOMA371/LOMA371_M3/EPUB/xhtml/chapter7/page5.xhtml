Measuring Product Profitability

The actuarial techniques that estimate a product’s potential earnings—and therefore inform technical product design—are also useful for determining a product’s actual earnings. Once an insurer releases a product, it can use present value calculations to model how profitable the product has become. To be successful, a new product must generate at least enough money to cover the product’s initial investment, or the amount of capital that an insurer must invest to establish the product. This investment includes the insurer’s costs of developing, pricing, and marketing the product; its costs of establishing reserves and administrative processes for the product; and its costs of issuing and underwriting contracts and compensating sales agents.

A company generally measures profits for a new product in terms of either the net cash flow or the earnings that the new product is expected to generate.

  • Net cash flow is a product’s total cash inflows generated from premiums, investments, and other sources, minus the total cash outflows generated by such items as commissions, expenses, and benefit payments. Net cash flow focuses only on cash. Because initial product sales generally are not high enough to recover all the costs associated with the initial investment, an insurance product’s first-year net cash flow is typically negative.

  • Earnings for a product are the amount that the product adds to the insurance company’s capital in a given period. Unlike net cash flow, earnings reflect noncash adjustments that figure into a product’s contribution to capital.

Like other companies, insurers typically use several tools for evaluating product profitability, such as net present value (NPV), internal rate of return (IRR), and profit margin. Note that because insurers typically perform these calculations at the beginning of a product’s life, the profitability evaluations are estimates and targets rather than certainties.

 

Net Present Value

An insurer calculates the net present value (NPV) of an investment project by subtracting the project’s initial investment from the present value of the product’s earnings over a certain period, usually the entire expected life of the product, as follows:

NPV = PV of earnings – Initial investment

If NPV is a positive number, then the product is expected to earn a profit as long as the assumptions are accurate. The general rule for NPV is that as long as a proposed product’s NPV is positive, the insurer should consider pursuing the product.

The NPV approach uses present value calculations. When calculating the PV of earnings, an insurer selects an interest rate, i, that reflects the return the company believes would be necessary to accept the risk inherent in the product. So, for instance, if an insurer selects a 10 percent interest rate, the company has decided it is willing to accept the risk the new product represents as long as the product’s estimated return over the period is at least 10 percent. Insurers generally select lower interest rates for products that represent lower risks and higher interest rates for products that represent higher risks, with a general eye towards conservatism.

Internal Rate of Return

When selecting an interest rate to calculate the present value of earnings, a company typically considers the internal rate of return in conjunction with NPV. The internal rate of return (IRR) is the interest rate, i, at which the product’s net cash flows must be discounted, using present value techniques, in order to exactly repay the insurer’s initial investment in the product. In other words, the IRR for a product is the interest rate at which the product’s PV of earnings is equal to its initial investment, and its NPV is equal to zero. Some insurers refer to the IRR as the return on investment (ROI).

Using present value calculators, employees of Amaranth Life Insurance determined that the NPV for its proposed new product would be equal to zero when the interest rate is 8.54 percent. Therefore, the projected IRR for the product is 8.54 percent.

An insurer can instead use the company’s hurdle rate as its required IRR. A hurdle rate, or required return, is a minimum percentage rate of return on capital that an insurer must earn to cover its cost of capital. From an insurer’s perspective, cost of capital is the overall percentage cost the insurer pays for the funds it employs. Although the methods used to calculate the cost of capital vary, they generally involve a combination of the various interest costs, transaction costs, and cost of equity an insurer pays for funds. An insurer’s hurdle rate, which is independent of product risk, typically applies to all products and all lines of business.

IRR is not suited for making subtle distinctions, because it relies on a large number of assumptions such as whether or not assets can be reinvested at comparable interest rates. Moreover, IRR does not automatically incorporate the risk involved with a given product. Insurers wishing to take into consideration the level of risk in a product’s cash flows can set a risk-adjusted minimum IRR for a product and then compare the product’s projected IRR to the risk-adjusted minimum IRR.

Whether a product’s projected IRR is acceptable for the level of risk it represents depends on the decision rule the insurer establishes. A simple decision rule is to require that the projected IRR be equal or greater to the insurer’s risk-adjusted minimum IRR for the product. Therefore, if the projected IRR is less than the risk-adjusted minimum IRR, then the insurer will likely not proceed with developing the product as is. We can summarize this rule as follows:

ACCEPT if Projected IRR ≥ Risk-adjusted minimum IRR

REJECT if Projected IRR < Risk-adjusted minimum IRR

After taking into account the level of risk in their new product’s cash flows, Amaranth determined that the risk-adjusted minimum IRR for its new product would be 9.50 percent. Because the product’s projected IRR of 8.54 is less than this number, the product’s current design would likely not be profitable. Amaranth decides to make changes to the product design before pursuing further development.
