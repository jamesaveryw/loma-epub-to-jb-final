A Strategically Aligned Company

In successful companies, strategic plans align with organizational resources and operational activities in ways that clearly support the company’s mission and move the company in a coordinated manner toward common goals.

A company’s most senior management, working with the company’s board of directors, maintains ultimate responsibility for this company alignment. Corporate governanceis the responsibility and authority of a company’s board of directors to direct senior management to fulfill its mission on behalf of the company’s stakeholders in a legal and fiscally responsible manner. The Potential for Misalignment

Unfortunately, strategic plans and operational activities do not always move in the same well-defined direction. Misalignment is particularly common in large companies with complex organizational structures and many specialized and often geographically dispersed operational units. What are the possible reasons for that misalignment?

  • Senior management might not view the entire organization as an interconnected value chain but as distinct business units, each with its own value chain. As a result, management misses opportunities for efficiencies and coordination in company operations.

  • Operational managers, with a narrower planning focus, sometimes focus on short-term results to the detriment of long-term strategies.

  • Operational employees operate in silos—operational units that act in isolation from other operating units—and often do not understand how their jobs relate to different operational units or how strategic goals relate to their jobs.

How does a company know if strategic plans and operational activities are misaligned? 1

  • Missed financial projections. When operational units do not have clear direction, some units may operate in ways that are counterproductive to company goals and revenue streams. Duplicated efforts in multiple operating units increase expenses. Confusion about which operating unit is doing what often causes missed deadlines and delays and a decline in customer satisfaction.

  • Erosion of employee morale and increased turnover. The chaos and confusion of misalignment takes its toll on employees, causing morale to decline. Declining morale often leads to lower productivity and increased employee turnover.

 

The Strategic Alignment Matrix

A strategic alignment matrix can be helpful in determining whether the company’s organizational purpose actually aligns with the company’s strategy, and whether the company’s strategy aligns with organizational capabilities. A company’s organizational capabilities are the extent and quality of its financial, human, and technology resources. Figure 2.3 shows that, at the best companies, purpose, strategy, and capabilities are in sync.

The four types of companies shown in the matrix include

  • Best companies. Successful companies have a purpose that effectively addresses customer needs and market conditions, and this purpose aligns with corporate strategy and organizational capabilities. Successful companies translate strategic plans into viable strategies for guiding operational planning. In such companies, employees understand the company’s purpose and share a set of core values that go beyond a job description.

  • Companies with the best of intentions. Sometimes a company’s purpose and strategy align but the company is incapable of implementing its strategy. The company may lack the needed human or financial resources, or the company’s organizational structure may cause dysfunction during implementation. For example, siloed operating units often have competing values and practices that inadvertently sabotage corporate strategy.

  • Companies going nowhere. Some companies have the organizational capabilities to support their strategy but a lack of alignment in purpose and strategy. For example, a company may have talented and committed employees, and abundant financial resources, but without a coherent strategy and purpose, the company will flounder as it squanders market opportunities.

  • Failing companies. Finally, some companies maintain strategies that do not align with the organization’s purpose or organizational capabilities. Without significant changes to strategy and the organization, these companies will eventually cease to exist.

 

Correcting Misalignment

Correcting misalignment often requires that company leaders make tough choices about the company’s purpose, people, and processes, which can involve significant organizational and operational changes. Management uses questions like those outlined in Figure 2.2 to identify the areas that require change. Strategic management then creates plans designed to accomplish those changes and communicates the plans to employees.

Organizational realignment often breaks down when communicating these changes to employees. Unless employees understand the importance of the change strategies and believe the strategies will work to accomplish a stated goal that is beneficial to the company and to employees, company morale will suffer. Strong leadership and honest communication are necessary, as are policies that reward desired behaviors. Later chapters delve more deeply into the leadership, company culture, and changing business processes that are required to keep a company aligned or correct misalignment.

EXAMPLE

Realignment

The Fine Life Insurance Company’s mission is to provide insurance products with the highest level of quality service at affordable prices. Initially, the goal of quality customer service was clear in company strategies. However, as time passed, the company instituted many strategies designed for operational efficiencies. Over time, the decisions made to improve operational efficiency negatively affected the quality of service. To realign its purpose and its strategies, Fine Life Insurance had to refocus its processes on how to provide quality service. Among the actions taken during realignment were

—Determining customers’ wants and needs

—Hiring additional employees

—Retraining existing employees

—Providing employees with meaningful rewards for providing quality service

—Implementing new automated processes that provide customization of services to customer needs
