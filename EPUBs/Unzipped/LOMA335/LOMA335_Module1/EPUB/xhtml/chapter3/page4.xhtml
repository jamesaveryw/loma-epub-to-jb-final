Behavior Modification

Behavior modification refers to techniques used to encourage desirable behavior and discourage undesirable behavior. According to behavior modification theory, individuals can learn to become effective leaders by modifying their own behaviors. Effective leaders can also use techniques, such as influence tactics and rewards, to influence or modify the behaviors of others.

 

Influence Tactics

Effective leaders develop certain tactics for influencing others to act in specified ways, and such leaders also recognize that certain tactics work better in some situations than others. Nine commonly used influence tactics are shown below.

Tactic

Method of Influence

Effectiveness

Rational persuasion

Uses logic, reason, education, or a detailed plan to convince others that a certain point of view is best

Effective for influencing all organizational levels including upper management, subordinates, and peers

Consultation

Asks for input or participation in order to gain acceptance

Most effective for influencing subordinates and peers

Coalition tactics

Enlists the aid or support of others in getting someone to agree on a common goal

Most effective for influencing peers who are susceptible to peer pressure

Inspirational appeals

Targets the emotions, values, or ideals of others to gain support for an alternative

Most effective for influencing subordinates

Ingratiating tactics

Uses flattery, praise, or humble behavior to gain acceptance

Most effective for influencing subordinates and peers

Personal appeals

Uses friendship or loyalty to gain acceptance

Most effective for influencing peers

Legitimacy

Uses the authority of a position, rules, or laws to obtain compliance with a request

Most effective for influencing subordinates and peers

Exchange tactics

Uses the rule of reciprocation: something is given with the expectation that something will be received in return

Most effective for influencing subordinates and peers

Pressure tactics

Uses intimidation, demands, or threats to achieve compliance

Most effective for influencing subordinates

Overall, the most effective influence tactics tend to be rational persuasion and consultation. Pressure tactics are rarely appropriate in the workplace unless other methods of influencing an employee’s behavior have failed. For example, a manager may need to threaten termination of employment if other methods of influence have not changed an employee’s actions.

Any of these tactics may influence others to accept or agree to what a leader wants in the short term. However, rational persuasion and consultation are the best tactics for motivating others and gaining acceptance or agreement over the long term.

Drucker suggests that organizations need effective leaders at every level of an organization to be successful. Thus, all employees regardless of their organizational level can benefit from developing effective leadership practices. For example, an employee who does not host group meetings still has meetings with other individuals. Taking control of time and running productive meetings are important leadership skills.

 

Taking Control of Time

Effective leaders are good time managers because they know that the supply of time is finite and perishable. 5 Each day has only 24 hours in it, and one cannot save yesterday’s time to use today. Yet many people squander this irreplaceable resource, often unintentionally. The only way to determine how time is being spent is to keep a log of real-time activities. After keeping an accurate log for two to four weeks, most people find that they actually spend a large amount of time doing nonproductive, time-wasting activities. Once people identify these time-wasting activities, they can determine whether the activity can be

  • Eliminated because it isn’t needed

  • Delegated to someone who can do the activity as well or better

Another valuable exercise is to look at all activities, not just the ones identified as time-wasting activities, with an eye for efficiency. People often do some activities or work tasks in the way they were taught without ever questioning why. By reexamining the desired result, people often find a more efficient way to achieve it.

 

Running Productive Meetings

People who analyze their time usually find that they spend a lot of it in meetings. Some meetings are necessary; many others are not. For example, it may be more efficient to send out an email update than to gather everyone for an update meeting. Effective leaders eliminate unnecessary meetings that waste their own time and the time of others. Even necessary meetings are time wasters when not run efficiently. Drucker suggests that productive meetings have the following characteristics:

  • A purpose: Productive meetings have a purpose that is determined before the meeting and end as soon as the purpose is accomplished. An agenda should be created with set time frames for each topic. The leader needs to keep the group on time and task. Meetings should not be general chat sessions unless the stated purpose of the meeting is to explore opinions and brainstorm.

  • An opportunity for advanced preparation: If participants in a meeting are expected to comment about information, attendees should receive that information for review prior to the meeting.

  • The presence of all required attendees: If a key decision maker or required expert is unable to attend a meeting, the meeting should be canceled and rescheduled to a time when all required attendees are available.

  • Communication of decisions made and next steps: After a meeting, attendees should receive a summary of the meeting. The summary should include decisions made, who is responsible for what follow-up activities, and relevant deadlines for assigned activities. Attendees can then notify the leader if they need clarification. A summary of the meeting promotes clear communication among meeting attendees and directs activities to the completion of goals.

 

Managing Antecedents and Consequences

The workplace is in some ways ideally suited to behavior modification techniques because a company can control many aspects of an employee’s work environment. Behavior modification in the workplace generally seeks to manage the antecedents or consequences of employee behaviors.

 

Antecedents

An antecedent is an environmental cue that prompts a person to act in a particular manner. In the workplace, an antecedent can be something that hinders or assists an employee in the efforts to perform one’s job effectively.

EXAMPLE

Customer Service Examples

Antecedent that prompts negative behavior: A computer system that freezes periodically causes employees to complain to customers about the slow system, which negatively affects the quality of customer service.

Antecedent that prompts positive behavior: A checklist of frequently asked questions about a variable annuity product assists employees in answering customer questions quickly and correctly.

Antecedents come first; behavior follows. Identifying and removing performance barriers and proactively providing performance guidance are necessary to creating effective antecedents that promote productive employee behaviors.

 

Consequences

Behavioral theory shows that people tend to repeat behaviors that have favorable consequences and avoid behaviors that have unfavorable consequences. Four strategies for managing the consequences of behavior are positive reinforcement, negative reinforcement, extinction, and punishment.

  • Positive reinforcement is the process of encouraging a specific behavior by immediately following it with a consequence that the individual finds pleasing or positive.

Tina Snow’s manager asked her to make a presentation during a group meeting. Tina had never spoken in front of a group of people, and she felt anxious and uncertain about doing the presentation. However, she did the presentation, and her manager praised her for her good work. Tina felt proud of her presentation and decided she would like to do more in the future.

  • Negative reinforcement is the process of encouraging a specific behavior by immediately withdrawing or terminating something that an individual finds unpleasant or negative.

Assume that before Tina made her presentation to the group, she asked her manager to assign the presentation to someone else. The manager asked why, and Tina said she felt anxious about standing in front of the group and being the center of attention. The manager suggested that Tina create slides for her presentation so that the slides and not Tina would be the focus. Tina felt immediate relief from her anxiety, knowing that she could do the presentation without having to be the focus of the group’s attention. She was suddenly motivated to work hard on her presentation.

  • Punishment is the process of discouraging a specific behavior by immediately presenting an undesirable consequence or removing something desirable.

Assume that when Tina asked her manager to assign the presentation to someone else, her manager threatened to take away her quarterly performance bonus. In this case, Tina managed to do a presentation (she overcame her anxiety about the presentation) but she was not motivated to do her best work.

  • Extinction is the process of discouraging a specific behavior by ignoring it.

Assume that Tina emailed her manager to ask that she assign the presentation to someone else, and that the manager never responded to her emails. Eventually, after getting no response, Tina accepted her fate and did the presentation but she was not motivated to do her best work.

Negative reinforcement and punishment seem similar but they are not. In these examples, Tina’s manager used negative reinforcement when she realized that Tina’s unwillingness to do the presentation was due to her anxiety about standing in front of the group. By removing the unpleasant aspect of the situation that was causing Tina’s anxiety, Tina’s manager used negative reinforcement appropriately. People often use negative reinforcement inappropriately without realizing it. For example, an employee who finds a task boring may complain about the task to his manager until the manager reassigns the task to another employee. The complaining employee is then encouraged in the future to complain about other tasks he does not enjoy doing.

Punishment, on the other hand, requires applying something unpleasant to influence behavior. In Tina’s case, her punishment would have been no quarterly bonus if she did not do the presentation. Acting to avoid something negative typically results in less than optimal performance.

Of these strategies, positive reinforcement, such as praise and recognition, tends to be the most effective in ongoing behavior management. Studies show that praise and recognition from superiors are highly effective ways of boosting morale and productivity. In addition, the timing of behavioral consequences is important. Continuous reinforcement is the process of either encouraging or discouraging every instance of a specific behavior. In contrast, intermittent reinforcement is the process of encouraging or discouraging some, rather than all, instances of a specific behavior. Continuous reinforcement is often required when employees are learning new behaviors. Once behaviors are learned, intermittent reinforcement is adequate.

EXAMPLE

Continuous and Intermittent Reinforcement

A supervisor in an underwriting department is training a new underwriter to use the new business processing system. The supervisor initially uses continuous reinforcement to praise or correct the employee’s behavior each time he completes an application correctly. Once the employee appears to understand the system, the supervisor uses intermittent reinforcement to ensure that the correct behavior continues.
