Insurance Company Operations

Like any company, an insurance company operates with strategic levels of management and operational levels of management. Strategic management focuses on fulfilling the company’s mission, achieving corporate growth, and competing effectively in chosen markets. Strategic management also provides direction for operational activities. Operations are the activities any company undertakes to produce goods and services. Operations management focuses on carrying out the day-to-day business activities within the parameters established by strategic management. The operational level is responsible, to a great degree, for how successful the insurance company will be in satisfying its stakeholders because, in these areas of the companies, insurers process applications, answer phone calls, and pay claims.

Stakeholders

A stakeholder is any party to an organization that has an interest in how the company conducts its business. Stakeholders can be external or internal to an organization. They affect and can be affected by the organization’s decisions and include customers, owners, employees, governments, suppliers, vendors, and partners. Figure 1.1 identifies the primary insurance company stakeholders and describes what each stakeholder typically wants in its relationship with the company.

Management, at both the strategic and operational levels, is continually evaluating decisions against the requirements of stakeholders, balancing the needs and desires of one group with those of another group, as well as the overall costs to the company. An opportunity cost is a benefit that the decision maker forfeits or gives up in choosing one alternative over another. Every decision involves opportunity costs. For example, when budgeting, a decision to spend money on a new technology results in the company having less money to spend in other areas. Even a decision to do nothing involves forfeiting whatever value might have resulted from making a change from the status quo. The challenge for management is to make decisions that are within legal, ethical, and financial limits while maximizing stakeholders’ value and minimizing costs to the company.

 

Operations Management

Quality, timeliness, and cost control are the primary tenets of general operations management. However, service companies apply these concepts differently than do manufacturing companies. A manufacturing company transforms raw materials into a finished product that customers want or need and are willing to buy. In such a company, profits are very product-centered and depend on the costs of the raw materials needed to manufacture the products, the timeliness of the company’s production and shipping methods, and the quality and innovation inherent in the finished product.

Insurers create profits through the sale of intangible products and services. Insurance products consist of an insurer’s contractual financial commitments as well as services such as claim payments, interest payments, and expert and friendly customer assistance. The elements of quality, timeliness, and cost control apply more to the operations supporting these intangible insurance products than to the actual product. Figure 1.2 shows insurance company operations categorized as either primary or support operations. Note that company operations may be categorized differently but these are typical categories. Primary and support operations both contribute to the overall success and profitability of the insurance company.

If any operational area within a company is experiencing problems with timeliness, cost control, or quality, other operational areas are likely to suffer.

EXAMPLE

The information/technology department incorrectly designs a compliance feature into the system for a new product. New product sales suffer when the product’s rollout is delayed.

The legal department is slow to approve the contract for a new product idea. New product sales suffer when a competitor enters the market first with a similar product.

 

Insurance company profits are the result of

  • Innovative and desired products and services that satisfy customer needs

  • Risk minimization

  • Sound investment strategies

  • Operational efficiencies in the creation, sale, and maintenance of products and services

Operational excellence in insurance companies results when companies maximize productivity by optimizing operations and reducing company expenses, while improving customer satisfaction and service quality.

 
 

Functions of Operational Employees

The operational employees of an insurance company generally carry out one of several broad functions:

  • New business acquisition functions. Employees perform duties that assist in initiating and closing a sale.

  • Administrative or technical functions. Employees perform duties that support a company’s operational areas. Employees may provide support for an operational area in which they work or other operational areas. Employees are unlikely to interact directly with a company’s external customers.

 

EXAMPLES

The accounting/payroll function processes compensation payments for all employees within a company.

The compliance function maintains licensing and compliance records for sales professionals.

 

  • Service functions. Employees perform duties directly for a company’s external customers either in real-time interactions or through transaction-based mail or email interactions.

 

EXAMPLES

The policy administration function explains policy riders and endorsements to a policyowner.

The claims administration function pays an insurance policy claim.

 

  • Managerial functions. Employees supervise other staff members and provide guidance for a particular area of operations.

 

EXAMPLE

A product development director leads a cross-departmental team of insurance professionals to create a new retirement annuity.

 

Traditionally, insurance company operations involved a large amount of transactional work—routine and repetitive tasks with little flexibility in how processes are completed. Insurers have now automated many transactional tasks in underwriting, claims processing, and customer service. Automation is the operation of a process, system, or piece of equipment without human intervention. Automation allows insurance companies to create jobs that make better use of workers’ talents and knowledge. Knowledge workersare employees who can interpret information within a specific, yet broad domain; use skills and knowledge to define problems; and identify alternative solutions to problems. Companies that effectively employ knowledge workers can earn a competitive edge in the marketplace as these workers tend to be self-managing and continuously innovating. Intellectual capital is the sum of all employee knowledge that a company can use to drive profits as well as other proprietary information that a company owns. 1 Companies that emphasize training and education for workers contribute to the development of the company’s intellectual capital and add value to the company.
