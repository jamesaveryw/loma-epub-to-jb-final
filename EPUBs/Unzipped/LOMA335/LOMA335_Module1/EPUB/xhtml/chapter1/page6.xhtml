The Changing Operating Environment

According to Pricewaterhouse Coopers’s 20 CEO Report in early 2017, the insurance industry is potentially exposed to the most disruption of any industry. 5 Figure 1.5 graphically displays a portion of the results from the report indicating the likelihood of disruption from over-regulation, changing consumer behavior, technological change, and new market entrants in selected industries.

Technology is the driving force behind much of the disruption in the insurance industry. Technology has opened the door for new market competitors to enter and created new opportunities for fraud. Other forces, such as a changing workforce, regulatory uncertainty, and the continued low-interest-rate environment are adding complexity to an already challenging operating environment.

 

Technology and Operations

Insurers are analyzing every aspect of their value chains to see how they might use new technologies to realign operations and deliver greater customer value. Artificial intelligence (AI) is the area of computer science that emphasizes the development of intelligent machines that work, “learn,” and react like humans. Figure 1.6 illustrates potential capabilities of AI.

The goal of AI is to go beyond automation and design processes for activities that previously required human intelligence. For example, a chatbox is a system that uses AI and spoken language or text to answer the most commonly asked customer questions. When companies use AI, workers can focus their efforts more effectively on less routine activities. Insurers also use AI to gain new insights into customers and their behaviors that can lead to improved customer service, claims processing, and underwriting operations.

The most common operational problem insurers face in connection with technological change is whether to modify or replace the extensive and expensive technology already in place in established companies. Legacy systems are older computer systems or applications that perform essential functions and are costly to replace or redesign. In addition to the financial costs of changing systems, established companies have company cultures associated with these systems and established operational processes that are often difficult to change.

Technology can create efficiencies in operations and drive increased market share and company growth. However, a company must have a coordinated technology and data strategy for operations to achieve the maximum return on investment.

 

New Market Competitors

Insurance companies typically build operating strategies on past success. New competitors are not held to this status quo because they have no past experience. New market competitors may be able to establish a different type of insurance business model that will disrupt the insurance business in the future. For example, LIMRA research shows that the Internet is close to surpassing financial professionals as the most valuable source of information when researching annuities and insurance. 6 What if Google or Amazon, with their Internet platforms and vast knowledge of consumers, entered the insurance market and began selectively targeting insurance prospects with the lowest risk potential? Would traditional insurers be able to compete?

Vijay Govindarajan, Dartmouth College professor, suggests that companies need to focus more on the future of their industry and less on the past. His three-box solution, shown in Figure 1.7, recognizes the competing challenges insurers face in innovating for the future while running a high-performing business.

Successful companies often focus on Box 1 because their success comes from optimizing what they are currently doing. Govindarajan cautions that such companies need to manage their present business for profitability but always be looking to the future. To ensure continued success, Govindarajan recommends that companies let go of attitudes and assumptions that might fuel current business but interfere with future innovation. Box 2 requires companies to discontinue activities or even lines of business that might be successful today but are unlikely to be successful in the future because some aspect of the environment is changing. Box 2 requires difficult and often painful decisions. Box 3 is where a company’s future lies. Focusing the company’s attention on new ideas and innovations allows companies to create new products and business models that take advantage of new or emerging technologies. Box 3 represents how a company remains relevant in the future.

 

Insurance Fraud and Cybersecurity

The Coalition Against Insurance Fraud estimates that insurers suffer losses of $80 billion each year from fraud across all lines of insurance. 7 Although a certain amount of insurance fraud has always existed, it is currently increasing as criminals employ technology for fraudulent activities. Hacking is the process of using a computer and technology to gain unauthorized access to another system’s data. Hardly a day goes by without a company reporting that its computer systems have been hacked and that the personal information of untold numbers of people is at risk. Criminals can use stolen Social Security numbers and other personal information, such as date of birth, for a variety of illegal purposes. Recently the insurance industry has seen an increase in the incidence of unauthorized insurance policy loans and annuity contract withdrawals.

As a result, fraud detection and prevention in life insurance company operations are increasingly important. Cybersecurity encompasses all of the efforts a company takes to protect its computer systems and networks from criminal activity. Traditionally, cybersecurity involved protecting an insurer’s internal systems and networks from criminal activity. Today, however, as companies partner with third parties to satisfy various business needs, a customer’s information may be spread across multiple external business entities with vulnerabilities that are completely outside the insurance company’s control. An effective cybersecurity program must determine the path along which a customer’s information flows for each transaction, and then identify areas of risk and ways to reduce or eliminate that risk.

EXAMPLES

Using Technology to Identify Fraud

The EndiFirst Insurance Company uses data analytics to identify life insurance policies submitted without an accompanying initial premium, which is a red flag for an unauthorized policy.

EndiFirst also is utilizing Endpoint Detection and Response (EDR) technology to detect, investigate, and respond swiftly to suspicious activities and issues at network sites.

Companies are also implementing more sophisticated authentication measures for verifying a policy or contract owner’s identity before processing a policy loan or annuity withdrawal. Effective cybersecurity programs and fraud prevention systems build customer trust and add value to the company.

 

The Workforce

Workforce demographics have changed, as illustrated in Figure 1.8, and these changes present challenges for operations management.

The Millennial population (those people born from the early 1980s until 2000) has now surpassed the baby boomer population (those people born from 1946 until 1964) as the largest living population segment. Millennials, as a whole, have been slow to adopt many of the markers commonly associated with baby boomer adulthood, such as permanent employment, home ownership, or marriage. This demographic trend has effects outside of the workforce in that such characteristics are often associated with insurance ownership. The Great Recession, with its high unemployment and subsequent modest job recovery, is often viewed as contributing to this trend. 8

Millennials are active participants in the gig economy. A gig economy is an environment in which an on-demand labor force is utilized to fill temporary or short-term jobs, and there is no formal employer-employee relationship between the independent worker and the employer. Independent workers do not usually qualify for regular wages or employer-provided benefits. However, independent workers do have flexibility in managing their work/life balance and career. According to MBO Partners, a management service for independent contractors, “By the year 2027, 58 percent of Americans will be classified as independent workers or will have worked as an independent worker at some point in their careers.” 9 Independent workers range in job skills from unskilled laborers to highly skilled contractors.

Insurance companies use independent workers to tap into specialized skills or for temporary, short-term job needs. A challenge for operations management is how to effectively hire, recruit, and manage a multigenerational and blended workforce of full-time, part-time, and independent workers to best maximize productivity and remain in compliance with worker protection regulations. Other challenges include leading and managing diverse work teams when workers regularly cycle into and out of the work team, and instilling in temporary or part-time workers the company’s brand image, corporate values, and culture.

 

Regulatory Uncertainty

Well-designed regulations are beneficial to customers and to market participants. Some regulations create opportunities for the insurance industry. For example, the passage of the Employee Retirement Income Security Act of 1974 created individual retirement accounts (IRAs).

Yet regulation also has the potential to disrupt insurance operations. For example, in 2015, the Department of Labor’s now abandoned fiduciary rule addressed the potential for conflicts of interest in commission-based compensation structures by requiring financial professionals who provide retirement investment advice to follow specific steps designed to enhance transparency. In response, some companies changed their operations and compensation structures, while others completely exited certain distribution channels or discontinued some products.  Experts believe that this rule was a factor in the decline of variable annuity sales by 21% from 2015 to 2016. 10 As the political environment remains volatile, regulatory uncertainty will continue.  Operations management must be vigilant in monitoring the current regulatory environment and it must also be prepared to change rapidly to comply with new regulations.

Economic Environment

Life insurance companies collect premium income and hold some of this money as reserves to pay benefits when they come due. Insurers invest the money they hold in excess of reserves. However, they must invest funds cautiously to ensure that they will have the funds available should benefit payments be greater than expected. As a result, insurers invest conservatively, with a significant amount invested in interest-bearing bonds such as U.S. Treasury bonds. Since the Great Recession in 2008, bond interest rates have been at historic lows. For example, in April 2017, Treasury bonds were trading at 2.28 percent. 11

What does this mean for an insurer’s operations? Insurers sold products during the 1970s, 1980s, and even 1990s with a pricing structure that assumed a much higher interest rate for investment income than was actually earned. With investment income lower than anticipated, insurers need to operate more efficiently and less expensively in order to remain profitable.
