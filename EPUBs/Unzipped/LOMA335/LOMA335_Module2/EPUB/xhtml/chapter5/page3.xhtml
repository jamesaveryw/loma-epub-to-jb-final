Project Management Basics

At any given time, insurance companies have ongoing projects across operational areas. Examples of projects that an insurance company might undertake are

  • Developing new underwriting processes

  • Updating IT systems

  • Conducting a regulatory examination

  • Launching a new subsidiary

  • Creating a new marketing initiative

  • Improving a website design

  • Implementing a chatbot on the customer portal

 

Characteristics of a Project

Projects can vary widely in complexity and scope. For example, a company celebration would generally represent a low-complexity project with a narrow scope. On the other hand, a product development project would generally represent a high-complexity project with a broad scope.

Generally, the goal of a project is to either solve a problem (as in the case of reducing costs, responding to customer complaints, or accommodating industry change) or seize an opportunity (as in the case of growing product lines, expanding target markets, or investing in new technologies). In either case, projects implement the changes necessary to continue to deliver value to customers. A successful project typically has the following characteristics:

  • Clearly defined objectives

  • Constraints on resources, such as staff time, staff expertise, and funding

  • A beginning point and an ending point

  • A unique accomplishment that will signal the project’s completion

  • A team of dedicated individuals

  • Aspects that are likely to change over time, such as initial planning assumptions

  • Sequenced activities and events

Clearly defined objectives are perhaps the most important precursor to project success. When a project’s team members do not understand what the project is supposed to accomplish, the project is likely to fail.

 

The Project Manager’s Role

A project manager is the person who oversees the project team. A project manager is accountable for planning, organizing, directing, and controlling resources to achieve an established goal. Successful project management requires specialized skills, including both hard skills such as data management knowledge and soft skills such as communication abilities. In addition to guiding the individual components of a project to completion, project managers today have to

  • Understand the corporate mission and business needs. 2 A successful project manager understands the corporate mission and business needs. This person is also aware of both the internal and external circumstances that contribute to changing needs. Without such an understanding, the project manager might deliver a completed project that offers very little value to company stakeholders.

  • Cultivate a “project spirit.” Contributors to the learning library at the Project Management Institute (PMI), an organization that maintains a body of knowledge for the project management community, define project spirit as “the collective attitudes, emotions, and behavioral norms that are focused on the project’s expected outcome and achievements.” 3 A successful project manager knows how to motivate and lead a team. She constructs a vision that she uses to nurture enthusiasm about a project. She inspires employees to value the project as an essential contribution to the company’s mission.

  • Practice emotional intelligence. 4 A successful project manager knows how to manage emotions in the workplace. He has the interpersonal awareness to gain the support of stakeholders within the company and work with team members to achieve results. He can stay calm under pressure in order to collaborate across teams, negotiate changes, and address shortfalls and competing resource needs. He is also empathetic: he understands different employee working styles and the extenuating circumstances that can affect project completion.

  • Use technology effectively. 5 A successful project manager uses technology to streamline and standardize processes. She can access and use real-time data to keep track of a project’s direction. She stays up to date on industry regulations to ensure that the use of project management technology complies with regulations for security and privacy. If the project manager works with remote or partially remote teams, she can facilitate the virtual meetings and check-ins essential to project control.

  • Establish an ongoing knowledge base of project management standards. 6 A successful project manager is knowledgeable about best practices in project management and remains informed when standards change. The preeminent resource for project management standards is the Project Management Institute (PMI). The insight below describes some PMI publications.

INSIGHT

PMI Publications

A Guide to the Project Management Body of Knowledge, 5th edition

  • Known as the PMBOK® Guide, this resource represents a standard of generally recognized good practices in project management.

ISO 21500:2012 (Guidance on Project Management)

  • ISO 21500:2012 describes concepts and processes that are considered best practices in project management. PMI worked with the International Organization for Standardization (ISO) to develop this guide.

Organizational Project Management Maturity Model (OPM3®)

  • A PMI standard that provides the tools that organizations need to measure their maturity against a comprehensive set of organizational best practices.

The Standard for Portfolio Management, 3rd edition

  • This standard provides a guide to processes recognized as good practices in portfolio management, including portfolio strategic management, portfolio performance management, and portfolio communication management.

Practice Standard for Earned Value Management, 2nd edition

  • A publication that offers a description of best practices when using the earned value management methodology for projects.
