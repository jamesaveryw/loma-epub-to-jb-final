The Project Lifecycle

We can think of project management as the application of management functions (planning, organizing, directing, and controlling) to all the phases within a project. A project phase is a segment of the project lifecycle consisting of a set of related project activities. A project lifecycle is the combination of all project phases, from project initiation to project closure. The phases are generally sequential in terms of when they begin, but can overlap. For example, successful project planning often continues through execution.

The name and number of project lifecycle phases vary among authorities. Companies use whatever model best fits their immediate needs. Many sources group the project lifecycle into four phases, as illustrated in Figure 5.1: an initiation phase, a planning phase, an execution phase, and a closure phase.

 

Project Initiation

The initiation phase of the project lifecycle consists of evaluating a proposed project to determine whether to proceed. During the initiation phase, stakeholders define the project’s purpose, review the project’s concept, and provide input on the project’s benefits and challenges. Project stakeholders are internal and external parties who have an interest in a project and expect to benefit from it.

EXAMPLES

Project Stakeholders

An employee whose duties are associated with the project

Members of a department that gains a new capability from a project

A customer who gains a new service as the result of a project

A vendor who sells services involved in completing the project

Obtaining initial stakeholder support is critical to a project’s success. However, the value of the project needs to be defined first.

 

Defining the Project’s Value

The question to answer during this step is, What’s the point of this project? Answering the question starts with identifying a project purpose. A project purpose is vague: it aligns broadly with the company mission, and describes why the project should be pursued. 7

EXAMPLE

Project Purpose

Deliver easy-to-access value to customers.

More specific than the purpose are project goals and objectives: they provide the boundaries for all future project-related activities. In other words, goals and objectives help create targets so that a team can turn an idea into a plan at a later stage. When it comes to projects, goals and objectives have different uses.

A project goal is a broad statement about what a project aims to accomplish in relation to business needs. Because the goal is such a high-level statement, it may require more than one project to achieve. Goals are important from a strategic standpoint because they tell the project manager what to work toward.

EXAMPLE

Project Goal

Improve EndiFirst’s online customer experience.

A project objective is a statement about how the company will accomplish the project goal. Good objectives should be SMART: Specific, Measurable, Achievable, Realistic, and Time-bound. 8 Objectives should also be concise: they should briefly describe actions needed to achieve the goal, but not go into detail about plans.

EXAMPLES

Project Objectives

Objective 1: Conduct three usability testing events and 1,000 customer surveys, and analyze results by January 20XX.

Objective 2: Provide developer training on best practices for portal development by March 20XX.

Objective 3: Redesign and test the self-service portal by August 20XX.

Objective 4: Provide IT and CSR training on best practices for portal support by September 20XX.

Objective 5: Launch self-service portal by October 20XX.

Purposes are established before goals because a goal without a purpose could result in a deviation from the overall mission. For instance, a project might improve the online experience for customers, but make it more difficult for customers to figure out how to access other communication channels.

Goals are established before objectives because an objective without a goal could result in completion of a project that does not add value. For example, a team could design a self-service portal that looks better and works faster but is not easier to use for customers.

Defining a project’s goals and objectives often requires research into the business problem. Managers can use this research to develop a business case.

 

Establishing a Business Case

A business case is a justification for a proposed project that highlights how a project’s benefits to the company will outweigh its costs and risks. Large or complex projects need a business case to convince the key decision maker that the project is worth pursuing.

The management responsible for the team or unit proposing the project usually develops the business case. Management can enlist the help of project managers, business analysts, team members, subject matter experts, or other relevant stakeholders to draft a case for presentation. Formats vary by organization, but will probably include information about

  • The reasons for pursuing the project

  • How the project contributes to the company mission and strategy

  • Existing research that supports the project

  • Different options considered and why they were or were not selected

  • Measurable benefits of pursuing the project

  • Risks and assumptions involved with pursuing the project

  • Expected cost and method of funding for the project

  • Timeframe for the project 9

Business cases for small projects might contain minimal information and only require manager approval. Business cases for complex projects might contain detailed information, such as action steps and cost breakdown per task. If the information used to develop the business case reveals issues with a project’s feasibility—that is, the degree to which company resources will be able to carry a project to completion—the business case should address those directly.

When the business case is complete, the person responsible presents it to one or more decision makers who can, based upon business needs, (1) accept the proposal to continue the project, (2) request modifications to the project goals or resources, or (3) reject pursuit of the project altogether.

 

Developing the Project Charter

Once a business case receives approval, the project takes its formal identity from either the project charter or a contractual agreement, depending on whether the project is internal or encompasses more than one business entity. For a project performed under contract by an external vendor, a contractual agreement covers rights and obligations. For an internal project, companies use project charters. A project charter is a document that formally authorizes the project to take place and appoints a project manager. A project charter identifies the business purpose of the project and grants appropriate authority to the project manager to move the project to the planning phase. Figure 5.2 offers an example of a project charter including some of the information a typical charter might contain.

 

Designating Team Members

The next step is to designate team members. In addition to a project manager, most projects include a project sponsor and a project team. A project sponsor is typically a company executive who authorizes the project, provides support and resources for the project, and communicates project information up the organizational chain of command. A project team consists of all project team members assigned to perform any portion of the project activities. An important type of project team is a product development team, which is a group that performs hands-on development of a new product.

A project team is temporary, and its members can be fluid. Appropriate team members have the skills and means required to accomplish the project’s objectives. To this end, project teams are often cross-functional. A cross-functional team consists of representatives from two or more work groups that perform related business activities across organizational boundaries. Members of a cross-functional team communicate regularly to make sure that each organizational unit is aware of the other’s activities.

EXAMPLE

Team Member Identification

Ruby, the project manager; Rich, the unit manager; and Anderson, a member of the customer service team, are discussing Anderson’s current projects to decide if a new project would work for his schedule.

EndiFirst hired Anderson after launching the original self-service portal. He has spent much of his employment at EndiFirst responding to calls about the online experience. Anderson also has experience performing analysis of customer surveys. Anderson would be a valuable team member because his skill set is a good match for some of the project objectives.

Many project teams have one or more core team members, assigned to perform a significant portion of project activities. The company defines what constitutes a core team member.

EXAMPLES

Core Team Members

—At EndiFirst Insurance, individuals assigned to perform at least 10% of the budgeted staff time for a project are core team members.

—At Foundation Financial, individuals assigned to perform at least 25% of the budgeted staff time for a project are core team members.

Companies often record the relationships for managing company projects in an org chart, short for organizational chart. An org chart is a hierarchically patterned array of boxes and lines depicting the formal lines of authority, responsibility, and communication in an organization. Figure 5.3 illustrates an org chart for one company’s project management efforts. In this hierarchy, the company has permanent project management elements consisting of an executive-level project management committee with a project management office and project management office staff.

Although projects are temporary, large insurance companies typically have a project management office (PMO) or project management unit with a permanent staff. Such companies maintain a pool of seasoned project staff and assign these people to successive projects. Among organizations with PMOs, half report having enterprise project management offices (EPMOs). 10 EPMOs operate at the strategic level by assessing risk and organizing resources in relation to the company mission. The number of EPMOs is growing; traditional PMOs don’t always receive the amount of enterprise-level support crucial for managing operational risk.

Because projects take place in many contexts, the overall organization of a project team can take a number of forms. For a given project, companies usually provide a structure consisting of at least a project sponsor, a project manager, and a project team. Companies with strong project management resources and activities often have more levels in their hierarchies than in the example in Figure 5.3.

Project Planning

Appointing a project team allows the project to move to the planning phase of the lifecycle, where the project manager can use information about team members’ resource, schedule, and training needs to help organize project details. In the planning phase, the project manager establishes boundaries for the project, determines needed resources, and develops a project plan document. Project managers typically spend most of their time in the planning phase. Project planning begins before project execution and continues to some extent throughout project execution.

 

Developing the Project Scope

Planning begins with a scope statement. A scope statement is a report that clearly states the limits on a project by specifying the project size, resources, limitations, and deliverables. A project deliverable is a measurable outcome from a project. The project manager, project sponsor, project team members, and other stakeholders can all contribute to the information in a scope statement.

To develop the boundaries for the information in the scope statement, it helps to establish baselines. A project baseline is a measurable starting point agreed upon by various stakeholders as performance standards for a project. Stakeholders usually establish performance standards for scope (scope baselines), time (schedule baselines), and cost (budget baselines) based on the results from past projects. A scope baseline defines the scale of the project and describes the deliverables needed from planning through closure. A schedule baseline is a general timeline that respects the needs of the project and the capabilities of company resources. A budget baseline is a reasonable estimate of project cost. Teams can use all three to develop accurate scope information.

Scope statements do not have a standard format. Instead, the format varies to suit the company and project. Typically, information presented in a project scope statement includes

  • Purpose, goals, and objectives

  • Deliverables

  • Staff requirements

  • Resource requirements

  • Timelines

  • Budget

  • Constraints

A project manager can document part of the timeline using a time requirements budget. A time requirements budget is a type of budget that shows calendar units of time—days, weeks, or months—and the expected amount of staff time required during each specified unit. During the execution phase, when actual times become available, the time requirements budget becomes useful for tracking exceptions. Project constraints are circumstances that place limitations on a project, and traditionally include scope, time, and cost. Each constraint assumes a different priority level within a project. Moreover, the importance of these three basic constraints differs from one project to another. One system for prioritizing constraints is to rank each of the constraint categories—scope (the characteristics of the deliverable), time, and cost—according to importance, using the labels driving constraint, middle constraint, and weak constraint. For one project, time constraints might have the highest priority, whereas cost constraints might be more important on another project. The project manager needs to identify the constraints with the highest priorities so that adjustments can be made later as needed. For example, if time is the driving constraint and a project gets behind schedule, one viable solution would be to commit more resources to the project. On the other hand, if cost is the driving constraint and a project is likely to exceed expected costs, then scaling back the scope of the project might be necessary.

 

Classifying Activities and Events

With a developed scope, a project manager can pursue more detailed planning. Detailed project planning requires basic knowledge about the activities likely to occur during the project. Remember that an activity is one of a set of actions that, together, accomplish a goal.

EXAMPLES

Project Activities in an Insurance Company

—Developing required policy forms

—Purchasing new software

—Filing policy forms with regulators

—Conducting training for employees

—Developing surveys for customers

Project managers sometimes conduct an activity analysis to determine how the activities in a project relate. An activity analysis is a report that documents the expected tasks and estimated time for completing a given activity, based on observation of or interviews with an individual performing the activity.

The minimum time required to complete a project is the sum of the minimum durations for all project activities. Therefore, a minimum time for each major activity is necessary to create a schedule. Relationships between project activities help establish the order of the activities in the schedule. For this reason, scheduling requires a project manager to determine whether activities are independent or dependent.

  • Independent activities. Team members can complete some activities without regard to the status of any other activities. Pairs of activities that can progress concurrently with no change to resource needs are called independent activities.

EXAMPLE

Independent Activities

EndiFirst software developers can make an update to a website at the same time a manager conducts a training session on customer service. These activities do not rely on each other for completion.

  • Dependent activities. Some project activities must be completed before others can be started because the activity requires shared company resources, such as staffing or technology. These activities are called dependent activities—activities that cannot be started until other activities have been completed because the outputs from one activity are required inputs for another activity. For time-sensitive projects, some dependent activities can occur at the same time as long as one reaches a certain point—for instance, no longer needing a particular staff member—before the other begins.

EXAMPLE

Dependent Activities

The project team cannot use the voice of the customer to guide web design until after the analysis of usability testing and customer surveys.

The completion of an activity is an event. An event may also be a milestone—an important interim goal for a project.

EXAMPLES

Project Milestones in an Insurance Company

—Regulatory approval of a policy form

—The arrival of a shipment of printed material

—The arrival of an application for new business

—Obtaining agent licenses and appointments

—Securing corporate approval for new underwriting rules, guidelines, and risk classes for existing or new markets

—Gaining corporate approval for a new claim policy of offering death benefit payments in a choice of a lump-sum payment or various settlement options

 

Visual Tools for Project Planning

To help with planning, project managers can represent project needs visually. For example, to make it easier to establish baselines, some project managers use work breakdown structures. A work breakdown structure (WBS) is a graphical display of project elements that often includes an estimate of the labor effort required for each project activity. A detailed WBS might include the availability and cost for each required resource. An example of a basic WBS chart appears in Figure 5.4.

Another method for project visualization is a simple flow chart. A flow chartshows the systematic progression of a series of activities, using connecting lines and conventional symbols. Although flow charts can involve many levels of detail, a macro flow chart that shows the broadest view is often adequate for a simple project overview. Flow charts usually consist of

  • Ovals that represent starting and stopping points

  • Boxes that represent important activities

  • Diamonds that indicate yes/no decision points

  • Circles that represent a point at which the flow chart connects with another process

  • Arrows that represent the flow of a project

Figure 5.5 offers an example of a macro flow chart for a customer service improvement project.

To create a flowchart, a project manager needs to consider all the relevant activities and events in a particular project and determine their proper sequence. Flowcharts are effective planning tools for simple, short-term projects. However, flowcharts are not always practical for complex projects in which multiple activities take place at the same time. In addition, flowcharts don’t indicate the total amount of time necessary to complete individual activities and events. For a more detailed visualization that includes time estimates, a project manager can use Gantt charts and network models.

 

Gantt Charts

A Gantt chart is a graphical scheduling tool that separates projects into critical activities and plots starting and ending dates for each activity. Gantt chart software is widely available, but project managers can also create Gantt charts using Excel spreadsheets.

Gantt charts help users show dependencies among activities, assign staff to activities, define project milestones, and share information throughout the organization. The horizontal axis of a Gantt chart represents the total time span of the project broken into increments of days, weeks, or months. The vertical axis of a Gantt chart represents separate activities that make up the project. The graph area of the Gantt chart, known as the schedule timeframe, displays a stack of horizontal bars with one for each activity. The stack of bars fills in the time between the beginning and end of a project.

During a project, the Gantt chart display is updated regularly to indicate which activities have been completed and which remain to be performed. By showing the progress of activities, a Gantt chart helps companies determine if corrective action is needed as the project is under way. Figure 5.6 is a Gantt chart for a new product launch.

Gantt charts often incorporate the following information:

  • Project status refers to the project’s progress toward completion at a given point in time. A project manager can update a Gantt chart by extending the bar for each activity to a length proportional to the amount of work completed.

  • A milestone schedule, or master schedule, is a summary listing of major activities and key milestones. A project manager can incorporate milestones into a Gantt chart.

  • Some Gantt charts use initials, numbers, or colors to indicate which team member is responsible for each activity.

Specific types of Gantt charts include

  • A daily schedule Gantt chart, which displays a repeating daily schedule, such as a routine schedule for project update meetings. One example of a daily schedule Gantt chart is the function offered by most email clients for scheduling meetings.

  • A baseline Gantt chart, which is used as the starting point for a project plan. To highlight departures from the plan or budget, a baseline Gantt chart can be placed alongside another display that shows actual project progress.

  • A stoplight Gantt chart, which offers the typical Gantt chart display plus the added feature of a stoplight status column indicating progress for a given row. A green stoplight indicates that a row for an activity is on or ahead of schedule. A yellow (or amber) stoplight indicates that a project is perhaps not entirely on schedule, but has not met the criteria for intervention. A red stoplight indicates that a project is receiving corrective action from the project manager.11

EXAMPLE

The actual finish date for the development of EndiFirst’s training initiative fell on March 1, one month before the scheduled finish date. This stoplight status column is coded in green.

 

Estimating Time

For scheduling purposes, a project manager has to know the expected time, which is an estimate of the time needed to complete project activities. If an activity analysis provides the time requirements, then each activity only needs one expected time. If time requirements are untested, a project manager can use a weighted average of three time estimates: an optimistic time, a most probable time, and a pessimistic time.

  • Optimistic time, noted as to , is the shortest amount of time the activity is likely to require.

  • Most probable time, denoted as tm , is the amount of time that the activity is most likely to require.

  • Pessimistic time, denoted as tp , is the longest amount of time the activity is likely to require.

Using all three estimates above, a project manager can calculate the expected time, denoted as te . The expected time for an activity is a weighted average of the optimistic, most probable, and pessimistic times. It’s called the weighted average because the most probable time is given more weight than the other two less probable values. The equation for using a weighted average as the expected time for an activity can be expressed as

 

te = to + 4(tm) + tp

Network Models

Network models are useful to analyze a sequence of events and activities for project planning and project control purposes. A network model, sometimes referred to as a program evaluation and review technique (PERT) network, is a visual representation of the activities in a process or a project used to identify the best use of resources. Project managers can use network models to

  • Illustrate or map activities for an entire project.

  • Identify all the activities needed to complete the project on time.

  • Highlight activity sequences and relationships that are critical to successful project completion, and further, to illustrate the role of each activity within the entire project.

  • Specify details of the project in the planning phase for later analysis.

  • Support monitoring and controlling the project by providing a way to quickly identify deviations from the plan, the schedule, or (in some cases) the budget.

Network modeling software allows the user to create a chart of activities and events, estimate project costs, and display a schedule of activities for managing time and staffing needs. Depending on user preferences, most software can display the results as a chart, line diagram, or table.

Network models include, at a minimum, events, activities, times, network paths, and critical paths. A network path is a sequence of network activities from project inception to project end. A critical path is the most time-consuming chain of events in a network model. The critical path consists of the unique sequence of activities that, if delayed, would delay the entire project. Identifying the critical path is important because it highlights activities for which timely completion is crucial. A delay in an activity not on the critical path will not necessarily delay the project.

Steps for Finding a Project’s Critical Path:

  1. Divide the project elements into activities and events.
  2. Identify dependencies.
  3. Construct a network diagram to illustrate the required sequences of activities and events based on activity dependencies.
  4. Estimate the expected time for each activity, and enter the estimates onto the network diagram.
  5. Identify all alternative network paths from project start to finish.
  6. Calculate the sum of expected times for activities on each network path to determine the total expected time for completion of each alternative network path.
  7. Identify the network path with the longest expected time. This path is the critical path.

A network model can be used to identify the presence and amount of float in a project. Float, also known as slack time, is the length of time an activity can be delayed without delaying the entire project. Float helps with mistake-proofing project execution, such that if an activity is derailed, the work group has margin for getting it back on track.

No activity on the critical path can have float, but any other network activity can have float. To find the float in a network, subtract the expected time for each noncritical path from the expected time for the critical path. Project Schedules

A project manager can use information about baselines, activities, expected times, and networks to help establish comprehensive schedules, which provide information about the sequence and timing of events and how staff time should be allocated. A project schedule is a document that shows links between project elements, identifies dependencies, and shows a sequence that satisfies time and other constraints.

Some projects specify an early finish date and a late finish date, to allow for schedule variances. Generally, a schedule variance is the difference between the scheduled finish date for an activity and the actual finish date. Most members on a project team refer to the schedule to know when to complete activities. Figure 5.8 illustrates one type of project schedule.

 

Project Plan

A project plan is a comprehensive, formally executed document used to guide a project through closure. The primary uses of a project plan are to consolidate the information gathered throughout the initiation and planning phases and facilitate communication among stakeholders.

A project plan should document negotiated aspects of the project objectives, project performance standards relative to required outcomes, project schedule, project cost, and other resource requirements. A project plan is designed to be revised and updated as time passes and new information becomes available.

Keep in mind that projects differ in level of complexity and risk. The plan for a short, simple project can be informal. In this case, the plan could contain only a schedule and an executive summary, which is a concise overview of the project purpose. A more complex project plan might be a bound collection of related information and an appendix, with a table of contents like the one in Figure 5.9.

Project Execution

After authorization, team members can begin working on the project. The execution phase of project management consists of formally launching a project, communicating with team members, and progressing toward negotiated outcomes. The project team performs activities as scheduled during this phase.

An effective project team understands that issues will occur, that the planning process is ongoing, and that individual elements of a project will need adjustments over time. In other words, the project team has to remain agile to succeed.

As we mentioned in Chapter 4, agile management is an approach to business effectiveness focused on finding and seizing opportunities to improve operations. With agile project management, project components are analyzed, adjusted, and delivered as individual units. The ability to adjust along the way increases attention to, and improves the quality of, each project deliverable.

There is some controversy surrounding the feasibility of using an agile management style for complex projects. 12 However, as a testament to its name, a successful agile approach should be responsive to alterations based on level of complexity. In other words, even if the approach to a project is not purely agile, a project team can use agile thinking to help the project survive change.

EXAMPLE

Agile Approach

Ruby, the project manager at EndiFirst, updates the schedule based on her daily check-ins with team members. She can enter a change to an activity completion date, and the schedule will automatically update the total completion date according to the change made. Periodically, she revisits the total completion date in relation to project objectives. If needed, she negotiates adjustments to other activities to compensate for lost time.

 

Project Control

Because change is inevitable, project execution relies on project control. During project control, the project manager monitors progress and takes any corrective action in conjunction with members of the team. All aspects of a project are subject to project control.

To determine the status of activities, a project manager can conduct periodic meetings. A stand-up meeting is a meeting during which employees stand up as they offer summaries about their progress on an activity. The unintuitive feature of standing is intended to keep meetings short. (Not all companies use the term literally—in some cases, these status checks don’t require standing or are conducted virtually and still retain the name “stand-up meeting.”)

The project manager uses information about the status of activities to gauge project performance. Documents created in the planning phase serve as items for comparison. For example, the schedule can help a project manager compare expected dates to actual dates. Earned Value Management

A specific technique for identifying discrepancies between performance and plans is earned value management (EVM). Earned value management (EVM) involves using monetary values to express performance feedback. Companies with relatively elaborate project management activities are the most likely to use this approach.

An earned value is a monetary value assigned incrementally to all project steps, with the project earning another increment of monetary value as more work is completed. Thus, EVM involves expressing physical progress toward project completion in monetary units. EVM also involves applying monetary values when analyzing project timeliness and cost.

A project team can quickly grasp the summary aspects of feedback expressed in monetary units. Moreover, project managers and team members can gauge a current project’s performance by comparing its monetary values to those produced by past projects.

EXAMPLE

EVM Approach

On the basis of a project budget, one project activity has been assigned a total estimated value of $20,000. When that activity is 50% complete, the activity’s earned value is set at $10,000. In this example, the assigned $10,000 value at the given time is the activity’s earned value.

Earned value management provides a straightforward picture of project performance in terms of a critical metric: cost. However, EVM is a very limited measure of project performance and does not account for other features of a project. In addition, EVM often requires special analytic tools.

Whether a project uses EVM or traditional project plan information for project control, comparing the latest information to previously established information is key. If there are discrepancies between plans and performance, the project manager makes a plan to act.

 

Project Changes

A project change is an acknowledged deviation from the original plan. Changes to projects happen, especially early in execution. When a departure from plan indicates that project objectives could be compromised, the team must be prepared to adjust. A project manager will negotiate project changes with stakeholders and revise the schedule whenever significant new schedule-related information becomes available.

The following reasons are typical triggers for project changes:

  • A more important project arises and takes resources from the current project.

  • An activity takes longer to complete than the estimated time.

  • The project team encounters new information or unforeseen obstacles.

  • Performance indicators show lapses in the quality of work already completed.

  • A required resource does not arrive on time, or arrives on time but is defective.

A poorly defined project scope—possibly the result of a company’s inexperience with a given project—often leads to a high rate of change. Scope creep refers to gradually modifying features of a project and its objectives without formally addressing and negotiating changes with project stakeholders. When a project moves too far beyond its original plans, boundaries become difficult to maintain. A lack of defined boundaries makes it harder to achieve project goals.

EXAMPLE

Scope Creep

EndiFirst Insurance Company consults with Berryworth, Inc. for its advertising. Marco, an EndiFirst marketing manager, wants Berryworth to create a web advertising campaign that includes basic search engine marketing, video ads, and nonvideo display (banner) advertising. Marco speaks with Sarah, the project manager at Berryworth, and they agree on a schedule and budget. Sarah starts project initiation the following Monday.

Six weeks later, Marco calls Sarah to ask the team to add social media marketing to the campaign. The additional resources required by the social media marketing will change the schedule and the budget, but Sarah thinks the project can handle the change. She informs her team about the addition to the campaign.

A month before the deadline, development of video ads are in progress. One of the actors in the video has an emergency, and the team has to hire and train a new actor. That same week, Marco calls Sarah and lets her know that there has been a small change to the product offerings. He asks her to include the new information in the advertising. The project is already behind schedule due to the addition, but this change seems minor, so Sarah agrees.

With just one week before the deadline, video ads are still in development, and the team has only had time to incorporate product changes into the banner ads.

To minimize scope creep, changes in project objectives or constraints should be negotiated, approved, and reflected in the project plan documents. To reach agreement on a project change, the project manager and team work together to evaluate options and impacts on costs, scope, and schedule. Then the project manager negotiates accommodations with various stakeholders and gains formal approval for the change.

A project manager might employ any one or combination of the following strategies to address a necessary project change:

  • Reduce the time—and possibly the quality—of subsequent activities in the path, while recognizing that doing so will affect the baseline project scope. One method for this is fast tracking, where a project manager revisits the network’s critical path to decide if any sequential activities can be performed simultaneously.

  • Employ additional resources to get the project back on schedule while recognizing that doing so will increase costs above those specified in the baseline resources. This is a technique known as crashing.

  • Deliver only part of the project, a strategy that essentially narrows the project scope.

EXAMPLE

RayLite planned an outreach event to improve customer satisfaction. The initial plan was to invite several dozen customers to the branch to discuss how Raylite can better serve them. The team realized that some customers’ commutes would likely keep them from attending.

The project team revisited their idea and, with the sponsor’s approval, decided that the location head and a few team members would schedule personal phone calls with the 10 customers who expressed the most grievances over the past six months.

  • Accept the delay.

  • Cancel the project.

Project Closure

Project closure is the phase of the lifecycle that occurs after a project meets its objectives. Here, the project manager performs the administrative activities of winding down and ending a project. At this point, most team members have transitioned to other projects, leaving the project manager to wrap up.

During the closure phase, the project manager, perhaps with help from team members, documents project deliverables and presents the document to the project sponsor for her signature. The project manager also meets with the project team to debrief and record lessons learned.

EXAMPLE

Lessons Learned

While brainstorming methods for improving their self-service portal, EndiFirst used customer feedback from the survey and the group debriefing session that occurred after the usability tests. During analysis of the feedback, team members realized that many of the answers on the survey about the portal were similar and reflected conversations from the group debriefing session.

At project closure, team members decided that they could have received more useful responses by offering the survey prior to conducting the usability test with debrief session. Ruby documented this as a lesson learned.

Most companies have formal arrangements for quality management and continuous improvement in their projects. For instance, companies might set consistent project documentation standards for all projects, and provide a mechanism for sharing best practices. Companies typically maintain a repository of lessons learned to benefit successive project teams. A new project team may be assigned to read and discuss reports from past projects to help the next project undertaking go smoother than the last.
