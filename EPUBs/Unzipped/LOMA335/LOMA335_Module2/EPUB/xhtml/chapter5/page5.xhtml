Project Portfolio Management

Today’s increased regulatory scrutiny, higher customer expectations, and global operations make a failed project an even riskier investment than in the past. One method for collectively managing projects in a way that supports optimal business outcomes is project portfolio management.

Project portfolio management (PPM) consists of treating all company projects as a project portfolio and coordinating all projects with one another across the company. In a PPM approach, a committee analyzes a project for both its intrinsic benefits to the organization and for its relative fit with the organization’s existing resources, processes, and projects.

The objective of PPM is to create a viable project mix. For example, although one or more projects might exceed their individual budgets, if the entire project mix is over budget, the company should find ways to decrease project expenditures. Portfolio project management often involves a committee that reviews both completed and proposed projects to make sure the company is not taking on more risk than it can handle.

EXAMPLE

EndiFirst Insurance Company has a steering committee, which is a group of stakeholders with diverse company backgrounds who guide and oversee the planning and implementation of a project. EndiFirst’s steering committee meets monthly to consider new projects and review ongoing projects in light of organizational goals and strategies. They then offer recommendations to improve the value of an organization’s projects.

EndiFirst’s steering committee is a standing committee, or a permanent committee that company executives use as a continuing source of advice.

The PPM approach can call for changes to or even the cancellation of an ongoing project. The PPM approach advocates ending an ongoing project before its completion if, during implementation, circumstances change so that the project becomes less attractive than it once seemed. Under the PPM approach, a project could be ended before its completion if expected benefits do not seem to outweigh expected costs, perhaps because

  • Project cost overruns make the project more costly than was estimated

  • Project outcomes are less beneficial to the company than anticipated

  • Business objectives have changed, and the project’s objectives are less closely aligned to new business objectives than they were to old business objectives

  • Regulatory developments require allocation of resources to new projects designed to meet new mandates

Project portfolio management is one method for ensuring that business deliverables offer the most possible value to a company and its customers. We discuss other methods in later chapters.
