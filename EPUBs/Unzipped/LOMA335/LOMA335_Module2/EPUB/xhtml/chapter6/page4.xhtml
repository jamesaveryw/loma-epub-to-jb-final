The Quality Control Cycle

To improve on a specific product or service, an organization needs to have an established cycle of quality control activities. A quality control cycle is a feedback loop of quality management—similar to the feedback loop of any other operations management cycle—in which management makes a plan to achieve a goal, takes action toward achieving the goal, compares actual performance to the goal, and then adjusts the plan to reflect any mismatches discovered during the implementation process.

At the very least, a quality control cycle consists of quality planning, quality monitoring, quality analysis, and quality improvement, as shown in Figure 6.2. In other words, a company must set standards for its products and services; deliver, track, and evaluate results; and take actions to correct identified defects. Companies should incorporate as many of the principles of quality management as possible into every decision they make toward quality improvement.

 

Quality Planning

In this context, quality planning refers to establishing the concepts, activities, and tools that employees can use to keep the company operating on a stable basis and meeting customer needs. Specific activities of quality planning include undertaking research to identify customers and their needs, planning for outcomes that will meet customers’ needs, assigning authority, and designing process controls that will measure success in meeting customers’ needs.

 

Identifying Customer Needs

Insurers can analyze existing business data or gather information from surveys to draw conclusions about customer needs. Additionally, every interaction between a customer and a company offers an opportunity for the company to increase its value to the customer. Insurers can compile the information gathered during interactions into profiles called customer personas. They can use the information in the customer personas to identify preferred communication channels, current and future product needs, and the causes of customer complaints. A Structure for Accountability and Authority

Quality assurance refers to the activities a company undertakes to make certain the company delivers satisfactory products and services to customers. Everyone in a company has some role in quality assurance. However, to make sure no activities are overlooked, companies assign certain employees or managers to support, monitor, and report quality for a particular area. These roles can be temporary or ongoing.

One way to establish accountability is by using quality circles. A quality circle (QC)is a problem-solving group of five to 10 employees who meet regularly to discuss quality improvement, suggest ways to reduce costs, and present recommendations to management. QCs might periodically review grievances from stakeholders and work to develop interventions.

The Six Sigma teams for process improvement mentioned in Chapter 4 consist of designated members with specific continuous improvement expertise. By contrast, QCs are often temporary and voluntary. QCs make decisions by consensus, which is an approach that requires all group members to understand and accept a solution before jointly choosing a course of action. In a QC consensus process, group members have the opportunity to negotiate which quality improvement activities to implement.

 

Establishing Quality Measures

To evaluate performance on a given activity, companies need to determine what constitutes acceptable performance on that activity. Insurers often use benchmarks to establish measures for quality. Remember that a benchmark is a performance standard that a company aspires to achieve.

EXAMPLE

Benchmark

Jerri, the customer experience manager at EndiFirst, gathered online information about average wait times for email responses. She supplemented this information with validated, public industry data from a benchmarking study of several comparable companies with similar products and markets.

After averaging these companies’ email turnaround times, she found that EndiFirst representatives respond four hours sooner than average. Jerri concluded that customer service representatives are doing a good job prioritizing requests made through email.

Alternatively, companies can opt for qualitative measures, such as informally monitoring customer responses to customer service interactions. Companies can also use formal statistical analysis—which we will address in the next module—to determine acceptable performance standards.

 

Quality Monitoring

Quality monitoring primarily consists of documenting occurrences and comparing them to the standards established during planning to identify exceptions. Most companies today automate monitoring activities whenever possible. First, software measures the work for some attribute of work quality. Then, the system checks the measure for consistency with a programmed quality standard. If the system finds a discrepancy, it alerts the user, who can either address the feasibility of the quality standards or correct the cause of the defects.

Often, the staff assigned to the area needs to refer the issue to someone in a more senior position. In most quality monitoring systems, each subsequent level up in the company hierarchy receives increasingly summarized and filtered reporting about controls and violations of standards. Within a quality control cycle, the role of executives is to review the violations and devise a plan to address them.

 

Performance Dashboards

A common automated system for quality monitoring is a performance dashboard. A performance dashboard is a type of software that graphically displays the status of a company’s most important performance indicators. A dashboard can show real-time values for performance indicators or it can compare actual performance on indicators to performance standards. Performance indicators typically found on a performance dashboard for an insurance company include

  • Business process metrics, such as premiums processed daily, claims paid daily, customer service response time, and underwriting response time

  • Financial metrics, such as cash on hand, economic capital, and uncommitted capital

  • New business metrics, such as year-to-date sales and new product sales as a percentage of new business

Some dashboards contain stoplight indicators to show the status of certain metrics. Stoplight indicators are red, yellow (or amber), green, and sometimes blue symbols that indicate how favorably the company’s actual performance compares with its performance standards. A dashboard displays indicators when a measure reaches critical limits. Managers can use these indicators to make decisions about how, or whether, to proceed with certain processes. Generally, stoplight colors have the following meanings:

  • Green means that the relevant area of performance is within an acceptable range

  • Yellow (or amber) means that the relevant area of performance is below goals or standards

  • Red means that performance needs urgent correction

  • Blue, in most cases, means that available information is not relevant to a particular indicator

You can see an example of a dashboard graphic with stoplight indicators in Figure 6.4.

Dashboards are convenient because they can show information in an easily digestible format in real time. Companies can make dashboards available to managers at different organizational levels using cascading reports. Cascading reportsare electronic reports that bring together related data sets and offer distinctive data views suitable for various user needs.

In a set of linked cascading reports, a user can choose to see summary data or detailed data using a drill-through option. A drill-through option is a link that the user of a summary report can click to see increased detail of the information. Drill-through options in dashboards link a user to reports known as parent and child reports. In this context, a parent report is a master or summary report, whereas a child report is a detailed report that supports the master report. A drill-through option allows a user to drill down or drill up.

  • A drill-down option enables linking from a parent report down to a child report.

  • A drill-up option enables linking from a child report up to a parent report.

Service Dashboards

In a service dashboard, the indicator next to the top summary is a green light indicating that the expected level of service is being achieved, or another color when the expected service level is not being achieved.

Suppose the target level of service is to complete 80% of customer service transactions within five days. As long as the actual service level is at least 80%, the light remains green. If the actual service level drops below 80%, the indicator color turns to yellow. Below a specified lower level of tolerance (for example, 50%), the indicator color turns red, suggesting an immediate need for corrective action.

Some dashboards include control charts. A control chart is a chart showing a plot of data observations about a given process against a measure of time. Insurers often use control charts as a way to visually identify performance items that fall outside of a standard quality range. Figure 6.5 depicts the usual form of a control chart. This chart shows observations for the average customer hold time during 18 observed shifts.

In a control chart, the threshold values are the control limits. A control chart has two potential control limits, set by the user based on target performance. The upper control limit is the highest acceptable value for an observation within a normal range. The lower control limit is the lowest acceptable value within a normal range.

For some observations, such as the number of insureds, both upper and lower control limits are relevant. For other observations, such as the level of a cash account, the lower control limit is more important than the upper control limit. For many purposes, only one control limit is significant.

EXAMPLE

Stefan, a manager at EndiFirst, uses a control chart to monitor the range of first-contact resolutions per day. In this chart, the normal range for first-contact resolutions is between 78% and 96%. The control chart should alert Stefan if results are higher or lower than the control limits. A result greater than 96% is good news and does not indicate a problem. A result lower than 78% indicates a problem that requires attention.

Quality Management Software

In addition to dashboards, insurers can use other types of quality management software to monitor the quality of their products and services. Some software programs offer integrated suites for monitoring, performing complex analysis, and correcting quality concerns. The disadvantage of using this type of quality management software is that it does not account for extenuating circumstances that affect performance, such as a flu virus that limits the number of employees available to handle a particular activity.

 

Quality Analysis

Insurers analyze new and existing processes to evaluate the quality of their offerings. Quality analysis is any type of research or investigation to understand an aspect of preventing defects or meeting customer needs.

EXAMPLE

After redesigning one of its claims forms, EndiFirst experienced a sharp increase in NIGO rates. NIGO rates measure the percentage share of all incoming paperwork received with incomplete documentation. A quality circle examined the new claim form and found the following:

  • The form had been shortened to only one page and used a smaller type size than the previous claim form.
  • The form was available in only one language, although many of EndiFirst’s customers speak other languages.
  • New instructions were unclear because of terminology or jargon.
  • Limited space on the form made it difficult for claimants to provide all of the requested information.

Insurers can use a variety of techniques to analyze the quality of their administrative functions. Let’s consider a few traditional approaches to quality analysis.

 

PDCA, DMAIC, and DMADV

In Chapter 4, you learned how insurers use root-cause analysis to analyze and resolve problems that affect the quality of company services and operations. Here, we introduce three additional approaches: PDCA, DMAIC, and DMADV. In many cases, root-cause analysis happens at the analyzing phase of DMAIC and DMADV approaches.

A simple approach for identifying and resolving quality problems is the Plan-Do-Check-Act cycle. 6 The Plan-Do-Check-Act (PDCA) cycleis a four-step control cycle technique for supporting quality improvement. The example below shows how EndiFirst could use PDCA to resolve its problem with claim forms.

EXAMPLE

EndiFirst Claim Forms and PDCA

To address problems with its claim forms, EndiFirst should

Plan to make changes:

  • Analyze recurring form errors
  • Recruit a development team or QC
  • Brainstorm to identify possible changes

Do changes on a small scale:

  • Create a prototype form with changes
  • Test the prototype form with a small group of volunteers

Check to see if changes are working:

  • Gather feedback from volunteers
  • Review completed prototype forms for errors
  • Address feedback and errors
  • Revise the form as needed

Act to implement the changes:

  • Develop and introduce the new form
  • Implement the electronic equivalent
  • Monitor new form completion
  • Document patterns that indicate areas for improvement
  • Repeat cycle as needed

 

DMAIC is a five-step approach to solving a problem with the goal of improving an existing business process. The steps of DMAIC are

  • Defining processes that need improvement

  • Measuring the performance of defined processes

  • Analyzing the performance of defined processes and other data to determine the root cause of the problem

  • Improving defined processes and eliminating errors

  • Controlling, or monitoring, defined processes to verify performance and prevent future problems

DMADV is a five-step approach to solving a problem with the goal of creating a new business process. The steps of DMADV are

  • Designing strategies to ensure total customer satisfaction

  • Measuring and identifying parameters for quality

  • Analyzing and developing high-level process alternatives that will result in improved quality

  • Designing detailed new processes to support goals and strategies

  • Verifying identified processes and implementing those processes

The example below shows how DMAIC could be used to resolve EndiFirst’s trouble with incomplete claims forms.

EXAMPLE

EndiFirst Claim Forms and DMAIC

EndiFirst can use the five steps in the DMAIC process to get to the root cause of the incomplete claim forms:

  • Define: Incoming claim forms are not completed correctly.
  • Measure: Compare the current error rates to past error rates.
  • Analyze: Review the form.

    Identify which sections of the claim form aren’t being completed correctly.

    Determine what aspect of the claim form is creating problems (i.e., the root cause of the problem).

    Consider alternatives, such as amended instructions and online claim filing.

  • Improve: Implement the necessary changes in the paper form and create an electronic equivalent.
  • Control: Monitor error rates on incoming claims using new forms.

Most of the time, the measure and analyze steps involve extensive statistical analysis. As a result, DMAIC can be time-consuming, and is usually only useful for large-scale problems.

 

Pareto Principle/Chart/Analysis

Pareto analysis follows a guideline known as the Pareto principle. The Pareto principlestates that a small percentage of causes contributes to a large percentage of the results. A similar guideline, the 80-20 principle, expands the Pareto principle to other situations and states that, in any given situation, 20 percent of the components of a situation account for 80 percent of the outcomes. The primary message of both of these principles is that not all factors in a situation contribute equally to an outcome. As a result, when faced with a problem, a person or company should focus on the causes that contribute most to the problem. Resolving the most significant contributing cause or causes will produce a reasonably effective, if not perfect, solution.

The graphical construct for applying Pareto analysis is called a Pareto chart. A basic Pareto chart is a bar chart that depicts how much each factor contributes to a specified defect, with all sources arranged in descending order from left to right. Some Pareto charts also describe the cost of defects.

Fishbone Diagram

A fishbone diagram, also called an Ishikawa diagram, is a graphical tool used for organizing potential causes for a problem and sorting the causes into defined categories. The primary benefit of the diagram is that it identifies problems as well as potential causes of those problems.

A fishbone diagram takes the form of a central horizontal line with diagonal lines, or “fish bones,” extending upward and downward from the horizontal line, and with a “fish head” at the right end. The fish head in the diagram is labeled with a name for the problem to be analyzed. The fish bones that extend diagonally behind the fish head represent potential causes of the problem, presented either in chronological order or in some other order logically suggested by the nature of the problem.

Each fish bone is labeled with a potential cause. For example, in a chronologically arranged problem, the most recent potential cause is the label closest to the fish head and the earliest cause of the problem is closest to the fish tail. Another way to arrange causes is by degree of challenge posed, with the easiest problems to fix nearest the head.

A fishbone diagram can offer a focal point for quality circle meetings. The structure helps employees remain focused on the causes of problems (for example, not enough space available) rather than the symptoms (for example, incomplete responses). As a result, teams are more likely to brainstorm solutions that prevent the problem from occurring again.

 

Quality Improvement

The final step in most control processes is quality improvement, which involves correcting the defects and taking steps to prevent them from recurring. In this step, a company acts on its findings and incorporates lessons learned. Specific activities within the quality improvement phase of quality management include (1) performing further research as needed, (2) establishing a project to design and implement a specified quality improvement, and (3) designing or programming controls for the new improvements.
