What Is Quality?

One way to define the quality of a product or service is “fitness for use.” 1 This means that every product or service must have few failures and have the right features to satisfy customer needs. When it comes to insurance, the customer’s greatest need often occurs at the time of a loss. Within this context, the customer associates the quality of the product with the experts who help the customer navigate the consequences of a loss.

As we describe quality management, keep in mind that a positive customer journey—the path each customer travels with a company from beginning to end, including every interaction across every point of contact—is the ultimate quality goal for an insurer. The following insight offers some examples of ways that insurance companies deliver quality to customers.

INSIGHT

Attributes of Quality Service in Insurance Companies

  • Short wait times
  • First-contact resolution
  • Easy-to-use websites and apps
  • Easy-to-reach customer service
  • Low customer effort with minimal pain points
  • Consistent service levels
  • Consistent information
  • Straightforward application and claims processes
  • Flexibility in handling unexpected needs
  • Clarity of explanations
  • Accuracy of explanations
  • Courtesy in conversation
  • Empathy in conversation

Customers expect insurers to demonstrate the attributes in the insight above. Therefore, having these attributes does not differentiate one company from the next. A company that delivers quality service meets the customer’s expectation. On the other hand, a lack of quality will make a company stand out, and often cause a company to lose customers. As a result, most successful companies adopt a system for quality management early on and then refine the system continuously.

Quality management—sometimes called performance management—refers to the activities an organization conducts on an ongoing basis to plan, control, measure, and improve its performance to ensure that its products or services consistently meet or exceed customer expectations.

 

Quality Management Principles

Although the methods undertaken to manage quality are unique to each company, some standards can offer guidance. The International Organization for Standardization (ISO) is a global federation composed of representatives from standards-setting organizations all over the world. 2 Founded in the 1940s, the organization publishes a variety of international standards to help companies improve the quality of their products and services.

Developed by the ISO, the ISO 9000 is the largest standardized collection of quality management fundamentals, designed to help companies meet customer needs and follow regulatory requirements related to products and services. 3 The ISO 9000 bases its standards on seven principles of quality management, applicable to any organization regardless of size or industry.

The seven principles of quality management include

  • Customer focus. Quality is about (1) meeting customer needs and (2) aiming to exceed customer expectations in all company interactions. If a product or service doesn’t match the customer’s perception of quality, it will not satisfy the customer, even if it satisfies the company’s quality metrics. To determine how customers perceive quality, companies should regularly monitor customer satisfaction through surveys and other feedback tools. Companies can also perform analytics to understand current needs, anticipate future needs, and communicate those needs to employees.

  • Leadership. Effective leaders use their people skills to establish a clear vision and inspire a company-wide commitment to quality. Effective leaders also ensure that employees have the training and resources necessary for them to deliver their best work.

  • Engagement of employees. Engaged employees produce higher-quality work than disengaged employees. In Chapter 3, we defined employee engagement as an individual’s involvement with, satisfaction with, and enthusiasm for the work he does. Employees who feel respected, recognized, and included are more satisfied with their employment than those who do not. When employees feel their values align with those of their organization, they are more likely to display engagement. Similarly, the more employees feel that the work they do directly contributes to the success of the organization, the more empowered they feel to perform well and communicate new ideas.

  • A process approach. Processes that are easy to understand and follow lead to predictable results with few quality errors. When process steps are clear, companies can manage risks by addressing limitations and interdependencies before they become problems. Clear processes also allow companies to quickly identify breakdowns and improve efficiency. Ideally, companies should automate processes whenever possible.

  • Improvement. Quality is unsustainable without continuous improvement because a company’s internal and external environments change constantly. For example, a new regulation or a new technology can influence quality positively or negatively. As a result, quality-focused companies assess opportunities and threats regularly. They also reward innovative solutions.

  • Evidence-based decision-making. An organization that delivers quality products and services anticipates uncertainty. Companies can maintain a culture of preparedness by making decisions based on accurate and reliable data. A data-driven approach allows companies to evaluate the effectiveness of past decisions, monitor key indicators, and make predictions about the future. By training employees to understand the data relevant to their roles, companies empower employees at all levels to review, challenge, and contribute to decision making. You’ll learn more about decision making in the next module.

  • Relationship management. Companies have to know who their stakeholders are to establish strong stakeholder relationships. In addition to creating a positive reputation, strong stakeholder relationships facilitate the sharing of information, resources, and expertise, which are important to improving the quality of products and services. They also create more opportunities for companies to respond to needs, recognize and encourage contributions, and develop a sense of evolving values and perceptions within the industry. 4
