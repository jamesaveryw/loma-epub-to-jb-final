Business Process Reengineering

Sometimes companies might need to discard existing business processes and develop better ones. To transform operations or avoid recurring problems, or when the survival of the company depends on fundamental change, companies can use business process reengineering. Business process reengineering (BPR) is a formal program for fundamentally redesigning business processes, structures, and software with the goal of substantially improving cost, quality, service levels, and speed. For reengineering to be justified, it should lead to significantly better, faster, or less expensive operations.

To reengineer a business process, decision makers begin with an “as is” analysis. During this analysis, they identify which processes to discontinue and which, if any, to maintain. Then decision makers follow a series of steps similar to the steps of process improvement.

The difference between continuous process improvement and BPR is a matter of scope. Continuous process improvement involves incremental change, whereas BPR advocates major—if not total—reinvention. 6 Instead of working with an existing process and finding ways to make it better, BPR eliminates the existing process and begins anew. In some cases, companies completely reorganize departments into cross-functional teams, rethink company values, reassign responsibilities, and develop new information systems.

BPR involves greater operational risk than does continuous process improvement. Therefore, implementation of the new design should consist of significant analysis, simulation, prototyping, and training. After implementation, team members review the process by monitoring benchmarks and KPIs. Employee and customer attitudes toward the new design are prime indicators of performance.

EXAMPLE

Raylite Insurance Company’s new business numbers were in decline for almost 10 years. The insurer decided to address growing customer complaints about its “old-fashioned” approach to policy issue and maintenance.

Raylite reimagined its business to focus on increasing access to and ease of application. The company organized the ability to offer nonmedical underwriting directly through its website, a process in which examination-based proof of medical insurability is not required from a proposed insured. Instead of medical evaluations, Raylite would use the application, phone interviews with customers, and external sources such as online medical records to make its underwriting decisions.

After extensive preparation, the process team rolled out plans for reengineering. The company would develop a new website, place a new emphasis upon driving traffic to its website, and implement trainings on improving customer experience. The company would also invest in an intelligent underwriting system, which is software that performs underwriting steps based on preprogrammed rules and parses language to assess risk automatically.

The result of the reengineering initiative was greater traffic, fewer underwriting mistakes, and more time for the company to focus on improving business processes. The initiative also increased the number of completed applications. Survey results indicated that the new process improved employee morale.

Over time, as revenue increased and the need for manual labor decreased, Raylite realized substantial cost savings. The savings allowed the company to devote additional resources toward enhancing the customer experience even further.

An effective business process reengineering undertaking

  • Considers the customer first

  • Aims to increase the speed of an activity

  • Aims to reduce the cost of an activity

  • Eliminates processes known to produce a high rate of errors

  • Consolidates similar activities into one process, in part by eliminating handoffs

  • Acknowledges and, if possible, accommodates the preferences of employees and process team members

  • Designs with the awareness that humans are fallible and a process will not always conform to the plan, an approach called mistake-proofing (also known as fail-safe design)

  • Implements new processes only after thorough testing

  • Offers training on new processes as necessary

  • Automates processes to the extent possible

The goal of business process reengineering is to substantially improve cost, quality, service levels, and speed by redesigning business processes, structures, and software.
