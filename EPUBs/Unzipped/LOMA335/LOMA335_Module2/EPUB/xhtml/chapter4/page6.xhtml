Process Automation

For peak efficiency, companies automate processes as much as they can. Information technology (IT)is the use of computer systems to gather, record, manage, analyze, and transmit company information. Automation helps to reduce the costs associated with slow response times, excess paperwork, document storage, and reporting errors. The result is higher-quality customer service, greater access to information, and increased opportunities to evaluate and improve company performance.

 

Cybersecurity

Companies today might not have to buy as much IT infrastructure as in the past because they can store their information in the cloud—a virtual data storage system that maintains digital data on multiple connected servers owned and managed by a hosting company. This advantage has a caveat, however: companies must still be able to secure their data. For greater control and visibility, many insurance companies still keep much of their information in-house.

Cyber breaches damage an insurance company’s reputation. Moreover, laws in most jurisdictions require insurance companies to take action to prevent the loss or corruption of customers’ nonpublic personal information. Most insurance companies are also required to protect proprietary information about the company itself. The insight below describes some regulations that apply to the insurance industry.

INSIGHT

The Regulation of Nonpublic Personal Information

At the federal level, the Gramm-Leach-Bliley (GLB) Act requires financial services companies operating in the United States to protect the security and confidentiality of customers’ nonpublic personal information.

At the state level, the National Association of Insurance Commissioners’ (NAIC’s) Model Privacy Act establishes standards that insurers operating in the United States must follow when collecting, using, and disclosing personal information related to insurance transactions.

The Health Information Technology for Electronic and Clinical Health (HITECH) Act, signed into law in 2009, addresses privacy and security issues associated with the electronic transmission of protected health information.

Similar regulations exist in other countries. For example, the Canadian provinces of Quebec, British Columbia, and Alberta have laws regulating how private-sector organizations can collect, use, and disclose personal information. Other Canadian provinces are governed by similar federal requirements in the Personal Information Protection and Electronic Documents Act (PIPEDA).

Methods for controlling against security risks include the use of

  • Intranets, which are private networks accessible only to people within an organization

  • Extranets, which are limited-access networks that allow people within the organization, and select external stakeholders, to access company information

  • Virtual private networks, which are systems that provide private access to authorized users who are not on location

  • Firewalls, which are system components designed to prevent unauthorized access to a network

  • Intrusion detection software, which is technology that monitors system traffic and alerts administrators to potential security issues

Companies can also require administrative sign-offs on system changes or implement security awareness programs to help minimize risks. United States law requires companies to have critically tested disaster recovery plans in place. 7 Disaster recovery plans mitigate risk in the case of uncontrollable circumstances such as extended power outages.

 

Integrated Business Process Management

To support management and automation, most companies use business process management systems. Although systems differ somewhat, they typically provide process modeling tools for analysis; developer tools for system integration; dashboards for measuring, monitoring, and control; and user interaction to facilitate work.

Business process management systems typically rely on rules engines . Rules enginesare software-based repositories of key business process rules that can be consistently applied in specified circumstances to produce the desired results. These programmed rules are the basis for automation. Rules engines require frequent review by IT based on business experience, feedback, and new findings. The insight below provides examples of business process management systems and their functions.

INSIGHT

Types of Business Process Management Systems

Customer experience management systems manage all aspects of company interactions with and knowledge of each customer. Each interaction with a customer generates data—on demographics, behavior, satisfaction level, and more—that insurers can compile into profiles used to build long-term relationships, evaluate risk, and identify product needs.

Automated workflow systems support business process control by coordinating the flow of documents between process steps. These systems automatically prioritize, schedule, distribute, trigger, and even complete certain process steps.

Queueing management systems model and analyze the work processes affected by waiting lines and establish rules for automatically routing items to service destinations. Insurers often use the information they gather from queueing management systems to measure the impact of volume changes on turnaround times or staffing needs.

Financial management systems keep track of income, expenses, and assets. They coordinate invoices, payments due and processed, and the security of payment information. They also provide decision makers with real-time, comprehensive access to financial information.

Revenue management systems apply models and measurements to predict how certain business decisions or market trends will affect company revenue.

Knowledge management systems gather, evaluate, and share a company’s information assets. They combine all of a company’s information resources and make that accumulated knowledge available to decision makers. Many insurers use knowledge management systems to give customer service representatives the information they need to answer customer questions.

Document management systems manage electronic documents by centralizing and organizing document storage, controlling access to documents, automating routines for archiving, and ensuring regulatory compliance with records retention and privacy requirement.

Content management systems manage and store electronic content, including graphics and videos.

 

Artificial Intelligence

We can expect technology use in the insurance industry to increase, fueling customers’ and companies’ expectations for self-service, mobile access, and personalization. 8 To remain ahead of disruption from insurance companies as well as other participants moving into the insurance market, insurers should keep up with digital trends.

Artificial intelligence, introduced in Chapter 1, refers to the growing area of computer science that emphasizes the development of intelligent machines that work, learn, and react like humans. AI involves machine learning, which allows computers to identify patterns without being expressly told where or how to find them. AI has important implications for business process improvement in the insurance industry. For example, computers today can learn to categorize the severity of vehicle damage after an accident to help evaluate car insurance claims. 9

AI can also make better use of data by analyzing the same data in different ways across business units. From customer experience management system data, an analyst can draw conclusions about both contact center initiatives and the company’s IT capabilities. Advanced AI can figure out and carry out both of these uses at once, allowing insurers more time for value-adding activities. The following insight summarizes some of the growing uses of AI in the insurance industry.

INSIGHT

Uses of AI in the Insurance Industry

Insurance companies can use AI to

  • Facilitate new product design through a reduced need for manual labor
  • Identify multiple uses for the same data
  • Find and address process errors before customers do
  • Offer personalized self-service options to streamline customer experience
  • Perform intelligent and nonmedical underwriting
  • Monitor behavior patterns to detect and prevent fraud
  • Recommend coaching steps for agents based on sales performance
  • Model life events to recommend products to customers
  • Acquire data from the internet of things (wherein everyday items such as cars access the internet) to enhance the data that guides process design
  • Communicate with customers as chatbots that transfer issues to humans after reaching their capacity to help

Much of today’s automation involves some element of AI. However, business processes in the near future are likely to involve even more, making efficient and effective processes easier to achieve.
