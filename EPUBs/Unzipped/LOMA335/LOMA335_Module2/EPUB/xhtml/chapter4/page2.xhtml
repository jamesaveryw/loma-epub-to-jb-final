In Module 1, you learned how insurance companies strategize to achieve goals and deliver value to customers. In effect, companies deliver value through sets of processes designed to achieve corporate goals. Business process excellence requires management to understand how current processes work and how they affect customers. Consider how the business process described in this example affects this insurance customer:

EXAMPLE

Maya, a new EndiFirst customer, logged into EndiFirst’s self-service portal to make a premium payment. After entering her payment information, Maya clicked “confirm” and received a message that the system was currently unavailable. The message instructed her to call customer service to complete her payment.

When she called customer service, she was prompted to press 1 for questions about an existing policy, 2 to make a payment, 3 to check on the status of an application, or 4 for other questions. Maya selected 2. After waiting several minutes, she was directed to a representative. The representative, who misunderstood Maya’s desire to “complete her payment” as a request to “make a payment,” gathered her information and processed the payment.

Later, Maya received two separate emails confirming pending payments that would post within four to five business days. She went online to try to cancel the duplicate pending payment, and the system instructed her to call customer service to cancel the payment.

Maya thought going online would be more convenient. Instead, the process was time-consuming and frustrating. Companies can avoid occurrences like Maya’s by continuously monitoring business processes to ensure they work well.

Successful business processes are effective and efficient for the company and its customers. Effectiveness means completing the activities necessary to achieve the desired outcome. Efficiency means completing an activity with minimal waste of human or financial resources. In simple terms, effectiveness is getting the right things done. Efficiency is getting things done right. A process should also be accurate, meaning free from errors.

Additionally, successful processes are adaptable and scalable. An adaptable process remains efficient and effective as the environment changes. A scalable process can adjust to changes in volume without losing efficiency or effectiveness. (In general, an adaptable process is inherently scalable. However, scalable processes aren’t inherently adaptable. For example, a new business process might work regardless of the volume of incoming applications, but may not survive a change to new business software.)

All work involves processes, and understanding them is a necessary precursor to any business decision. In this chapter, you’ll learn how companies design and manage processes, implement programs to improve processes, and use technology to streamline processes and achieve continued success.
