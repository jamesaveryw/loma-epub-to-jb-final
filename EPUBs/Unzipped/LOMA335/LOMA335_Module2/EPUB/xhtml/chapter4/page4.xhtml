Steps of Process Improvement

Process management is a formal method of defining, documenting, evaluating, and improving resources and workflow so that processes better achieve the tasks they were designed to accomplish. EndiFirst, the insurer from our example, could have made Maya’s customer journey much better by envisioning what a good journey would look like and then creating or improving processes to make that journey a reality.

Defining a Process

A company should establish parameters before improving a process. First, companies should identify the key process in need of improvement. A key processis one of several operational processes critical to meeting customer needs, maintaining a competitive position, or implementing the corporate plan. Issuing policies, for example, is a key process in life insurance companies. When a company can focus its key processes on meeting customer needs, such as by making the premium payment process faster and simpler for customers, it is easier for the company to respond to changes in those needs.

As part of defining the process, employees involved in the process develop a process charter. A process charter establishes the authority and accountability, or process ownership, of a process team. The charter usually contains the following information: 1

  • A scope statement that defines the process, identifies its beginning and end points, and addresses existing resource constraints

  • A business case that identifies why the process needs to be implemented or changed at this particular point in time

  • An estimate of the process costs

  • A goal statement that identifies the measurable outcomes of this process initiative

  • A plan that identifies milestone activities and offers a tentative time frame for completion

  • A list of participants with assigned roles—such as process sponsor (typically a company executive who authorizes a process design), working process owner (an employee responsible for the direction of the process but who does not perform duties related to the process), and process team members (all employees assembled to accomplish a process)

  • An explanation of the researched needs of process stakeholders, who are any internal and external parties expecting to benefit from process outcomes but who do not directly participate in performing the process, such as a customer who gains a new service

 

Process Mapping

A process charter may contain or allude to a process map, which is a graphical representation of a business process, typically using a recognized set of graphical symbols. In the case of improving the premium payment process, EndiFirst might map out its current payment processing system like the example in Figure 4.1.

This example is simplified. However, more complex flow charts—which we discuss in Chapter 5—are also popular tools for process mapping.

 

Process Activities

Businesses usually map processes down to the activity level. Within a process, an activity is any part of a set of actions that generates work to accomplish a job, problem, or assignment. Examples of activities within a life insurance company include processing a policy application, sending a premium due notice, and investigating claims.

A value-adding activity makes the product or service more valuable to the customer, such as customer service or claims check issuing. A non-value-adding activity, on the other hand, does not make the product or service more valuable to the customer. Costs associated with unnecessary steps in processing claims, time spent taking inventory, and energy expended moving materials from one place to another are examples of non-value-adding activities.

To minimize non-value-adding activities, most companies automate some of the tasks within an activity. Tasks are the smallest units in a process to undergo analysis. Some processes can be mapped to the task level for greater specificity.

EXAMPLE

Self-service is an important way to streamline activities. Instead of waiting for an available human, customers can request information through technology. Instead of spending time answering basic questions, employees can devote time to value-adding activities such as product improvement.

If self-service processes require substantial human intervention or extra steps, however, then those processes lose value to customers.

Process Analysis and Control

An insurance company could start its process improvement initiative with this question: How can we increase value to our customers? Then the company should evaluate the existing process to see how it aligns with the company’s goals.

 

Analyzing Processes

Process analysis is a systemic evaluation of all aspects of a process to make the process faster, more efficient, less expensive, and more focused on the customer. Given the variety of techniques for process analysis, process teams should identify the technique most likely to help them achieve their goals.

Thorough analysis of a business process requires, at least, a process map to determine which activities are impeding success. Companies can also use swim lane diagrams, which elaborate on simple process maps by identifying the parties responsible for each activity. Additionally, companies can use value stream maps, which are visual representations of processes that identify each activity’s end value to the customer.

Value stream maps help identify the parts of a process that are not adding value to the customer, or that are slowing down the continuous flow process of value creation known as a value stream. People can use value streams to study how materials and information flow through a current process. A value stream is different from a value chain, in part because a value chain focuses on organizational functions, whereas a value stream usually focuses on cross-functional processes.

Process analysis requires evaluating processes to determine the effectiveness of existing resources (called resource analysis) and the efficiency of workflow (called workflow analysis). Managers can use the results of a process analysis to control ongoing processes, identify training opportunities, or make decisions about whether to keep the process.

 

Controlling Processes

After analyzing a process for deficiencies, decision makers should seek to control those deficiencies by measuring performance in line with specific goals. Benchmarks and key performance indicators can serve as measurements of how well a process is working.

Benchmarking is a formal program for measuring company performance results, identifying best practices for the same performance areas, and emulating best practices for company processes. A benchmark is a performance standard that a company aspires to achieve. To identify appropriate benchmarks, companies often look at industry benchmarks—high performance standards achieved by other companies in their same industry—or at universal benchmarks—positive results achieved by another company performing the same process, regardless of industry. Companies can also use previous internal results as benchmarks. Managers should review benchmarks at periodic intervals and make updates that reflect changing needs.

EXAMPLE

Analysts at Raylite Insurance Company gathered information about the time required for underwriting and policy issue. They supplemented this information with validated, public industry data from a benchmarking study of several comparable companies with similar products and markets.

After averaging these companies’ new business turnaround times, the analysts found that they were exceeding the turnaround time benchmarks by an average of two weeks. The employees concluded that the company takes longer than necessary to issue policies, and should look for ways to streamline the new business process.

Recall that a key performance indicator (KPI) is a measure of a key process outcome. Executive-level managers use KPIs as an indication of company performance relative to a strategic goal. KPIs help companies identify gaps between what customers need and what the company offers.

EXAMPLE

From customer surveys, Crystal Insurance Company learns that customers want their requests handled more quickly. Crystal Insurance Company uses a contact center display, similar to the one in Figure 4.2 that displays the number of callers on hold, the longest current hold time, and the percentage of calls answered within a specific number of seconds. Managers can use this information to generate reports on specific measures, like timeliness. Then managers can use the numbers in these reports as KPIs to determine which part of the customer service process needs attention.

 

A company might also use KPIs to implement a balanced scorecard approach. The balanced scorecard approach relies on two types of graphic displays—balanced scorecards and dashboards, the latter of which are discussed later in the text—that give decision makers actionable information about the progress of operations.

A balanced scorecard displays a set of KPIs and compares the values to performance standards. It provides a comprehensive overview of the alignment of organizational perspectives with organizational goals. A typical balanced scorecard keeps track of a company’s activities in terms of four perspectives. In some cases, each perspective has a comprehensive list of objectives beneath it. The four perspectives are largely constant from one company to the next, though the details and definitions vary. Figure 4.3 summarizes the four perspectives.

If a company finds that data from any one of its four perspectives fails to meet corporate goals, the company should pursue process improvements. The examples below address unmet goals from each of the four perspectives.

EXAMPLES

Financial Performance

Managers at Raylite Insurance Company learn that they can improve upon financial performance by implementing better financial tracking processes.

They decide to provide company executives with virtual alerts so that the executives can respond immediately to deviations.

Customer Satisfaction

Occasionally, customers of Crystal Insurance indicate dissatisfaction with resolution speed. Decision makers at Crystal hope to increase the timeliness of customer service interactions.

After each completed interaction, the system offers a survey asking customers to rate their satisfaction with how quickly the representative solved their problem. At the end of each quarter, decision makers look at the percentage of interactions with customers who were “extremely satisfied” with the timeliness of the resolution. To explore customer satisfaction over time, they compare the new percentage to percentages from previous quarters.

Internal Operations

Raylite’s process for issuing new policies involves several manual interventions.

Linda Luongo, Raylite’s process improvement specialist, decides that Raylite can reduce the manual labor involved by automating several of the steps.

Learning and Growth

John Green, a manager at Crystal Insurance Company, studies customer call logs. He learns that customer service representatives have trouble helping customers who call in with questions about the new website.

John decides to hold more comprehensive employee training sessions on using the new website.

 

Methods of Process Improvement

Once decision makers decide which process needs improvement, they can decide on the method of action to fix the causes behind the inefficiencies. In many cases, incremental improvements are enough to fine-tune the process so that it accomplishes desired results.

EXAMPLE

EndiFirst sees declining performance in the perspective of customer satisfaction since rolling out their new self-service portal. Lately, customers such as Maya respond to surveys with complaints about error messages, the number of steps to complete a transaction, and the inability of customer service representatives to answer questions about transaction issues.

Decision makers at EndiFirst decide they need to improve the speed, accuracy, and ease of self-service portal transactions.

EndiFirst’s self-service portal does not seem to be working well. However, companies should continue to improve processes and increase value to customers even if processes run smoothly. Continuous process improvement uses strategic initiatives to make ongoing, incremental improvements in processes and technology. Continuous process improvement assigns accountability and provides a framework for incremental decisions. It forces team members to focus on a key process for meeting customer needs.

Many methodologies exist for continuous process improvement. Using these methodologies, companies see improvements in customer experience, employee satisfaction, speed-to-market, expense reduction, and overall market position. 2 Among the most common methodologies are kaizen, Lean management, Six Sigma, Lean Six Sigma, and agile management.

 

Kaizen

Kaizen is a gradual and continuous approach to process improvement using a series of modest changes and feedback from process team members. Historically, kaizen is a Japanese way-of-life philosophy that holds that every aspect of a life should be continually improved. 3 In business applications of kaizen, teams of workers apply the principles of this philosophy to their work through small process adjustments.

The application of kaizen in business relies on five principles and three key values. We can summarize the five principles of kaizen as teamwork, quality control, personal discipline, improved morale, and suggestions for improvement. We can summarize the three key values or factors of kaizen as elimination of waste, good housekeeping, and standardization.

EXAMPLE

Members of the process team at Crystal Insurance conduct closure debriefings with executives after every process implementation. Based on lessons learned, they tweak their process design strategy to move it further away from the problem points of the most recent implementation. As a result, the team improves standardization and efficiency with each meeting.

 

For kaizen to work, companies need to create open communication at every level. This can challenge companies whose employees do not often work collaboratively or share ideas. In addition, kaizen does not lead to immediate results. Companies using kaizen need to help employees think in terms of the big picture, which sometimes meets resistance.

Despite drawbacks, kaizen process improvements tend to be simple and inexpensive to implement. This simplicity makes kaizen a useful addition to other process improvement approaches, such as Lean management.

 

Lean Management

Lean management is a continuous process improvement approach that emphasizes creating a value stream comprising Lean processes with minimal waste. (In most sources, the “L” in the word “Lean,” considered a proper noun, is capitalized.) According to this methodology, the biggest opportunity for process improvement comes from identifying and eliminating waste, thereby creating value streams. Lean management promotes five main principles and reduces seven wastes, as follows:

 

Five Lean

Seven Wastes of Lean

•  Define value from the perspective of the customer

•  Identify the value stream

•  Create a continuous flow of value-adding steps

•  Allow processes to respond to the pull of the customer

•  Pursue perfection

•  Overproduction

•  Defects

•  Unnecessary inventory

•  Inappropriate processing

•  Excessive transportation

•  Waiting

•  Excessive motion

Lean thinking places less emphasis on optimizing individual activities and more emphasis on optimizing the cross-departmental, continuous flow of entire processes. Eliminating waste across value streams requires fewer resources than eliminating waste at certain points.

Lean principles apply to the insurance industry in terms of seeking to understand customer needs, identifying the value stream in a process, implementing a continuous flow, maintaining clear communication, improving speed, and eliminating handoffs. Lean principles can also apply directly to policy issue and updates to customer information.

Lean management emphasizes the use of technology in order to reduce time and expense. For instance, one way to make processes leaner is by eliminating handoffs. A handoff is a transfer of work from one work team member to another, typically across organizational boundaries. To eliminate handoffs, many insurers use straight-through processing (STP), which refers to a fully automated process in which the steps in a specified transaction type are conducted without human intervention. A company new to STP might have to invest in new technology.

EXAMPLE

Using STP, an agent can submit an application electronically. When the application appears in the system, the system can automatically conduct all of the required processing steps, issue the contract to the customer, and arrange the commission payment to the agent without any manual intervention.

Since Lean principles recommend maintaining a continuous flow, applying the principles to a financial services process requires either stabilizing the flow of inputs or increasing capacity. As a result, making existing processes Lean calls for considerable effort over a long period. The ultimate results of Lean management, however, are smoother processes, reduced need for resources, and improved employee morale.

Most Lean management practices apply to manufacturing processes. Therefore, financial services companies typically combine it with other process improvement techniques.

 

Six Sigma

Six Sigma is a disciplined approach for defining, measuring, analyzing, improving, and controlling quality by reducing process defects so that results fall within established parameters. A defect refers to anything that produces waste. Sigma is a measure of how close performance is to specifications. Six refers to the possible performance levels a company can achieve. Sigma levels range from one (significant defects) to six (minimal defects). Despite its origin in manufacturing, many companies in the service sector employ Six Sigma successfully.

Insurance companies use Six Sigma to identify and eliminate defects such as those that result in

  • Difficulty in accessing or navigating a company website

  • Improper denial of insurance claims

  • Denial of coverage for a proposed insured who should have been covered or provision of coverage for a proposed insured who should have been denied

  • Failure to process client transactions correctly

  • Failure to meet regulatory requirements related to product advertising or claim investigation

  • Submission of insurance applications that contain incomplete or incorrect information

Six Sigma programs use terms derived from the martial arts to describe team member status, and levels range from master black belts to yellow belts. In this hierarchy, some individuals spend more time on the initiative than others do. Insurance companies that implement Six Sigma programs can train their employees to be Six Sigma team members. Alternatively, they can employ outside vendors who specialize in Six Sigma to establish programs and train employees. Figure 4.4 illustrates Six Sigma team roles.

Six Sigma teams can use an approach called root-cause analysis to identify the cause of process failures. Root-cause analysis (RCA) is a set of problem-solving methods and tools designed to determine the actual causal factors that led to a particular incident or result. Unlike other statistical tools, which ask “What?” and “How?” RCA focuses on “Why?” and directs users to continue to ask questions until they identify the root cause of a problem. In addition to being specific and identifiable, a root cause must fall under the company’s control and be capable of reasonable remedy or resolution. Regarding its self-service transactions, EndiFirst might ask:

  • Why are customers dissatisfied with the self-service portal? The portal often directs users to call customer service upon submission of a transaction.

  • Why do customers need to call customer service? The submission often results in an error message.

  • Why are customers receiving error messages? There seems to be a glitch causing the portal to time out unpredictably. On reload—whether or not the payment goes through—the system provides an error message.

  • Why does the system have glitches? Not all components of the portal received the same thorough testing.

  • Why weren’t some components tested thoroughly? The update took longer than anticipated. In order to introduce the update on schedule, developers had to forgo some final tests.

  • Why did the developers run out of time? The three software developers at EndiFirst were working on several projects at once with similar completion dates.

The answer to the team’s last question helped identify one cause of the process inefficiency. EndiFirst’s development team was working on several projects at once, and there were too few developers for the number of tasks. To ensure better systems going forward, EndiFirst might consider hiring more developers so that they have the resources for careful testing and implementation.

If a process is inefficient on more than one level, there can be many root causes. For instance, EndiFirst developers might need more training on best practices to reduce the likelihood of future glitches. EndiFirst customer service representatives might need training on how to interact with the portal, so that customers get consistent information through both communication channels. Before making changes like these, however, EndiFirst should analyze and model the effects of these changes on budgeting, scheduling, and job structure.

When used correctly, Six Sigma results in measurable improvements. However, Six Sigma requires a lot of support from management to be successful and often results in high upfront costs for training or outside expertise. Six Sigma also relies on extensive data, which can be difficult and time-consuming to collect. Finally, Six Sigma does not work for every functional area or project. It isn’t ideal for nonstandard processes with unclear defects, for example.

 

Lean Six Sigma

Many companies experience positive results 4 when they combine Lean management principles with those of Six Sigma to create a hybrid quality improvement method known as Lean Six Sigma. The Lean part of this approach applies the philosophical view of efficiency. Its focus is on better serving customers’ needs by removing processes that create waste. The Six Sigma part of the approach requires using rigorous analytical approaches to quantify hidden instances of waste. Its focus is on continually improving processes that add value for the customer.

EXAMPLE

Paradise Insurance employees are encouraged to save time by streamlining tasks with technology and conducting brief check-ins instead of having frequent planned meetings.

A group of Paradise Insurance employees received training to achieve the Six Sigma status of green belt. In addition to the business-wide time-saving initiative, the group feels empowered to identify and address process defects.

Because Lean Six Sigma combines two distinct approaches to waste elimination, it often requires twice the analysis. However, a company that uses a tailored combination of tools from Lean and Six Sigma has more tools available than a company that uses just one of these approaches.

 

Agile Management and Organizational Agility

Agile management is an approach to business effectiveness focused on finding and seizing opportunities to improve operations and processes. Agile management requires employees to develop a growth mindset that emphasizes both the incompleteness of existing knowledge and the importance of continuous learning. The agile outlook fits with other continuous process improvement approaches because agile management emphasizes the benefits that companies expect from constantly improving their operational efficiency and rethinking customer needs.

Agile management supports employee empowerment, helping employees at every organizational level gain comfort with change and decision-making. Organizational agilityis an organization’s ability to adapt quickly to changes in the external environment. Organizational agility is especially important when the pace of environmental change is great.

EXAMPLE

During product development, Raylite Insurance uses a live, collaborative document that employees update with examples of lessons learned. The document empowers the team to make and keep track of real-time adjustments to ongoing projects. This fluid approach to continuous improvement makes it easier to adapt to change and manage several projects at once.

Technology helps address environmental change, and companies often use agile methodology when developing their information systems. A common agile approach is scrum—a framework for managing the processes involved in software development. Scrum teams typically break their processes into micro-activities called sprints, monitor progress, and adjust plans daily to address change. Scrum teams consist of the product owner, various team members, and a scrum master, who facilitates the team by managing all of the business processes required for software development. 5 Scrum is practical for software used in the current insurance industry climate, where new regulatory, actuarial, and technological considerations surface daily.

Agile management supports traits that increase efficiency: up-to-date schedules, fast turnaround times, and constant feedback. On the other hand, agile management’s frequent redirection demands more energy from employees, produces less complete documentation, and can make it challenging to measure overall progress toward a large goal. However, due to its success in handling multiple moving parts, agile management is a popular approach to project management.

 

Process Review

After the implementation of a process improvement, members of a process team conduct periodic review and assessment meetings to measure performance. Process review is the iterative, or cyclic, evaluation of an existing business process to find opportunities for improvement.

In the case of EndiFirst’s process improvement, the company needs to ensure at least the following outcomes on a regular basis:

  • Developers, including new hires, test all changes to the portal extensively

  • Customers experience few, if any, system errors

  • Representatives understand the system and know about all updates that can affect customer experience

As process teams identify opportunities for improvement, they should repeat the steps from Video 4.1:

Step 1: Define a key process.

Step 2: Map the process.

Step 3: Identify problems in the existing process using analysis and control.

Step 4: Implement process improvement.

Step 5: Review process improvement.
