The Operating Cycle

Tracking the flow of funds through a company is a primary focus of the accounting function. We call the way in which funds flow through a company as it conducts its day-to-day business activities the operating cycle. In accounting terminology, funds flowing into a company are a source of funds and funds flowing out of a company are a use of funds.

 

Sources of Funds

Typical sources of an insurer’s funds are the revenues—typically income—generated from the insurer’s core business activities. Because they are revenues, an insurer’s sources of funds answer the question: what has the insurer earned?

Insurers have three main types of income that serve as sources of funds:

  • Premium income. An insurer earns premium income when it agrees to accept financial risk and issues an insurance policy or annuity contract.

  • Investment income. As the insurer receives premium income, it invests a portion of that income, thereby earning additional income—known as investment income—in the form of interest, dividends, or rental income.

  • Fee income. Insurers typically obtain fee income by providing brokerage services, management services for separate accounts, third-party administration (TPA) services for other insurers, and retirement administration services. Fee income is a growing source of funds for some life insurance companies.

 

Uses of Funds

A life insurance company’s primary uses of funds include paying

  • Contractual benefits

  • Policy dividends

  • Various operating expenses, such as agent commissions and salaries, taxes, licenses, and fees

 

Contractual Benefit Payments

Insurance companies exist to provide promised benefits to owners of their insurance products, so one of an insurer’s main uses of funds is to pay contractual benefits. Contractual benefits are amounts that an insurer is legally required to pay under the terms of an insurance contract when a covered loss occurs.

 

Policy Dividends

Insurers also use funds to pay policy dividends on participating policies. A policy dividend is a portion of an insurer’s surplus that the insurer pays to the owner of a participating policy. An insurer typically pays policy dividends when its actual mortality experience is more favorable than expected. In essence, a policy dividend is a return of excess premium to the policyowner. Do not confuse policy dividends with stockholder dividends, which are another use of an insurer’s funds. Stockholder dividends are payments to the owners of stock in a stock insurance company.

 

Operating Expenses

All companies pay various operating expenses, which a company incurs in conducting its normal business operations. An insurer’s operating expenses are similar to those of many noninsurance companies and include such items as employee salaries, rent, maintenance, advertising, travel, and legal expenses.

Among the most significant operating expenses for insurers are agent commissions and salaries, which also represent a large use of an insurer’s funds. Most individual life insurance policies are sold on a commission basis. However, insurers typically compensate agents who sell group life insurance products on a salary basis.

In addition, life insurers pay taxes that many noninsurance companies pay, including property taxes and unemployment taxes. Life insurers also pay some insurance-specific taxes and fees, such as premium taxes and licensing fees.
