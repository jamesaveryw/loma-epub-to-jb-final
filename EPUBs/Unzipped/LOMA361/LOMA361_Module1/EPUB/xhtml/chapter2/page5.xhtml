Double-Entry Accounting

All companies, including insurance companies, use double-entry accounting to record business transactions in their accounting systems. With double-entry accounting, every accounting entry must (1) have at least one debit and one credit, (2) have an equal monetary amount of debits and credits, and (3) maintain the balance of the basic accounting equation.

The cornerstone of double-entry accounting is the dual concept, which states that every business transaction has two aspects—debits and credits—that always equal each other in their monetary amounts:

  • A debit is a change made to the monetary value of an account that (1) increases the value of asset accounts and expense accounts, and (2) decreases the value of liability accounts, owners’ equity accounts, and revenue accounts.

  • A credit is a change made to the monetary value of an account that (1) increases the value of liability accounts, owners’ equity accounts, and revenue accounts, and (2) decreases the value of asset accounts and expense accounts.

When recording an accounting entry, you usually list debits first, followed by credits.

 

Accounting Entries

An accounting entry, also called a journal entry, is a record of a business transaction that includes at least one debit and one credit and shows the monetary value of the transaction in balance. For an activity to qualify as a business transaction, the activity must have a financial impact on the company—that is, the company must be able to assign an objective monetary value to the transaction.

A company determines the monetary value of a business transaction from source documents. Source documents are the various business papers and electronic forms that contain original information about a company’s business transactions. Source documents verify that a business transaction occurred, as well as the details of the transaction, such as the date, monetary amount, and who approved the transaction. Many insurance companies now track source documents by scanning them and storing them in an electronic document database. Examples of typical source documents for insurance companies are approved policy applications, premium notices, policy surrender forms, claim forms, invoices, and confirmations of investment purchases and sales.

 

Simple Accounting Entries

A simple accounting entry is a record of a business transaction that affects only two accounts, and thus has only one debit and one credit. The accounting entry for the receipt of an annual premium is an example of a transaction that affects only two accounts. Suppose the Steadfast Life Insurance Company receives a $2,000 annual premium payment on a life insurance policy. This business transaction affects only two accounts—Cash and Premium Income—as shown below:

Cash.........................2,000

Premium Income………….2,000

To record the receipt of a $2,000 annual premium.

The example above shows a debit to Cash and a credit to Premium Income. The effect of this business transaction is to increase both Cash and Premium Income by $2,000, the amount of the premium payment. Note that journal entries list debits first and to the left and then list credits offset, or indented, to the right. In reality, most insurers have automated, electronic accounting entries; in these entries, debits might be listed in one column and credits in another column.

The accounting entry above also includes a brief verbal description underneath it. The purpose of including verbal descriptions in accounting entries is to help anyone reviewing the company’s accounts to understand the details of a given transaction. Most automated systems contain clickable phrases that describe common transactions and that include links to each affected account.

 

Compound Accounting Entries

Many business transactions affect more than two accounts. A compound accounting entry is an accounting record of a business transaction that affects more than two accounts. Suppose Steadfast paid a claim of $10,000 plus interest of $100 to the beneficiary of a life insurance policy. This business transaction affects three accounts—Death Claims Paid, Interest Payable, and Cash—as shown below:

Death Claims Paid............ 10,000

Interest Payable.......................... 100

Cash......................................... 10,100

To record the payment of a $10,000 death claim plus $100 interest. 

The debit to Death Claims Paid, an expense account, increases the balance of this account, whereas the debit to Interest Payable, a liability account, and the credit to Cash, an asset account, decrease the balance in these accounts.

Permanent Accounts and Temporary Accounts

Accounts are either permanent or temporary. In accounting, a permanent account is a balance sheet account that has a balance at the beginning of each accounting period. Permanent accounts include asset, liability, and owners’ equity accounts. In contrast, a temporary account, also called a nominal account, is an account that is closed out to a permanent account at the end of each accounting period. In other words, at the beginning of each accounting period, all temporary accounts have a zero balance. Temporary accounts comprise the account classifications included on the income statement: revenue accounts and expense accounts. Most temporary accounts are closed out to an owners’ equity account, such as Retained Earnings or Surplus.

 

Closing Entries

Companies use closing entries to move the balances in their temporary accounts to permanent accounts at the end of the accounting period. A closing entry is an accounting entry that a company makes at the end of each accounting period to start the next accounting period with a zero balance in its temporary accounts. Because a revenue earned or an expense incurred in one period pertains only to that period, revenues and expenses must be closed out at the end of each accounting period. Adjusting Entries

An adjusting entry is an accounting entry that a company makes to record accruals for revenues and expenses, unearned revenues, and prepaid expenses. Accruals allow an insurer to record expenses incurred but not yet paid and to record revenues earned but not yet received. Other adjusting entries include depreciation, changes made to reserve accounts, and corrections to previous accounting periods. After making the appropriate adjusting entries at the end of each accounting period, a company prepares its financial statements; it then records the applicable closing entries to ready its accounting records for the start of the next accounting period.

Adjusting entries typically apply to transactions of which a company has knowledge but lacks the appropriate source documents. Regardless, the company is still required to provide some type of documentation to support these entries. For example, salaries that an insurance company’s employees have earned during this year, but that the company will not pay until next year, may not yet have a time sheet or the equivalent source document available. Nonetheless, at the end of the accounting period, the company will prepare documentation that shows the amount of the adjusting entry, along with supporting details about how the monetary amounts were calculated. Using the general format of debiting an expense account—Salaries Expense, in this case—and crediting a payable account—Salaries Payable—the company prepares an adjusting entry as follows:

Salaries Expense........................ xxx

Salaries Payable........................ xxx

To adjust for salaries that have been earned but not yet paid.

Next year, upon paying the accrued salaries, the company would debit Salaries Payable and credit Cash to record this transaction, as follows:

Salaries  Payable.................. xxx

Cash......................................... xxx

To pay accrued salaries from the previous year.

In describing double-entry accounting and the different types of accounting entries, we generally state that the “company” makes various entries in its accounting system. In reality, most insurance companies use sophisticated accounting software, which performs many accounting tasks with minimal human intervention.

INSIGHT

Robotic Process Automation

Robotic process automation (RPA) refers to the use of sophisticated computer software that is configured to automate manual and repetitive rules-based tasks without the need for constant human supervision. The word “robotic” in RPA conjures up the steel-encased robots from science fiction lore. In this context, though, the word refers to the computer software, which has been programmed to perform various tasks in an automatic, “robotic” way. In insurance accounting, companies can use RPA to record and process transactions, manipulate data, and communicate with other company systems when necessary.

What can a typical company expect using RPA?

  • A reduction in data entry costs of up to 70%
  • A double-digit reduction in error rates
  • Automated solutions that can work 24 hours a day, 7 days a week
  • An increase in efficiency of up to 50%
  • A 40% reduction in the workforce
  • A 100% reduction in human error, along with a 100% recorded audit trail

As noted above, a company using RPA typically sees a reduction in its workforce. However, with “robots” (or “bots”) performing the tedious, repetitive tasks associated with many business transactions, financial professionals can potentially spend more of their time on high-value activities, such as analyzing and interpreting data.

Although RPA is relatively new, its potential to increase operating efficiencies has gained the attention of insurers—as well as companies in other industries—around the world.

Normal Balances

An account’s balance is the monetary amount in the account as of a specified date. To obtain the balance in an account, you calculate the difference between the account’s debits and its credits:

  • If an account’s debits typically exceed its credits, the account is said to have a normal debit balance.

  • If an account’s credits typically exceed its debits, the account is said to have a normal credit balance.

In accounting, an account’s normal balance is the side of the account, either debit or credit, to which increases to the account are recorded. Accounts that are increased by

  • Debits typically have a normal debit balance at the end of the accounting period

  • Credits usually have a normal credit balance at the end of the accounting period

Normal balances are an important tool that managers (and others) can use to evaluate accounts and look for problems. For example, sometimes an account that has a normal debit balance shows a credit balance; alternatively, an account that has a normal credit balance shows a debit balance. In such cases, the manager who is responsible for that specific account balance must review it. For example, Cash has a normal debit balance. A credit balance in Cash means that the cash balance is below zero and must be investigated.
