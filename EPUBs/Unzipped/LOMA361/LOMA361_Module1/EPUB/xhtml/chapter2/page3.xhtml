Account Classification

An account is the basic tool that a company uses to record, group, and summarize similar types of business transactions. An insurance company’s business transactions, like those of other companies, typically involve five major account classifications: (1) assets, (2) liabilities, (3) owners’ equity, (4) revenues, and (5) expenses. A company’s account classifications make up its chart of accounts, which is a numbered or alphabetical list of all the company’s account names. Developing and maintaining the chart of accounts is typically under the controller’s span of control. Account titles and numbering schemes vary from insurer to insurer, but a simple chart of accounts appears below:

1000 Assets
  1001 Bonds
  1002 Common Stocks
  1003 Mortgages
2000 Liabilities
  2001 Contractual Reserves
  2002 Other Liabilities
3000 Owners' Equity
  3001 Capital
  3002 Surplus
4000 Revenues

  4002 Investment Income
  4003 Fee Income
5000 Expenses
  5001 Contractual Benefits
  5002 Operating Expenses

Balance Sheet Accounts

The first three account classifications we discuss—assets, liabilities, and owners’ equity—are found on a company’s balance sheet. A balance sheet, also called a statement of financial position or statement of financial condition, is a financial statement that shows a company’s financial condition or position by summarizing the company’s assets, liabilities, and owners’ equity as of a specified date. Let’s take a look at each of these three accounts now.

 

Assets: What Does the Company Own?

Assets are the items of value owned by a company. Assets may be

  • Tangible (physical), such as real estate, office supplies, and computer equipment, or

  • Intangible (not physical), such as securities (including stocks and bonds), trademarks, and goodwill

Insurers use their assets as a resource to generate cash, which they can use to fulfill their financial obligations.

EXAMPLE

Example. Insurance companies typically invest in bonds, which are assets. Whenever the bonds pay interest, mature, or are sold by an insurer, the insurer receives cash, which it can use to settle policyowner claims or other financial commitments.

One important characteristic of an asset is its liquidity, which is the ease with which the asset can be converted to cash for an approximation of its underlying value. In the context of liquidity, insurers may categorize their assets as either short-term or long-term:

  • Short-term assets, also called current assets, are assets that a company expects to readily convert into cash or consume within the current accounting period, typically one year. Short-term assets generally have high liquidity.

  • Long-term assets, also called noncurrent assets, are assets that a company plans to hold indefinitely or for a long time—generally longer than one year—to generate income. Compared to short-term assets, long-term assets usually take longer to convert to cash and are generally illiquid (the opposite of liquid).

Liabilities: What Does the Company Owe?

Liabilities represent the monetary value of a company’s current and future obligations. Liabilities result from a monetary commitment that the company’s management has made on behalf of the company. As such, liabilities represent demands against the company’s assets. A payable—an amount the company has promised to pay to another party—is a liability.

To remain in business, an insurer must have a higher monetary value for its assets than for its liabilities:

Insurer assets ˃ Insurer liabilities

An insurer’s liabilities consist primarily of contractual reserves for future obligations, which are the amounts it expects to pay its policyowners, annuity contract owners, and beneficiaries.

As with assets, insurers may categorize their liabilities as either short-term or long-term:

  • Short-term liabilities, also called current liabilities, are financial obligations that the company must pay in one year or less. Agent commissions payable is one example of a short-term liability for insurers.

  • Long-term liabilities, also called noncurrent liabilities, are liabilities that the company does not have to pay in full during the current accounting period—usually one year. Bonds that an insurer issues—rather than purchases—are an example of a long-term liability for an insurer.

 

Owners’ Equity: What Has the Company Retained?

Owners’ equity represents all owner investments in the company. These investments represent a residual claim against a company’s assets. In other words, if a company goes out of business, its owners—that is, the stockholders of a stock insurer or the policyowners of a mutual insurer or fraternal insurer—are entitled to whatever remains of the company’s assets after all creditor claims and policyowner claims have been paid. In this sense, owners’ equity is the difference between a company’s assets and its liabilities:

Owners’ Equity = Assets – Liabilities

Sometimes, insurance companies replace the term owners’ equity with capital and surplus:

Capital + Surplus = Assets – Liabilities

Recall from Chapter 1 that, in the context of accounting, capital represents the amount of money invested in a company by its owners, usually through the purchase of the company’s stock.

One important example of a capital account is Retained Earnings. Retained earnings represents the net income that an insurance company holds to reinvest in the company instead of paying the money out in dividends to its stockholders. Stock insurers use the Retained Earnings account when preparing GAAP financial reports. To comply with statutory financial reporting requirements, insurance companies use Surplus instead of Retained Earnings. Surplus is the cumulative amount of money—calculated as an insurance company’s assets minus its liabilities and capital—that remains in the company over time. Because mutual insurers and fraternal insurers do not have stockholders, they have no capital accounts. Thus, for mutual and fraternal insurers, we can say that

Surplus = Assets – Liabilities

 

Income Statement Accounts

The remaining two account classifications—revenues and expenses—are found on a company’s income statement. An income statement, also called a statement of operations, is a financial statement that reports an insurer’s revenues and expenses during a specified period and indicates whether the insurer experienced net income or a net loss during the accounting period. We explain all of these terms below.

 

Revenues: What Has the Company Earned?

Revenues represent the amount of earnings from a company’s core business operations. For insurance companies, core business operations include selling insurance and annuity products and services to customers, collecting investment income from investments that they own, and collecting fee income from providing administrative or management services to customers.

In general, an increase in a company’s revenues ultimately results in an increase in the company’s assets. For example, when a policyowner pays a policy premium, this transaction ultimately increases not only an insurer’s revenues but also its cash, which is an asset.

 

Expenses: What Has the Company Spent?

Expenses, also known as costs, are the amounts that a company spends in the course of conducting business. Expenses are a necessary part of doing business, because a company cannot generate revenues without incurring expenses. Typical expenses for an insurer include investment expenses, contractual benefit expenses, and operating expenses. Employee salaries usually are a large percentage of an insurer’s expenses.

Unlike revenues, which ultimately increase a company’s assets, expenses ultimately decrease the company’s assets. For example, the act of paying a policy claim (an expense) decreases cash (an asset).

The relationship between an insurer’s revenues and expenses determines the insurer’s net income or net loss. Net income, or net profit, is any money that results from an excess of total revenues over total expenses during a specified period.

Total revenues > Total expenses = Net income

Likewise, a net loss is the amount of a company’s total expenses that exceeds its total revenues during a specified period. A company usually reports a net loss, which is a negative amount, in parentheses or in a different color, such as red, on its financial statements.

Total expenses > Total revenues = (Net loss)

OR

Total expenses > Total revenues = Net loss

The Basic Accounting Equation

To understand the relationship between the five major account classifications, we need to look at a fundamental part of the accounting function known as the basic accounting equation. The basic accounting equation, sometimes called the balance sheet equation, is the formula that expresses the relationship among the three key account classifications that a company reports on its balance sheet: Assets, Liabilities, and Owners’ Equity. We can express the basic accounting equation as follows:

Assets = Liabilities + Owners’ Equity

Like all companies, insurance companies must keep both sides of this equation in balance—hence, the “balance” in “balance sheet.” Therefore, every business transaction that changes one side of the equation must be offset by at least one change to the other side of the equation.

For stock insurance companies, we can substitute the term Capital and Surplus for Owners’ Equity, as follows:

Assets = Liabilities + Capital + Surplus

EXAMPLE

Example. To illustrate how the basic accounting equation stays in balance, let’s look at a simple transaction for a stock insurer.

A stock insurer issues additional stock to investors. This transaction increases Cash (part of Assets) on the left side of the equation, and this change is exactly offset by an equal increase to Capital on the right side of the equation.

Recall that mutual insurers and fraternal insurers have no capital accounts, so we can simplify the basic accounting equation for these types of insurers as follows:

Assets = Liabilities + Surplus

EXAMPLE

Example. Let’s look at another simple transaction, this time for a mutual insurer.

A mutual insurer pays out policy dividends in cash to owners of its participating life insurance policies. This transaction decreases Cash (part of Assets) on the left side of the equation, and this change is exactly offset by an equal decrease in the insurer’s Liabilities on the right side of the equation. (We discuss policy dividends as a liability for insurers in a later chapter.)

The basic accounting equation can be expanded to include an insurer’s revenues and expenses. At the end of an accounting period, the insurer closes out all its revenue and expense accounts on the income statement and transfers the net income or net loss—that is, total revenues minus total expenses—for the period to the Owners’ Equity account on the balance sheet.

Therefore, we can expand the basic accounting equation to include all five major account classifications:

Assets = Liabilities + Owners’ Equity + (Revenues – Expenses)
