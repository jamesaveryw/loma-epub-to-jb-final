Regulatory Agencies and Organizations

Because the insurance industry manages large amounts of money and because many people rely on insurers for financial protection, the public can be negatively affected if insurers do not adhere to prudent standards in their business transactions. Therefore, the financial health of the insurance industry is deemed to be in the public interest. Compliance with financial reporting requirements helps ensure that each insurance company adequately prepares for the eventual payment of contractual benefits. In addition, to make informed decisions, policyowners, investors, and other interested parties must be able to rely on the information presented in an insurer’s financial statements.

The following sections discuss agencies and organizations that have a significant impact on accounting and financial reporting for life insurance companies in the United States. Note, however, that other countries have a variety of agencies and organizations that provide similar regulatory and professional oversight of the insurance industry.

 

Securities and Exchange Commission

The Securities and Exchange Commission (SEC) is a federal government agency that regulates the investment industry in the United States. Its main purpose is to protect investors. The SEC has jurisdiction over all publicly traded companies in the United States. It also holds the legal authority to prescribe the accounting principles—in this case, GAAP—that public companies use. Many insurers are subject to SEC regulations. The following tests determine whether SEC regulations apply to an insurer operating in the United States:

  • Is the insurer a stock company? All publicly traded companies, including stock insurers, are subject to SEC regulation.

  • Does the insurer sell variable products, such as variable life insurance and variable annuities? Variable products are investment products as well as insurance products; therefore, an insurer that sells variable products is subject to SEC regulation. Under this test, mutual insurers and fraternal insurers that sell variable products are subject to SEC regulation, even though they are not publicly traded stock companies.

Insurers that are subject to SEC regulation must generally submit U.S. GAAP financial statements to the SEC. However, the SEC now accepts a non-U.S. company’s financial statements that were prepared according to IFRS without requiring the company to restate its financial statements according to U.S. GAAP. Currently, U.S. mutual insurers and fraternal insurers that sell variable products can submit statutory financial statements to the SEC.

EXAMPLE

Example. The Stanza Mutual Life Insurance Company sells term life and whole life insurance products and variable annuity products. The Lyrical Life Insurance Company sells fixed and variable life and annuity products. Stanza is a U.S. company, and Lyrical is a non-U.S. company that prepares its financial statements according to IFRS.

Analysis. Because Stanza sells variable products, it is required to submit financial statements to the SEC; however, because Stanza is a mutual insurer, these statements can be statutory financial statements rather than GAAP financial statements. Although it is a non-U.S. company, Lyrical can submit its financial statements to the SEC without restating them according to U.S. GAAP because it prepares its financial statements according to IFRS.

Financial Accounting Standards Board

The SEC has delegated its responsibility for establishing U.S. GAAP to the Financial Accounting Standards Board (FASB), a private organization. The primary role of the FASB (pronounced FAZ-bee) is to establish and promote the use of U.S. GAAP. Historically, the FASB issued authoritative publications, most of which are known as Statements of Financial Accounting Standards. FASB publications apply to insurance companies that conduct business in the United States.

EXAMPLE

Example. In 1982, the FASB issued Statement 60, which was intended to explain the accounting requirements for all types of life insurance contracts. However, in practice, it dealt primarily with accounting for traditional whole life insurance policies. Therefore, in 1987, the FASB issued Statement 97, which presented the accounting requirements for nontraditional life insurance contracts, such as universal life insurance policies.

In 2009, the FASB completed the Accounting Standards Codification (ASC), which codified all U.S. GAAP into a single source. This compilation includes all the FASB’s previous publications, as well as other authoritative guidance. With the ASC, new FASB publications take the form of Accounting Standards Updates (ASUs).

EXAMPLE

Example. In 2018, the FASB issued an Accounting Standards Update (ASU) titled Targeted Improvements to the Accounting for Long-Duration Contracts. Long-duration contracts are contracts, such as whole life insurance policies or annuity contracts, that are expected to remain in force for an extended period. Among other matters, this ASU provides a simplified method of amortizing deferred acquisition costs (DAC). 1 We discuss DAC in a later chapter.

A company must disclose in its financial reports all significant departures from FASB guidance. The FASB has representation on the IASB.

 

American Institute of Certified Public Accountants

The American Institute of Certified Public Accountants (AICPA) is a professional association of CPAs in the United States. Although the AICPA has no regulatory powers, its influence is widespread. Through its Auditing Standards Board, the AICPA develops generally accepted auditing standards (GAAS), which public accountants use when conducting audits of an insurer’s financial statements.

EXAMPLE

Example. In January 2010, the AICPA finalized a new auditing standard for insurance companies and other financial services organizations: The Statement on Standards for Attestation Engagements (SSAE) No. 16 (SSAE 16).

The AICPA also influences accounting practices through its technical advisory committees, its publications, and its code of professional conduct for accounting professionals.

EXAMPLE

Example. The AICPA’s Audit & Accounting Guide: Life and Health Insurance Entities is a useful text for individuals who work in the accounting and financial reporting areas of life and health insurance companies. This technical guide is designed to help insurance companies (1) prepare their GAAP and statutory financial statements and (2) perform and report on their auditing activities. 2

 

Public Company Auditing Oversight Board

As a result of several high-profile accounting scandals in the early 2000s, Congress passed the U.S. federal law known as the Sarbanes-Oxley Act of 2002. The Sarbanes-Oxley Act of 2002 (SOX) requires public companies to take specific precautions to prevent fraudulent behavior on the part of management and the company’s external auditors. SOX established the Public Company Auditing Oversight Board (PCAOB), which is a private-sector, nonprofit corporation that sets the auditing standards for external auditors of public companies. The purpose of the PCAOB is to protect the interests of investors by ensuring that audit reports are informative, fair, and independent.

 

National Association of Insurance Commissioners

The National Association of Insurance Commissioners (NAIC) is a private, nonprofit association of the commissioners of all 50 states, the District of Columbia, and the U.S. territories that promotes uniformity of state regulation by developing model laws and regulations that serve as guidelines for the states. Although the NAIC has no legislative power, its recommendations and actions strongly influence the individual state insurance commissioners, the state legislatures, and the insurance industry. Each state insurance department or commission determines which NAIC recommendations it approves or modifies, and then incorporates these recommendations into its state reporting requirements for insurers.

The NAIC’s influence on insurance accounting is strongest with respect to the U.S. Annual Statement, which we discussed earlier. The NAIC specifies the format and content of the Annual Statement. The NAIC also uses information in the Annual Statement to evaluate an insurer’s financial condition, as well as its compliance with insurance laws and regulatory requirements.

 

Internal Revenue Service

The Internal Revenue Service (IRS), a part of the U.S. Department of the Treasury, is the governmental agency responsible for enforcing the provisions of laws and regulations concerning income taxes. Insurers in the United States must adhere to the tax code of the IRS. The IRS significantly influences the way in which insurance companies conduct business. A complete discussion of tax accounting, the tax code, and specific tax effects on an insurer’s products is beyond the scope of this textbook.
