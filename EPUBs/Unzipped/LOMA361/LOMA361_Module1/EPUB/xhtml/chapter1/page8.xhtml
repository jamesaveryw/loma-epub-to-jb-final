Financial Tools and Insurer Solvency

Insurance company financial statements come under intense scrutiny from government regulators, including state insurance departments. This scrutiny is intended to identify potential solvency problems so that regulators can initiate preventive measures to avert insurer insolvency—the inability to meet financial obligations on time. Specific tools used to measure and monitor solvency differ among nations. In the following sections, we describe several tools commonly used in the United States.

 

Insurance Regulatory Information System

In the United States, the NAIC has established a solvency framework designed to enhance state regulatory efforts. 3 One important component of this framework, known as the Insurance Regulatory Information System (IRIS), is a collection of financial tools and databases designed to help state insurance regulators evaluate the financial condition of insurers that operate in their states. The goal of IRIS is to enable regulators to allocate resources to those insurers in greatest need of regulatory attention.

IRIS consists of two phases: the statistical phase, which features a tool known as the IRIS ratio application, and the analytical phase, in which a group of financial examiners and analysts works together on an analyst team:

  1. During the statistical phase, the IRIS ratio application extracts data from each insurer’s electronically filed Annual Statement and automatically calculates the results of 12 financial ratios for each insurer. The first eight ratios are tests of an insurer’s solvency and profitability. The remaining four ratios assess the insurer’s operations by measuring changes in premiums, product mix, asset mix, and reserving. The ratio results are then compiled in a report that presents an alphabetical listing of all the insurers in the state by type of insurer, along with their ratio results, the usual ranges for results, and the identification of values outside the ranges.
  2. In the analytical phase, the analyst team evaluates the IRIS ratio results of specified insurance companies. (An NAIC working group determines the criteria to be used in targeting specific insurers for the analyst team review.) After reviewing the results, the analyst team designates certain insurers as “Level A,” “Level B,” or “Reviewed, no level”:

  • “Level A” means that state regulators should give this insurer the highest priority in the review process. A “Level A” insurer is not necessarily facing insolvency; rather, this designation means that the insurer’s financial condition bears greater scrutiny.

  • “Level B” means that, although the insurer has some adverse results, it is a lower priority for state regulators than a “Level A” insurer.

  • “Reviewed, no level” refers to an insurer whose results have the lowest priority for state regulators.

Note that IRIS ratio results are only useful to regulators if the information in an insurer’s Annual Statement is accurate and complete. If an insurer’s financial information is missing or incorrect, its ratio results will be flawed. In this event, regulators generally ask the insurer to correct the mistakes in its Annual Statement and resubmit it, a process known as an amended filing. Then the IRIS ratio application generates a new set of results for the insurer.

 

Risk-Based Capital Requirements

Each country sets minimum capital requirements for domestic and international insurance companies. Capital is the amount of money invested in a company by its owners, usually through the purchase of the company’s stock. In the United States, one serious shortcoming of the minimum capital requirements is that they ignore the different risks to which insurers may be exposed.

To address this situation, the NAIC has established risk-based capital (RBC) requirements, which evaluate the adequacy of an insurer’s capital based on the insurer’s size and risk profile. 4 The RBC requirements are determined using a ratio known as the risk-based capital (RBC) ratio, which is calculated by applying various risk factors to specific asset, premium, claim, expense, and reserve items found in the insurer’s Annual Statement. Each insurer calculates its own RBC ratio results and includes them in a detailed report that must be electronically filed with the NAIC.

In evaluating a given insurer, an insurance regulator compares the results of the RBC ratios with specified trigger points to determine whether regulatory intervention is necessary and, if so, to what degree. Because risk-based capital is the minimum threshold of capital required, insurance companies aim for a significantly higher amount of capital and surplus—in effect, a safety cushion—than is necessary to satisfy regulatory requirements.

Financial Condition Examinations

IRIS ratios and RBC requirements are both examples of offsite investigations of insurer solvency. State regulators also conduct onsite investigations of insurers, including financial condition examinations. A financial condition examination is an onsite regulatory examination of an insurer that is carried out by one or more state insurance departments and is designed to identify and monitor any threats to the insurer’s solvency. In most U.S. states, each insurer domiciled in the state must undergo a financial condition examination once every three to five years by the state insurance department. More frequent examinations are conducted if the state insurance commissioner believes they are warranted. The insurer usually bears the cost of a financial condition examination.

In conducting a financial condition examination, state examiners

  • Verify the operating results reported in the insurer’s U.S. Annual Statement.

  • Examine the insurer’s accounting records to determine whether the insurer is operating on a sound and lawful basis. Inaccurate financial records may conceal financial problems or unlawful activities.

  • Investigate the insurer’s financial and business activities to ensure that they do not contribute to the insurer’s insolvency.

State examiners also review other factors that help them evaluate whether the insurer is in compliance with the law. For example, examiners might perform a broad-based analysis of the insurer’s financial accounts and lines of business, or they might focus their analysis on specified problem areas, as identified from the insurer’s Annual Statement, IRIS ratio results, RBC ratio results, previous examinations, or any other source.

One increasingly important source of information for examiners is an insurer’s ORSA. In an Own Risk and Solvency Assessment (ORSA), an insurer conducts an internal assessment of the risks associated with its current business plan and the adequacy of its capital resources to support those risks. The ORSA includes a high-level summary of an insurer’s risk management framework, as well as details on the insurer’s process for assessing risks in both normal and stressed environments. The NAIC typically requires insurers to undergo an ORSA on at least an annual basis.

Working through the NAIC, the states have developed a zone system to oversee the examinations of multistate insurers. According to this system, each insurance company is domiciled within one of four geographic zones in the United States. For any insurer that operates in more than one zone or in more than three states, the NAIC recommends that the insurer undergo an association examination. In an association examination, also known as a coordinated exam, examiners representing various states in the geographic zones in which the insurer is licensed participate in examining the insurer. Most financial condition examinations are association examinations.

The results of a financial condition examination are summarized in a document called an examination report, which is submitted to state regulators and the insurance company’s officers. The examination report

  • Emphasizes any adverse conditions the examiners discover and any significant changes in the insurer’s operations or financial condition since the last report

  • Identifies and reports any problems uncovered during the most recent examination

  • May explain the causes of problems and suggestions for how the insurer might correct the problems
