Accounting and Financial Reporting

Accounting is a system or set of rules and methods for collecting, processing, reporting, and monitoring financial information about a company. Accounting can focus on the company as a whole, or it can focus on segments, product lines, or divisions within the company. Closely related to accounting is financial reporting, which is the process of presenting financial information about a company’s financial position, operating performance, and flow of funds into and out of a company.

The accounting function consists of processes and activities that help answer five key business questions, which are presented in Figure 1.1.

To provide answers to these five questions, the accounting function monitors and measures a company’s business transactions. A business transaction is a transaction to which a company must assign an objective monetary value, whether the impact on the company is large or small, actual or expected. In the context of accounting, virtually all business transactions are financial in nature, because they involve either a transfer of funds or some other resource that has monetary value.

The answers to the questions in Figure 1.1 directly affect an insurer’s primary financial goals, which are solvency and profitability:

  • Solvency is a company’s ability to meet its financial obligations when they become due. Generally, a company is considered solvent if it owns more than it owes.

  • Profitability is the overall degree of success a company has in generating positive returns for its owners, including the company’s ability to generate profits and increase the value of the company. A company tends to be profitable if it earns more than it spends.

One of the most important goals of an insurer’s accounting function is to generate reports that allow interested parties to evaluate an insurer’s solvency and profitability.
