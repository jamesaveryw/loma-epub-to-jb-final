Accounting Bases

The application of both the realization principle and the matching principle depends in part on the accounting basis that a company uses. The following sections discuss two major accounting bases: (1) accrual-basis accounting and (2) cash-basis accounting.

 

Accrual-Basis Accounting

Accrual-basis accounting is an accounting system under which a company records revenues when they are earned and expenses when they are incurred, even if the company has not yet received the revenues or paid the expenses. So, accrual-basis accounting

  • Records revenues according to the realization principle and expenses according to the matching principle

  • Helps match specific expenses with specific revenues, a process that typically enables companies to develop relevant, reliable, and comparable financial statements

The purpose of accrual-basis accounting is to record a business transaction when a company incurs a financial obligation or expects to receive a payment. Thus, accrual-basis accounting enables interested users of a company’s financial information to view the consequences of a company’s activities, including the company’s earning potential and financial performance. For this reason, accrual-basis accounting is generally regarded as more relevant, compared to cash-basis accounting. Accounting regulations generally mandate the use of accrual-basis accounting for financial reporting purposes.

Often, two separate accounting entries are needed to record a business transaction under accrual-basis accounting:

  1. The first accounting entry records the revenue earned or the expense incurred.
  2. The second accounting entry records the revenue received or the expense disbursed.

Companies that use accrual-basis accounting generally must also make adjusting entries to their accounting records at the end of each accounting period to match revenues and expenses in their financial statements for that accounting period.

 

Cash-Basis Accounting

Cash-basis accounting is an accounting system in which a company recognizes revenues or expenses only when it receives or disburses cash. In contrast, under accrual-basis accounting, a company’s financial statements must report the results of both cash and noncash transactions—for example, purchases on account—that occur during the accounting period. Neither the realization principle nor the matching principle applies under cash-basis accounting.

Cash receipts and cash disbursements are the most important components under cash-basis accounting. A cash receipt is a check, money order, electronic funds transfer (EFT), or other cash transaction that is remitted to a company as a form of payment for services rendered. A cash disbursement is the payment of cash by a company.

Under cash-basis accounting, a company records a

  • Cash receipt in the same accounting period in which it receives the cash

  • Cash disbursement in the same accounting period in which it remits payment

In a pure cash-basis system, a company makes no accounting entries to record pending revenues or expenses. Instead, the company calculates its net income by subtracting paid expenses from received revenues. Cash-basis accounting provides an accurate summary of a company’s current cash flows, but does not provide as accurate a picture of that company’s obligations or income in the immediate future, which may be significant.

EXAMPLE

Example. On December 31, 2018, the last day of its annual accounting period, the Samarkand Life Insurance Company purchased $50,000 of office equipment on account from the Maxis Office Equipment Company. Maxis delivered the equipment to Samarkand on the same day. Samarkand remitted payment in full on January 10, 2019. Below are the accounting entries for this transaction under cash-basis accounting and accrual-basis accounting.

Under cash-basis accounting, Samarkand would record the following transaction on January 10, 2019.

Office Equipment…………………………..……..….$50,000

Cash………………………………………………….…..$50,000

Under accrual-basis accounting, Samarkand would record the following transactions on December 31, 2018, and January 10, 2019, respectively:

Office Equipment………………………….……..….$50,000

Accounts Payable…………………………………..$50,000

Accounts Payable…………………………………..$50,000

Cash……………………………………………………..$50,000

Because cash-basis accounting does not apply the realization principle or the matching principle, the resulting financial statements can be misleading. For example, under cash-basis accounting, Samarkand’s balance sheet of December 31, 2018, would not show that the balance in Samarkand’s Office Equipment account has increased by $50,000, nor would it show that Samarkand owes Maxis $50,000 for the new office equipment. However, this transaction may be material to Samarkand’s potential policyowners or investors. In contrast, under accrual-basis accounting, Samarkand’s balance sheet would show the financial effect of this pending business transaction.

Historically, life insurance companies in the United States accounted for their financial transactions by making adjusting entries from cash-basis accounting to accrual-basis accounting for some accounts on the financial statements. Today, accounting software makes it much easier for insurers to maintain their accounting records simultaneously on both a cash basis and an accrual basis.
