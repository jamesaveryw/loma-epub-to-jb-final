The Blueberry Mutual Life Insurance Company, a U.S. mutual insurer, reported the following values on its year-end financial statements:

Assets = $3,500,000

Liabilities = $2,500,000

Based on this information, Blueberry’s surplus at the end of the year was equal to

  1. $1,000,000, and this figure represents the excess of revenues over expenses for a given accounting period
  2. $1,000,000, and this figure represents the cumulative amount of money that remains in Blueberry over time
  3. $6,000,000, and this figure represents the excess of revenues over expenses for a given accounting period
  4. $6,000,000, and this figure represents the cumulative amount of money that remains in Blueberry over time

 

Mutual insurers have no capital accounts, so the basic accounting equation for Blueberry is:

Assets = Liabilities + Surplus

Plugging the values into the equation gives us

$3,500,000 = $2,500,000 + Surplus

So, Blueberry’s surplus is $1,000,000, found as $3,500,000 ‒ $2,500,000. Surplus represents the cumulative amount of money that remains in a company over time. The excess of revenues over expenses in a given accounting period is known as net income.

  2. The Dulcimer Life Insurance Company listed the following assets on its balance sheet:

    • Cash equivalents = $150,000

    Short-term assets, also called current assets, are assets that a company expects to readily convert into cash or consume within the current accounting period, typically one year. Short-term assets generally have high liquidity. Examples of short-term assets include cash, cash equivalents, and Treasury bills. In this situation, Dulcimer’s total short-term assets are equal to $150,000 + $100,000 + $500,000 = $750,000.

    Long-term assets, also called noncurrent assets, are assets that a company plans to hold indefinitely or for a long time—generally longer than one year—to generate income. Compared to short-term assets, long-term assets usually take longer to convert to cash and are generally illiquid (the opposite of liquid). Examples of long-term assets include equipment and real estate.

  5. Expenses, also known as costs, are the amounts that a company spends in the course of conducting business. The act of paying a policy claim (increases / decreases) the insurer’s cash.     Expenses are a necessary part of doing business, because an insurer cannot generate revenues without incurring expenses.     3. Because other comprehensive income (OCI) is not reported on an insurer’s income statement, it is not included in the calculation of the insurer’s net income.
    1. A, B, and C
    2. A and B only
    3. A and C only
    4. B and C only

    Statement A is correct. Life insurers can have substantial capital gains and losses on their invested assets, and these capital gains and losses can be categorized as either realized or unrealized. Realized capital gains and losses on invested assets must be reported on an insurer’s GAAP income statement.

    Statement B is correct. CI, which measures the changes in equity that result from non-owner transactions, is the sum of net income and other items—known as other comprehensive income (OCI)—that are not reported on the income statement because they have not been realized: CI = Net income + OCI.

    Statement C is correct. Components of OCI generally come from non-core business operations and one-time or unusual transactions. Examples of OCI include unrealized capital gains and losses on certain investments, gains and losses resulting from translating the financial statements of foreign subsidiaries, and actuarial gains and losses related to pensions and other postretirement benefits. If these components were added to net income, the resulting amount could present an inaccurate view of an insurer’s operating performance. To prevent this result, OCI accumulates on the statement of owners’ equity in a section called “accumulated other comprehensive income” or “AOCI.”

  6. Last year, the Cooper Life Insurance Company experienced cash flows from the following activities:

    • Activity A—Cooper received $23 million in premium income and paid $18 million in contractual benefits.
    • Activity B—Cooper paid $110,000 in cash dividends to stockholders.
    • Activity C—Cooper received $195,000 of principal on loan repayments.
    • Activity D—Cooper purchased $237,000 of bonds.

    Changes in an insurer’s cash inflows and cash outflows occur as a result of three activities: operating activities, investing activities, and financing activities. With regard to Cooper’s activities, the activity that represents a cash outflow from an investing activity is

    1. Activity A
    2. Activity B
    3. Activity C
    4. Activity D

    A cash flow is any movement of cash into or out of an organization. In the context of accounting, a cash inflow is a movement of cash into an organization; cash inflows are sources of funds. Examples include premium income, investment income, and fee income. A cash outflow is a movement of cash out of an organization; cash outflows are uses of funds. Examples include payments for contractual policy benefits, ongoing business expenses, and investment purchases, such as Cooper’s bond purchase in Activity D.

    Changes in an insurer’s cash flows occur as a result of three activities. Operating activities are transactions that involve a company’s core business operations and that directly determine the company’s net income. Investing activities are transactions that involve a company’s purchases or sales of long-term assets and its lending funds to other companies. Investing activities involve the securities of other companies. Financing activities are transactions that involve borrowed funds and cash payments to or from a company’s owners. Financing activities involve the company’s own securities.

  7. In the context of financial statements, financial statement integration means that a company’s financial statements are interrelated. All other factors remaining equal, when an insurer pays contractual benefits or agent commissions, these transactions (decrease / increase) the insurer’s expenses and (decrease / increase) its cash.

    1. decrease / decrease
    2. decrease / increase
    3. increase / decrease
    4. increase / increase

    Changes in the accounts on an insurer’s income statement ultimately affect accounts on the insurer’s balance sheet. An increase in an insurer’s revenues (income statement) generally results in an increase in the insurer’s assets (balance sheet), assuming income was received in the same accounting period in which it was earned. Likewise, an increase in an insurer’s expenses (income statement) generally results in a decrease in the insurer’s assets (usually cash on the balance sheet). The amount of a company’s net income or net loss reported on the income statement and the statement of owners’ equity is reflected in the owners’ equity portion of the company’s balance sheet.

    The cash flow statement is also linked to the balance sheet. For a given accounting period, the final entry on the cash flow statement—”Cash and cash equivalents as of end of year”—is equal to the amount listed in the Cash and cash equivalents account on the insurer’s balance sheet. Similarly, the statement of owners’ equity (for stock insurers: the statement of stockholders’ equity) is linked to the balance sheet. The end-of-year balances for common stock, retained earnings, accumulated other comprehensive income (AOCI), and total stockholders’ equity match those shown on the balance sheet for each account.
