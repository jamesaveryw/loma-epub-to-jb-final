Figure 1.8

Regulatory Agencies and Organizations

Important regulatory agencies and organizations for insurance companies include

  • Securities and Exchange Commission (SEC): regulates insurers that are public companies and insurers that sell variable products
  • American Institute of Certified Public Accountants (AICPA): influences accounting practices and develops generally accepted auditing standards (GAAS)
  • Financial Accounting Standards Board (FASB): Establishes and promotes the use of U.S. GAAP
  • Public Company Auditing Oversight Board (PCAOB): sets standards for external auditors of public companies
  • National Association of Insurance Commissioners (NAIC): Specifies the format and content of the U.S. Annual Statement and uses it to evaluate insurer financial condition and compliance
  • Internal Revenue Service (IRS): Enforces the tax code and strongly influences the practice of tax accounting

The Balance Sheet and the Income Statement

[A timeline a year long, with the beginning and end each labeled “December 31”]

A major goal of a company’s accounting activities is producing two major financial statements: the balance sheet and the income statement.

[An arrow appears pointing to beginning of the timeline labeled “Balance Sheet”]

The balance sheet shows the company’s financial position at a specific moment in time. It summarizes the company’s assets, liabilities, and owners’ equity on a certain date, usually the end of a specific accounting period.

[The space between the two ends of the time line is labeled “Income Statement]

The income statement summarizes what the company earned and what it spent (revenues and expenses) during a specific period.

[An arrow appears pointing to end of the timeline labeled “Balance Sheet”]

At the end of the accounting period covered by the income statement, the new balance sheet summarizes the company’s financial position on the new date.

Short-term assets. Converted to Cash or Consumed within 1 year.

[A business person holding fanned out cash bills.]

The most liquid asset is cash. Cash does not even need to be converted into cash. Cash is already cash!

Closing out Temporary Accounts

Suppose a company had the following revenues and expenses during the year.

[Timeline with Revenues above and Expenses below. Revenues of $1,000, $800, $500, $900 are marked above the timeline. Expenses of $2,000, $100, and $500 are marked below the timeline.]

Totals for this year: Revenues ($3,200) minus Expenses ($3,600) = Net Income ($600).

At the end of the accounting period, the company closes out the Revenue and Expense accounts on the income statement, so they start with a zero balance at the beginning of the new accounting period.

[Timeline fades out and previous Revenues and Expenses are wiped out showing only $0 for each.]

[Balance sheet appears with Assets = $5,000, Liabilities = $4,000, Owners’ Equity = $1,000.]

Then the company adds the net income from the income statement to its Owners’ Equity account on the balance sheet.

The $600 in net income increases Owners’ Equity by $600.

[Owners’ Equity on the balance sheet increases to $1,600.]

To keep the equation in balance, total assets and/or liabilities also change. For simplicity, we’ll say that our company’s assets increase by $600.

[Assets on the balances sheet increase to $5,600.]

After closing out the temporary accounts (income statement) to the permanent accounts (balance sheet), the operating cycle begins anew with the next accounting period.

The timeline moves to the left off screen. A new timeline with the same labels moves on screen from the right.]

Video 2.4

Normal Debit and Credit Balances

Accounts with normal debit balance:

  • Debits increase the account balance
  • When debits are greater than credits, the account balance is positive (> 0)
  • At the end of the accounting period, debits normally exceed credits

Accounts with normal credit balance:

  • Credits increase the account balance
  • When credits are greater than debits, the account balance is positive (> 0)
  • At the end of the accounting period, credits normally exceed debits

Accounts with normal debit balance:

  • Asset accounts
  • Expense accounts

Accounts with normal credit balance:

  • Liability accounts
  • Revenue accounts
  • Owners’ equity accounts

[Man walks on screen]

You might be thinking, “How am I supposed to remember that!?” One way is to memorize it.

Here’s another way. Remember the basic accounting equation?

[Screen is wiped blank.]

Assets = Liabilities + Owners’ Equity

Revenues usually increase Assets, while expenses usually reduce them. So, we can expand the equation like this:

Assets = Liabilities + Owners’ Equity + Revenues - Expenses

If you add Expenses to both sides of the equation, you get:

Assets + Expenses = Liabilities + Owners’ Equity + Revenues

[An arrow pointing to the left side of the equation.]

Assets and Expenses are on the left side.

[An arrow pointing to the right side of the equation.]

Liabilities, Owners’ Equity, and Revenues are on the right side.

[Arrows pointing to both sides of the equation.]

Debits are associated with the left, and credits with the right.

[Label under the left arrow, “Normal Debit Balance,” appears.]

Therefore, asset accounts and expense accounts have a normal debit balance.

[Label under the right arrow, “Normal Credit Balance,” appears.]

And liability, owners’ equity, and revenue accounts all have a normal credit balance.

GAAP Characteristics

Predictive quality has five primary characteristics:

Understandable: People can comprehend the information and use it to make decisions

Relevant: Useful, timely, and likely to affect decisions

Reliable: Accurate, objective, free of bias and misrepresentation

Comparable: Users can compare different companies or the same company over time

Consistent: Accounting approach is the same over time

Two secondary characteristics also contribute to predictive quality:

Material: Contains all significant information

Cost effective: Cost to report information is reasonable for its importance

Depreciation

Emphatic Life Insurance Company purchases a new computer system for $1,000,000. The system’s estimated useful life is 5 years, and the salvage value is $200,000.

Yearly depreciated expense is ($1,000,000 – $200,000) ÷ 5; or $800,000 ÷ 5; or $160,000

The system’s value depreciates by $160,000 every year until it reaches the salvage value of $200,000 five years later.

Video 3.1

The Realization Principle and the Matching Principle

The Gunnison Life Insurance Company issues a group disability insurance policy to the Coney Hill Company.

[A timeline appears with markers for January 1, March 31, June 30, September 30, and December 31.]

The policy will provide coverage to Coney Hill’s employees from January 1 through December 31. The annual premium for this policy is $40,000. The premium is payable in quarterly installments of $10,000.

[An arrow appears below the timeline marker for January 1, labeled “$10,000”. An arrow appears below the timeline marker for March 31, labeled “$10,000, April 1.” An arrow appears below the timeline marker for June 30, labeled “$10,000, July 1.” An arrow appears below the timeline marker for September 30, labeled “$10,000, October 1.” The arrows fade out.]

However, Coney Hill decides to prepay the entire $40,000 annual premium on January 1. How will Gunnison recognize this annual premium?

[An arrow appears below the timeline marker for January 1, labeled “$40,000.”]

In accordance with the realization principle, it recognizes revenue when it is earned.

[The arrow below January 1 fades out.]

So even though Gunnison received $40,000 at the start of the first quarter…

[“$40,000 received” appears above the January 1 marker.]

… it earned only $10,000 during the first quarter.

[“$10,000 earned” appears below “$40,000 received.” They both fade out.]

It recognizes $10,000 on its first quarterly income statement.

[“Income Statement, $10,000” appears above the March 31 marker.]

It earned $10,000 more in each of the second, third, and fourth quarters and so recognizes $10,000 on each quarterly income statement.

[“Income Statement, $10,000” appears above the June 30 marker while an arrow appears below. Then both fade out. “Income Statement, $10,000” appears above the September 30 marker while an arrow appears below. Then both fade out. “Income Statement, $10,000” appears above the December 31 marker while an arrow appears below. Then both fade out.]

Suppose Gunnison incurred $6,800 of expenses for Coney Hill’s insurance coverage during the first quarter. How will the insurer recognize this expense?

[“Expenses, $6,800” appears between January 1 and March 31 on the timeline.]

According to the matching principle, the company recognizes expenses as it earns the revenues related to those expenses.

[“Expenses, $6,800” fades out.]

So Gunnison recognizes the first quarter expenses on the first quarter income statement.

[An arrow appears under the March 31 marker, while “Expenses, $6,800” appears above.]

Financial Statement Users

Financial Statement Users:

  • Policyowners and employees seek a general status report about the company
  • Company managers and directors use information to make certain decisions
  • Investors decide whether to purchase a company’s stocks or bonds or make some other kind of investment in the company

  • Potential creditors decide whether to make a loan or extend some other form of credit to a company

Cash Flow Example

Last year, the Origami Life Insurance Company received $10 million in premium income and paid $8 million in contractual benefits.

[An office building appears. “$10,000,000” flows into the building, and “8,000,000” comes out the other side.]

Origami’s cash flow statement therefore includes a $2 million net cash inflow from operating activities.

Operating Activities:

$10,000,000 - $8,000,000 = $2,000,000.

The company also sold $150,000 of bonds from its asset portfolio and purchased $160,000 in mortgages.

[A bond certificate for $150,000 flows into the building, and a contract with $160,000 on it flows out.]

The cash flow statement includes a $10,000 net cash outflow from investing activities.

Investing Activities:

$150,000 - $160,000 = -$10,000.

Last, Origami paid $50,000 in cash dividends to stockholders during 2018.

[A money bag labeled “$50,000 Cash Dividends” flows out of the building.]

So, Origami’s cash flow statement includes a $50,000 net cash outflow from financing activities.

[The office building fades out.]

Financing Activities:

$0 - $50,000 = -$50,000.

Cash Flow from Operating Activities: $2,000,000.

Cash Flow from Investing Activities: -$10,000.

Cash Flow from Financing Activities: -$50,000.

Cash Flow from All Activities: $1,940,000.
