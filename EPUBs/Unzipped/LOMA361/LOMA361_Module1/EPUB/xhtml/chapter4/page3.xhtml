Financial Statements

Recall that financial statements are summary reports of a company’s financial condition on a specified date or of its performance over a specified period. The preparation of financial statements is the end product of financial accounting, which we discussed in Chapter 1.

An insurer’s financial statements must contain enough information for users—both internal and external—to gain a general understanding of the insurer’s financial condition, activities, and prospects for growth. Figure 4.1 presents examples of users of an insurance company’s financial statements.

Financial statements follow a general protocol to make them easy to read, interpret, and compare. The heading of each financial statement generally contains the (1) name of the company, (2) name of the financial statement, (3) specified date or accounting (reporting) period covered by the financial statement, and (4) denomination used in reporting the financial information. Companies usually round off the reported monetary amounts to the nearest thousand or million. In addition to these general conventions, each type of financial statement has a specific format that is best suited to the kinds of financial information contained in that statement.

The following sections discuss a company’s key financial statements: the balance sheet, the income statement, the cash flow statement, and the statement of owners’ equity. Our focus is on the GAAP financial statements that an insurer includes in its Annual Report.

 

Balance Sheet

Recall that a balance sheet is a financial statement that shows a company’s financial condition or position by summarizing the company’s assets, liabilities, and owners’ equity as of a specified date. Typically that date is the last day of the accounting period, such as March 31, June 30, September 30, or December 31. A company’s balance sheet is considered to be a static report because it provides a snapshot of the company’s financial position as of a specified date.

On their GAAP balance sheets, most U.S. insurers present their assets in accordance with SEC regulations, which state that investments are listed first, followed by cash and other assets. The financial report below is an example of an insurer’s GAAP balance sheet.

The balance sheet shows a user what a life insurer owns (its assets), what it owes (its liabilities), and what it has retained (its owners’ equity). A user can also determine whether an insurer is solvent by examining its balance sheet. Remember that, to be considered solvent, a life insurer must have an amount of assets that exceeds the amount of its liabilities. Steadfast is solvent, because its total assets of $251,846,000 exceed its total liabilities of $238,229,000.

 

Income Statement

As we’ve discussed, an income statement is a financial statement that reports an insurer’s revenues and expenses during a specified accounting period and indicates whether the insurer experienced net income or a net loss during the accounting period. The income statement is considered to be a dynamic report because it presents the flow of a company’s financial transactions during a specified accounting period, rather than as of a specified date.

The income statement shows a user whether a life insurer is profitable: Is it earning net income or incurring a net loss? Remember that, for a specific accounting period, an insurer experiences (1) net income when its revenues exceed its expenses, and (2) a net loss when its expenses exceed its revenues. Users also examine this statement to identify how an insurer is earning funds (its revenues), as well as how it is spending them (its expenses).

Statement of Comprehensive Income

Investments play a critical role in the operations of life insurers, and life insurers can have substantial capital gains and losses on those investments. These capital gains and losses can be categorized as either realized or unrealized:

  • A realized capital gain (loss) generally results from the actual sale of an asset.

  • An unrealized capital gain (loss) generally occurs while the insurer is still holding the asset; in other words, an unrealized capital gain or loss does not result from the sale of an asset.

Realized capital gains and losses on invested assets must be reported on an insurer’s GAAP income statement. According to the FASB, a life insurer has two options for reporting unrealized capital gains and losses on its invested assets: (1) in its income statement or (2) in a separate statement of comprehensive income, which immediately follows the insurer’s income statement.

The statement of comprehensive income presents a company’s comprehensive income for a given reporting period. Comprehensive income (CI) is the total change in equity for a reporting period, excluding changes resulting from investments by owners and distributions to owners. In other words, CI measures the changes in equity that result from non-owner transactions. Comprehensive income is the sum of net income and other items—known as other comprehensive income (OCI)—that are not reported on the income statement because they have not been realized.

In equation format, we have:

CI = Net income + OCI

Examples of OCI include

  • Unrealized capital gains and losses on certain investments, which we discuss in detail in the next chapter

  • Gains and losses resulting from translating the financial statements of foreign subsidiaries

  • Actuarial gains and losses related to pensions and other postretirement benefits

Because OCI is not reported on an insurer’s income statement, it is not included in the calculation of the insurer’s net income. Why is OCI excluded from an insurer’s net income? The components of OCI generally come from noncore business operations and onetime or unusual transactions. If these components were added to net income, the resulting figure could present an inaccurate view of the company’s operating performance.

To prevent this result, OCI accumulates on the statement of owners’ equity in a section called “accumulated other comprehensive income” or “AOCI.” We discuss the statement of owners’ equity later in this chapter. The balance of AOCI is also presented in the owners’ equity section of the balance sheet.

Cash Flow Statement

A cash flow statement, also called a statement of cash flows, is a financial statement that shows a company’s cash receipts, cash disbursements, and the net change in cash during a specified accounting period. It provides information about a company’s change in cash between the company’s current balance sheet and previous balance sheet. Like the income statement, the cash flow statement is a dynamic report.

The primary purpose of a cash flow statement is to demonstrate how a company manages its cash over time. Like all companies, insurance companies need to ensure that they have enough cash available to pay expenses. An insurer’s balance sheet may reveal billions in assets, but a portion of those assets need to be in cash. Otherwise, an insurer could be forced to sell assets for less than they are worth to pay its expenses. On the other hand, an insurer that holds too much idle cash could be missing out on investment opportunities. Therefore, managing cash effectively is one of an insurer’s most important tasks.

Cash Inflows and Cash Outflows

A cash flow is any movement of cash into or out of an organization; cash flows may either be sources of funds or uses of funds for the organization. In the context of accounting,

  • A cash inflow is a movement of cash into an organization; cash inflows are sources of funds. Cash inflows include cash receipts, or money received by an insurer—from premium income, investment income, and fee income, for example.

  • A cash outflow is a movement of cash out of an organization; cash outflows are uses of funds. Cash outflows include cash disbursements, or money that the insurer has paid for policy benefits, expenses associated with investment purchases, ongoing business expenses, and so on.

Changes in an insurer’s cash flows occur as a result of three activities, as presented in the chart below.

Type of Activity

Transactions Resulting in Cash Inflows

Transactions Resulting in Cash Outflows

Operating activities

 

Transactions that involve a company’s core business operations and that directly determine the company’s net income

 

  • Premiums paid by customers

  • Receipt of interest and dividends

  • Fee income and other revenue

  • Receipts from reinsurance claims

 

  • Contractual benefit payments to policyowners and beneficiaries

  • Payments to employees and suppliers

  • Payment of taxes

Investing activities

 

Transactions that involve a company’s purchase or sale of long-term assets and the lending of funds to other companies

 

  • Sales of bonds, mortgages, stocks, property, buildings, and other long-term assets

  • Receipt of principal on loan repayments

 

  • Purchases of bonds, mortgages, stocks, property, buildings, and other long-term assets

  • Disbursements of loans to borrowers

Financing activities

 

Transactions that involve borrowed funds and cash payments to or from a company’s owners*

 

*Note that a company’s investing activities involve the securities of other companies, whereas financing activities involve the company’s own securities.

 

  • Common stock issue and sale

  • Bond (or note) issue and sale

  • Receipt of loan proceeds from creditor

 

  • Payments of cash dividends to stockholders

  • Payment of principal on debt securities such as bonds, loans, and notes

If the cash inflows exceed the cash outflows for an activity, then the result for that activity is a net cash inflow. Conversely, if the cash outflows exceed the cash inflows, then the result is a net cash outflow. For an accounting period, the net cash inflow (outflow), which is the difference between cash inflows and cash outflows, represents the net change in cash—that is, the increase or decrease in cash.

Theoretically, a company’s net change in cash equals the difference between the amount of cash and cash equivalents as shown on any two consecutive balance sheets, such as the company’s current and previous balance sheet. You can calculate a company’s net cash flow during a specified period by subtracting the company’s cash outflows from its cash inflows. You can also determine net cash flow by subtracting the company’s uses of funds from its sources of funds.

Net cash inflow (Net cash outflow) = Cash inflows – Cash outflows

OR

Net cash inflow (Net cash outflow) = Sources of funds – Uses of funds

On a typical cash flow statement for a life insurer, you won’t see the terms cash inflow (from cash receipts), cash outflow (from cash disbursements), or net cash inflow/outflow. Instead,

  • Cash inflow is represented by a positive amount

  • Cash outflow is represented by an amount in parentheses—that is, a negative amount

For example, looking back at Steadfast’s cash flow statement, under Cash Flows from Operating Activities, Steadfast had a “Change in premiums and fees receivable” of 97, representing a cash inflow of $97,000. In contrast, Steadfast had a “Change in accrued investment income” of (21), representing a cash outflow of $21,000. In addition, for the three types of activities—operating, investing, and financing—the summary line for each generally uses the phrase “net cash” rather than “net cash inflow” or “net cash outflow.” Again, whether the amount is an inflow or outflow is indicated by a positive number (no parentheses) or a negative number (in parentheses).

Statement of Owners’ Equity

The statement of owners’ equity, also known as the statement of changes in owners’ equity, is a financial statement that provides information about changes in owners’ equity accounts between two sequential balance sheets. Stock insurers typically refer to the statement of owners’ equity as either the statement of stockholders’ equity or the statement of changes in stockholders’ equity. Like the income statement and cash flow statement, the statement of owners’ equity is considered to be a dynamic report.

For a stock insurer, events that cause its stockholders’ equity accounts to change include

  • Issuing stock

  • Purchasing treasury stock

  • Retaining net income

  • Paying cash dividends on stock

  • Accumulating other comprehensive income

Mutual insurers and fraternal insurers often prepare a statement of policyowners’ equity or statement of policyholders’ equity for their customers. Because these companies do not issue common stock, mutual insurers and fraternal insurers typically experience changes in owners’ equity only as a result of changes in net income and surplus. Financial Statement Integration

In the context of financial statements, one phrase you might read or hear is financial statement integration. This phrase simply means that a company’s financial statements are interrelated.

First of all, for a given accounting period, the income statement is linked to the balance sheet, because changes in the accounts on an insurer’s income statement ultimately affect accounts on the insurer’s balance sheet. For example, in general, an increase in an insurer’s revenues (income statement) ultimately results in an increase in the insurer’s assets (balance sheet).

EXAMPLE

Example. When an insurer receives investment income or a premium from a policyowner, these transactions ultimately increase not only the insurer’s revenues (income statement), but also its cash (balance sheet).

Conversely, in general, an increase in an insurer’s expenses (income statement) ultimately results in a decrease in the insurer’s assets (balance sheet).

EXAMPLE

Example. When an insurer pays contractual benefits or agent commissions, these transactions ultimately increase the insurer’s expenses (income statement) but decrease its cash (balance sheet).
