Financial Reports

A U.S. insurance company includes its financial statements in a variety of reports, the most important of which are its Annual Report and its Annual Statement. Recall that the Annual Report is a GAAP financial report, whereas the Annual Statement is a statutory financial report. The following sections discuss these two key financial reports, along with related reports.

 

Annual Report

As we’ve discussed, an Annual Report is a document that a company’s management sends to interested parties to explain the company’s financial performance during the past year. Think of a company’s Annual Report as a report card; investors, regulators, potential customers, and others use the information in this report to judge the company’s performance. For insurers, the Annual Report is an opportunity to advertise its products and services to customers, and, if the company is a stock insurer, promote its stock to investors.

In the United States, all publicly traded companies, including stock insurers, must publish an Annual Report for their stockholders. In addition, mutual insurers and fraternal insurers that offer variable products are required to publish an Annual Report for their policyowners. These days, most Annual Reports are electronic documents that individuals can access through a company’s website.

A stock insurer’s Annual Report usually includes the following information:

  • A letter to stockholders. Generally, the letter comes from the company’s president or chief executive officer (CEO) and describes the company’s performance over the past year, emphasizing particular successes and examining possible areas in need of improvement.

  • A description of financial highlights for the year. The letter is typically followed by a table of financial highlights that summarizes the company’s financial achievements during the previous five to 10 years. This section usually includes graphs or charts designed to illustrate trends, such as growth in the company’s net income or product revenues.

  • An independent auditor’s report. This report is a statement by an independent public accountant who has audited the financial information contained in the Annual Report. In this report, the auditor attests that the information fairly represents the operations of the company in accordance with GAAP. An auditor does not, however, issue an opinion on the accuracy of the information in the company’s financial statements.

  • The audited financial statements—the balance sheet, income statement, cash flow statement, and statement of owners’ equity. The balance sheet must show data covering the past two years, whereas the other financial statements must show data for the past three years.

  • Notes to the financial statements and supplementary information. Accompanying or immediately following the financial statements are the notes to the financial statements, which are factual in nature and disclose the details behind some of the amounts presented in the financial statements. For example, one typical note explains the accounting policies that the company followed in preparing its financial statements.

  • Management discussion and analysis of financial condition and results of operations (MD&A). The MD&A is in narrative form and presents an analysis and interpretation of the company’s financial statements and company performance from management’s point of view. For example, during a period of low interest rates, management might explain why the company’s interest-sensitive products and investments did not perform as well as expected.

In the United States, insurers that are required to publish an Annual Report must also file the following reports with the SEC:

  • The Form 10-K is an SEC-required version of the Annual Report. To eliminate the need to create two separate reports, many insurers include in their Annual Reports all the information that the SEC requires for the Form 10-K.

  • The Form 10-Q is a quarterly report designed to keep the SEC and investors apprised of the company’s performance and condition during the year. The Form 10-Q differs from the Form 10-K in several ways. For example, the financial statements in the Form 10-K must be audited, whereas those in the Form 10-Q do not have to be audited. In addition, whereas the Form 10-K must include complete financial statements, the Form 10-Q can contain condensed versions of the financial statements.

  • The Form 8-K discloses any material event not previously reported that is important to investors. Examples of material events reported in the Form 8-K include mergers, acquisitions, and a change in auditors.

Annual Statement

As described earlier, the Annual Statement is a financial report that every insurer in the United States must file annually with the NAIC and the insurance regulatory organization in each state in which the insurer conducts business. The Annual Statement contains data that authorities need to evaluate a company’s solvency and compliance with insurance regulations. The Annual Statement filing for life insurance companies—which can contain more than 200 pages of financial statements, supporting schedules, and exhibits—is sometimes called the blank or the blue book.

The NAIC and most state insurance departments require insurers to file the Annual Statement electronically. Although regulators are the primary audience for the Annual Statement, other users include rating agencies, investment analysts, and a company’s managers. For example, managers frequently use Annual Statement data to compare their company’s performance to that of other companies.

In addition to the Annual Statement, life insurers must file a Quarterly Statement with the NAIC and the insurance regulatory organization in each state in which they conduct business. Although the Quarterly Statement contains a company’s complete financial statements, overall it is a much shorter report, containing fewer exhibits and schedules than the Annual Statement.

In this discussion, we focus primarily on the financial statements included in the Annual Statement. Note, however, that the Annual Statement contains many other sections, some of which are comparable to those found in the Annual Report, such as a Five-Year Historical Data exhibit, Notes to the Financial Statements, and an MD&A.

The beginning of the Annual Statement features separate pages titled

  • Assets

  • Liabilities, Surplus and Other Funds

  • Summary of Operations

  • Cash Flow

The first two pages on this list—Assets and Liabilities, Surplus and Other Funds—comprise the insurer’s statutory balance sheet. The Summary of Operations page, which shows an insurer’s net income, is the statutory version of the GAAP income statement. The Summary of Operations page also includes a section highlighting the changes in the company’s capital and surplus accounts; this section functions as the statutory counterpart to the GAAP statement of owners’ equity. Finally, Cash Flow is the statutory version of the GAAP cash flow statement. Each of these statutory financial statements contains data for the current year and the previous year.
