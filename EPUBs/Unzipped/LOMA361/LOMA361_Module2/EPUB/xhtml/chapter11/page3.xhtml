Financial Ratio Analysis

A ratio is a comparison of two numeric values expressed in the form of a fraction or a percentage. Here’s a simple example.

EXAMPLE

Example. When Charles went to work today, he had four major tasks to do by the end of the day. By 3:00, he had completed three of the tasks.

Analysis. In fraction form, Charles has completed ¾ of his tasks. To turn this fraction into a percentage, you would calculate 3 ÷ 4, which equals 0.75, and then multiply this by 100, to get 75%. So, by 3:00, Charles had completed 75% of his tasks for the day.

In general, financial ratio results of less than 1 are expressed as a percentage—for example, a result of 0.05 is presented as 5 percent. On the other hand, ratio results of 100 percent or more are typically expressed as a number—for example, a result of 500 percent would be presented as 5.

When a ratio is expressed in fraction form, the number in the top part of the fraction is the numerator and the number in the bottom part of the fraction is the denominator:

Ratio = Numerator
Denominator

Figure 11.1 summarizes the rules for interpreting changes in ratios when the ratios are expressed as fractions. We apply these rules later when we discuss specific types of ratios.

Financial ratio analysis, also called ratio analysis, consists of calculating the relationships between various pairs of financial statement values. The purpose of financial ratio analysis is to assess a company’s financial condition or performance. For example, financial ratio analysis can

  • Indicate changes or trends in a company’s operating performance

  • Help to identify a company’s strengths and weaknesses

  • Answer yes/no questions, such as whether the company’s short-term obligations can be paid on time and whether the company is collecting enough in premiums to cover its claims

An insurance company’s management uses financial ratios to measure performance at all levels of the company. In this case, the managers are engaged in internal analysis, which is financial ratio analysis undertaken by employees of the company being analyzed.

In contrast, external analysis is financial ratio analysis performed by someone outside of the company being analyzed. The regulatory ratios that we discussed in Chapter 1—IRIS ratios and the risk-based capital (RBC) ratio—are both examples of external analysis, in this case used to evaluate insurer solvency. Other stakeholders that perform external analysis include

  • Rating agencies, which need to determine the rating to assign to a particular insurance company

  • Insurance company stockholders and other investors, who need to evaluate the company’s overall profitability

  • Policyowners, who need to be able to evaluate the financial strength of insurers from whom they purchase policies

  • Reinsurers, which need to determine whether to enter into a reinsurance arrangement with a particular insurer

  • Independent agents, who need to be able to assess the financial strength of the insurers whose products they sell

Sometimes internal analysis and external analysis are combined. For example, within an insurer’s marketing department, the competitive intelligence area may take the insurer’s financial ratio results and compare them to those of the insurer’s major competitors.

In this chapter, we discuss types of financial ratios commonly used in the life insurance industry. We categorize these ratios according to what they measure:

  • Liquidity

  • Operating performance

  • Leverage

  • Capital and surplus adequacy

  • Profitability

Throughout the chapter, we illustrate the different types of ratios by applying them to a fictional U.S. stock insurer, the Evergreen Life Insurance Company. The data for these ratios comes from Evergreen’s statutory and GAAP-based financial statements.

 

Liquidity

In Chapter 2, we defined liquidity as the ease with which an asset can be converted into cash for an approximation of its underlying value. Recall that insurers must have enough cash and other liquid assets when needed to pay their contractual benefits and general business expenses on time. For insurers, illiquidity—that is, the lack of liquidity—means that an insurer lacks sufficient cash, which is not an option for an insurer, even on a short-term basis. On the other hand, highly liquid assets produce little or no return. Thus, excessive liquidity damages profits. The goal for insurance companies is to maintain a moderate level of overall liquidity. So, what’s a “moderate level”? We explain this phrase in a minute.

Liquidity ratios measure a company’s ability to meet its maturing short-term obligations. The basic liquidity ratio contains assets in the numerator and liabilities in the denominator.

Liquidity ratio = Assets
Liabilities
 

Current Ratio

The current ratio, which compares an insurer’s current assets to its current liabilities, indicates an insurer’s ability to satisfy short-term obligations without having to sell long-term investments or borrow money.

Current ratio = Current assets
Current liabilities

Recall from Chapter 2 that

  • Short-term assets—or current assets—are assets that an insurer expects to readily convert into cash or consume within the current accounting period, typically one year. For insurers, current assets include cash and cash equivalents.

  • Short-term liabilities—or current liabilities—are financial obligations that a company must pay in one year or less. A significant portion of an insurer’s current liabilities are contractual benefit obligations expected to come due within one year.

Now let’s look at the phrase “moderate level of overall liquidity.” In terms of the current ratio, life insurers generally aim for a value of at least 1.0. A current ratio result of 1.0 indicates that an insurer’s current assets equal its current liabilities. What if the value for this ratio is less than 1.0? In this case, the insurer has less in current assets than it has in current liabilities. This insurer could be forced to sell long-term or less liquid investments at a discount or borrow money to satisfy its short-term obligations.

What about values of greater than 1.0? An insurer with a current ratio result of 2.0 has twice as much in current assets as it has in current liabilities. Remember, though, that an insurer doesn’t want the value of this ratio to be too high, because a high value typically indicates that the insurer is holding too much in current assets, which don’t provide much of a return.

So far, we’ve been discussing the values of the current ratio at a single point in time. However, insurers and analysts generally track the changes in the current ratio over time. For example, an increase in the value of a current ratio between two consecutive balance sheet dates generally indicates that the insurer has more liquidity now than during the previous reporting period. An increase in the value of the current ratio results when there is (1) an increase in the value of current assets in the numerator and/or (2) a decrease in the value of current liabilities in the denominator.

EXAMPLE

Example. In 2018, Evergreen had current assets of $9,900,000 and current liabilities of $11,000,000.

Analysis. For 2018, Evergreen’s current ratio was 0.90, found as $9,900,000 ÷ $11,000,000. In 2017, Evergreen’s current ratio was 1.25, so Evergreen had lower liquidity in 2018 than it had in 2017.

Analysts also find it helpful to compare one company’s ratio results with that of another.

EXAMPLE

Example. In 2018, the Poplar Life Insurance Company had current assets of $12,000,000 and current liabilities of $6,000,000.

Analysis. Poplar’s current ratio for 2018 was 2.0, found as $12,000,000 ÷ $6,000,000, which means that Poplar had greater liquidity in 2018 than did Evergreen, all other factors remaining equal.

 

Nonadmitted Assets Ratio

Another liquidity ratio that insurers use is the nonadmitted assets ratio, which compares an insurer’s nonadmitted assets to its surplus.

Nonadmitted assets ratio = Nonadmitted assets
Surplus

Recall that, under SAP, nonadmitted assets comprise an insurer’s highly illiquid assets. Therefore, this ratio measures the extent to which an insurer’s asset portfolio may be illiquid. In general, the lower the value for this ratio, the better, because a lower value indicates that the insurer has a smaller amount of nonadmitted, or highly illiquid, assets.

EXAMPLE

Example. Use the following statutory data for 2018 to calculate the nonadmitted assets ratios for Evergreen and Poplar:

Annual Statement Value Evergreen Poplar
Nonadmitted Assets $172,800 $200,000
Surplus $2,880,000 $5,000,000

Analysis. For 2018, Evergreen’s nonadmitted assets ratio was 0.06, or 6%, found as $172,800 ÷ $2,880,000. Poplar’s nonadmitted assets ratio was 0.04, or 4%, found as $200,000 ÷ $5,000,000. Because it was lower, Poplar’s nonadmitted assets ratio would be considered better than Evergreen’s ratio, all other factors remaining equal.

Operating Performance

Insurers use several types of ratios to measure their operating performance, such as the general expense ratio, the benefits ratio, and the lapse and surrender ratio. The first two ratios indicate how an insurer’s products are performing, by comparing expenses or benefits paid to premium income. The lapse and surrender ratio measures how well the company is doing in retaining its customers.

Insurers generally use statutory data to calculate these three ratios, because this data is more conservative than is GAAP data.

 

General Expense Ratio

For insurance companies, the general expense ratio expresses, for a given reporting period, an insurer’s general insurance expenses as a percentage of premiums, annuity considerations, and considerations on supplementary contracts with life contingencies. General insurance expenses include such items as rent, salaries and wages, legal fees, medical examination fees, advertising, and rental of equipment. Note that contractual benefit expenses are not a component of general insurance expenses.

General expense ratio = General insurance expenses
Premiums and considerations

Analysts use general expense ratios to compare insurers, as well as to analyze an insurer’s performance over time. In general, the lower the general expense ratio, the better. For example, a general expense ratio below 100 percent indicates that the insurer is collecting more premiums and considerations than it is paying out in general insurance expenses. Conversely, an insurer that has a general expense ratio above 100 percent is paying out more in general insurance expenses than it is collecting in premiums and considerations. In recent years, average general expense ratios for life insurers have ranged from 8.5 percent to 10.0 percent. 1

EXAMPLE

Example. Use the following statutory data to calculate the general expense ratios for Evergreen and Poplar:

2018 Statement Value Evergreen Poplar
General Insurance Expenses $700,000 $975,000
Premiums and Considerations $7,000,000 $6,500,000

Analysis. For 2018, Evergreen’s general expense ratio was 0.10, or 10%, found as $700,000 ÷ $7,000,000, whereas Poplar’s general expense ratio was 0.15, or 15%, found as $975,000 ÷ $6,500,000. These results indicate that Evergreen’s expenditures were less of a percentage of new premiums written than were Poplar’s. In addition, Poplar’s results exceed the typical range in the industry, indicating that it is not managing its general insurance expenses as well as most other life insurers.

 

Benefits Ratio

For life insurance companies, the benefits ratio compares, for a reporting period, the benefits paid by an insurer to premiums, annuity considerations, and considerations on supplementary contracts with life contingencies.

Benefits ratio = Benefits paid
Premiums and considerations

Examples of benefits paid include death benefits on life insurance policies, benefit payments on annuities, and disability benefits.

For this ratio, insurers typically aim for a result of less than 90 percent, and the recent industry average ranges from 40 percent to 45 percent. 2

EXAMPLE

Example. In 2018, Evergreen’s benefits paid were $2,940,000, and its premiums and considerations were $7,000,000.

Analysis. For 2018, Evergreen’s benefits ratio was 0.42, or 42%, found as $2,940,000 ÷ $7,000,000. For 2018, Evergreen’s benefits ratio fell within the range for the industry average.

 

Lapse and Surrender Ratio

Another important ratio for insurers is the lapse and surrender ratio, which compares the number of life insurance policies or annuity contracts that either lapsed or were surrendered during the current year to the average number of policies in force during the current year.

Lapse and surrender ratio = # of policies lapsed or surrendered during current year
Average number of policies in force during current year

where

Average number of policies in force during current year = (Number of policies in force at end of prior year + Number of policies in force at end of current year)
2

In this context, a lapse is defined as the termination of an insurance policy or annuity contract for nonpayment of premium. Lapse and surrender ratios can vary from product to product, but in general, an insurer wants its lapse and surrender ratios to match those specified by actuaries when they priced the product. In recent years, the industry average for this ratio has been between 5 percent and 6 percent. 3

EXAMPLE

Example. During 2018, 5,500 of Evergreen’s term life insurance policies either lapsed or were surrendered. The number of policies in force at the end of 2017 was 105,000; and the number of policies in force at the end of 2018 was 95,000.

Analysis. The average number of policies in force during 2018 was 100,000, calculated as (105,000 + 95,000) ÷ 2. Therefore, the lapse and surrender ratio for these policies was 5.5%, calculated as 5,500 ÷ 100,000, which falls within the range for the industry average.

Leverage

The leverage effect is an important concept in finance. The leverage effect, also called leverage, is a financial effect in which the presence of fixed costs—either operating costs or financing costs—automatically magnifies the potential risks and returns to the company’s owners.

The relationship between leverage, risk, and returns (earnings) is such that, as a company’s leverage

  • Increases, the company’s exposure to risk of loss increases, and the company’s potential for earnings also increases

  • Decreases, the company’s exposure to risk of loss decreases, and the company’s potential for earnings also decreases

EXAMPLE

Example. Evergreen can borrow money at 6% interest. If Evergreen can invest the borrowed money and earn a return of 8%, then Evergreen will earn a 2% return on the borrowed funds. In this case, the increase in leverage magnifies the returns to the company’s owners. However, by borrowing this money at 6% interest, Evergreen has accepted an obligation to meet a fixed interest expense. This obligation magnifies the risk to Evergreen: if Evergreen can’t find an investment that earns at least 6%, the company will lose money every time Evergreen pays interest on the borrowed funds.

 

Types of Leverage

The leverage effect applies to all companies, including insurance companies. Three main types of leverage are

  • Operating leverage, which is the effect whereby incurring fixed operating costs automatically magnifies a company’s risks and potential returns.

  • Financial leverage, which is the effect whereby incurring fixed financing costs—usually borrowed funds—automatically magnifies a company’s risks and potential returns. The previous example illustrates financial leverage.

  • Total leverage, which is the combined effect of operating leverage and financial leverage; total leverage represents the effect whereby incurring these fixed costs automatically magnifies both a company’s risks and potential returns.

 

Positive Leverage and Negative Leverage

Operating leverage, financial leverage, and total leverage can be either positive or negative:

  • The effect of earning a larger profit or higher potential returns as a result of leverage is called a positive leverage effect.

  • The effect of earning a smaller profit, lower potential returns, or incurring a loss as a result of leverage is called a negative leverage effect. When a company experiences negative leverage, it finds itself in a worse position than if it had not taken on the additional fixed costs in the first place.

EXAMPLE

Example. Returning to our previous example, if Evergreen borrows money at 6% interest, and then invests the borrowed funds to earn 8% interest, Evergreen will experience positive financial leverage. Conversely, if Evergreen earns only 5% on the invested funds, the company will experience negative financial leverage.

 

Leverage Ratios

Now that we’ve examined the concept of leverage, we’re ready to discuss leverage ratios. Leverage ratios are financial ratios that compare the amount of an insurer’s obligations with the insurer’s ability to meet those obligations. The basic leverage ratio consists of liabilities in the numerator and capital in the denominator.

Basic leverage ratio = Liabilities
Capital

In general, the lower the value of a leverage ratio, the stronger an insurer’s financial position. A decrease in the value of a leverage ratio results from (1) a decrease in the value of liabilities in the numerator and/or (2) an increase in the value of capital in the denominator.

The basic leverage ratio focuses on a company’s debt obligations in relation to its ability to meet those obligations. Remember, though, that life insurance companies typically have little or no debt in the form of borrowed money that must be repaid with interest. Instead, life insurers have a significant amount of their liabilities in the form of reserves for contractual benefits. Thus, a more meaningful leverage ratio for insurers replaces (1) liabilities with contractual reserves and (2) capital with capital and surplus.

The insurance leverage ratio, also called the gross leverage ratio, does just that: it compares an insurer’s contractual reserves to its capital and surplus.

Insurance leverage ratio = Contractual reserves
Capital and surplus

For this ratio, a low result, such as a range of 4.0 to 6.5, is generally more desirable than a high result.

EXAMPLE

Example. At year-end 2018, Evergreen had $16,000,000 in contractual reserves and $4,000,000 in capital and surplus.

Analysis. Evergreen’s insurance leverage ratio in 2018 was 4.0, found as $16,000,000 ÷ $4,000,000. This result falls within the range of 4.0 to 6.5 mentioned above.

Another important leverage ratio for insurers is the debt-to-surplus ratio, which measures the relationship between an insurer’s total liabilities and its surplus.

Debt-to-surplus ratio = Total liabilities
Surplus

This ratio is especially important for mutual insurers and fraternal insurers, because they have no capital accounts but do have surplus. In general, insurers aim to have a low debt-to-surplus ratio, such as between 4.0 and 6.5.

EXAMPLE

Example. For 2018, Evergreen had $17,280,000 in total liabilities and $2,880,000 in surplus.

Analysis. Evergreen’s debt-to-surplus ratio in 2018 was 6.0, found as $17,280,000 ÷ $2,880,000. This result falls within the range of 4.0 to 6.5 mentioned above.

Capital and Surplus Adequacy

Insurance companies measure their financial strength using capital and surplus ratios. Capital and surplus ratios, also called capitalization ratios, are financial ratios used to express the relationship between an insurer’s capital and surplus and its liabilities. Capital and surplus ratios are sometimes referred to as simply capital ratios for stock companies or surplus ratios for mutual companies. In general, the higher the value of capital and surplus ratios, the stronger the insurer’s financial position.

The combined capital and surplus ratio, which measures an insurer’s long-term solvency, consists of capital and surplus in the numerator divided by total liabilities in the denominator:

Capital and surplus ratio = Capital and surplus
Total liabilities

EXAMPLE

Example. In 2018, Evergreen had $4,000,000 in capital and surplus and $17,280,000 in total liabilities.

Analysis. Evergreen’s capital and surplus ratio for 2018 was 23.15%, found as $4,000,000 ÷ $17,280,000, which we round to 23%. For 2017, Evergreen’s capital and surplus ratio was 21%. Therefore, the company’s financial strength increased from 2017 to 2018, based on these results.

One drawback of the basic capital and surplus ratio is that it is based on unweighted values, which result in unweighted ratios. Unweighted capital and surplus ratios are not suitable for comparing insurers that have different exposures to risk. To account for different risk levels, financial analysts typically apply weighted values. A weighted value is an amount that has been multiplied by a specified percentage.

One example of a weighted ratio is the risk-based capital (RBC) ratio we discussed in Chapter 1. A common formula for calculating the RBC ratio is

RBC ratio = Total adjusted capital (TAC)
Authorized control level risk-based capital (ACL RBC)

Although details on the components of the RBC ratio—TAC and ACL RBC—are beyond the scope of this text, you should be aware that insurers compare their own RBC ratio results to those of other insurers. So what’s a typical RBC ratio result for a U.S. life insurer? Virtually all U.S. life insurers have RBC ratio results of greater than 250 percent. In fact, as of year-end 2016, the average RBC ratio result for U.S. life insurers was 1,040%. 4

The basic capital ratio looks like the capital and surplus ratio, except without surplus:

Capital ratio = Capital
Total Liabilities

EXAMPLE

Example. For 2018, Evergreen’s capital was $1,120,000, and its total liabilities were $17,280,000.

Analysis. Evergreen’s capital ratio in 2018 was 6.48%, found as $1,120,000 ÷ $17,280,000, which we round to 6.5%. For 2017, Evergreen’s capital ratio was 5.0%. So, these ratios’ results indicate an increase in the company’s financial strength from 2017 to 2018.

Profitability

Profitability ratios measure how efficiently a company uses its assets and equity to generate net income or a net gain from operations. Thus, profitability ratios measure the success of a company’s growth and focus on whether the company is prospering. The basic profitability ratio consists of net income or a gain from operations in the numerator, divided by the assets invested or equity used to earn that net income or gain in the denominator.

Profitability ratio = Net income or gain from operations
Assets invested or equity used to earn net income or gain

Generally, an increase in the value of a profitability ratio indicates higher profitability. An increase in the value of a profitability ratio results from (1) an increase in the value of net income or a gain from operations in the numerator and/or (2) a decrease in the value of the assets invested or equity used in the denominator. In choosing an appropriate measure of profit, you could choose either an insurer’s profit before taxes—its “gain (or income) from operations”—or its profit after taxes—its “net income.”

Insurers and analysts use a variety of profitability ratios to evaluate a company’s ability to generate returns consistently to the company’s owners. In this section, we discuss the return on equity ratio (ROE), return on assets ratio (ROA), investment yield ratio, and net profit margin. The data for these ratios comes from an insurer’s GAAP-based balance sheet and income statement.

 

Return on Equity Ratio

The return on equity ratio (ROE) measures how efficiently an insurer uses its owners’ equity to generate net income. The formula is

ROE = Net income
Average owners’ equity

where

Average owners’ equity = (Owners’ equity as of beginning of period + Owners’ equity as of end of period)
2

Stock insurers usually try to achieve ROE results between 6 percent and 15 percent. In contrast, mutual insurers generally aim for results between 6 percent and 8 percent. (In general, mutual insurers don’t focus as heavily on profits as do stock insurers.) However, the ROE target partly depends on how much risk the company assumes. The higher the risk, the higher the ROE that company owners expect. For example, a risk-averse insurer might find a ROE of 5 percent to be acceptable; on the other hand, an insurer with a higher risk tolerance might be dissatisfied with anything less than 15 percent for its ROE.

EXAMPLE

Example. For 2018, Evergreen’s net income was $637,000, and its owners’ equity as of the beginning of the reporting period was $3,962,250; its owners’ equity was $4,000,000 at the end of the reporting period.

Analysis. Evergreen’s average owners’ equity was $3,981,125, calculated as ($3,962,250 + $4,000,000) ÷ 2, or $7,962,250 ÷ 2. Therefore, Evergreen’s ROE was 16%, calculated as $637,000 ÷ $3,981,125, which slightly exceeds the range listed above. However, Evergreen has a relatively high risk tolerance, so management is satisfied with this result.

 

Return on Assets Ratio

The return on assets ratio (ROA) indicates an insurer’s success in using assets to generate net income by comparing net income to average total assets.

ROA = Net income
Average total assets

where

Average total assets = (Total assets as of beginning of period + Total assets as of end of period)
2

In the insurance industry, the average ROA is generally between 0.5 percent and 1.0 percent. Company owners like to see high ROA results, because they indicate substantial profitability; however, insurers and regulators typically prefer lower results, because they indicate lower risk on an insurer’s invested assets. Rating agencies often use ROA to rank companies within the same industry.

EXAMPLE

Example. For 2018, Evergreen’s net income was $637,000, and its total assets as of the beginning of the reporting period were $23,000,000; its total assets at the end of the reporting period were $21,280,000.

Analysis. Evergreen’s average total assets for the period were $22,140,000, calculated as ($23,000,000 + $21,280,000) ÷ 2, or $44,280,000 ÷ 2. Therefore, Evergreen’s ROA was 2.87%, found as $637,000 ÷ $22,140,000, which we round to 2.9%. This result exceeds the industry average, so Evergreen’s management would want to investigate the reasons for this result.

 

Investment Yield Ratio

The investment yield ratio, also called the net yield, shows how efficiently the insurer used its invested assets to earn a return. This ratio compares the insurer’s net investment income to the insurer’s average invested assets for the reporting period.

Investment yield ratio = Net investment income
Average invested assets

where

Average invested assets = (Total invested assets as of beginning of period + Total invested assets as of end of period)
2

An unusually high investment yield could indicate excessively risky investments, whereas a low investment yield usually indicates inadequate returns on invested assets. Thus, an insurer’s investment managers seek an investment yield that is neither too high nor too low, although the definition of these extremes can vary by insurance company. In recent years, the industry average for this ratio has been between 4.6 percent and 5.1 percent. 5

EXAMPLE

Example. For 2018, Evergreen’s net investment income was $900,000. Its total invested assets as of the beginning of the reporting period were $17,000,000, and its total invested assets at the end of the reporting period were $19,000,000.

Analysis. Evergreen’s average invested assets for the period were $18,000,000, calculated as ($17,000,000 + $19,000,000) ÷ 2, or $36,000,000 ÷ 2. Therefore, Evergreen’s net investment yield was 5%, calculated as $900,000 ÷ $18,000,000, which falls within the range listed above.

 

Net Profit Margin

The net profit margin, or return on revenue ratio, shows how much after-tax profit is generated by each dollar of total revenue.

Net profit margin = Net income
Total revenues

In the life insurance industry, net profit margins generally range from 3 percent to 8 percent.

EXAMPLE

Example. In 2018, Evergreen’s net income was $637,000, and its total revenues were $7,965,000.

Analysis. For 2018, Evergreen’s net profit margin was 7.9%, found as $637,000 ÷ $7,965,000, which we round to 8.0%. This result falls within the range of 3% to 8%.

Type of Profitability Ratio

Formula

Description

Preferred Value or Range

Return on Equity Ratio (ROE)

Net income ÷ Average owners’ equity

Measures how efficiently an insurer uses its owners’ equity to generate net income.

Stock insurers generally aim for 6% to 15%, whereas mutuals aim for 6% to 8%.

Return on Assets Ratio (ROA)

Net income ÷ Average total assets

Indicates a company’s success in using its assets to generate net income.

The industry average is generally between 0.5% and 1.0%.

Investment Yield Ratio

Net investment income ÷ Average invested assets

Shows how efficiently the insurer used its invested assets to earn a return.

In recent years, the industry average has been between 4.6% and 5.1%.

Net Profit Margin

Net income ÷ Total revenues

Shows how much after-tax profit, or net income, is generated by each dollar of total revenue.

In the life insurance industry, values generally range from 3% to 8%.
