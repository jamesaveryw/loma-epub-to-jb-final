Glossary

ABB
See activity-based budgeting. See Module 3, Chapter 15.
ABC
See activity-based costing. See Module 3, Chapter 14.
account
The basic tool that a company uses to record, group, and summarize similar types of business transactions. See Module 1, Chapter 2.
accounting
A system or set of rules and methods for collecting, processing, reporting, and monitoring financial information about a company. See Module 1, Chapter 1.
accounting controls
The policies and procedures used to authorize financial transactions, safeguard assets, and provide reliable, timely, and fairly presented financial information about a company. See Module 3, Chapter 16.
accounting entry
A record of a business transaction that includes at least one debit and one credit and shows the monetary value of the transaction in balance. See Module 1, Chapter 2.
accounting period
A specified length of time during which a company records, summarizes, and reports its financial transactions. See Module 1, Chapter 3.
accrual-basis accounting
An accounting system under which a company records revenues when they are earned and expenses when they are incurred, even if the company has not yet received the revenues or paid the expenses. See Module 1, Chapter 3.
accrued income
Income that the insurer has already earned but that is receivable after the end of the accounting period. Return to text.
accumulated depreciation
The total amount of depreciation allocated to an asset as of a specified date. Return to text.
acquisition expenses
The category of operating expenses that includes the costs for obtaining and issuing new business. Return to text.
activity
Any procedure that generates work and that incurs a cost. See Module 3, Chapter 13.
activity-based budgeting
A method for developing a budget that begins with the premise that costs are linked to products or services based on the activities performed in producing the products or services. See Module 3, Chapter 15.
activity-based costing
The process of linking costs to products or services based on the activities performed in producing the products or services. See Module 3, Chapter 14.
activity cost
The cost attributable to a specified activity. See Module 3, Chapter 14.
activity driver
The output of an activity being performed; the purpose that propels the activity. See Module 3, Chapter 13.
actuarial assumptions
The assumed values used in a life insurance or an annuity product design. Return to text.
actuary
An expert in financial risk management and the mathematics and modeling of insurance, annuities, and financial instruments. Return to text.
adjusting entry
An accounting entry that a company makes to record accruals for revenues and expenses, unearned revenues, and prepaid expenses. See Module 1, Chapter 2.
administrative controls
The policies and procedures that guide employees in conducting their normal day-to-day tasks. See Module 3, Chapter 16.
admitted asset
An asset whose full value is reported on the Assets page of the Annual Statement. Return to text.
adverse opinion
A type of modified auditor’s opinion that states that the financial statements do not fairly present the insurer’s financial condition. See Module 3, Chapter 16.
aggregate reserves
Reserves for a block of insurance policies or contracts, a line of business, or an entire company. Return to text.
AICPA
See American Institute of Certified Public Accountants. See Module 1, Chapter 1.
allocation base
A measure of use that exhibits a logical relationship between indirect costs and cost centers. See Module 3, Chapter 13.
American Institute of Certified Public Accountants
A professional association of Certified Public Accountants (CPAs) in the United States. See Module 1, Chapter 1.
amortized cost
An asset’s book value after its cost has been adjusted for applicable amortization. Return to text.
Annual Financial Reporting Model Regulation
Developed by the National Association of Insurance Commissioners (NAIC) to improve the ability of state regulators to oversee the financial condition of insurance companies, it has three requirements: (1) an annual audit of an insurer’s financial statements by an independent certified public accountant (CPA); (2) the filing of a report called Communication of Internal Control Related to Matters Noted in the Audit; and (3) the filing of a report called Management’s Report of Internal Control over Financial Reporting. See Module 3, Chapter 16.
Annual Report
In the United States, a document that a company’s management sends to interested parties to explain the company’s financial performance during the past year. See Module 1, Chapter 1.
Annual Statement
A financial report that every insurer in the United States must file annually with the National Association of Insurance Commissioners (NAIC) and the insurance regulatory organization in each state in which the insurer conducts business. See Module 1, Chapter 1.
assets
The items of value owned by a company. See Module 1, Chapter 2.
asset valuation reserve
For life insurance companies in the United States, a reserve that minimizes the effects of both realized and unrealized gains and losses on an insurer’s invested assets when those gains and losses are related to the credit quality of the assets—a risk known as credit risk—or fluctuations in the prices of securities—a risk known as equity risk. Return to text.
associating cause and effect
Under GAAP, an expense recognition concept that states that expenses that have a direct relationship to specified future revenues are charged to future accounting periods instead of being charged to the current accounting period. See Module 1, Chapter 3.
association examination
In the United States, a financial condition examination of an insurer conducted by examiners representing various states in the geographic zones in which the insurer is licensed. See Module 1, Chapter 1.
audit
The examination and evaluation of company accounting records, policies, and procedures to ensure that (1) the financial information, financial statements, and source documents comply with accounting standards and are a fair and consistent depiction of the company’s financial condition and performance and (2) a company’s operational procedures and policies are effective. See Module 3, Chapter 16.
auditing
The process of conducting an audit. See Module 3, Chapter 16.
auditor’s opinion
A statement, prepared by a public accounting company, that indicates (1) whether the information contained in an insurer’s Annual Report fairly represents the insurer’s financial position, the results of its operations, and its cash flows and (2) whether the audit was conducted in accordance with applicable auditing standards. See Module 3, Chapter 16.
audit trail
A chronological, sequential set of accounting records and reports from the beginning to the end of a business transaction. See Module 3, Chapter 16.
authorization level
Within the context of an internal control system, the maximum monetary amount that a company employee has been granted official power to approve for disbursement. See Module 3, Chapter 16.
AVR
See asset valuation reserve. Return to text.
balance sheet
A financial statement that shows a company’s financial condition or position by summarizing the company’s assets, liabilities, and owners’ equity as of a specified date. See Module 1, Chapter 2.
base period
The financial reporting period chosen as the reference point for horizontal analysis. See Module 3, Chapter 14.
basic accounting equation
The formula that expresses the relationship among the three key account classifications that a company reports on its balance sheet: assets, liabilities, and owners’ equity. See Module 1, Chapter 2.
benchmarking
A process that consists of (1) identifying the best outcomes that other companies have achieved for a specific activity or process and the practices that produced those outcomes, and (2) implementing those practices to equal or surpass the best outcomes. See Module 3, Chapter 14.
benefits budget
A type of expense budget that indicates the amount of money the insurer expects to pay for insurance policy or annuity contract benefits, such as claims, cash surrenders, and policyowner dividends, during the next accounting period. See Module 3, Chapter 15.
benefits ratio
For a given reporting period, a ratio that compares an insurer’s benefits paid to its premiums, annuity considerations, and considerations on supplementary contracts with life contingencies. Return to text.
bond
A debt security issued by a borrower, typically a corporation or government entity. Return to text.
bond discount
The amount by which a bond’s purchase price is lower than its par value. Return to text.
bondholder
The purchaser, or owner, of a bond. Return to text.
bond issuer
The entity that sells a bond to raise money. Return to text.
bond premium
The amount by which a bond’s purchase price is higher than its par value. Return to text.
book value
The value at which an asset is recorded in a company’s accounting records. See Module 1, Chapter 3.
bottom-up budgeting
A budgeting approach that starts at the bottom of a company, with lower-level managers generating budgets for their areas, which are then presented in the form of recommendations to senior management. See Module 3, Chapter 15.
breakeven point
The point at which a product’s revenues are equal to its costs (expenses). See Module 3, Chapter 14.
budget
A financial plan of action, expressed in monetary terms, that covers a specified period, such as one year. See Module 3, Chapter 12.
budgeting
A management accounting process that includes creating a financial plan of action that an organization believes will help it achieve its goals. See Module 3, Chapter 15.
business transaction
A transaction to which a company must assign an objective monetary value, whether the impact on the company is large or small, actual or expected. See Module 1, Chapter 1.
capital
The amount of money invested in a company by its owners, usually through the purchase of the company’s stock. See Module 1, Chapter 1.
capital and surplus ratios
Financial ratios that measure an insurer’s financial strength by comparing the insurer’s capital and surplus to its liabilities. Return to text.
capital budget
A budget that shows a company’s plans for the financial management of its long-term, high-cost investment proposals. See Module 3, Chapter 15.
capital budgeting
The process companies undertake to analyze decisions about investing in long-term projects or assets. See Module 3, Chapter 15.
capitalize
In the context of accounting for specified costs, to record the costs as an asset. Return to text.
cash
The amount of currency on hand or on deposit at an insurer’s bank. Return to text.
cash accounting
The management and maintenance of records and reports for all of an insurer’s cash transactions, specifically money deposited or withdrawn from an insurer’s accounts at a bank or other financial institution. Return to text.
cash-basis accounting
An accounting system in which a company recognizes revenues or expenses only when it receives or disburses cash. See Module 1, Chapter 3.
cash budget
A type of budget that projects a company’s beginning cash balance, cash inflows, cash outflows, and ending cash balance for a specified accounting period, typically by quarter. See Module 3, Chapter 15.
cash budgeting
A budgeting activity that anticipates the cash flows into and out of a company during an accounting period. See Module 3, Chapter 15.
cash disbursement
The payment of cash by a company. See Module 1, Chapter 3.
cash disbursements budget
A schedule of the timing and amount of cash disbursements expected during the accounting period. See Module 3, Chapter 15.
cash dividend on stock
A cash payment that a company periodically distributes to the owners of its preferred stock and common stock. Return to text.
cash equivalents
Short-term assets that mature in 90 days or less from the date of purchase. Return to text.
cash flow
Any movement of cash into or out of an organization; cash flows may be either sources of funds or uses of funds for the organization. See Module 1, Chapter 4.
cash flow statement
A financial statement that shows a company’s cash receipts, cash disbursements, and the net change in cash during a specified accounting period. See Module 1, Chapter 4.
cash inflow
A movement of cash into an organization; cash inflows are sources of funds, which include cash receipts. See Module 1, Chapter 4.
cash outflow
A movement of cash out of an organization; cash outflows are uses of funds, which include cash disbursements. See Module 1, Chapter 4.
cash receipt
A check, money order, electronic funds transfer, or other cash transaction that is remitted to a company as a form of payment for goods or services rendered. See Module 1, Chapter 3.
cash receipts budget
A schedule of cash receipts expected during the accounting period. See Module 3, Chapter 15.
Certified Public Accountant
In the United States, the professional accounting credential that accountants who work in public accounting generally earn. See Module 1, Chapter 1.
chargeback method
A service department cost allocation method in which a department allocates its indirect costs to departments based on usage. See Module 3, Chapter 13.
chart of accounts
A numbered or alphabetical listing of all of a company’s account names. See Module 1, Chapter 2.
closing entry
An accounting entry that a company makes at the end of each accounting period to start the next accounting period with a zero balance in its temporary accounts. See Module 1, Chapter 2.
CMBS
See commercial mortgage-backed security. Return to text.
collected income
The amount of income, including certain adjustments, received in cash during an accounting period. Return to text.
collusion
A secret agreement entered into by two or more persons to perpetrate an illegal act. See Module 3, Chapter 16.
commercial mortgage-backed security
A type of mortgage-backed security that is secured by a pool of commercial mortgages. Return to text.
commission
Monetary compensation paid to an agent; usually expressed as a percentage of the gross premiums paid by customers. Return to text.
common-size financial statement
A financial statement that includes the percentages obtained from vertical analysis. See Module 3, Chapter 14.
common stock
A type of stock that usually entitles the owner to (1) vote on the selection of directors and on other important company matters and (2) receive dividends on the stock, if they are declared. Return to text.
comparability
The quality of accounting information that requires a company’s financial statements to be presented in such a way that interested users can identify similarities and differences in financial information across accounting periods and among different companies. See Module 1, Chapter 3.
comparative financial statements
Financial statements that present data for two or more accounting periods side by side. See Module 3, Chapter 14.
compliance audit
A type of audit that verifies that an insurer’s financial statements adhere to the laws, regulations, policies, and procedures prescribed by internal or external regulatory authorities. See Module 3, Chapter 16.
compound accounting entry
A record of a business transaction that affects more than two accounts. See Module 1, Chapter 2.
conservatism
The quality of accounting information that calls for a prudent reaction for dealing with uncertainty in accounting records and reports. See Module 1, Chapter 3.
consistency
The quality of accounting information that requires a company to use the same accounting policies and procedures in its financial statements from one accounting period to the next, unless there is a sound reason for changing a specified policy or procedure. See Module 1, Chapter 3.
contra account
An account that accompanies a specified "companion" account—typically an asset account—and that has a normal balance that is the opposite of the companion account. Return to text.
contractual benefit expenses
The category of general and administrative expenses that includes the costs of paying contractual obligations to policyowners and beneficiaries. Return to text.
contractual payments
Payments on supplementary contracts. Return to text.
contractual reserve
A liability that identifies the amount that, together with future premiums and investment earnings, represents the expected amount of future benefits payable on an insurer’s in-force business. Return to text.
contribution margin
The difference between a product’s total revenues and its total variable costs. See Module 3, Chapter 14.
contribution margin ratio
The relationship between a product’s contribution margin and its revenues. See Module 3, Chapter 14.
control account
An account that summarizes accounting entries and monitors the expenses that are reported in a company’s financial statements. Return to text.
controllable cost
A cost over which a responsibility manager has decision-making authority. See Module 3, Chapter 12.
controller
The head of a company’s accounting function. See Module 1, Chapter 1.
corporate bond
A bond issued by a corporation. Return to text.
cost accounting
A system for accumulating and categorizing expense data that is used to facilitate effective cost control and to generate accurate estimates of costs to use in the pricing of a company’s products. See Module 3, Chapter 13.
cost accumulation
The practice of capturing all company costs and categorizing them in meaningful ways. See Module 3, Chapter 13.
cost allocation
The accounting process of assigning or distributing to responsibility centers an indirect cost (expense) according to a specified method or formula. See Module 3, Chapter 12.
cost center
A department or division to which costs (expenses) can be traced. See Module 3, Chapter 12.
cost concept
The accounting concept that states that companies should include items on the balance sheet and income statement according to their actual cost at the time of purchase. See Module 1, Chapter 3.
cost object
Any purpose for which a company measures costs. See Module 3, Chapter 12.
cost-volume-profit analysis
A type of cost analysis used to determine how changes in product prices, sales volume, fixed costs, variable costs, and the type of products a company offers affect its profit. See Module 3, Chapter 14.
coupon payment
A periodic interest payment made on a bond. Return to text.
coupon rate
The interest rate specified on a bond. Return to text.
CPA
See Certified Public Accountant. See Module 1, Chapter 1.
credit
In accounting, a change made to the monetary value of an account that (1) increases the value of liability accounts, owners’ equity accounts, and revenue accounts and (2) decreases the value of asset accounts and expense accounts. See Module 1, Chapter 2.
credit risk
In the context of the asset valuation reserve, the risk that a financial obligation will not be paid. Return to text.
current ratio
A type of liquidity ratio that compares an insurer’s current assets to its current liabilities. Return to text.
CVP analysis
See cost-volume-profit analysis. See Module 3, Chapter 14.
DAC
See deferred acquisition costs. Return to text.
debit
In accounting, a change made to the monetary value of an account that (1) increases the value of asset accounts and expense accounts and (2) decreases the value of liability accounts, owners’ equity accounts, and revenue accounts. See Module 1, Chapter 2.
debt security
A security that represents an obligation of indebtedness owed by a business, a government, or an agency. Return to text.
debt-to-surplus ratio
A leverage ratio that measures the relationship between an insurer’s total liabilities and its surplus. Return to text.
deferred acquisition costs
According to the Financial Accounting Standards Board (FASB), costs that are related directly to the successful acquisition of new or renewal insurance contracts. Return to text.
deferred income
For individual life insurance policies, premiums that are due after the statutory reporting date but before the next policy anniversary date. Return to text.
deferred premiums
Life insurance premiums due after the statutory reporting date but before the next policy anniversary date. Return to text.
deposit-type contract
Under statutory accounting principles, a contract that does not expose the insurer to mortality risk or morbidity risk. Return to text.
depreciation
The process of allocating (spreading) the cost of an asset over the asset’s estimated useful life; depreciation typically applies to an insurer’s long-term fixed assets, such as buildings and other real estate. See Module 1, Chapter 3.
derivative security
A security that derives its value from other securities. Return to text.
detective control
An internal control that identifies—or "detects"—an error or a problem after it has occurred. See Module 3, Chapter 16.
development expenses
The category of operating expenses that includes the costs related to designing, testing, and implementing a new product. Return to text.
differential cost
The difference in cost between two alternative choices. See Module 3, Chapter 13.
direct cost
A cost identified specifically with a single cost object. See Module 3, Chapter 12.
direct method
A service department cost allocation method that assumes that service departments benefit operating departments only; thus, no consideration is given to services provided by one service department to another. See Module 3, Chapter 13.
disclaimer of opinion
A type of modified auditor’s opinion that states that the auditor has no opinion on the insurer’s financial statements. See Module 3, Chapter 16.
discretionary cost
A cost that can be decreased or even eliminated as a result of a management decision without stopping the production of a good or service. See Module 3, Chapter 13.
dividend accumulations
Dividends that a policyowner elects to leave on deposit at interest with the insurer. Return to text.
double-entry accounting
An accounting method of tracking and reporting a company’s transactions under which every accounting entry must (1) have at least one debit and one credit, (2) have an equal monetary amount of debits and credits, and (3) maintain the balance of the basic accounting equation. See Module 1, Chapter 2.
dual concept
The cornerstone of double-entry accounting, a concept that states that every business transaction has two aspects—debits and credits—that always equal each other in monetary amount. See Module 1, Chapter 2.
effective rate of return
The actual rate of return on a bond, found by dividing the bond’s annual interest by its purchase price. Return to text.
entity
The basic business unit for which a company keeps separate accounting records and prepares reports. See Module 1, Chapter 3.
entity concept
A financial statement concept that states that a company must account separately for the business activities of each business unit or economic unit. See Module 1, Chapter 3.
equity security
A security that represents an ownership interest in a business. Return to text.
escrow account
A trust account used to pay property maintenance expenses, property taxes, and other expenses related to a mortgaged property. Return to text.
examination report
A document that summarizes the results of an insurance company’s financial condition examination and that is submitted to both state regulators and the company’s officers. See Module 1, Chapter 1.
ex-dividend date
The date used for determining whether a stockholder is eligible to receive a declared cash dividend on stock. Return to text.
execution of transactions as authorized
A principle of an effective internal control system that concerns the delegation of authority to perform specified tasks and the communication of that authority. See Module 3, Chapter 16.
expense budget
A schedule of expenses expected during an accounting period. See Module 3, Chapter 15.
expenses
The amounts that a company spends in the course of conducting business. See Module 1, Chapter 2.
external analysis
A type of financial ratio analysis performed by someone outside of the company being analyzed. Return to text.
external audit
A financial audit performed by external auditors, who are employed by public accounting companies, insurance regulators, or reinsurers. See Module 3, Chapter 16.
fair value
The price that would be received to sell an asset or paid to transfer a liability in an orderly transaction between market participants on the valuation date. Return to text.
fair value accounting
The process of valuing a company’s balance sheet accounts, including assets, on the price that the asset would bring in an active, organized market in which (1) the asset sale is not the result of liquidation and (2) neither the buyer nor the seller is forced to enter the transaction. Return to text.
FASB
See Financial Accounting Standards Board. See Module 1, Chapter 1.
favorable variance
A variance in which the actual cost is lower than the standard cost. See Module 3, Chapter 12.
fidelity bond
An indemnity insurance agreement that provides coverage for a financial loss caused by (1) the act or default of a third party, such as an employee or a business partner or (2) some contingency, such as fire, over which the third party may have no control. See Module 3, Chapter 16.
financial accounting
The field of accounting that focuses primarily on reporting a company’s financial information to meet the needs of the company’s external users. See Module 1, Chapter 1.
Financial Accounting Standards Board
In the United States, a private organization to which the Securities and Exchange Commission (SEC) has delegated the responsibility for establishing generally accepted accounting principles (GAAP). See Module 1, Chapter 1.
financial condition examination
In the United States, an onsite regulatory examination of an insurer that is carried out by one or more state insurance departments and is designed to identify and monitor any threats to the insurer’s solvency. See Module 1, Chapter 1.
financial leverage
A type of leverage effect in which incurring fixed financing costs—usually borrowed funds—automatically magnifies a company’s risks and potential returns. Return to text.
financial ratio analysis
A financial analysis that consists of calculating the relationships between various pairs of financial statement values to assess a company’s financial condition or performance. Return to text.
financial reporting
The process of presenting financial information about a company’s financial position, operating performance, and flow of funds into and out of a company. See Module 1, Chapter 1.
financial statement
A summary report of a company’s financial condition on a specified date or of its performance during a specified period. See Module 1, Chapter 1.
financing activities
Transactions that involve borrowed funds and cash payments to or from a company’s owners. See Module 1, Chapter 4.
fiscal year
A 12-month accounting period chosen by a company for financial reporting purposes. See Module 1, Chapter 3.
fixed cost
A cost that remains constant for all levels of operating activity or production. See Module 3, Chapter 13.
flexible budget
A budget that provides alternative sets of budget estimates to use under different circumstances that may arise during an accounting period. See Module 3, Chapter 15.
foreclosure
A legal proceeding by which a lender can take possession of and sell a mortgaged property to recover the unpaid loan balance if the borrower fails to make timely contractual principal and interest payments on the loan. Return to text.
Form 10-K
In the United States, a version of the Annual Report that public companies, including stock insurers, must file with the Securities and Exchange Commission (SEC). See Module 1, Chapter 4.
Form 10-Q
In the United States, a quarterly report that public companies, including stock insurers, must file with the Securities and Exchange Commission (SEC) to keep it apprised of their financial performance and condition during the year. See Module 1, Chapter 4.
Form 8-K
In the United States, a special report that public companies, including stock insurers, must file with the Securities and Exchange Commission (SEC) to disclose any material event not previously reported that is important to investors. See Module 1, Chapter 4.
fraud
The intentional use of false information to mislead another party. See Module 3, Chapter 16.
full-disclosure concept
The accounting principle that states that a financial statement must contain all material information about a company and that the company must disclose any additional information or fact that, by its omission, could mislead an interested user of the company’s financial information. See Module 1, Chapter 3.
function
A series of activities that serve a specific purpose. See Module 3, Chapter 13.
functional cost analysis
A subset of activity-based costing; the process of accumulating the costs that are involved in each activity within a function. See Module 3, Chapter 14.
functional costs
The accumulated costs of the activities involved within a certain function, without regard to organizational units. See Module 3, Chapter 13.
functional unit cost
A type of functional cost that is calculated by dividing the total functional cost by an appropriate base unit. See Module 3, Chapter 14.
GAAP
See generally accepted accounting principles. See Module 1, Chapter 1.
GAAP accounting records
Accounting records designed for financial reporting to investors, noninsurance regulators (such as the SEC in the United States), and the general public. Return to text.
general account
The account in which an insurer maintains assets that support its contractual obligations for guaranteed insurance products, such as individual permanent life insurance products, fixed-rate annuities, and guaranteed minimum benefit riders under variable products. Return to text.
general and administrative expense budget
A type of expense budget that includes expenses—typically fixed expenses—needed for insurance company operations. See Module 3, Chapter 15.
general and administrative expenses
The costs—contractual benefit expenses and operating expenses—incurred as a result of undertaking normal business activities to generate sales and to support products. Return to text.
general expense ratio
For a given reporting period, a ratio that compares an insurer’s general insurance expenses to its premiums, annuity considerations, and considerations on supplementary contracts with life contingencies. Return to text.
generally accepted accounting principles
A set of financial accounting standards, conventions, and rules that all publicly traded companies must follow when summarizing transactions and preparing their financial statements. See Module 1, Chapter 1.
GIC
See guaranteed interest contract. Return to text.
going-concern concept
An accounting principle that requires a company’s accounting records to reflect the assumption that the company will continue to operate indefinitely. See Module 1, Chapter 3.
government bond
A bond issued by a government—including federal, state, provincial, county, city, and other local governments. Return to text.
gross investment income
The accrual-basis amount of investment income representing the total amount of income an insurer earned during the reporting period, before deducting expenses and amortization. Return to text.
gross paid-in and contributed surplus
Under statutory accounting, the aggregate amount paid above the par value for shares of an insurer’s own stock. Return to text.
guaranteed interest contract
A contract in which the contract holder places one or more deposits with an insurer for a stated period in exchange for repayment of those deposits plus interest at a guaranteed rate. Return to text.
hedging
The process of taking an action—such as entering into a derivatives contract—that minimizes a risk or counterbalances a given risk exposure against another. Return to text.
historical cost
The purchase price originally paid for an asset. See Module 1, Chapter 3.
horizontal analysis
A type of comparative analysis that involves calculating the absolute monetary amount of change in an item on a specified financial statement from one reporting period to another. See Module 3, Chapter 14.
hybrid security
A security that has characteristics of both debt and equity securities. Return to text.
IASB
See International Accounting Standards Board. See Module 1, Chapter 1.
IFRS Standards
A single set of global accounting standards established by the International Accounting Standards Board (IASB). See Module 1, Chapter 1.
immediate recognition
An expense recognition concept that states that a company recognizes all costs as expenses during the current accounting period. See Module 1, Chapter 3.
IMR
See interest maintenance reserve. Return to text.
income due and accrued
Income that was earned by the insurer before the financial reporting date but that has not yet been received. Return to text.
income statement
A financial statement that reports an insurer’s revenues and expenses during a specified accounting period and indicates whether the insurer experienced net income or a net loss during the accounting period. See Module 1, Chapter 2.
incompatible functions
In the context of internal control, job duties that, when combined, place an employee in a position to commit an illegal act or to conceal errors or irregularities in the normal course of his or her employment. See Module 3, Chapter 16.
index number
A statistical method for measuring the change in a variable. See Module 3, Chapter 14.
indirect cost
A cost that cannot be identified specifically with a single cost object. See Module 3, Chapter 12.
insurance leverage ratio
A leverage ratio that compares a company’s contractual reserves to its capital and surplus. Return to text.
Insurance Regulatory Information System
In the United States, a collection of financial tools and databases designed to help state insurance regulators evaluate the financial condition of insurers that operate in their states. See Module 1, Chapter 1.
interest maintenance reserve
For life insurance companies in the United States, a reserve that helps absorb realized gains and losses on an insurer’s debt securities, such as bonds, mortgages, and preferred stock, when those gains or losses are caused by changes in market interest rates rather than changes in the credit quality of the assets. Return to text.
interest-rate risk
In the context of the interest maintenance reserve (IMR), the risk that unpredictable movements in market interest rates will cause the value of a life insurer’s debt securities to change. Return to text.
internal accounting records
Accounting records designed for financial reporting to a company’s management team, whose primary interest is in having appropriate data for making decisions. Return to text.
internal analysis
A type of financial ratio analysis undertaken by employees of the company being analyzed. Return to text.
internal audit
A financial audit that is performed by company employees. See Module 3, Chapter 16.
internal control
The process in which a company takes actions designed to protect its assets, monitor the reliability of its accounting records, encourage both operational efficiency and adherence to the company’s management policies, and comply with applicable laws and regulations. See Module 3, Chapter 16.
Internal Revenue Service
Part of the U.S. Department of the Treasury, the governmental agency responsible for enforcing the provisions of laws and regulations concerning income taxes. See Module 1, Chapter 1.
International Accounting Standards Board
The standard-setting organization charged with developing and promulgating international financial reporting standards (IFRS). See Module 1, Chapter 1.
inventory
An itemized count and listing of a company’s assets, such as property, products, materials, or securities. See Module 3, Chapter 16.
investable funds
Under the investment year method of revenue allocation, funds that consist of (1) net income before taxes, (2) investment income received on prior investments, and (3) the maturities of principal on prior investments. See Module 3, Chapter 12.
investing
Employing a principal sum of money to generate earnings. Return to text.
investing activities
Transactions that involve a company’s purchase or sale of long-term assets and the lending of funds to other companies. See Module 1, Chapter 4.
investment accounting
The area of accounting that is responsible for recording all accounting entries related to the invested assets held in an insurer’s investment portfolio. Return to text.
investment budget
A budget that projects the types of investments the insurer will make and the expected amount of investment income for each type of investment. See Module 3, Chapter 15.
investment center
A department or division to which costs (expenses), revenues, and capital or investment funds can be traced. See Module 3, Chapter 12.
investment contract
According to GAAP, a long-duration contract that does not expose the insurer to significant risks arising from policyowner or contract owner mortality. Return to text.
investment expenses
The costs associated with investing the insurer’s assets. Return to text.
investment portfolio
