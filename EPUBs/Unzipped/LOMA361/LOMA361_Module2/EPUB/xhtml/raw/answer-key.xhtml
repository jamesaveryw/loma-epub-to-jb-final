  2. The following information relates to a bond sold by the Crane Life Insurance Company:

    • Bond principal = $1,000
    • Purchase price = $1,200
    • Book value of bond = $1,100
    • Net sales proceeds (NSP) = $1,350

    Select the answer choice that correctly identifies, under SAP, the amount of the gain on the sale of this bond and whether the bond sale resulted in a realized capital gain or an unrealized capital gain.

      Amount of gain Realized/Unrealized?
    A. $250 realized
    B. $250 unrealized
    C. $350 realized
    D. $350 unrealized

    Under SAP, for debt securities in general, the sale of a debt security is a Realized Capital Gain if NSP > Book Value, and a Realized Capital Loss if NSP < Book Value. In this situation, the amount of the gain on Crane’s bond sale is equal to $1,350 NSP – $1,100 Book Value = $250. An unrealized capital gain or loss does not result from the actual sale of an asset. Under SAP, an Unrealized Capital Gain results if a debt security’s Fair Value > Book Value; an Unrealized Capital Loss results if its Fair Value < Book Value.

    Under GAAP, for debt securities in general, the sale of a debt security is a Realized Capital Gain if NSP > Amortized Cost, and a Realized Capital Loss if NSP < Amortized Cost. Under GAAP, an Unrealized Capital Gain results if a debt security’s Fair Value > the Amortized Cost; an Unrealized Capital Loss results if its Fair Value < the Amortized Cost.

  3. To comply with statutory reporting requirements, U.S. life insurers must classify their assets as either admitted assets or nonadmitted assets—usually based on their liquidity—on the Annual Statement. Of the following assets, the only one that typically represents a nonadmitted asset for statutory accounting purposes is

    1. cash equivalents
    2. cash
    3. furniture and equipment
    4. most invested assets

    Admitted assets are assets whose full value is reported on the Assets page of the Annual Statement. Examples of admitted assets are cash, cash equivalents, and most invested assets. Nonadmitted assets are reported on the Assets page of the Annual Statement but are accorded no value. Most nonadmitted assets are used in the normal process of doing business, such as office furniture and equipment. Some assets are partially nonadmitted, which means that only a portion of their value is reported on the Assets page of the Annual Statement. One example of a partially nonadmitted asset is deferred tax assets.

    Admitted assets can easily be converted to cash—without a loss in value—to pay for an insurer’s obligations, whereas nonadmitted assets do not possess this quality. By not allowing an insurer to report a value (or a full value) for a nonliquid asset, statutory regulators enforce the concept of conservatism.

    Admitted assets and nonadmitted assets are only relevant for statutory reporting purposes. Under GAAP, an insurer includes values for all of its assets in its financial statements. Therefore, the asset amounts in an insurer’s Annual Report rarely equal those shown on the Assets page of the insurer’s Annual Statement.

In this situation, $40 annual bond interest ÷ $900 bond purchase price = 4.44% effective rate of return.

  6. The Stateside Company borrowed funds at a fixed interest rate of 7%, and used the borrowed funds to invest in securities that earned an 8% return. In this situation, Stateside experienced the type of leverage known as

    1. negative operating leverage
    2. negative financial leverage
    3. positive operating leverage
    4. positive financial leverage

    The relationship between leverage, risk, and returns (earnings) is such that, as a company’s leverage increases, the company’s exposure to risk of loss increases, and the company’s potential for earnings also increases. Similarly, as a company’s leverage decreases, the company’s exposure to risk of loss decreases, and the company’s potential for earnings also decreases.

    The three main types of leverage are operating leverage, financial leverage, and total leverage. Operating leverage is the effect whereby incurring fixed operating costs automatically magnifies a company’s risks and potential returns. Financial leverage is the effect whereby incurring fixed financing costs—usually borrowed funds—automatically magnifies a company’s risks and potential returns. Total leverage is the combined effect of operating leverage and financial leverage.

    Leverage can be either positive or negative. The effect of earning a larger profit or higher potential returns as a result of leverage is called a positive leverage effect. The effect of earning a smaller profit, lower potential returns, or incurring a loss as a result of leverage is called a negative leverage effect. When a company experiences negative leverage, it finds itself in a worse position than if it had not taken on the additional fixed costs in the first place. In this situation, Stateside earned a return on its invested funds that was greater than the interest rate Stateside paid on its borrowed funds; therefore, Stateside experienced positive financial leverage.
