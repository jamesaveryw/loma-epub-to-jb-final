Video 5.1

Realized/Unrealized Capital Gains/Losses

Insurers must recognize their investment gains and losses on their financial statements. That sounds simple enough, but it is done differently depending on the situation. Let’s look at the different situations.

Let’s look at the different situations.

Question 1: “Did the company sell the investment during the accounting period?”

If yes, then the gain or loss is realized.

The insurer must determine the net sales proceeds (NSP): selling price minus commissions or other costs.

If the insurer still owns the investment, the gain or loss is unrealized.

The insurer must determine, as of the statement date, the investment’s fair value—the amount the insurer could sell the investment for in the current market (not counting commissions).

If you sold the investment, you’d ask “how much did I sell it for?” If you still owned the investment, you’d ask “how much could I sell it for?”

To determine your gain or loss, compare NSP or fair value to…what?

That depends on the type of investment (debt or equity) accounting practice (GAAP or SAP).

 

Asset Sold

Asset Not Sold

Debt securities (SAP)

Compare net sales proceeds to book value

NSP ˃ Book value = Realized capital gain

NSP ˂ Book value = Realized capital loss

Compare fair value to book value

Fair value ˃ Book value = Unrealized capital gain

Fair value ˂ Book value = Unrealized capital loss

Debt securities (GAAP)

Compare net sales proceeds to amortized cost

NSP ˃ Amortized cost = Realized capital gain

NSP ˂ Amortized cost = Realized capital loss

Compare fair value to book value

Fair value ˃ Book value = Unrealized capital gain

Fair value ˂ Book value = Unrealized capital loss

Equity securities (SAP & GAAP)

Compare net sales proceeds to historical cost

NSP ˃ Historical cost = Realized capital gain

NSP ˂ Historical cost = Realized capital loss

Compare fair value to historical cost

Fair value ˃ Historical cost = Unrealized capital gain

Fair value ˂ Historical cost = Unrealized capital loss

For debt securities, like bonds, statutory accounting uses book value—the value of the investment in the company’s accounting records.

For GAAP, use the debt investment’s amortized cost—the book value reduced by an amount for amortization. Amortization is spreading out an asset’s cost over a number of years.

For equity securities, like stocks, GAAP and statutory accounting compare the NSP or fair value to the investment’s historical cost—the price originally paid for the asset.

If market interest rates fall, then the price of existing bonds rise.

[The Market Interest Rate graph shrinks below the “Coupon Rate” line and the Bond Price graph grows above the “Par Value” line.]

If market interest rates are lower than the bond’s coupon rate, the bond will sell for more than the par value. This is known as “selling at a premium.”

None of these affect the amount of interest the bond issuer pays:

Characteristics of Contra Accounts

Characteristics of Contra Accounts

How does a contra account appear in a company’s records?

It typically accompanies a companion account.

The companion account is typically what kind of account?

An asset account.

What is the normal balance of a contra account?

The opposite of the normal balance of the companion account.

For example, if the companion account is an asset account, it has a debit balance, which means the contra account has a credit balance.

What effect does a contra account typically have on its companion account?

The contra account usually decreases the balance of the companion account.

Can the amount in a contra account be larger than the amount in the companion account?

No.

What is the purpose of a contra account?

To provide additional information on the transactions that occur in the companion account.

What is a typical example of a contra account?

Accumulated Depreciation is a contra account; its companion asset account is Real Estate.

Chapter 8

Figure 8.1

AVR vs. IMR

The purpose of both the AVR and the IMR is to allow investment losses to affect capital and surplus gradually, avoiding short-term fluctuations.

The ending balance of both the AVR and the IMR are generally reported on the statutory balance sheet as a liability.

The AVR absorbs gains and losses caused by credit risk and equity risk; the IMR absorbs gains and losses caused by interest-rate risk.

The AVR typically applies to all types of invested assets, including bonds, preferred stock, derivatives, mortgages, common stock, and real estate; the IMR typically applies to debt securities, such as bonds, mortgages, mortgage-backed securities, and preferred stock.

The AVR applies to both realized and unrealized gains and losses; the IMR applies to realized gains and losses only.

Selling Stock and the Balance Sheet

Assets = Liabilities + Owners’ Equity

When an insurer issues common or preferred stock, it receives cash, which increases its total assets.

It doesn’t receive all the proceeds from the sale. It pays transaction costs, such as to an investment bank to facilitate the stock issue.

Issuing stock does not affect liabilities.

Issuing stock increases Owners’ Equity by the same amount as Assets, keeping the accounting equation in balance.

Video 9.3

Effects of Nonadmitted Assets on Capital and Surplus

Under SAP, nonadmitted assets, such as office equipment, are accorded no value on the Annual Statement.

Because they are given no value, you might think they don’t affect an insurer’s surplus. But they do, when they are bought or sold.

When an insurer sells a nonadmitted asset…it generally receives cash, an admitted asset.

The insurer’s admitted assets increase. Its capital and surplus also increases, to keep the accounting equation in balance.

Assets = Liabilities + Owners’ Equity

The insurer generally pays cash, an admitted asset…when it purchases a nonadmitted asset.

The insurer’s admitted assets decrease. The insurer’s surplus also decreases, to keep the accounting equation in balance.

To summarize, reducing (by selling) nonadmitted assets causes both its admitted assets and capital and surplus to increase.

Increasing nonadmitted assets causes both admitted assets and capital and surplus to decrease.

Video 9.4

Surplus Notes under SAP and GAAP

Assets = Liabilities + Owners’ Equity

A surplus note is a special type of financing available only to insurers. It’s a debt security that an insurer can sell to an institutional investor to raise cash.

When an insurer sells a surplus note, the cash received increases the insurer’s assets.

Under SAP, the surplus note is classified as part of Surplus. So, selling a surplus note increases the insurer’s Surplus, which is part of owners’ equity.

Under GAAP, the surplus note is classified as a liability. So, selling a surplus note increases the insurer’s liabilities.

Repaying interest and principal on a surplus note decreases Cash and decreases…

  • Surplus under SAP
  • Liabilities under GAAP

Collected Income: The amount of income, including certain adjustments, received in cash during an accounting period. Collected premium income can have both an earned portion and an unearned portion if the policy anniversary date is after the financial reporting date.

Unearned income: A subset of collected income that has not yet been earned. An unearned premium is that portion of the insurance premium received in one reporting period, but applicable to the insurance coverage to be provided in the following period but before the next policy anniversary date. Unearned premiums usually apply to disability income (DI) insurance premiums and not to individual life insurance premiums or annuity considerations.

Uncollected income: Income, including uncollected premiums on individual life insurance, that is due before the financial reporting date, but which the insurer has not received as of the financial reporting date.

Accrued income: Income that the insurer has already earned but which is receivable after the end of the accounting period. The income is a result of a contractual arrangement and is legally collectible. Accrued income typically applies to investment income, such as interest or dividends on an insurer’s invested assets. Premium income typically is not classified as accrued income because individual policyowners have no legal obligation to pay premiums.

Deferred income: Deferred premiums for individual life insurance policies are due after the statutory reporting date but before the next policy anniversary date. Deferred premiums apply to premium payments made more frequently than annually—for example, monthly or quarterly. Deferred Premiums, an asset account, is established to offset the overstated reserve, a liability.

Figure 10.3

Major Insurance Company Expenses

Investment expenses are the costs associated with investing the insurer’s assets.

General and administrative expenses are the costs incurred as a result of undertaking normal business activities to generate sales and to support products. General and administrative expenses include contractual benefit expenses and operating expenses.

Contractual benefit expenses are the costs of paying contractual obligations to policyowners and beneficiaries.

Operating expenses are the costs of business operations other than contractual benefit expenses. Operating expenses include acquisition expenses, maintenance expenses, development expenses, and overhead expenses.

Acquisition expenses are the costs for optaining and issuing new business. Examples of acquisition expenses include first-year agent commissions, contract issue expenses, advertising expenses, and underwriting expenses.

Maintenance expenses are product-related costs that are incurred on in-force policies and contracts. Examples of maintenance expenses include renewal commissions, some agency or branch office expenses, and customer service operating expenses.

Development expenses are costs related to designing, testing, and implementing a new product. Examples of development expenses include new administrative software expenses and new business license fees.

Overhead expenses are the costs of normal business operations that are not directly connected with any product. Examples of overhead expenses include utility expenses, some agency or branch office expenses, and some information systems expenses.
