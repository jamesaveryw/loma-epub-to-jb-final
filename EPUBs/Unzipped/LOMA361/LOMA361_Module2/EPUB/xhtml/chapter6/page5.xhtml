Accounting for Other Assets

Insurance companies own and maintain many assets in addition to the ones we have discussed. This section briefly covers accounting for amounts recoverable from reinsurers, policy loans, deferred acquisition costs, and the insurer’s general account and separate accounts.

 

Amounts Recoverable from Reinsurers

Reinsurance is insurance that one insurance company, known as the direct writer or ceding company, purchases from another insurance company, known as the reinsurer or assuming company, to transfer all or part of the risk on insurance policies that the direct writer has issued. When an insurer obtains reinsurance on a policy, the insurer is usually responsible for paying benefits to any and all policy beneficiaries. However, because the reinsurer has assumed a portion or all of the risk associated with the policy, the reinsurer is typically obligated to pay its contracted share of the benefits to the direct writer—in this case, the insurer—to comply with the terms of the reinsurance agreement. The money that the reinsurer is obligated to pay the insurer is an asset to the insurer. Let’s look at a simple example of how an insurer would account for a death claim payment on a reinsured life insurance policy.

EXAMPLE

Example. The Sunflower Life Insurance Company issues an individual life insurance policy with a $250,000 death benefit. Sunflower reinsures $50,000 of the risk on this policy. While the policy is in force, the insured dies and Sunflower determines that the policy beneficiary is entitled to the $250,000 death benefit. To record the payment of the death benefit, Sunflower makes the following accounting entry:

Death Claims Paid…………………..……..….250,000

Cash…………………………………..………..…….………250,000

To record payment of the death benefit to a policy beneficiary.

Upon the death of the insured, the reinsurer owes Sunflower $50,000 under the terms of its reinsurance agreement. Sunflower records the amount recoverable from the reinsurer as follows:

Amount Recoverable from Reinsurer………50,000

Death Claims Paid………………………………………50,000

To record the amount recoverable from the reinsurer.

Upon receipt of the $50,000 from the reinsurer, Sunflower would record the following accounting entry:

Cash…………………………………………..………....50,000

Amount Recoverable from Reinsurer………………50,000

To record the receipt of payment for the claim recoverable from the reinsurer.

In the above example, Amount Recoverable from Reinsurer is a statutory account.

 

Policy Loans

Cash value life insurance policies include a savings element—known as the cash value—that a policyowner can use in a number of ways. One important use of the cash value is as a loan. A policy loan, called a certificate loan in fraternal benefit societies, is a loan made to a policyowner and secured by the policy’s cash value as collateral. Policy loans are unique to life insurance companies, and this asset account can have a substantial balance in it. The basic accounting entry to record a policy loan is a debit to an asset account, such as Policy Loans or Contract Loans, and a credit to Cash, another asset account.

Because insurers charge interest on policy loans, accounting for policy loans involves keeping detailed records of the principal and interest on each policy loan. Insurers typically calculate policy loan interest so that it is payable on the policy anniversary date. However, some insurers charge policy loan interest at the end of the policy year, and a few insurers charge interest in advance. Policyowners usually pay the accrued policy loan interest when repaying the principal on a policy loan. The typical accounting entry to record the receipt of principal and interest on a policy loan consists of a debit to Cash, a credit to Policy Loans or Contract Loans, and a credit to Investment Income for the interest portion of the loan.

For statutory reporting purposes, policy loans are typically considered admitted assets. They are reported at book value, which means the original loan amount, plus accrued interest, minus principal repayments. Contract Loans, an asset account, is reported separately from Investment Income, a revenue account. However, for financial reporting purposes, policy loan interest that is due and accrued is reported as part of Investment Income Due and Accrued, an asset account.

 

Deferred Acquisition Costs

The FASB defines deferred acquisition costs (DAC), also known as deferred policy acquisition costs (DPAC), as costs that are related directly to the successful acquisition of new or renewal insurance contracts. Under U.S. GAAP, insurers capitalize these costs and then amortize them over several years. Note that this definition includes the word successful. In the past, some companies were capitalizing all acquisition-related costs, even though some of these costs were related to unsuccessful attempts to acquire new or renewal business. This approach created problems, because when these costs were deferred and then amortized into earnings in future periods, the insurer had no corresponding revenue to which the expenses could be matched.

According to the FASB, typical examples of DAC are policy issue costs, underwriting costs, and agent commissions. Deferred Acquisition Costs is a long-term asset account that appears on a balance sheet prepared under U.S. GAAP, but not on the statutory balance sheet. Under statutory accounting in the United States, the insurer reports costs for new products during the accounting period in which they are incurred.

 

The General Account and Separate Accounts

In Chapter 1, you learned that U.S. life insurance companies that offer variable products—also known as nonguaranteed products—are subject to SEC regulation. Here we discuss another requirement for these companies: they must maintain one or more separate accounts to manage the funds associated with these variable products. So, what’s a separate account? And how is it related to an account that all life insurers have—the general account?

For a life insurer, the general account and any separate accounts contain assets that back the insurer’s products:

  • The general account is the account in which an insurer maintains assets that support its contractual obligations for guaranteed insurance products. Products typically backed by general account assets include individual permanent life insurance products—such as whole life insurance and universal life insurance—fixed-rate annuities, and guaranteed minimum benefit riders under variable products. General account assets typically are subject to the claims of an insurer’s general creditors in the event of the insurer’s insolvency. In addition, the insurer bears the investment risk associated with the assets in the general account.

  • A separate account, also called a segregated fund, is an account that the insurer maintains separately from its general account for managing the funds associated with nonguaranteed (variable) insurance products, such as variable universal life insurance and variable annuities. Although the vast majority of the products in separate accounts are nonguaranteed, some separate accounts contain guaranteed (nonvariable) products, such as modified guaranteed annuities. Unlike general account assets, separate account assets are generally insulated from the claims of an insurer’s general creditors in the event of the insurer’s insolvency. In general, the customer bears all or most of the investment risk associated with separate account products.

Just like general account assets, separate account assets belong to the insurer. The difference, however, is that the insurer generally establishes a liability to its customers who own separate account products. This liability reflects the customers’ direct financial interest in the separate account assets held by the insurer.

The discussion that follows applies to life insurers that offer both guaranteed and nonguaranteed insurance products—in other words, life insurers that maintain a general account and one or more separate accounts. For these life insurers, statutory reporting includes two types of annual statements: (1) the Annual Statement and (2) the Annual Statement of the Separate Accounts, which we abbreviate as the Separate Accounts Annual Statement.

 

The Annual Statement

The Annual Statement is “the blank” or “blue book” that we discussed earlier in the text. The Annual Statement reports in detail on an insurer’s general account business; however, it also contains summary information on the insurer’s separate accounts business. For example, any net income reported on the Separate Accounts Annual Statement—excluding unrealized capital gains and losses—is included on the Annual Statement’s Summary of Operations. In addition, in the notes to the financial statements, a note is included describing the characteristics of the company’s separate accounts business.

 

The Separate Accounts Annual Statement

The Separate Accounts Annual Statement, sometimes called the “green book,” generally reports on the investment activities of all the insurer’s separate accounts. Like the Annual Statement, the Separate Accounts Annual Statement includes the pages that form the statutory balance sheet and income statement, along with information on reserves and an analysis of operations by lines of business. However, it includes no notes to the financial statements nor any types of noninvestment schedules. Because of these differences, the Separate Accounts Annual Statement is typically much shorter than the Annual Statement.

In addition, whereas the Annual Statement has a quarterly and annual version, the Separate Accounts Annual Statement has only an annual version. The Separate Accounts Annual Statement also includes a unique exhibit called “Guaranteed insurance and annuity products”—or “Exhibit 6”—that separates the liabilities for guaranteed products from those for nonguaranteed products. Regulators use this exhibit to differentiate between the separate account liabilities that represent risks for the company versus those that represent risks for customers.

In addition to reporting on the investment activities of the insurer’s separate accounts, the Separate Accounts Annual Statement reports on the net flow of funds between the general account and the separate accounts. All inflows to and outflows from the Separate Accounts Annual Statement—excluding investment returns and activity—enter or leave the Statement through the general account Annual Statement. For example, premiums on separate accounts business are recorded as premiums in the Annual Statement’s Summary of Operations and are then transferred to the Summary of Operations page in the Separate Accounts Annual Statement. In contrast, the investment activities of an insurer’s separate accounts—investment purchases and sales, capital gains and losses, and investment income—are reported only in the Separate Accounts Annual Statement.
