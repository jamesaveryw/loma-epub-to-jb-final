Accounting for Short-Term Assets

Life insurers maintain detailed accounting records for short-term assets available for the payment of contractual benefits and business expenses. Recall that short-term assets are assets that a company expects to convert to cash or consume within the current accounting period, typically one year. Typical short-term assets held by insurers include cash and cash equivalents, investment income due and accrued, deferred premiums, uncollected premiums, and prepaid expenses.

 

Cash and Cash Equivalents

Cash is the amount of currency on hand or on deposit at an insurer’s bank. Cash equivalents are short-term assets that will mature in 90 days or less from the date of purchase. Cash equivalents are highly liquid investments—that is, they can be readily converted to cash with little or no loss in value. One example of a cash equivalent is a Treasury bill that will mature within 90 days or less of the date of purchase. Treasury bills, also called T-bills, are short-term assets issued and guaranteed by the U.S. government.

Cash accounting, sometimes called treasury operations or cash management, refers to the management and maintenance of records and reports for all of an insurer’s cash transactions, specifically money deposited in or withdrawn from an insurer’s accounts at a bank or other financial institution. The cash accounting function might also include the management of cash equivalents and short-term invested assets because these assets can usually be readily converted to cash.

Cash accounting typically involves the oversight of one or more transactions (checking) accounts with a bank or other financial institution. For example, an insurer might have separate checking accounts for premium collection, claim payments, and agent commissions. Because these accounts, even if interest-bearing, generally pay a lower rate of interest than is available on other short-term assets, the insurer tends to maintain in these accounts only the minimum cash balance required to pay its current obligations.

For financial reporting purposes, an insurer’s cash balance as of a specified date is the total amount of cash in its Cash account. In addition to cash and cash equivalents, insurers have other short-term assets, including (1) investment income due and accrued, (2) deferred premiums and uncollected premiums, and (3) prepaid expenses. Short-term assets, with the exception of prepaid expenses, are typically considered admitted assets for U.S. statutory accounting purposes.

 

Investment Income Due and Accrued

Investment income due and accrued is a short-term asset for insurers in the United States. Income due and accrued is income that was earned by the insurer before the financial reporting date but that has not yet been received. This income is classified as an asset because the insurer can expect to receive the income as a result of contractual arrangements. Investment Income Due and Accrued is the account used to report due and accrued investment income for U.S. statutory accounting purposes. An insurer indicates in a note to the financial statements the amount of investment income due and accrued that the insurer expects to receive after the financial reporting date.

Deferred Premiums and Uncollected Premiums

Life insurers use short-term asset accounts—such as Deferred Premiums or Uncollected Premiums—to record and report life insurance policy premiums that (1) are due after the statutory reporting date but before the next policy anniversary date or (2) were due before the statutory reporting date but have not been received yet.

Deferred premiums are life insurance premiums due after the statutory reporting date but before the next policy anniversary date. The Deferred Premiums account is specific to life insurance companies that comply with U.S. statutory accounting principles. Moreover, this account applies only to life insurance premium payments that (1) are made more frequently than annually and (2) have a due date that falls between the statutory reporting date and the policy’s next anniversary date.

Prepaid Expenses

In accounting, prepaid expenses are those expenditures, paid in advance, that the insurer expects will provide a future value or benefit. Examples of prepaid expenses include rent, subscriptions, service contracts, and any other charges that the insurer has paid before receiving the goods and services associated with those charges. Because prepaid expenses are not readily available to satisfy policyowner obligations, SAP considers them to be nonadmitted assets. Under GAAP, however, prepaid expenses are classified as short-term assets, and they are charged to expense in the accounting period in which the benefits are received or used.
