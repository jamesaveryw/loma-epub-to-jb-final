Accounting for Asset Transactions

Now we are ready to look at how insurance companies account for asset transactions. A company records the purchase of an asset by debiting an asset account and crediting another asset account, a liability account, an owners' equity account, or a combination, based on how the company pays for the asset. In most cases, the purchase of an asset affects another asset account, such as Cash, or a liability account, such as Accounts Payable, Notes Payable, or Mortgages Payable.

Similarly, the company records the sale of an asset by crediting the asset account and debiting another asset account, liability account, owners’ equity account, or a combination, according to how the company receives payment for the asset. Most asset sales affect another asset account—such as Accounts Receivable, Notes Receivable, or Mortgages Receivable—rather than an owners’ equity account.

The insight below presents several sample accounting entries to record the purchase and sale of an asset. Keep these general transactions in mind as we discuss how the terms of asset purchases and sales affect an insurer’s various accounts and, ultimately, the insurer’s financial condition.

INSIGHT

Accounting Entries for the Purchase and Sale of an Asset

In the following examples, we ignore the effects of transaction costs, such as brokerage commissions.

ASSET PURCHASES

 

When an insurer records the purchase of an asset, the accounting entry used depends on whether the purchase was for cash, on account, or on account with a cash down payment.

Assume that the insurer purchases Asset A for cash.

The appropriate accounting entry would be

Asset A……………..$$$

Cash…………………..$$$

To record the purchase of Asset A for cash.

The purchase of Asset A increased the value of the insurer’s Asset A account by the value of the purchased asset, and decreased the insurer’s Cash—another asset account—by the same amount. Note that this transaction does not change the insurer’s total assets, nor does it affect any of the insurer’s liability accounts or owners’ equity accounts.

Now assume that the insurer purchases Asset A on account.

The appropriate accounting entry would be

Asset A…………………………...…..$$$

Accounts Payable—Asset A……………..$$$

To record the purchase of Asset A on account.

In this transaction, the insurer incurs a liability—the Accounts Payable—that the insurer promises to pay the seller in the future. Recall that a liability account has a normal credit balance, so a credit is used to record an increase in the value of this liability. Here, the insurer’s total assets increased by the value of the purchased asset and the insurer’s total liabilities increased by the same amount because of the increase in Accounts Payable—Asset A.

Now assume that the insurer purchases Asset A by making a cash down payment and incurring the remaining amount as a liability to be paid in the future.

The appropriate accounting entry would be

Asset A………………………..$$$

Cash………………………………….......$

Accounts Payable—Asset A……..$$

To record the purchase of Asset A on account with a cash down payment.

Recall that each accounting entry must have an equal monetary value for its debits and credits. So, in this transaction, the sum of the debits—the monetary amount assigned to the Asset A account—must equal the sum of the credits—Cash plus Accounts Payable—Asset A.

ASSET SALES

 

When an insurer records the sale of an asset, the accounting entry used depends on whether the sale was for cash, on account, or on account with a cash down payment.

Assume that the insurer sells Asset A for cash.

The appropriate accounting entry would be

Cash..……………..$$$

Asset A……………..$$$

To record the sale of Asset A for cash.

Note that the accounting entry used to record the sale of an asset for cash is the opposite of the entry made when purchasing the asset for cash, assuming there is no gain or loss.

Now assume that the insurer sells Asset A on account.

The appropriate accounting entry would be

Accounts Receivable—Asset A……………………..$$$

Asset A……………..…………….…………………………..$$$

To record the sale of Asset A on account.

In this transaction, the insurer sold Asset A and, instead of cash, received an asset referred to as Accounts Receivable—Asset A. This account receivable is a contractual promise made by the purchaser of Asset A to the insurer, the seller of Asset A, to pay for the asset in the future. Accounts receivable are considered to be assets for the insurer, provided the purchaser pays the insurer for Asset A in the agreed-upon time.

Now assume that the insurer sells Asset A on account, with a cash down payment.

The appropriate accounting entry would be

Cash……………………………….……….....$

Accounts Receivable—Asset A……..$$

Asset A………………………..$$$

To record the sale of Asset A on account, with a cash down payment.

In this transaction, the insurer is the holder, or owner, of the receivable. As in the previous example, the receivable is considered an asset, provided the purchaser pays the insurer for Asset A in the agreed-upon time.
