Investment Accounting

When life insurance companies sell their products, they receive premiums from customers in return for promising to provide future benefits in specified circumstances. To ensure their ability to meet these benefit obligations as they come due, life insurers take part of these premium payments and invest them. Investing refers to employing a principal sum of money to generate earnings. In a financial sense, investing usually involves using a sum of money to (1) purchase assets or (2) deposit funds into a financial institution. Typical examples of an insurer’s investments, or invested assets, are bonds, mortgages, and stock.

Investment accounting is the area of accounting that is responsible for recording all accounting entries related to the invested assets held in an insurer’s investment portfolios. In this context, an investment portfolio is a collection of various investments owned by the same organization, usually assembled to meet a defined set of financial goals.

The invested assets in a portfolio can be further classified as securities. For insurance companies, important examples of securities are debt securities, equity securities, hybrid securities, and derivative securities.

  • A debt security is an obligation of indebtedness owed by a business, a government, or an agency. Important examples of debt securities for life insurers are bonds and mortgages issued by other companies or entities. Debt securities represent loans made by the purchaser of the security to the issuer of the security.

  • An equity security is an ownership interest in a business. The most important equity security for life insurers is common stock. By purchasing the common stock of another company, the insurer hopes to receive (1) income in the form of cash dividends and (2) growth in the value of that stock upon its eventual sale.

  • A hybrid security has characteristics of debt and equity securities. Hybrid securities are becoming more common in insurance company portfolios because they often combine the best features of debt securities and equity securities. Preferred stock is an example of a hybrid security.

  • A derivative security derives its value from other securities. Types of derivatives that are important to insurers are options, forward contracts, futures contracts, and swaps.

Life insurance companies hold a much greater monetary share of their assets in debt securities than in equity securities. Insurers prefer debt securities in part because their investment risk is generally lower than that of equity securities. Equally important to insurers, however, are the characteristics of debt securities—their fixed maturity dates and fixed interest-payment schedules. These characteristics ensure that adequate funds are available to insurers when needed to pay contractual obligations. Of the debt securities held by life insurance companies, bonds are the clear industry favorite, as illustrated in Figure 5.2.

 

Bonds

A bond is a debt security that a bond issuer, typically a corporation or government entity, sells to raise money. The purchaser of a bond, or owner of a bond, is known as the bondholder. In return for the purchase price of the bond, the bondholder typically receives from the bond issuer

  • Periodic interest payments, known as coupon payments, usually on a semiannual basis—every six months—in the United States. The interest rate specified on a bond is called the coupon rate, also known as the stated rate. A bond’s coupon rate is a percentage of its par value, which we discuss below.

  • Payment of the bond’s par value by a specified maturity date. Typical maturities for bonds fall in the five-year to 30-year range. The par value of a bond is the designated legal monetary value assigned to the bond. Other terms used for par value are principal and face amount. When a bond issuer first sells a bond, the selling price is usually the bond’s par value, typically $1,000. Thereafter, the value of the bond fluctuates according to competitive market forces.

EXAMPLE

Example. The Kona Corporation recently issued bonds with a par value of $1,000, a maturity date of 10 years, and semiannual coupon payments at a 5% annual coupon rate. The Peaberry Insurance Company purchased 100 Kona bonds at a par value of $1,000 per bond. Peaberry plans to hold the Kona bonds until maturity.

Analysis. In this situation, the bond issuer is Kona, and the bondholder is Peaberry. Over the 10-year period of holding the Kona bonds, Peaberry will receive a coupon payment of $50 in interest per year, or $25 in interest twice per year, on each bond. On the maturity date of these bonds, Peaberry will receive the par value of $1,000 on each bond.

Figure 5.3 presents a simplified example of a bond certificate.

Bonds issued by corporations are called corporate bonds, whereas those issued by governments—including federal, state, provincial, county, city, and other local governments—are called government bonds. Although the largest proportion of an insurer’s bond investments are corporate bonds, insurers also invest a considerable amount of money in government bonds.

Insurers typically maintain detailed accounting records—including the purchase price, par value, coupon rate, coupon payment dates, name of the bond issuer, issue date, and maturity date—for each bond that they own. They also report summary information about their bond investments within their financial statements.

 

Bond Purchases, Sales, and Valuation

When an insurer purchases a bond, it typically records the bond’s value at the price that it paid for the bond, whether or not that amount matches the bond’s par value. An insurer may purchase a bond at a price that differs from the bond’s par value. An insurer may also sell a bond that it owns at a price that differs from the bond’s par value.

EXAMPLE

Example. The Lentyl Insurance Company purchased for $950 a $1,000 par value bond issued by the Yuletide Corporation.

Analysis. A typical entry that Lentyl would make to record this purchase is a debit of $950 to an asset account, such as Yuletide Bonds, and a credit of $950 to Cash. If, instead, Lentyl purchased the bond for $1,200, it would most likely record a debit to Yuletide Bonds for $1,200 and a credit of $1,200 to Cash.

For U.S. statutory reporting purposes, a bond’s statement value generally depends on its NAIC rating. The NAIC has six ratings for bonds. Bonds rated NAIC 1 to NAIC 5 are reported at amortized cost. For bonds that are rated NAIC 6—the designation for bonds “in or near default”—life insurers typically use a valuation method known as the lower-of-cost-or-market (LCM) rule. According to this rule, the statement value of a bond rated NAIC 6 is the lower of its amortized cost or fair value. The difference between the bond’s amortized cost and its fair value is generally an unrealized loss.

Bond Interest

Recall that bond interest is typically paid on a semiannual basis. Let’s look at a simple example.

EXAMPLE

Example. An insurer is the bondholder of 5,000 bonds issued by the Triangle Corporation. Each bond has a $1,000 par value. The bonds pay an annual coupon rate of interest of 6%. In this situation, the insurer will receive $150,000 in interest income twice each year, calculated as follows: 5,000 bonds × $1,000 par value × (6% coupon rate ÷ 2 payments per year). In this case, the insurer would record its interest income as follows:

Cash……………………………150,000

Interest Income—Triangle Bonds…………..150,000

To record the receipt of semiannual bond interest income.

If an insurer buys a bond between coupon payment dates, accrued interest income for this bond was actually earned by the seller of the bond prior to the date of purchase. In the context of bond interest income, accrued income is the amount of interest that has been earned on a bond, but is not yet payable to the bondholder as of the financial reporting date. Typical financial reporting dates are month-end, quarter-end, and year-end. To compensate the seller for this lost interest, the bond’s purchase price must include an amount equal to the accrued interest income as of the date of purchase.

EXAMPLE

Example. An insurer purchases a $1,000 bond at par with a 7% coupon rate and semiannual payments—that is, two coupon payments per year of $35 each—with $20 accrued interest income as of the purchase date. The bond was issued by the Triangle Corporation.

The insurer’s purchase price is $1,020, calculated as follows:

$1,000 purchase price + $20 accrued income = $1,020

The insurer records the accrued interest as an asset—Investment Income Due and Accrued—as shown in the following accounting entry:

Triangle Bonds…………………………………………..…….…………1,000

Investment Income Due and Accrued—Bonds………………......20

Cash………………………………….………….…..…………….1,020

To record the purchase of Triangle bonds with accrued bond interest income.

The insurer expects to receive the full amount of the bond interest, $35 in our example, when the next semiannual interest payment is made. Upon receipt of the regularly scheduled semiannual interest payment, the insurer would record the following accounting entry:

Cash………………………………………..………..35

Investment Income Due and Accrued—Bonds…….20

Interest Income—Triangle Bonds…………………..…..15

To record accrued and received bond interest income.

Note that the insurer’s income statement would include only $15 of bond interest income because the bond seller earned the remaining $20 in interest income.

Keep in mind that Investment Income Due and Accrued is an asset account, whereas Bond Interest Income is a revenue account.

 

Bond Discounts and Bond Premiums

Changes in an existing bond’s price are generally inversely related to changes in market interest rates. This relationship can be illustrated as follows:

If market interest rates then the prices of existing bonds

Because of the inverse relationship between market interest rates and bond prices, a bond’s purchase price often differs from its par value. Thus, a bond’s fair value—usually its current market value—does not necessarily equal the bond’s par value. In general, a bond will sell at a discount—that is, less than the par value—if market interest rates are higher than the bond’s coupon rate and will sell at a premium—that is, greater than the par value—if market interest rates are lower than the bond’s coupon rate. EXAMPLE

Example. The Cardamom Life Insurance Company purchased a $1,000 par value bond for $1,200. The bond has an annual coupon rate of 4%.

Analysis. The bond’s purchase price of $1,200 is not relevant in calculating the bond’s semiannual coupon payments. The semiannual coupon payments on this bond are calculated as the bond’s par value of $1,000, multiplied by 4% divided by 2: $1,000 × (0.04 ÷ 2) = $20.

If the purchase price differs from the bond’s par value, then the actual rate of return—known as the effective rate of return or effective yield—on the amount paid for the bond will differ from the coupon rate. To obtain the effective rate of return, divide the annual bond interest by the purchase price paid for the bond.

Purchase price

A bond purchased at par value will have an effective rate of return equal to the bond’s coupon rate. In general, the effective rate of return on a bond is higher than the coupon rate for a bond purchased at a discount and lower than the coupon rate for a bond purchased at a premium.

Let’s look at a more detailed example of a bond sale at a premium and at a discount.

EXAMPLE

Example. The Willow Corporation issued a $1,000 par value bond with a coupon rate of 5%. Suppose that the Castle Life Insurance Company purchased these 5% bonds at their $1,000 par value.

Now assume that market interest rates fall to 3%. Willow, as the bond issuer, is still obligated to pay interest at a rate of 5% to its bondholders, including Castle. In the current market, the Willow bonds earn Castle $50 per year; new bonds issued at par value would have a coupon rate of 3% and, therefore, would earn bondholders only $30 per year.

If Castle chose to sell these bonds in the current market, it could demand a bond price higher than $1,000—that is, a premium—because the Willow bond’s coupon rate is higher than the current market interest rate of 3%. A new purchaser of the Willow bonds would be willing to pay more than the par value to lock in the higher coupon rate.

The accounting entry to record Castle’s sale of the Willow bonds at a premium would most likely be

Cash…………………$$$

Willow Bonds……………………………..………….$$

Gain on Sale of Willow Bonds…………………..$

To record the sale of Willow bonds at a premium.

The amount of cash received equals the amount credited to the bonds sold plus the amount of the realized gain on the sale of the bonds. Note that, if Castle were to keep the bonds, rather than sell them, Castle would have an unrealized gain on these bonds.

Now assume that market interest rates rise to 8%. Castle, as the bondholder, faces a current market price on the Willow bonds that is lower than their par value. The 8% bonds would earn $80 a year in interest income, whereas the Willow bonds would earn Castle only $50 a year in interest income. A new purchaser of the Willow bonds would be unwilling to pay the par value of the bond because it could purchase new bonds that have a higher coupon rate.

In order to persuade a purchaser to buy the Willow bonds, Castle would need to sell the bonds at a price below par value—that is, at a discount. In this case, Castle would receive less than the $1,000 par value per bond sold.

If Castle were to sell the Willow bonds in this situation, Castle would have a realized loss on the bonds. Upon the sale of the bonds at a discount, Castle would most likely record the following accounting entry:

Cash……………................……………….$$

Loss on Sale of Willow Bonds…….....$

Willow Bonds………………………………$$$

To record the sale of Willow bonds at a discount.

The amount of cash received equals the amount credited to the bonds sold minus the amount debited to the realized loss on the sale of the bonds. Castle would have an unrealized loss on these bonds if it chose to retain the bonds instead of selling them.

 

Mortgages

A mortgage is a long-term loan secured by a pledge of specified real property. In this context, the insurer is usually the purchaser, not the issuer (seller), of the mortgage. Under GAAP and SAP, mortgages are generally reported at their outstanding principal balance or amortized cost (if acquired at a discount or a premium).

Insurers also invest in mortgages through mortgage-backed securities, in which holders of mortgages form a pool of mortgages and sell shares in the pool to investors. Mortgage-backed securities can be classified as either residential or commercial mortgage-backed securities:

  • A residential mortgage-backed security (RMBS) is a type of mortgage-backed security that is secured by a pool of residential mortgages.

  • A commercial mortgage-backed security (CMBS) is a type of mortgage-backed security that is secured by a pool of commercial mortgages.

For U.S. statutory accounting purposes, mortgage-backed securities are classified as bonds.

EXAMPLE

Example: The Trellis Life Insurance Company purchased $2.5 million of Syllable RMBSs. A typical accounting entry that Trellis most likely would make to record this transaction is

RMBSs—Syllable…………….2,500,000

Cash………………………………………..2,500,000

To record the purchase of Syllable RMBSs.

Mortgages and mortgage-backed securities appeal to insurers because these investments generally return a predictable stream of cash flows by providing (1) monthly debt repayment schedules and (2) a minimum, often fixed, rate of return.

Sometimes an insurer is the mortgage lender rather than the purchaser of a pool of mortgages. Typically, insurers own the mortgages of large, commercial developments.

EXAMPLE

Example: The Roundel Life Insurance Company is the mortgage lender on the $20 million Origami Mall. In this case, the basic accounting entry to record this mortgage is

Mortgage Loan—Origami Mall……20,000,000

Cash………………………………………………………..…..20,000,000

To record the issue of a mortgage on Origami Mall.

As part of each mortgage payment, most mortgage lenders, including insurers like the one in the above example, also require borrowers to make deposits to an escrow account. An escrow account, a liability account for the lender, is a trust account used to pay property maintenance expenses, property taxes, and other expenses related to a mortgaged property. Many lenders establish escrow accounts to ensure that (1) the property is maintained at some minimum level and (2) property taxes and other routine costs are paid.

When the insurer pays property taxes or other property expenses related to the mortgaged property, the insurer debits the property’s escrow account and credits Cash. The amount deposited in escrow by the borrower remains the same each month until a change in taxes, property insurance premiums, or other expenses necessitates a change in the escrow deposit. Because the lender accounts for escrow deposits separately from the principal and interest payments on the mortgage loan, escrow account transactions do not affect the outstanding principal and interest payments on the mortgage.

 

Stocks

A stock is an asset that represents a stockholder’s ownership interest in a company. Stock is sold in shares, and anyone who owns shares of stock in a company is known as a stockholder or shareholder. The par value of a stock is the designated legal monetary value assigned to each outstanding share of stock. To the purchaser, the stock of other companies is considered an equity security. The two major classes of stock are common stock and preferred stock:

  • Common stock is a type of stock that usually entitles the owner to (1) vote on the selection of directors and on other important company matters and (2) receive dividends on the stock, if they are declared.

  • Preferred stock is a type of stock that typically does not carry the voting rights of common stock, but does carry a stated dividend rate that is paid before any payment of common stock dividends by the same company. In the event of the company’s liquidation, owners of preferred stock have a prior claim to assets over common stockholders. Preferred stock is considered a hybrid security: it resembles a debt security with its stated dividend rate, which functions like an interest payment; and it resembles an equity security with its potential for price appreciation.

Of these two classes, life insurers typically invest more heavily in common stock than preferred stock.

Typically, U.S. statutory accounting limits the percentage of stocks that insurers can hold in the general account. Insurers also hold stocks in their separate accounts. We discuss the general account and separate accounts in the next chapter. Although equity securities make up a much smaller portion of an insurer’s investment portfolio than do bonds and other debt securities, an insurer’s investments in equity securities can represent a large monetary amount. The following sections discuss accounting for stocks from the investor’s point of view, with the assumption that an insurance company is the investor. In a later chapter, we discuss accounting for stocks when the insurer issues its own stock.

 

Stock Purchases, Sales, and Valuation

Accounting for the purchase and sale of stock held for investment purposes is similar to that for the purchase and sale of bonds. In other words, Stock is debited and Cash is credited for the stock’s purchase price, net of fees, commissions, or other transaction costs.

In their GAAP financial statements, insurers report the fair value per share of each stock they own—whether the stock is common or preferred. Under U.S. statutory accounting, most common stock is reported at fair value as of the financial reporting date. In contrast, most preferred stock is reported in a manner similar to bonds. Preferred stock that has an NAIC designation of 1 to 3 is reported at its amortized cost. However, for preferred stock with an NAIC designation of 4 to 6, insurers use the lower-of-cost-or-market (LCM) rule to report the preferred stock’s value.

The Parapet Life Insurance Company purchased 100 shares of Dandelion common stock for $95 per share. Parapet records the transaction by debiting the cost of the common stock to the Dandelion Common Stock account and crediting Cash for the same amount. The accounting entry is as follows:

Dandelion Common Stock…9,500

Cash…………………………………..9,500

To record the purchase of Dandelion common stock.

Assume that Parapet decides to sell all 100 shares of the Dandelion common stock for $80 per share, the current market price. The cash proceeds for this sale can be found by multiplying the number of shares sold by the market price per share: 100 shares × $80 per share = $8,000. Because the net sales proceeds of $8,000 are less than the historical cost of $9,500, we know that this sale results in a realized capital loss.

We can compute the realized capital loss as follows:

Realized capital loss = Net sales proceeds – Asset’s historical cost

Plugging in the values from the sale gives us

$8,000 – $9,500 = –$1,500, or a realized capital loss of $1,500

The accounting entry to record this transaction is

Cash……………………………………………..…………..8,000

Loss on Sale of Dandelion Common Stock…...1,500

Dandelion Common Stock…………………………………………9,500

To record the sale of Dandelion common stock at a loss.

Suppose instead that Parapet sold the Dandelion common stock for $120 per share. Because the net sales proceeds of $12,000, or 100 × $120, are more than the historical cost of $9,500, we know that this sale results in a realized capital gain.

We can compute the realized capital gain as follows:

Realized capital gain = Net sales proceeds – Asset’s historical cost

Plugging in the values from the sale gives us

$12,000 – $9,500 = $2,500, or a realized capital gain of $2,500

The accounting entry to record this transaction is

Cash………………………………………………...12,000

Dandelion Common Stock……………………………………9,500

Gain on Sale of Dandelion Common Stock…….….….2,500

To record the sale of Dandelion common stock at a gain.

 

Cash Dividends

Owners of stock may receive two kinds of dividends:

  • A cash dividend is a cash payment that a company periodically distributes to the owners of its preferred stock and common stock.

  • A stock dividend is a type of dividend in which a company distributes additional shares of its stock to stockholders.

In this section, we discuss cash dividends. Cash dividends on common stock are usually expressed as an annual monetary amount per share. Cash dividends on preferred stock are typically expressed as a percentage of par value per share. Dividends are typically paid quarterly.

EXAMPLE

Examples.

Suppose a common stock has an annual cash dividend of $1. In this case, the quarterly dividend is $0.25 per share, found as $1 ÷ 4.

A preferred stock with a par value of $20 has a cash dividend expressed as 10% of its par value, resulting in an annual dividend of $2 per share, or a quarterly dividend of $0.50 per share, found as $2 ÷ 4.

A typical accounting entry to record the receipt of cash dividends is a debit to Cash, an asset account, and a credit to a revenue account, such as Dividend Income—Common Stock or Dividend Income—Preferred Stock. Like accrued interest and accrued investment income, cash dividends that have been earned but not yet received on stock that an insurer owns as of the insurer’s financial reporting date are reported under Investment Income Due and Accrued or a similar asset account. We discuss accrued investment income in a later chapter.

Cash dividends are payable to stockholders who own the stock on the ex-dividend date. The ex-dividend date, also called the ex-date, is the date following the declaration date that is used for determining whether a stockholder is eligible to receive a declared cash dividend. The ex-dividend date is typically two business days prior to the record date. The record date is the date on which parties who are listed on a company’s records as owning the company’s common stock are identified as being entitled to receive dividends on stock, which are declared by the issuing company’s board of directors. Derivatives

Recall that derivatives are securities whose value is based on other securities, such as stocks or bonds. Insurers employ different kinds of derivatives, each of which has its own terms and features. Insurers generally use derivatives to mitigate their exposure to various risks, such as interest-rate risk, currency risk, and market risk. A detailed discussion of the many types of derivatives is beyond the scope of this text. Instead, we focus on the regulatory requirements that insurers must satisfy for holding derivatives and the typical accounting treatment for derivatives.

Derivatives are highly complex instruments that in some cases come with high risks. Therefore, regulators require insurers to submit detailed information about the derivatives they hold. For both U.S. GAAP and statutory reporting, insurers typically include derivatives on the balance sheet or disclose them in notes that accompany their financial statements.

For statutory reporting purposes, insurers must also report their derivatives in Schedule DB of their Quarterly and Annual Statements. In this schedule, insurers must provide certain information about each derivative they hold, such as a description of the derivative, its face amount (also called notional amount), fair value, and maturity date. If the derivative is being held for a hedging purpose, the insurer must provide additional details about the hedge. Hedgingrefers to counterbalancing a given risk exposure against another. In other words, to protect itself from a given risk, an insurer takes an action—such as entering into a derivatives contract—that “hedges” or minimizes that risk.

U.S. GAAP requires insurers to recognize derivatives as either assets or liabilities on the balance sheet and measure them at fair value, regardless of the purpose for holding the derivative. Under SAP, the purpose for holding a derivative dictates the manner in which it is reported. For example, derivatives used for hedging purposes generally should be accounted for in a manner consistent with the item that is being hedged:

  • If the item being hedged is reported at amortized cost, then the derivative should be reported at amortized cost.

  • If the item being hedged is reported at fair value, then the derivative should be reported at fair value.

In contrast, derivatives held for purposes other than hedging—for example, to generate income—are typically reported at fair value, and any changes in the fair value of such a derivative are recognized in current operations.

The NAIC’s Derivative Instruments Model Regulation also establishes standards for the prudent use of derivatives by insurance companies. According to this Model Regulation, insurers must have written guidelines that govern their transactions in derivative instruments, and they also must have procedures in place for monitoring their derivatives. Insurers generally record these guidelines and procedures in a derivatives use plan (DUP). Many insurers conduct periodic audits of their DUPs to confirm that they are complying with the DUP.
