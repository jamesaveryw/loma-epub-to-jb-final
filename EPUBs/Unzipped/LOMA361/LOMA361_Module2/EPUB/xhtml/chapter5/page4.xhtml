Admitted Assets and Nonadmitted Assets

As we described earlier in the text, assets can be assigned to various classifications—such as tangible versus intangible and short-term versus long-term. To comply with statutory reporting requirements, U.S. life insurers also must classify their assets as either admitted assets or nonadmitted assets on the Annual Statement, usually based on their liquidity:

  • Admitted assets are assets whose full value is reported on the Assets page of the Annual Statement. Examples of admitted assets are cash, cash equivalents, and most invested assets.

  • Nonadmitted assets are assets that are reported on the Assets page of the Annual Statement but are accorded no value. Most nonadmitted assets are used in the normal process of doing business, such as office furniture and equipment. Some assets are partially nonadmitted, which means that only a portion of their value is reported on the Assets page of the Annual Statement. One example of a partially nonadmitted asset is deferred tax assets.

The purpose of these classifications is to enable regulators to identify which of an insurer’s assets possess adequate liquidity. Recall that a liquid asset is one that can be readily converted to cash at an approximation of its underlying value. Liquid assets are crucial in enabling an insurer to satisfy its contractual obligations. In other words, admitted assets are assets whose values are “admitted” on the Annual Statement because they can easily be converted to cash—without a loss in value—to pay for an insurer’s obligations, whereas nonadmitted assets are assets whose value is “not admitted” because they do not possess this quality. By not allowing an insurer to report a value (or a full value) for a nonliquid asset, statutory regulators enforce the concept of conservatism, as discussed earlier in this text.

Note that an insurer’s net admitted assets are found by subtracting nonadmitted assets from assets:

Net admitted assets = Assets – Nonadmitted assets

  • Cash is an admitted asset, so the full amount—$168,314,225—is shown as a net admitted asset. (Technically, the calculation is $168,314,225 – $0 = $168,314,225.)

  • Furniture and equipment is a nonadmitted asset, so its full value is “netted out,” as in $3,988,142 – $3,988,142 = $0, leaving no value in the net admitted assets column.

Note that the admitted and nonadmitted asset classifications are only relevant for statutory reporting purposes; under GAAP accounting, an insurer includes values for all of its assets in its financial statements. Because of this difference, the asset amounts in an insurer’s Annual Report rarely equal those shown on the Assets page of the insurer’s Annual Statement.
