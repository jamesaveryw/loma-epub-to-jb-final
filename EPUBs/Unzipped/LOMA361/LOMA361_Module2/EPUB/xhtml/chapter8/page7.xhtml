Premium Liabilities

In certain situations, an insurer may need to record specified premium payments as liabilities. Some of these situations depend on the timing of the premium payments, whereas others depend on the type of product or contract to which the premiums apply.

 

Premium Suspense Accounts

The accounting entry to record an insurer’s receipt of a premium typically includes a debit to Cash. However, the insurer may not be able to determine immediately the credit entry to offset the debit to Cash. In such situations, many insurers use premium suspense accounts. In accounting terminology, a suspense account is an account that is used to record transactions that cannot be posted immediately to a specified account. Specifically, Premium Suspense is the liability account used to record transactions that are intended as premiums, but that the insurer cannot accept as income until a particular event occurs.

Theoretically, upon policy issue, there is little need for a premium suspense account. However, in practice, many insurers initially suspense all insurance premiums. Insurers typically use premium suspense accounts for premium payment amounts that are (1) renewal premiums, (2) different from the amounts in the insurer’s records, or (3) lacking critical information, such as a policy number. Another typical use of premium suspense accounts is in situations in which an insurer has received the initial premium for a policy but is still waiting for an underwriting decision on the policy.

A typical accounting entry to record such transactions is as follows:

Cash………………………..xxx

Premium Suspense…………………xxx

To record receipt of a premium payment that cannot be immediately recorded to a premium income account.

After the particular event occurs—for example, the insurer confirms the correct amount or policy number—the insurer can then debit the premium suspense account and credit a revenue account for the premium amount, as follows:

Premium Suspense………………….xxx

Premium Income……………………………xxx

To record a suspensed premium to a premium income account.

Note that the end result here is a debit to Cash and a credit to Premium Income.

 

Premiums Paid in Advance

When an insurer receives premiums before they are due, it reports a liability for premiums paid in advance on its U.S. GAAP and statutory financial statements. For life insurance contracts, premiums paid in advance, also called advance premiums, are premiums that an insurer has received but that are due on or after the next policy anniversary date.

Sometimes included in the category of premiums paid in advance are premium deposits, which, in this context, are amounts that an insurer’s policyowners leave on deposit with the insurer to pay for several years of premiums at one time. Because the insurer has the use of these policyowner funds before they are due, it typically discounts the premiums—in effect, receiving a smaller amount in cash. However, a more common approach with these premium deposits is to place them in a liability account known as a premium deposit fund. Under this approach, when an individual premium becomes due, the insurer pays it from the premium deposit fund and credits Premium Income.

 

Premiums for Deposit-Type and Investment Contracts

Under both SAP and GAAP, special rules apply to payments received for deposit-type contracts and investment contracts.

 

SAP Deposit-Type Contracts

Under SAP, deposit-type contracts are contracts that do not expose the insurer to mortality risk or morbidity risk. In this discussion, we focus on deposit-type contracts that lack a mortality risk component—in other words, contracts that provide benefit payments that do not depend upon the death or continued survival of an individual or group of individuals. Examples of deposit-type contracts are

  • Guaranteed interest contracts (GICs), also known as guaranteed investment contracts, in which the contract holder places one or more deposits with the insurer for a stated period in exchange for repayment of those deposits plus interest at a guaranteed rate. GICs are often used as funding vehicles for group retirement plans.

  • Annuities certain, which are contracts under which the insurer makes periodic payments to an individual over a specified period or in a specified amount.

  • Supplemental contracts without life contingencies, which are supplemental contracts under which the insurer holds the policy proceeds and makes payments to an individual—usually the policyowner or beneficiary—without regard to the life of the individual.

  • Dividend accumulations, which we discussed earlier in the chapter.

For deposit-type contracts, amounts received as payments are reported to the appropriate liability account on the insurer’s statutory balance sheet, known as Liability for deposit-type contracts. The rationale for this treatment is that these payments are accounted for as policyowner account balances, which means that policyowners have more discretion over deposits and withdrawals.

GAAP Investment Contracts

According to GAAP, long-duration contracts that do not expose the insurer to significant risks arising from policyowner or contract owner mortality are classified as investment contracts. Recall that a long-duration contract is one that is expected to remain in force for an extended period. Examples of investment contracts are

  • Guaranteed interest contracts

  • Most deferred annuity contracts during the accumulation phase

Under GAAP, accounting for investment contracts is similar to accounting for interest-bearing or other financial instruments where the balance accrues to the benefit of the policyowner or contract owner. According to this approach, premiums or deposits received for investment contracts are recorded as a deposit liability rather than as revenue. A typical GAAP account title for this liability is Funds held under deposit contracts.

 

Premiums for Universal Life Insurance

Unlike traditional whole life insurance policies, UL insurance policies have premiums, expenses, and interest credits that may not be fixed or guaranteed. While a UL policy is in force, the insurer credits premiums and interest to the policy’s account balance and subtracts various expenses from it. Technically, the policyowner owns this account balance. Therefore, according to the FASB, under GAAP, an insurer must treat premium payments for a UL policy as a liability, usually referred to as contract holder liabilities.
