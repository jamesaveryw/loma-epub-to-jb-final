Policyowner Dividends

Policyowner dividends are another important liability for many life insurance companies. When an insurer’s actual experience on its products is more favorable than its expected experience, the insurer can refund money to participating policyowners. A policyowner dividend, also called a policy dividend, is the portion of an insurer’s surplus that is paid to participating policyowners and that is considered a partial refund of the policy premium that was not needed by the insurer for policy expenses.

Annual dividends on participating policies are generally payable on the policy anniversary date—the date on which coverage under an insurance policy became effective—after a policy has been in force for one or more years. Policyowner dividends are optional—that is, an insurer’s board of directors will declare these dividends only if the insurer’s financial results warrant them. In general, the board of directors declares policy dividends shortly before the end of the year.

The total amount of policyowner dividends payable on a particular product in each upcoming year is contingent upon several factors, including (1) the insurer’s financial performance with the product and (2) the number of policies still in force on their anniversary dates. Thus, the amount in Policyowner Dividends Payable, a liability account on the insurer’s balance sheet, is necessarily an estimate provided by the insurer’s actuaries. On U.S. GAAP-based financial statements, an insurer estimates the present value of all future policyowner dividends as a component of policy reserves for specific life insurance products. For U.S. statutory accounting purposes, the insurer estimates its policyowner dividend liabilities for only the next fiscal year.

 

Policyowner Dividend Liabilities

An insurer’s liabilities for policyowner dividends include (1) policyowner dividends due and unpaid, (2) policyowner dividends payable in the following calendar year, and (3) dividend accumulations.

 

Policyowner Dividends Due and Unpaid

Policyowner dividends due and unpaid represent those dividends payable to the policyowner in the current year that have not been disbursed or otherwise applied as of the financial reporting date for the current year. An insurer must establish a liability for policyowner dividends due and unpaid whenever the policyowner has earned the dividend but has not yet received it or applied it to a dividend payment option. Amounts recorded in the liability account Policyowner Dividends Due and Unpaid apply to the current year. These dividends have met all the criteria for payment, and the exact monetary amount of the dividends is known.

 

Policyowner Dividends Payable in the Following Calendar Year

Policyowner dividends payable in the following calendar year include two categories of dividends:

  • Dividends apportioned for payment are the estimated amount of all policyowner dividends declared by an insurance company’s board of directors prior to the end of the statement year that are not yet paid or due at the end of the year.

  • Dividends not yet apportioned for payment are all dividends payable in the following calendar year that have not been declared.

Amounts in the liability account Policyowner Dividends Payable in the Following Calendar Year are estimated on the basis of each policy’s anniversary date. Unlike the liability for policyowner dividends due and unpaid, the exact amount of the insurer’s liability for policyowner dividends payable in the following calendar year is unknown.

 

Dividend Accumulations

Dividend accumulations, also known as dividends left on deposit, result when a policyowner elects to leave policyowner dividends on deposit at interest with the insurer. The insurer establishes a liability for any dividend accumulations, as well as for the accrued interest on those accumulations.

 

Policyowner Dividend Payment Options

Owners of participating life insurance policies typically can select one of five policyowner dividend payment options, or dividend payment options, which enable participating policyowners to select the manner in which they want their policyowner dividends disbursed. The insurer pays the dividends to the policyowner according to the terms of the dividend payment option chosen.

The insight below summarizes the accounting treatment of common policyowner dividend payment options.

INSIGHT

Common Policyowner Dividend Payment Options

Policyowner Dividends Paid in Cash

When a policyowner elects to receive a dividend in cash, an insurer typically updates the dividend record—which is part of the policyowner master file—and records the appropriate accounting entries upon payment of the dividend.

A typical accounting entry to record the payment of a policyowner dividend is

Policyowner Dividends Paid in Cash………………………..xxx

Cash…………………………………………………………………….xxx

To record the payment of policyowner dividends.

Policyowner Dividends Applied to Pay Premiums

When a policyowner uses a dividend to reduce a policy premium, the insurer deducts the policyowner dividend from the premium due to determine the amount owed by the policyowner. The insurer records the accounting entry on the policy anniversary date. If the dividend is greater than the premium due, the policyowner can receive the excess in cash or apply it to another dividend payment option.

A typical accounting entry to record policyowner dividends applied to pay premiums due is

Policyowner Dividends Applied to Premiums………………………..xxx

Premium Income………………..……………………………………………….xxx

To record the application of a policyowner dividend to pay a current premium.

Policyowner Dividend Accumulations

For dividend accumulations, most insurers record the policyowner dividend and its accrued interest on the policy anniversary date—that is, the date on which the dividend becomes payable—provided the policy premiums have been paid to that date.

A typical accounting entry to record policyowner dividends applied to accumulate at interest with the insurer is

Policyowner Dividends Applied to Dividend Accumulations………..xxx

Dividend Accumulations..…………………………..…………………………….xxx

To record policyowner dividends applied to accumulate with the insurer at interest.

Note that this accounting entry records the initial deposit of the dividend. Separate accounting entries would record the interest earned on the dividend.

Policyowner Dividends Applied to Purchase Paid-Up Additions (PUAs)

When a policyowner uses a dividend to purchase paid-up additions (PUAs), the insurer determines the amount of the additional insurance purchased. The insurer then adds a rider to the basic policy to indicate the additional insurance coverage or treats the paid-up insurance as a separate policy.

A typical accounting entry to record policyowner dividends applied to buy PUAs is

Policyowner Dividends to Purchase PUAs………………………..xxx

Single Premiums for PUAs………………………………..…………….xxx

To record policyowner dividends applied to purchase paid-up additions.

Note that Single Premiums for PUAs is a type of premium income (revenue) account. We discuss revenue accounts in Chapter 10.

Policyowner Dividends Applied to Purchase One-Year Term Insurance

This policyowner dividend payment option is sometimes called the fifth dividend option. As with PUAs, the insurer adds a rider to the basic policy to indicate the additional insurance coverage or treats the paid-up insurance as a separate policy.

The entry to apply policyowner dividends to one-year term insurance is similar to that for PUAs, as shown below:

Policyowner Dividends to Purchase One-Year Term

Insurance..………………………..……………………………….……………..xxx

Single Premiums for One-Year Term Insurance ………………………….xxx

To record policyowner dividends applied to purchase one-year term insurance.

The insurer records accounting entries for the total monetary amount of all policyowner dividends being applied under each of the dividend payment options. After paying a dividend or applying it under a dividend payment option, the insurer updates its dividend record to provide a detailed history of each policy’s dividend transactions. The insurer uses this information to

  • Prepare summaries of dividend transactions for financial reporting purposes

  • Prepare lists by policy number for tracing dividend disbursements in case a policyowner should question whether she was sent a dividend or wants confirmation of how the dividend was applied

  • Facilitate audits by regulatory authorities

  • Report interest credited on policyowner dividends for income tax purposes
