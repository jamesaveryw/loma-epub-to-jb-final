Asset Fluctuation Reserves

The reserves we discussed in the previous chapter were those related to a life insurer’s obligations to its policyowners and beneficiaries—in other words, an insurer’s contractual reserves. In the United States, each life insurer also must establish two statutory reserves that are noncontractual in nature: the asset valuation reserve (AVR) and the interest maintenance reserve (IMR). The AVR and the IMR are designed to absorb gains and losses on the insurer’s invested assets.

You may recall from earlier chapters that the market values of an insurer’s invested assets can fluctuate widely. These two reserves are intended to minimize the effects of these fluctuations—thus earning them the name asset fluctuation reserves.

On an insurer’s statutory balance sheet, capital gains increase the insurer’s assets, whereas capital losses decrease them. The insurer must balance these changes in assets with a change in either liabilities or capital and surplus. If capital gains and losses were allowed to affect an insurer’s capital and surplus accounts, then the insurer’s capital and surplus would be subject to significant short-term fluctuations, resulting in an inaccurate picture of the insurer’s overall financial condition. Instead, these capital gains and losses are reflected in the AVR and the IMR. These two reserves act as a buffer for the insurer’s capital and surplus, causing capital and surplus to be affected only gradually by capital gains and losses. Thus, these reserves help to ensure the stability of an insurer’s capital and surplus accounts.

Note that the AVR and IMR are relevant only for statutory reporting purposes.

 

Asset Valuation Reserve

The asset valuation reserve (AVR) is a reserve that minimizes the effects of both realized and unrealized gains and losses on an insurer’s invested assets when those gains and losses are related to the credit quality of the assets—a risk known as credit risk—or fluctuations in the prices of equity securities—a risk known as equity risk. In this context, credit risk is the risk that a financial obligation will not be paid. Recall that a capital gain or loss generally is realized when an insurer sells an invested asset, whereas a capital gain or loss generally is unrealized as long as the insurer still holds the invested asset. Capital gains increase the AVR, whereas capital losses decrease the AVR.

Some common examples of credit risk for life insurers are as follows:

  • A bond issuer experiences financial difficulties and is unable to pay interest or principal on the bond.

  • A mortgage borrower stops making mortgage payments.

  • A stock issuer experiences unfavorable financial circumstances or files for bankruptcy.

Although these examples describe invested assets that have declined in value due to credit risk, the market value of invested assets can also increase due to credit risk.

At the end of the accounting year, an insurer may be required to increase its AVR by transferring an amount from its capital and surplus accounts. The amount that the insurer transfers depends on the risks associated with its invested assets: the riskier its invested assets, the larger the AVR the insurer must maintain. The ending balance of the AVR is reported on the statutory balance sheet as a liability.

 

Interest Maintenance Reserve

The interest maintenance reserve (IMR) is a reserve that helps absorb realized gains and losses on an insurer’s debt securities, such as bonds, mortgages, mortgage-backed securities, and preferred stock, when those gains or losses are caused by changes in market interest rates rather than changes in the credit quality of the assets. (Recall from Chapter 5 that we classified preferred stock as a hybrid security. For purposes of the IMR, however, preferred stock is considered to be a debt security.)

The IMR helps to protect an insurer’s net gain from operations and its capital and surplus from the effects of interest-rate risk. In this context, interest-rate riskis the risk that unpredictable movements in market interest rates will cause the value of a life insurer’s debt securities to change. The typical effects of changing market interest rates on an insurer’s debt securities are as follows:

  • When market interest rates increase, the value of an insurer’s debt securities generally decreases.

  • When market interest rates decrease, the value of an insurer’s debt securities generally increases.

Capital gains caused by changes in market interest rates increase the IMR, whereas capital losses caused by changes in market interest rates decrease the IMR.

After an insurer posts a realized gain or loss on an invested asset to the IMR, the insurer amortizes, or spreads out, the gain or loss into investment income for the remaining original term of that investment. As a result, the insurer reports only a portion of an invested asset’s gain or loss on the Statement of Operations in a given year. The insurer then transfers a portion of each capital gain or loss from the IMR to capital and surplus each year until, at the date the invested asset would have matured, the insurer has transferred the entire gain or loss to capital and surplus. Therefore, these capital gains and losses affect capital and surplus gradually, as if the insurer had held the asset until maturity. As with the AVR, the ending balance of the IMR is generally reported on the statutory balance sheet as a liability.

What do the asset valuation reserve (AVR) and the interest maintenance reserve (IMR) have in common?

  1. They both minimize the effects of unrealized gains and losses on an insurer’s invested assets.
  2. They both apply to all types of invested assets, such as bonds, preferred stock, derivatives, mortgages, common stock, and real estate.
  3. They both absorb capital gains and losses caused by market interest rates.
  4. They both allow gains and losses to gradually affect the insurer’s capital and surplus accounts.
