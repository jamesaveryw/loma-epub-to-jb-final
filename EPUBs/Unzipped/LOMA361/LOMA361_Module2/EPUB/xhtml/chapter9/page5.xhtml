Sources and Uses of Funds for Capital and Surplus

Earlier in the text, we discussed the flow of funds in an insurance company. Recall that, in general, sources of funds result from cash inflows, or money coming into the company. In contrast, uses of funds generally result from cash outflows, or money going out of the company. These cash inflows (sources) and cash outflows (uses) affect an insurer’s asset accounts—especially its Cash account—as well as its owners’ equity accounts—in particular, Capital and Surplus.

An insurer may generate cash flows internally or externally. The following table presents the differences between internal sources and uses of funds and external sources and uses of funds.

Internal Sources and Uses of Funds for Capital and Surplus

Typical internal sources and uses of funds for life insurers include net income or a net loss, increases or decreases in admitted assets, and reserve strengthening and destrengthening.

Net Income or Net Loss

The primary source of surplus for most insurers is net income, which includes

  • Gains from core insurance operations. Insurers can increase capital and surplus when they earn profits from their operations—that is, their revenues are greater than their expenses.

  • Gains from the sale of invested assets. Insurers also increase net income and ultimately surplus by selling invested assets for a gain.

Similarly, a loss from core insurance operations or a loss on the sale of an invested asset would result in a decrease in an insurer’s capital and surplus.

Increases or Decreases in Admitted Assets

Recall that, under statutory accounting,

  • Admitted assets are assets whose full value is reported on the Assets page of the Annual Statement

  • Nonadmitted assets are assets that are reported on the Assets page of the Annual Statement but are accorded no value

Increases or decreases in an insurer’s admitted assets ultimately affect its capital and surplus accounts. Video 9.3 illustrates the effects that sales and purchases of nonadmitted assets have on an insurer’s capital and surplus.

So far in this section, we have discussed single sales and purchases of nonadmitted assets. For a given reporting period, however, an insurer accounts for the total change in its nonadmitted assets as either a net increase or a net decrease:

  • A net increase in nonadmitted assets decreases the insurer’s capital and surplus.

  • A net decrease in nonadmitted assets increases the insurer’s capital and surplus.

 

Reserve Destrengthening and Reserve Strengthening

In general, an inverse relationship exists between changes in an insurer’s contractual reserves and changes in its surplus. Therefore, in general,

  • A decrease in an insurer’s contractual reserves increases the insurer’s surplus

  • An increase in the insurer’s contractual reserves decreases the insurer’s surplus

A detailed discussion of these effects is outside the scope of this textbook.

External Sources and Uses of Funds for Capital and Surplus

Some typical external sources and uses of funds for life insurers include ceding or assuming reinsurance, selling or purchasing in-force business, issuing and retiring stock, issuing and repaying surplus notes, and changing corporate form.

 

Ceding or Assuming Reinsurance

In certain situations, a direct writer that cedes insurance risk to a reinsurer is able to increase its capital and surplus. Recall that, under the terms of some types of reinsurance arrangements, the reinsurer assumes some of the direct writer’s liability for benefit payments and agrees to establish reserves for the reinsured portion of the risk. Because this use of reinsurance allows a direct writer to establish a lower amount of contractual reserves than it otherwise would, the direct writer’s surplus is higher than it would have been without the reinsurance.

In contrast, an insurer can serve as the reinsurer in a reinsurance transaction, assuming part or all of the risk from the direct writer. In this situation, the reinsurer’s surplus decreases because it has to increase its contractual reserves to cover the additional risk it has assumed. Simply put, if the insurer increases contractual reserves (a liability), all other factors remaining equal, then surplus (an owners’ equity account) must decrease to keep the basic accounting equation in balance.

 

Selling or Purchasing In-Force Business

Sometimes an insurer wishes to sell in-force business, perhaps to exit a current market or reduce its presence in a current market. An insurer that sells in-force business may be able to increase its capital and surplus. If the difference between the in-force business sold and the reserves released on that business produces a gain, then an insurer’s surplus increases.

Conversely, an insurer may decide to purchase in-force business from another insurer, perhaps to enter a new market or increase its presence in a current market. By purchasing in-force business from another insurer, an insurer typically experiences a decrease in its capital and surplus. The purchase decreases the insurer’s assets, or Cash account, and increases the insurer’s liabilities, because the insurer must establish reserves for the in-force business. To keep the basic accounting equation in balance, the insurer’s capital and surplus must decrease, as shown in the following balance sheet:

Issuing and Retiring Stock

Recall that a stock insurer can increase its capital by issuing common stock or preferred stock; in this situation, the cash received from the sale of the stock is an external source of funds for the insurer. In contrast, a stock insurer decreases its capital and surplus by retiring its own stock; the cash the insurer pays to retire the stock is an external use of funds.

 

Issuing and Repaying Surplus Notes

In the United States, stock insurers have the option of raising capital by issuing stocks or bonds, but mutual insurers and fraternal insurers have neither of these options. Enter the surplus note—also known as a surplus debenture or a contribution certificate. A surplus noteis a special type of unsecured debt security issued only by insurance companies. All types of insurance companies in the United States can issue surplus notes. Although mutual insurers and fraternal insurers are the most common issuers of surplus notes, stock insurers sometimes issue them. For stock insurers, surplus notes represent a way of raising capital in addition to—or in lieu of—issuing common or preferred stock.

Insurers typically issue and sell surplus notes to raise money for normal business operations or for expansion purposes. The issuer of a surplus note (the insurer) agrees to make payments of principal and interest according to a fixed schedule. Issuing surplus notes is an external source of funds for an insurer.

One key advantage of issuing surplus notes is that, under statutory accounting, surplus notes are classified as Surplus; in other words, issuing surplus notes increases an insurer’s surplus, not its liabilities. In order to receive this treatment, though, an insurer’s surplus notes must receive approval from the insurance commissioner in the insurer’s state of domicile. As part of the approval process, the surplus notes must contain a provision stating that interest payments and principal repayments require prior approval of the commissioner of the state of domicile.

Under statutory accounting, an insurer records the proceeds from a sale of surplus notes using one accounting entry that shows an increase in the issuing company’s capital and surplus accounts, and another entry that shows an increase in Cash, with no changes in the insurer’s liabilities, as illustrated in the following statutory balance sheet:

For statutory accounting purposes, issuing surplus notes improves an insurer’s ability to pay claims on a timely basis, because the increases in both Cash and Surplus strengthen the insurer’s balance sheet. Note that this advantage does not exist under U.S. GAAP, which requires insurers to report surplus notes as liabilities, and not surplus.

As mentioned earlier, making interest payments or repaying principal on a surplus note requires regulatory approval. Once the insurer receives regulatory approval, it must expense all interest approved for payment in its Statement of Operations. In addition, as of the date of approval of a principal repayment, the insurer must reclassify the approved payments from surplus to liabilities.

The effect of repaying interest and principal on surplus notes is a decrease in assets (Cash) and a decrease in

  • Owners’ equity (Surplus Notes) under U.S. statutory accounting

  • Liabilities (Surplus Notes) under U.S. GAAP

Note that repaying surplus notes is an external use of an insurer’s funds.

An insurer that issues surplus notes is also required to disclose detailed information about them in the notes to its financial statements. Examples of this required information include

  • The date the surplus notes were issued

  • A description of the assets received for the notes

  • The holder of each note

  • The amount of each note

  • The rate at which interest accrues

  • Interest and/or principal paid in the current year

  • Total interest and/or principal paid on surplus notes

 

Changing Corporate Form

To grow and increase capital, insurers can restructure the corporate form in various ways, such as through

  • Mergers, acquisitions, takeovers, and divestitures. Mergers, acquisitions, and takeovers typically combine formerly independent entities. For example, after a merger, the combined company typically has greater owners’ equity than either company had prior to the merger. The combined company’s assets, liabilities, and owners’ equity are reported using valuations as of the date of the merger.

  • Demutualization. Mutual insurance companies have limited access to capital markets. Therefore, they must rely primarily on premium income and investment income (both classified as revenues) to generate enough net income to increase their owners’ equity. For this reason, some mutual insurers have chosen demutualization—the process of converting a mutual insurance company to a stock insurance company—to compete more effectively in the capital markets. By demutualizing, a mutual insurer gains access to capital sources—such as the issue of common stock—available to stock insurers.

  • Financial holding companies. The primary impact of the United States federal legislation known as the Gramm-Leach-Bliley (GLB) Act of 1999 is that the traditionally separate components of the financial services industry—banks, securities firms, and insurance companies—can now structurally converge under the umbrella of a federally chartered financial holding company. The financial holding company can engage in a statutorily provided list of financial activities that were formerly divided by regulation.
