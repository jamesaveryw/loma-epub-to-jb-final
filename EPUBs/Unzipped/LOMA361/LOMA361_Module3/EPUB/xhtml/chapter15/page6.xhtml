Types of Budgets

When management engages in profit planning, which is the process of taking actions to achieve a specified profit level, the most critical element in the process is a sound master budget. The accounting function combines the individual budgets for each department or responsibility center into the company’s master budget, which shows the overall operating and financing plans for the company during a specified accounting period. Companies may refer to the master budget by different names, such as comprehensive budget, corporate budget, or performance plan. Most companies compile the master budget annually and update it semiannually.

To develop the master budget, a company begins by assembling a forecast of the company’s revenues, expenses, cash flows, and capital budgeting activities. The master budget generally consists of several types of budgets, including

  • Operational budgets

  • Cash budgets

  • Capital budgets

We’ll look at each of these types of budgets in the following sections.

 

Operational Budgets

An operational budget is a budget that covers part or all of a company’s core business operations, such as underwriting and claims. An operational budget reflects the financial steps the company will take during the coming year to achieve its objectives, such as releasing new products or reducing overhead. Typically, a company incorporates multiple operational budgets into the master budget, as discussed previously.

All operational budgets begin with a forecast of sales revenues and investment income. Insurers use one or more forecasting techniques to create this projection. Although the format and content of operational budgets vary widely among companies, operational budgets show the revenues or expenses that a company expects during a specified accounting period. Two basic types of operational budgets are revenue budgets and expense budgets.

 

Revenue Budgets

After developing the sales forecast and the investment forecast, a company then develops its revenue budget. A revenue budget indicates the amount of income from operations—premium income, investment income, and fee income—that the company expects in the coming budget period. The revenue budget may differ from the sales forecast and investment forecast; the forecasts are raw estimates, whereas the budget outlines management’s goals and expectations after reviewing other available data. For example, if an investment forecast anticipates that a market will continue to remain strong, management may instead create a more conservative revenue budget, to minimize the risk that an unforeseen drop in the market would negatively affect the company’s investments.

The revenue budget determines the limits of the other budgets, whether they are expense budgets (which also are operational budgets), cash budgets, or capital budgets. Therefore, a company must develop its revenue budget before other budgets. Some insurers divide the revenue budget into the sales budget and the investment budget:

  • A sales budget projects first-year and renewal premium income. The insurer bases its sales budget estimates on historical data, reviews of the marketplace, regulatory conditions, and premium rates charged by competitors, among other factors. An insurer often itemizes its sales budget by line of business, such as individual life insurance and individual annuities.

  • An investment budget projects the types of investments the insurer will make and the expected amount of investment income for each type of investment. A company often itemizes its investment budget by investment type, such as bonds, mortgages, and stocks.

 

Expense Budgets

After developing its revenue budget, a company then prepares its expense budget. An expense budget is a schedule of expenses expected during an accounting period. The insurer bases the expense budget in part on revenue projections, but also on historical expense data, economic trends, and management goals, among other factors. An expense budget is useful in controlling expenses, measuring management performance, and assigning responsibility for expenses. Common components of the expense budget for insurance companies include the

  • Benefits budget, which indicates the amount of money the insurer expects to pay for insurance policy or annuity contract benefits, such as claims, cash surrenders, and policyowner dividends, during the next accounting period. Most expenses in benefits budgets are variable expenses because these expenses vary with changes in sales volume.

  • Sales expense budget, which indicates the costs incurred in selling insurance. These expenses include the costs of commissions, advertising, promotions, travel, branch office operations, and salaries for marketing staff. Sales expense budget figures are also typically variable expenses.

  • General and administrative expense budget, which includes expenses—typically fixed expenses—needed for insurance company operations. Usually, this budget is the sum of all departmental expense budgets. The general and administrative expense budget also includes such company-wide expenses as administrative salaries, rent, and utilities.

Cash Budgets

Cash budgeting is a budgeting activity that anticipates the cash flows into and out of a company during an accounting period. Many companies divide the annual cash budget into quarterly, monthly, weekly, and sometimes daily budgets to monitor cash flow more closely. Automated accounting systems make real-time cash reporting available. A cash budget is a type of budget that projects a company’s beginning cash balance, cash inflows, cash outflows, and ending cash balance for a specified accounting period, typically by quarter.

The cash budget is not part of the operational budget, but it provides context for the operational budget by letting the insurer know how much cash is available to invest or to spend. Budgeting for cash helps a company in both the short term and the long term. In the short term, the company can plan for both cash shortages and cash excesses; in the long term, company management can decide the company’s investment strategy.

Cash inflows and outflows are unique to each company. Insurers use forecasting techniques to project cash inflows and cash outflows. A cash budget consists of a cash receipts budget (cash inflows) and a cash disbursements budget (cash outflows). A company plans for its cash inflows through a cash receipts budget, which is a schedule of cash receipts expected during the accounting period. To project both the timing and amount of its cash receipts, a company constructs the cash receipts budget using data from both the sales and investment forecasts. A cash receipts budget resembles a revenue budget, such as the budget shown in Figure 15.3. For simplicity’s sake, this budget assumes no timing differences between income and cash receipts. In most cases, the figures in a cash receipts budget will vary from the figures in a revenue budget because the timing of cash payments may vary.

In the cash disbursements budget, an insurer estimates the timing and amount of cash disbursements expected during the accounting period. Estimating cash disbursements for fixed expenses such as salaries is relatively easy and accurate. Cash disbursements for variable expenses, such as contractual benefit payments and commissions, can be less predictable. The accuracy of these projections depends in large part on the accuracy of the sales forecast. Figure 15.6 shows an example of a cash disbursements budget.

Once an insurer has created the cash receipts budget and the cash disbursements budget, the next step is to combine them into the cash budget. Information contained in the cash budget includes the

  • Beginning cash balance, which equals the ending cash balance from the prior period

  • Available cash for the period, which equals the beginning cash balance plus cash receipts expected during the period

  • Minimum cash balance, an amount that is determined by the company

  • Cash needed for the period, which equals cash disbursements plus the minimum cash balance

  • Excess cash or cash shortage, which equals available cash minus cash needed for the period

  • Amount of any cash borrowed or repaid, including interest

  • Ending cash balance, which equals excess cash or shortage of cash plus any borrowing or repayment activity

The minimum cash balance is the amount of cash that a company determines is necessary to pay all obligations in an accounting period without having excess cash. Setting this figure too low raises the risk of running out of cash. Setting this figure too high carries an opportunity cost because this cash is not earning a return for the insurer. Insurers use many methods to derive the minimum cash balance. Some insurers set the minimum cash balance equal to the budgeted cash disbursements for one month. Others use computer spreadsheet simulations to set the minimum cash balance.

By examining the cash budget, an insurer can estimate the timing and amount of cash shortages. The insurer can then plan to counteract any shortages, such as through short-term borrowing or liquidating assets. The insurer can also calculate the amount of excess cash to invest in liquid assets.

Capital Budgets

Capital budgeting is the process companies undertake to analyze decisions about investing in long-term projects or assets. Capital budgeting often includes many steps, which are beyond the scope of this textbook. A capital budget shows a company’s plans for the financial management of its long-term, high-cost investment proposals. In the context of capital budgeting, the word “investment” typically does not refer to the direct purchase of bonds, mortgages, or stocks. Rather, these long-term, high-cost investments include

  • New acquisitions of equipment or real estate

  • Remodeling of or major repairs to existing capital investments

  • Acquisitions of other companies or lines of business

  • Mandated safety and environmental improvements

  • Expense reduction projects

  • Purchases of new computer systems and equipment

  • Revenue expansion projects

A common trait of capital budgeting is that these investments are expected to produce income or other benefits for more than one year. Figure 15.8 presents an example of a capital budget for a proposed new facility.

 

Pro Forma Financial Statements

The master budget culminates in a set of pro forma financial statements. In other contexts, the term pro forma, meaning “for the sake of form,” describes an action taken for the purpose of formality or politeness. In accounting, a pro forma financial statement is a financial statement that a company develops using forecasted amounts. In this context, the term “pro forma” indicates that the statement is a projection. The term usually implies that the statement was prepared to consider the effects of a potential activity. For example, an insurer considering a major acquisition might create pro forma financial statements to review its projected post-acquisition financial situation.

Pro forma financial statements include a pro forma balance sheet, a pro forma income statement, and a pro forma cash flow statement. For example, a pro forma income statement contains projected amounts for a company’s revenues and expenses. Insurance companies typically develop a series of pro forma financial statements for

  • Financial modeling purposes

  • Reporting to external parties, such as the SEC or insurance regulatory authorities

  • Considering the financial effects of specific events, such as a new product launch, a change in corporate form, or a change in accounting methods
