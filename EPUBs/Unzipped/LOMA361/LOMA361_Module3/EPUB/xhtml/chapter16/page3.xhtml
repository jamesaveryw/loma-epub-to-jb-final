Internal Control

Two general types of internal control are administrative controls and accounting controls:

  • Administrative controls are the policies and procedures that guide employees in conducting their normal day-to-day tasks. An example of an administrative control is a policy that requires a manager to check business references before hiring a new employee.

  • Accounting controls are the policies and procedures used to authorize financial transactions, safeguard assets, and provide reliable, timely, and fairly presented financial information about a company. In insurance companies, accounting controls generally include the preparation and analysis of financial statements, internal auditing, and other measures of financial performance.

Although administrative controls and accounting controls have unique qualities, they are sometimes difficult to distinguish in practice.

By adhering to accounting controls, an insurer can be reasonably certain that only authorized employees

  • Record accounting entries for the insurer’s financial transactions

  • Prepare financial statements that comply with applicable accounting principles and regulatory requirements

  • Access the insurer’s assets

  • Compare the insurer’s assets with its accounting records for those assets

 

Preventive Controls and Detective Controls

An insurer’s internal controls should include both preventive controls and detective controls. A preventive control is an internal control that is designed to keep—that is, “prevent”—an error or a problem from occurring. Normally, preventive controls involve steps that must be taken before a business transaction occurs. Examples of preventive controls in insurance companies include internal procedures that require

  • Proper documentation—for example, original documents or acceptable copies of original documents—before claims processors disburse benefit payments

  • Two signatures on checks written over a specified dollar amount

  • Valid codes to be entered for each transaction

A detective control is an internal control that identifies—or “detects”—an error or a problem after it has occurred. Most detective controls are undertaken after a series of business transactions. Examples of detective controls for insurance companies include procedures such as a

  • Monthly review of claims disbursements

  • Daily balance of department accounts

  • Daily review of reports of questionable data

Preventive controls are usually more effective at reducing errors or problems than are detective controls, but preventive controls are also typically more expensive. As with any other expense, insurers analyze both preventive and detective controls to ensure that they provide benefits that are worth their costs.

Limitations of Internal Controls

Internal controls are a necessary part of a life insurance company’s operations. However, no system of internal controls is infallible. Internal controls are designed for specified conditions. As these conditions change, the internal controls must also be changed. An insurer may need to review its internal controls due to external or internal changes:

  • External changes would include changes to accounting standards or amendments to the federal income tax code.

  • Internal changes would include the introduction of a new product or the modification of an existing product.

An insurance company’s internal controls are designed in part to reduce or avoid employee errors. However, employees can make mistakes in implementing the internal controls. Improperly implemented controls are unlikely to detect the problems they were designed to detect. Common preventive controls that companies use to manage employee errors include

  • Hiring the best-qualified people available

  • Training new employees thoroughly in the proper use of established internal controls

  • Involving experienced employees in the development of new internal controls

Internal controls are also designed to reduce the incidence of criminal acts, such as theft against a company by employees. Because no system is perfect, however, a determined employee occasionally can circumvent a company’s internal controls, at least temporarily.

A company must examine and evaluate its policies and procedures, and then develop controls that fit the company’s operations. Internal controls must be workable from an operational point of view as well as provide greater benefits than they cost.

 

Principles of Effective Internal Control

To be effective, an insurer’s internal control system must

  • Facilitate employee training

  • Facilitate communication of policies and procedures

  • Provide warning signals for prompt error detection

  • Indicate whether the company is complying with regulatory requirements

Note that an insurer could be subject to fines and other penalties for noncompliance with regulatory requirements. For example, regulators can impose a fine on a life insurance company that continues to file late quarterly financial statements. The payment of fines and penalties decreases an insurer’s net income and ultimately its surplus.

All effective internal control systems follow certain principles, including segregation of duties, execution of transactions as authorized, recording of transactions as executed, safeguarding of assets, and physical comparison of recorded amounts.

 

Segregation of Duties

The principle of segregation of duties, also known as the principle of dual control, requires an employer to design jobs so that incompatible functions are assigned to different individuals. In this context, incompatible functions are job duties that, when combined, place an employee in a position to commit an illegal act or to conceal errors or irregularities in the normal course of his employment. When incompatible functions are combined, an insurer is also less likely to detect unintentional employee errors. Segregating—or “separating”—duties among employees provides an environment in which an insurer can protect its assets and detect and correct errors and irregularities quickly.

In general, an effective internal control system separates the (1) custodial tasks, (2) authorization tasks, and (3) accounting tasks associated with an insurer’s assets. In this context, custody is defined as the physical or electronic handling of assets. Under segregation of duties, the employees typically work in different areas or functions of the company.

EXAMPLE

Example. At the Evergreen Life Insurance Company, Julio assumes custody of all securities, whereas Shu-Ling is authorized to sell these securities. Finally, Marisela maintains the records associated with these securities.

Analysis. If Evergreen had not separated these incompatible functions, a dishonest employee would be able to conceal irregularities associated with selling these securities; even an honest employee could make a serious mistake that would potentially go undetected.

Complicating an insurer’s adherence to the segregation of duties is the trend toward employee empowerment, especially the delegation of start-to-finish processing by one employee. Under employee empowerment programs, many insurers delegate authority to employees at the lowest possible level in specified functions. This delegation can result in a blurring of incompatible functions. Therefore, under an employee empowerment program, an insurer should conduct random internal audits of business transactions more frequently than in a more hierarchical organization. We discuss internal auditing in detail later in the chapter.

Insurers usually purchase a fidelity bond on each employee who oversees particularly sensitive areas, such as investment management or cash management (treasury operations). A fidelity bond is an indemnity insurance agreement that provides coverage for a financial loss caused by (1) the act or default of a third party, such as an employee or a business partner, or (2) some contingency, such as fire, over which the third party may have no control. Fidelity bonds can help protect an insurer from the risks associated with employee violation of internal controls. Some insurance companies purchase fidelity bonds on all employees.

 

Execution of Transactions as Authorized

The execution of transactions as authorized is the element of the internal control system that concerns the delegation of authority to perform specified tasks and the communication of that authority. In an insurance company, many different types of monetary transactions—such as issuing policies, paying benefits, purchasing and selling securities, and paying operating expenses—require employee authorization. No financial transaction should occur without some form of official authorization.

Authorization levels should be realistic and a company should communicate them clearly. Within the context of an internal control system, an authorization level is the maximum monetary amount that a company employee has been granted official power to approve for disbursement. Whatever the type of transaction, a specified employee authorizes each transaction at the appropriate level. Each authorization level should reflect the transaction and the experience and responsibility of the employee concerned.

EXAMPLE

Example. A claims processor with only two months’ work experience may have to obtain authorization for all claim payments over $2,500, whereas an experienced claims processor may be authorized to approve claim payments up to $50,000, within specified guidelines.

Note that, under execution of transactions as authorized, the employees typically work in the same area or function, but at different levels.

 

Recording of Transactions as Executed

The recording of transactions as executed means that a company records all authorized and executed transactions in the correct accounting period, in the correct accounts, and in the correct monetary amounts. In this way, the company can summarize all transactions for that accounting period in its financial statements.

EXAMPLE

Example. An insurer records premium income when it is received. The accounting entry includes the proper amount of the cash receipt, posted to the correct record in the policyowner master file and to the correct bank account.

Communicating the policies and procedures necessary to document a financial transaction is an essential part of recording transactions as executed. Employee training sessions, prenumbered checks and purchase orders, and written procedures manuals help support the documentation of an insurer’s financial transactions.

 

Safeguarding of Assets

The safeguarding of assets is the process of (1) limiting access to offices, documents, and records; (2) storing certain documents in vaults to which no person has sole access; (3) maintaining written recovery plans and off-premises storage of back-up files for all critical records in case of a business disaster; (4) investigating the integrity of employees hired to fill sensitive positions; (5) taking a physical inventory of all assets and comparing this count to a written record; and (6) reviewing the adequacy of insurance coverage, given a company’s risk exposure.

Safeguarding assets is a balancing act. An insurer needs to make sure that certain assets are widely accessible so that it can conduct normal business operations, but access to other assets should be restricted to authorized persons. An insurer’s computer system may be safeguarded, for example, by

  • Requiring the use of passwords before employees are allowed access to software applications

  • Maintaining adequate insurance and service contracts on the system

  • Requiring employees to follow appropriate virus protection procedures

  • Providing back-up copies of critical records and housing them in an off-site facility

  • Separating the incompatible functions between those who program the database software and those who enter records into the database

Physical Comparison of Recorded Amounts

The physical comparison of recorded amounts means that a company counts its assets on a periodic basis and compares this count to the amounts in its accounting records. This physical comparison is typically accomplished by means of an inventory. An inventory is an itemized count and listing of a company’s assets, such as property, products, materials, or securities.

INSIGHT

Effective Internal Controls at an Insurance Company

 

Approval, review, checking, or recalculation

  • Senior management approves all claim payments over a specified dollar amount
  • Actuarial staff recalculates premiums charged on a block of policies
  • Claims processors complete a checklist that indicates the insurer has received all required documents before the insurer pays a claim

Prenumbering

  • Checks and purchase orders are printed with sequential numbers
  • Policy applications and claims are numbered as they are received
  • Internal auditors check the numbering sequence and reconcile any number that is missing or out of sequence

Maintenance of control totals

  • All policy loans are listed and subtotaled separately, and then the subtotals are reconciled to the insurer’s total policy loans
  • Premium checks are grouped and totaled to obtain a batch total, which is entered into the insurer’s automated system

Comparison with third-party information

  • The insurer’s cash receipts and disbursements are reconciled with the insurer’s bank statement, cash accounts, and suspense accounts
  • Information in the insurer’s accounting records concerning its notes payable is reconciled with statements provided by the notes’ owners
  • Notifications of errors in payments or policy records or confirmation of amounts receivable from persons or companies that owe the insurer money are routinely compared

Cancellation of documentation

  • Cancelled or voided documents are defaced or shredded in some way so that they cannot be sent through normal processing channels

Timeliness of operation

  • All transactions are processed promptly, including daily deposits of cash receipts
  • Periodic reviews of new business are conducted to resolve insurance policies that have neither been issued nor denied after a specified time
