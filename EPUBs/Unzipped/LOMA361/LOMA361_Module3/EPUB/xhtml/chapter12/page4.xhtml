Performance Measurement and Evaluation

How do companies know if their responsibility centers are performing well? Managers measure and evaluate their performance:

  • Measuring performance involves quantifying the responsibility center’s results.

  • Evaluating performance involves assessing, or analyzing, the responsibility center’s results.

Responsibility accounting requires reliable procedures for fairly and accurately measuring and evaluating the performance of responsibility centers and responsibility managers. One measure that managers use is variance analysis.

 

Variance Analysis

The difference between an actual result and an expected or assumed result is called a variance. By measuring the extent to which various responsibility centers are achieving the company’s goals, variances provide valuable feedback to management. Variance analysis is the study of the difference between expected or assumed results and actual results. Variance analysis is helpful in measuring and evaluating performance because variances quantify the unexpected results for which responsibility managers are accountable.

Variance analysis is an important component of management by exception (MBE), a management style that focuses on results that deviate from an established baseline. MBE differs from management styles such as management by objectives (MBO), which focuses on achieving specific goals commonly agreed upon by management and employees. In MBE, managers are alerted only when actual results vary by a specified amount or percentage from expected results. Therefore, MBE allows managers to spend less time monitoring results and more time on other tasks.

In variance analysis, budgets are an effective tool for pinpointing variances from a standard or an expected result. A budget is a financial plan of action, expressed in monetary terms, that covers a specified period, such as one year. Large variances between budgeted and actual revenues and expenses are of particular concern to a company’s managers, who may respond by revising budgets or making operational changes. Managers generally try to make these changes as soon as possible to minimize potential losses.

EXAMPLE

Example. The claims function at the Leonine Life Insurance Company has a $100,000 monthly budget for salaries expense. Actual salaries expense for September is $117,000. Suppose Leonine’s management established a policy stating that management must investigate and review any expense variance over 10%.

Analysis. The claims function’s variance in salaries expense would be 17%, calculated as ($117,000 – $100,000) ÷ $100,000. Because the variance is 17% over the budgeted amount, Leonine’s management would investigate and review it.

Although variance analysis usually involves studying expected and actual results, a company’s managers can also use variance analysis to compare other data. For example, a marketing manager may compare the number of new policies written during the current three-month period with the number of new policies written during the previous three-month period or during the same three-month period one year ago. Variance analysis can also compare actual costs with standard costs.

 

Types of Variances

Variances can be categorized as positive variances or negative variances:

  • A positive variance is generally one in which the actual amount or percentage of an amount is greater than expected. A positive variance is considered favorable for revenues because actual revenues are higher than expected, and unfavorable for expenses because actual expenses are higher than expected.

  • A negative variance is generally a variance in which the actual amount or percentage of an amount is less than expected. A negative variance is considered unfavorable for revenues because actual revenues are lower than expected, and favorable for expenses because actual expenses are lower than expected.

Variances may also be considered favorable or unfavorable, depending on whether actual costs are lower or higher than standard costs. In the context of cost accounting, a standard cost is a predetermined cost expected to be incurred for particular items during normal business operations. Many companies establish a standard cost for each item to use as a budgeted cost or for variance analysis.

We can define favorable variances and unfavorable variances as follows:

  • A favorable variance is a variance in which the actual cost is lower than the standard cost. A favorable variance also occurs when revenues are higher than expected and/or expenses are lower than expected.

  • An unfavorable variance is a variance in which the actual cost is higher than the standard cost. An unfavorable variance also occurs when revenues are lower than expected and/or expenses are higher than expected. Our earlier example of actual salaries exceeding the budgeted expense is an example of an unfavorable variance, because the $117,000 actual cost was higher than the $100,000 expected (standard) cost.

Two important standard costs for insurance companies are the unit cost of processing one claim and the unit cost of maintaining one policy. (We discuss unit costs in the next chapter.) An insurer first establishes a standard cost for processing one claim and then uses variance analysis to compare the actual cost with the standard cost of processing a claim. Rate Variances and Usage Variances

We can also categorize variances as either rate variances or usage variances. A product’s rate variance is the difference between the product’s actual rate (or unit cost or unit price) and the standard rate budgeted for that product, multiplied by the actual number of units sold or processed. Therefore, the general formula for calculating a rate variance is

Rate variance = (Actual rate – Standard rate) × Actual number of units sold or processed

Companies typically calculate rate variances for labor costs, product costs, selling price, and overhead expenses.

A product’s usage variance is the difference between the actual quantities sold or processed and the budgeted (standard) quantities to be sold or processed, multiplied by the standard rate or unit cost. We can express usage variance in equation form as

Usage variance = (Actual quantities sold or processed – Budgeted [Standard] quantities sold or processed) × Standard rate

Companies typically calculate usage variances for labor efficiency, units used in a process, and the cost of materials to provide a service.

The sum of a product’s rate variance and usage variance equals the product’s total variance, as follows:

Total variance = Rate variance + Usage variance

Responsibility Reports

A responsibility report, sometimes called a responsibility center report, is a management accounting report that itemizes standard (budgeted) and actual amounts that are under the responsibility manager’s sphere of control and indicates the corresponding variance for each revenue or cost (expense). The responsibility report either omits items that a responsibility manager cannot control, or lists these items separately. Segment Reports

The example in Video 12.3 shows monthly responsibility reports for the policyowner service function at several management levels. Because these departments and divisions are all cost centers, their evaluation is based primarily on cost control. However, the evaluation of a profit center should be based on the profits earned by the center, not exclusively on how the profit center controls costs. One way to monitor profit centers is to prepare a specific type of responsibility report, sometimes called a segment report. A segment report is a type of responsibility report that shows revenues and expenses—divided into fixed and variable components and direct and indirect costs—for a single responsibility center.

Segment reports for profit centers generally take the form of an income statement, because these reports show both revenues and expenses. A profit center’s segment report details activity at each management level, including condensed information from operations that are subordinate to the managerial level. Segment reports follow a similar pattern, as shown in the cost center responsibility reports in Video 12.3. As we move downward from one level to the next, segment reports examine a smaller portion of the company or division. Segment reports separate direct costs and indirect costs at each management level. Although segment reporting is primarily a management accounting tool, segment reports are sometimes required for financial reporting purposes.

Problems with Performance Evaluation Criteria

Responsibility managers have their performance evaluated based on a variety of factors, depending on the nature of a given responsibility center:

  • For cost centers, the evaluation typically relies on comparing actual costs to standard or budgeted costs and how managers are able to control their direct costs.

  • Revenue center responsibility managers typically are evaluated by comparing actual revenues to standard or budgeted revenues.

  • Responsibility managers of profit centers generally are evaluated on how they generate revenues while controlling their direct costs.

  • Responsibility managers of investment centers typically are evaluated on cost control, revenue generation, and the profit, earnings, and return on the assets or investments under their direct control.

The performance evaluation process of a responsibility center must consider several potential problems, some of which are inherent in the evaluation process itself. Managers must know how the financial information about a responsibility center was calculated, recorded, and reported before evaluating the center’s performance. For example, establishing standard costs is a reactive, rather than proactive, approach to estimating costs, and therefore based on existing experience.

Standard costs are more suited for a company’s core products and services, rather than products and services that are customized or unique in nature, and that therefore would require extrapolation. Therefore, comparing actual costs to standard costs may not be the appropriate method of evaluation. Other issues—including the time and resources used to estimate standard costs and the potential conflict between using standard costs to evaluate responsibility managers and the company’s overall performance evaluation process—can directly affect a responsibility center’s performance. Thus, the evaluation criteria used can be as important as the actual performance in the evaluative process. Figure 12.3 summarizes some of the more common mistakes a company might make during performance evaluation.

 

Narrowly Focused Variance Analysis

Isolating budget variances is a common method for evaluating a responsibility center’s performance. But unless these budget variances are examined in detail, potential problems might go undetected. For instance, senior management may have a tendency to focus on variances that negatively affect a responsibility center. However, some problems might be revealed by analyzing variances that appear to affect a responsibility center positively. For example, a dramatic increase in first-year premium revenues that might appear to benefit the company may be the result of using lax underwriting standards that could result in extensive losses to the insurer later.

EXAMPLE

Example. At Battlement Life, the claims department manager kept costs under control by assigning more work to each claims employee, rather than hiring more employees.

Analysis. While variance analysis indicates that the claims department is performing as expected, the department faces the risk of heavy employee turnover due to the unsustainable workload, which would have a strong negative effect on future productivity.

 

Use of a Single Evaluation Criterion

Sometimes a company’s senior management focuses a responsibility manager’s performance evaluation on one goal and ignores all other company goals. Such a simple criterion is easy for a manager to keep in mind at all times, but it can have unintended negative results.

EXAMPLE

Example. Battlement Life evaluates the manager of its underwriting department solely on the number of policies underwriters approve.

Analysis. The underwriting manager has the incentive to pressure the underwriters to approve every policy application they receive. As a result, Battlement could suffer losses—such as higher lapse rates than expected—from poorly written business. Therefore, Battlement should evaluate the performance of its underwriting manager on additional criteria, such as placement rates.

 

Use of Inappropriate Criteria

If performance criteria fail to measure the results of a responsibility center against a company’s goals, the criteria are ineffective. For example, some companies focus management evaluations on short-term results. This focus may be tempting because of its immediate application and emphasis on swift correction of issues. However, responsibility managers in such cases may be tempted to forsake long-term company goals and overuse current resources in order to earn a satisfactory short-term performance evaluation. The long-term consequences may include insufficient resources to achieve future company goals.

EXAMPLE

Example. Battlement Life evaluates its call center employees primarily on call time length. The call center manager encourages employees to resolve customer calls as quickly as possible.

Analysis. By stressing that a call center employee’s first priority is to resolve a call quickly, Battlement inadvertently discourages employees from providing thorough customer service. Employees who do not obtain all of the details of a customer’s problem or who fail to adequately resolve a problem might still be commended for their work if the call is ended on a timely basis. Thus, Battlement might develop a reputation for poor customer service despite the intentions of its managers.

 

Unattainable Goals

Sometimes a company may set unrealistically high performance standards. When a goal is not realistically attainable by responsibility managers, a responsibility manager will receive a poor evaluation for failing to achieve the goal. Senior management can avoid this problem by ensuring that a responsibility manager’s goals are realistic and attainable, by understanding how the budgets and other standards are determined, and by ensuring that the evaluation process considers only comparisons of budgeted and actual results that are controlled by the responsibility manager. A company can also involve its responsibility managers in budget preparation, relying on their experience to help determine which goals are realistically attainable.

 

Evaluation Solely on Responsibility Center Performance

A responsibility manager’s performance may be good, even though the responsibility center’s performance is poor. The reverse is also true, when a line of business benefits from a growing market regardless of the responsibility manager’s actions. Senior management must consider whether the responsibility manager’s performance has affected the center’s performance or whether the center’s performance was primarily a function of its environment.

EXAMPLE

Example. Over the course of a fiscal year, many financial companies experienced decreasing sales for their variable annuities products. The responsibility manager for Battlement Life’s variable annuities line received a poor performance evaluation based on a slight decrease in variable annuity sales. However, variable annuity sales at Battlement did not decrease as much as they did at many other companies.

Analysis. Since many companies experienced decreasing variable annuity sales, the market for variable annuities as a whole was in decline. Under these conditions, sales growth of variable annuities may have been an unachievable goal. Battlement’s responsibility manager for variable annuities likely deserves a better evaluation for keeping the decrease in sales minimal.

 
 

Arbitrary Cost Allocation

Cost allocation is the accounting process of assigning or distributing to responsibility centers an indirect cost (expense) according to a specified method or formula. Sometimes internal influences, such as insufficient data or a flawed allocation method or formula, can prevent managers from allocating costs properly. Keep in mind that the choice of method used for cost allocation, as well as the selection of allocation bases for each method, will affect the results of an insurer’s responsibility centers and their managers. We discuss cost allocation bases in Chapter 13.

 

Arbitrary Revenue Allocation

Similarly, unless a company accurately allocates its revenues to the responsibility centers that are accountable for those revenues, the performance of the company’s responsibility centers and managers cannot be fairly measured and evaluated. Revenue allocation is the accounting process of assigning or distributing income or revenue items according to a specified method or formula. If a responsibility center’s revenues are overstated or understated, management decisions about the center can be impaired.

Allocating premium income and fee income to the appropriate responsibility center is generally straightforward because income items (revenues) are typically identified by product line, such as individual life insurance or annuities. However, allocating investment income to the appropriate responsibility center is more difficult. The method used depends in part on the insurer’s objectives.

The following are the most common revenue allocation methods that life insurance companies use to allocate investment income to responsibility centers:

  • The mean liabilities method allocates investment income to lines of business based on the reserves that are attributable to each line. Recall that a life insurance company’s reserves typically are the company’s largest liability. For example, if 40 percent of an insurer’s reserves are attributable to group life insurance, then the insurer allocates 40 percent of its investment income to the group life insurance responsibility center. One potential issue associated with the mean liabilities method is that it does not consider the impact of the surplus required to support a line of business. Newer lines of business may suffer from a lack of resources if they are not allocated a share of the investment income generated by older, high-yielding investments that were purchased before the new products existed. The mean liabilities method is used in preparing the U.S. Annual Statement.

  • The investment year method forms separate investment pools that contain the investments made during each year, and then, a share of the investment pool for each calendar year is allocated to each line of business. Each line’s share of investment income is determined by the investable funds generated by that line each year. Investable funds consist of (1) net income before taxes, (2) investment income received on prior investments, and (3) the maturities of principal on prior investments. This method overcomes one weakness of the mean liabilities method, as an insurer’s newer lines of business—which have lower amounts of accumulated funds and larger cash outflows from current operations—receive relatively larger shares of investment income from newer investments. In addition, the insurer’s older lines of business—which typically have larger reserves and lower amounts of cash outflows—receive larger shares of investment income from older investments.

  • In the investment segmentation method, an insurer uses the cash flows of a line of business to purchase specific investments for that line. The insurer then allocates the investment income from each investment to the line that acquired the investment. Under this method, an insurer can develop separate investment strategies for different lines of business. Such versatility is important because cash flow patterns and investment income requirements differ along lines of business. For example, the cash flows of an insurer’s annuity business differ from those of its whole life insurance business.

 

Inappropriate Use of Transfer Pricing

Insurers may find it more challenging to evaluate the performance of specific responsibility centers, such as profit centers and investment centers, when transfer pricing is involved. A transfer price is the price of a good or service that one function or line of business charges another of the same company. For example, the human resources department of an insurer may use transfer pricing to charge the insurer’s group life insurance line of business for employee recruitment or training expenses. Unlike intercompany transactions, in which prices are determined by market factors such as supply and demand, transfer pricing arrangements are intracompany in nature—that is, they are established by a company’s internal management team.

Transfer pricing is especially important for insurance companies that have subsidiaries or are themselves subsidiaries. The transfer pricing method that an insurer selects is critical, because transfer prices directly affect the profits for which the responsibility managers of profit centers and investment centers are held accountable. An inappropriate transfer price might result in a misleading evaluation of a responsibility center’s performance. Moreover, inappropriate transfer prices may lead a responsibility manager to make decisions that are not in the insurer’s best interest. A discussion of the process of setting transfer prices is beyond the scope of this textbook.
