Cost Classification

Generally, companies have the freedom to design cost accounting systems that satisfy their unique needs. As a result, cost accounting systems vary greatly among companies and possibly even among different divisions of the same company. Though these systems are very diverse in form, most effective cost accounting systems categorize costs by (1) type of cost, (2) line of business, (3) department or cost center, or (4) function.

Cost classification requires practical definitions that reflect both the attributes of a cost and the circumstances surrounding a cost. Generally, the more accurately an insurer can classify its costs in the context of its core business transactions, the more effectively the insurer can achieve its management accounting goals. Insurers typically classify costs by one of three categories:

  • Description

  • Behavior

  • Measurement

Many of these costs have similar or shared characteristics. We describe these shared characteristics where appropriate.

 
 

Descriptive Classification

The most common way to classify costs is by their descriptive characteristics. Figure 13.2 presents definitions and examples of cost classifications by description. Direct and indirect costs and controllable and noncontrollable costs, as discussed in the previous chapter, are examples of descriptive classification.

An insurer can classify a cost in several different ways, depending on the point of view of the person analyzing the cost and the point of view of the department incurring the cost. This flexibility helps illustrate the impact that individual costs have on each department. For example, the salary of an insurer’s vice president of marketing is

  • A direct cost of the marketing division as a whole

  • An indirect cost with respect to the individual life insurance marketing department within the marketing division

  • A controllable cost to the chief executive officer of the company

 

Sunk Costs

Sunk costs are distinct from most other costs because a company has already incurred them. In the context of management accounting, a manager’s decisions cannot influence or recover sunk costs. Sunk costs also should not influence a manager’s decisions. Although it can be tempting to consider a sunk cost as an “investment” that the company must recoup, this attitude can lead to costly mistakes.

EXAMPLE

Example. Last year, Forge Life Insurance spent $500,000 to upgrade its call center. This year, fewer customers are using the call center, and more customers are requesting online assistance. The estimated cost to upgrade Forge’s online service systems is $700,000. Forge’s CEO argues that it would be more economical to spend a smaller amount to encourage customers to use the call center, which the company has already paid for. The CEO proposes that the company should instead invest $150,000 in a program to promote the call center.

Analysis. Forge will not recover the sunk cost of $500,000, regardless of the decision the company makes. Forge should exclude this sunk cost and compare the relevant cost of $700,000 against the potential losses the company could see if Forge fails to meet its customers’ online expectations. Consider if Forge were to view the online service upgrades in terms of “total service investment,” and calculate the investment costs as $1,200,000 (last year’s $500,000 sunk cost + the $700,000 cost of the online service system upgrade) versus $650,000 (last year’s $500,000 sunk cost + the $150,000 cost of call center promotion). By focusing on sunk costs and not potential future losses, the company would assess this year’s potential improvements as more costly than they actually are. Therefore, the company might miss an affordable opportunity to increase customer loyalty.

 

Discretionary Costs

Although discretionary costs are not immediately necessary to the production of a good or service, a discretionary cost may still play a large role in the success of a product, and therefore be justifiable. Some discretionary costs may ultimately prove to be necessary over the long term. However, by definition, a manager can decrease or even eliminate discretionary costs as needed. Examples of discretionary costs include advertising, employee training, quality control, and research.

EXAMPLE

Example. Last year, Strategist Life Insurance established a $750,000 advertising budget to promote its new line of annuity products. This year, Strategist reduced the advertising budget for these annuity products to $250,000.

Analysis. The advertising budget is a discretionary cost because this budget is not necessary for Strategist to create annuity products. Strategist could eliminate the entire advertising budget for its annuity line and still offer annuities to its customers. Therefore, Strategist can analyze the cost of advertising in the context of how much additional annuity revenue it is likely to generate.

 

Classification by Behavior

The behavior of a cost describes how the cost changes with time and volume. Some costs change as the amount of time needed to complete an activity changes. An activity is any procedure that generates work. An activity driver, also known as a cost driver, is the output of an activity being performed; an activity driver is the reason or purpose for which an activity’s cost is incurred. We discuss activities and activity drivers in the next chapter. Other costs change as the volume of activity changes. Many costs change with both time and volume, and some costs do not change over time or by the volume of activity. The most common cost classifications by behavior are fixed costs, variable costs, and semi-variable costs:

  • A fixed cost is a cost that remains constant for all levels of operating activity or production. An example of a fixed cost is rent for a branch office. Rent, like other fixed costs, would remain the same whether the insurer processed a single policy or one million policies during a given period. We can further classify fixed costs as either sunk costs or discretionary costs.

  • A variable cost changes in direct response to changes in the level of operating activity. An example of a variable cost is agent commissions. If an insurer sells no insurance policies, it incurs no agent commission costs. However, as sales volume increases, the cost of commissions also increases. The cost of premium processing is another example of a variable cost.

  • A semi-variable cost, also called a mixed cost, contains elements of both fixed costs and variable costs and comprises a fixed cost component plus the amount of a variable component over time and at each volume level. Semi-variable costs include the costs for electricity, heat, repairs, telephone, and building maintenance. For example, the cost of electricity involves a basic monthly service charge to have electricity available for use. This charge is the fixed component, and it remains the same each month regardless of how much electricity the insurer uses, even if the insurer uses no electricity at all. The variable component of the insurer’s electricity cost includes the number of kilowatt-hours of electricity the company uses. If the insurer shuts off all its office lights every night, it uses fewer kilowatt-hours of electricity—and therefore incurs a lower variable electricity cost—than if it keeps its lights on all night. Classification by Measurement

Costs classified by measurement are especially helpful for management reports and for cost-volume-profit analysis, which we describe in the next chapter. Costs classified by measurement include

  • Standard costs

  • Unit costs

  • Marginal costs

  • Opportunity costs

We discussed standard costs in the previous chapter; now we’ll examine unit costs, marginal costs, and opportunity costs.

 

Unit Costs

Unit costs are a basic measurement in cost accounting. A unit cost, also known as an average cost, is the incurred expense attributable to a single measured amount of work. The single measured amount of work is usually one unit of a product or a service. Unit costs include all fixed costs and all variable costs. However, we can establish unit costs for both direct and indirect expenses and for both fixed and variable expenses. The formula is the same in all instances:

Unit cost = Total cost (fixed and variable)
Number of units

Normally, as production volume or the amount of activity increases, unit costs decrease. Conversely, as volume decreases, unit costs generally increase. Managers typically use unit costs as a means of evaluating or adjusting a product’s profitability. An insurance company usually expresses unit costs in the form of unit cost per

  • Policy issued

  • $1,000 of insurance coverage (face amount) issued

  • $100 of agent commissions paid

  • $1,000 of investment income

 

Marginal Costs

A marginal cost, also called an incremental cost, is the additional cost incurred as a result of producing one additional unit of an existing product or service. While differential costs can measure the expected increase or decrease in cost for undertaking a new product or service, marginal costs solely measure the additional total expense of increased volume of an already existing product or service. The marginal cost formula is as follows:

Marginal cost = Change in costs
Additional units produced

A company incurs a marginal cost after all fixed costs applicable to a product or service have already been accounted for; therefore, marginal costs are always variable costs. Marginal cost information helps managers determine the monetary effect of a specific action and, in certain cases, whether an action should or should not be taken. Managers usually use this information to guide short-term decisions such as selling price or the amount of materials purchased.

Note that an increase in volume generally increases only variable costs. Therefore, an increase in volume can lower unit costs. Watch Video 13.1 for an example of how both unit costs and marginal costs are calculated.

Opportunity Costs

When making decisions about expenditures, managers consider out-of-pocket costs. An out-of-pocket cost is a cost that requires outlays of cash or other resources. However, managers also need to consider the opportunity costs associated with a decision. An opportunity cost is the benefit that is forfeited or given up in choosing one decision alternative over another. Managers must be aware that all out-of-pocket costs carry associated opportunity costs—essentially, what the company could have gained by expending the out-of-pocket resources on another option. Note that sunk costs are not opportunity costs. An opportunity cost is a cost that may or may not yet be incurred, while a sunk cost is an actual financial cost that the company has already incurred.

A company does not actually know opportunity costs in advance; the company must estimate the costs for use in analysis and decision-making. As costs may change over time, a second, separate opportunity cost may be the cost of waiting as opposed to not having to wait.

In addition, because no transactions are involved, a company’s accounting system does not capture the financial impact of opportunity costs. For management accounting purposes, financial analysis typically includes the impact of selecting one option over another, so both out-of-pocket and opportunity costs of various options are included for comparative purposes. However, for financial reporting purposes, a company’s financial statements do not include opportunity costs because these costs are not actual, objective costs. Managers analyze various alternatives to determine the out-of-pocket and opportunity costs of each option. Then a manager selects the option for which the potential benefits gained are greater than the opportunity costs of the remaining options.

EXAMPLE

Example. Ranger Life Insurance is considering a new universal life product. The estimated out-of-pocket cost will be $1 million for research, actuarial work, systems, and marketing. Ranger’s management is also considering two other options: (1) a proposal for improving Ranger’s current universal life operating system or (2) a proposal for investing in new assets. Ranger can invest in only one of the three options: new product, improved operating system, or new assets. Alternatively, Ranger might choose not to invest the money at all, instead leaving it in an account to generate interest income.

Analysis. The operating system, new assets, and interest income are opportunity costs of the new product, as Ranger would be forfeiting their benefits if Ranger decides to pursue the new product. Similarly, if Ranger decides to use the funds either to improve the operating system or to invest in new assets, the new universal life product would be an opportunity cost of those decisions. The project manager therefore makes revenue and expense projections associated with (1) undertaking the new product, (2) enhancing the existing operating system, (3) investing in new assets, and (4) making no investment and instead generating interest income. In this way Ranger can assess which option would provide the greatest benefit, and which opportunity costs would be acceptable.
