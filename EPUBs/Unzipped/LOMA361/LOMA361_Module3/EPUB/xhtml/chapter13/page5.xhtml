Cost Accumulation

Ideally, a cost accounting system should provide responsibility managers with sufficient information to make informed decisions about the operations for which they are accountable. A cost accounting system should accumulate costs and allocate costs accurately and fairly. Cost accumulation is the process of capturing all company costs and categorizing them in meaningful ways.

Four methods of accumulating cost data are by

  • Type of cost

  • Line of business

  • Department or cost center

  • Function

Many insurers accumulate costs by two or more methods. This approach makes it easier to determine whether any specific cost is greater or less than expected.

 

Accumulating Costs by Type

In accumulating costs by type, a company accumulates costs without regard to the specified area of the company that incurs the expense. For example, a company can include all company costs relating to salaries in the “Salaries” classification, instead of itemizing these costs by department or line of business. Accumulating costs by type enables companies to satisfy financial reporting requirements. For example, the U.S. Annual Statement requires insurers to classify costs by the class or nature of the expense—such as rent, salaries, and travel expenses. This method also assists managers in determining which costs are increasing or decreasing over time. However, accumulating costs by type does not explain which areas of the company incur each cost and, therefore, who or what is responsible for changes in cost levels.

 

Accumulating Costs by Line of Business

When accumulating costs by line of business, an insurer identifies all costs that are directly attributable to a particular line and charges these costs to that line. For indirect expenses, an insurer generally first accumulates costs according to type and then determines the monetary amount or percentage that applies to each line of business, such as annuities or group life insurance, and charges those expenses to that line.

 

Accumulating Costs by Department or Cost Center

Cost centers typically accumulate their costs by type or function. When accumulating costs by department or cost center, insurers consolidate the costs of departments at each level of a company into the cost reports for departments at higher levels in the organization. This type of cost accumulation enables management to judge the performance of individual cost centers.

 

Accumulating Costs by Function

To accumulate costs by function, an insurer directs the costs to the company operation that generates the costs. In this context, a function consists of a series of activities that serve a specific purpose. For example, policy maintenance is a typical life insurance company function, and a company typically charges to that function all the costs associated with the maintenance of in-force policies. Activities within the policy maintenance function include collecting renewal premiums, processing agent renewal commissions, administering policy loans, and processing policy surrenders.

The accumulated costs of the activities involved within a certain function, without regard to organizational units, are known as functional costs. Within each function, managers accumulate costs by behavior, and then by type, such as salaries, supplies, and equipment. So, for example, when accumulating costs by function, management might first charge the cost of an agent’s commission to the sales function. Within the sales function, the manager would then accumulate the cost as a variable cost (by behavior), and then as a commission (by type).

Cost Perspectives

Recall that we can classify a single cost in different ways, depending on the perspective of the person analyzing the cost. The same principle applies to the process of cost accumulation. For example, the cost of underwriting applications for a new term life insurance product is

  • An underwriting cost when accumulated by type

  • A term life insurance cost when accumulated by line of business

  • An underwriting cost when accumulated by cost center

  • A policy acquisition cost when accumulated by function
