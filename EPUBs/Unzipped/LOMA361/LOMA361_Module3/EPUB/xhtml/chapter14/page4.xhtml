Activity-Based Costing

Recall from Chapter 13 that an activity is any procedure that generates work. Activity-based costing (ABC) is the process of linking costs to products or services based on the activities performed in producing the products or services. In other words, ABC identifies the costs of each activity within a particular function, and then assigns the activity costs to the appropriate products. For example, an insurer might first trace the activity of printing marketing brochures for agents to the payments made to an outside printer, and then trace the printer payments (costs) to the lines of business that used the marketing brochures.

Note that activity-based costing recognizes direct costs in the same way that other forms of costing do; ABC differs in how it assigns indirect costs. Traditional cost accounting systems assume that products generate costs; ABC assumes that activities generate costs. Compared to traditional cost accounting systems, ABC is more complex but provides greater accuracy. Companies that use ABC consistently are practicing activity-based management (ABM).

 

Activity-Based Costing Terminology

ABC uses special terminology. Recall that an activity driver is the output of an activity being performed. For example, investigating a claim is an activity for an insurer, and a processed claim is an activity driver. The following terms are also important to ABC:

  • An activity cost is the cost attributable to a specified activity. For example, a customer service call is a specified activity, and telephone charges therefore are an activity cost of customer service centers.

  • A value-added activity is an activity that makes the product more valuable to the customer. Because a customer is generally willing to pay more for better products or service, a company can typically pass costs associated with value-added activities through to a company’s customers in the form of higher prices. For example, mobile-friendly online tools are the outcome of value-added activities because they strongly appeal to customers, and therefore the costs associated with developing such tools typically can be passed on to customers.

  • A non-value-added activity is an activity that does not make the product or service more valuable to the customer. Because customers are not generally willing to pay more for non-value-added activities—such as fixing an error in a flawed product—such activities do not generate profit. Therefore, a company should usually minimize these activities. For example, because unnecessary steps in processing claims are non-value-added activities, a company would minimize these costs by streamlining the claims process rather than attempting to pass these costs on to customers by charging higher premiums.

To provide products, a company engages in activities. These activities consume resources—such as labor, supplies, and computer time—and produce outputs, such as a check for an approved claim. ABC identifies each activity, calculates the costs of performing each unit of activity, and then assigns the cost of each unit of activity to a unit of output or production. ABC also includes activities that do not link directly to a specific product. For instance, an online security upgrade safeguards all of a company’s products; therefore, ABC might classify the upgrade as an indirect cost and distribute the cost of this upgrade among all of the company’s products.

The primary benefits of ABC are that it provides a solid basis for assigning costs to products and assists management in identifying non-value-added activities. By identifying the specific costs of performing tasks and activities, management can determine the relationship between costs and products. Functional Cost Analysis

Recall that functional costs are the total activity costs within a function, regardless of which department or cost center incurred the costs. A company should design its cost accounting system to support functional cost analysis, which is a subset of activity-based costing. Functional cost analysis, or function cost analysis, is the process of accumulating the costs that are involved in each activity within a function. Functional cost analysis is distinct from ABC in that it groups costs by function as well as activity. For example, to apply functional cost analysis to the function of collecting renewal premiums, an insurer would accumulate the following types of costs:

  • Cost data from the automated processes associated with receiving and recording premium income

  • Machine costs for preparing and mailing premium notices

  • Selling costs and administrative costs by product line

  • Other indirect costs incurred by any department involved in collecting renewal premiums

An insurer’s accounting system should accumulate costs for workflow processes, organizational structures, or departments. Insurers use functional cost analysis to

  • Price products

  • Monitor and control current operational procedures

  • Identify trends that are not recognizable with conventional forms of analysis

  • Develop more accurate budget estimates

  • Benchmark operations against other companies

 

Functional Cost Accounting System

A functional cost accounting system requires a granular level of detail. The system must be able to identify each function within the company—marketing, claims processing, information processing, underwriting, etc.—as well as each line of business. These functions, lines of business, and so on, may not coincide with the company’s departmental units. For example, at one company the policy issue function might be the responsibility of a single subdivision of one department, whereas at another company the function might span two or more departments. When developing a functional cost accounting system, system designers must be careful to include all activities and costs—and only those activities and costs—that are relevant to the specific function under study. The entire functional cost accounting system should be flexible enough to change as the company grows or diversifies. The system should also be able to provide for exceptions and peculiarities within any function or line of business.

 

Functional Unit Costs

To analyze functional costs, a company usually establishes a functional unit cost as a basis. A functional unit cost is a type of functional cost that is calculated by dividing the total functional cost by an appropriate base unit. This appropriate base unit of measure depends on both the function and the line of business being analyzed. The unit of measure may be monetary or nonmonetary in nature: a payment, a product, or some other trackable unit.
