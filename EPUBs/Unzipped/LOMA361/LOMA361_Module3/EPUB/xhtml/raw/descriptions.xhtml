Video 12.2

Rate, Usage, and Total Variances

The Salmon Life Insurance Company’s claims department budgeted a standard rate of $40 to process a single claim, and expected to process 1,400 claims per quarter. Salmon’s actual cost to process a claim was $42, and it processed 1,300 claims rather than the expected 1,400.

Rate Variance = (Actual rate – Standard rate) x Actual number of units processed

Rate variance = ($42 – $40) x 1,300

Rate variance = $2 x 1,300

Rate variance = $2,600

Because the cost increased, the rate variance is unfavorable.

Now let’s examine the usage variance.

Usage variance = (Actual quantities processed – Budgeted [Standard] quantities processed) x Standard rate

Usage variance = (1,300 – 1,400) x $40

Usage variance = (-100) x $40

Usage variance = -$4,000

Because Salmon processed fewer claims, costs were lower than budgeted, and the usage variance is a favorable variance.

With the rate variance and the usage variance, we can calculate the total variance.

Total variance = Rate variance + Usage variance.

Total variance = $2,600 + (-$4,000)

Total variance = -$1,400

The result of the two variances is a total variance of -$1,400, meaning costs were overall $1,400 lower than budgeted. Although the claims department spent more money to process each claim, the lower total number of claims helped this cost center spend less money than budgeted.

The additional cost incurred as a result of producing one additional unit of an existing product or service

Figure 14.4

The Process of Activity-Based Costing

Step 1: Identify activities. Example: Underwriting an individual life insurance application.

Step 2: Identify the activity drivers for each activity. Example: The underwriting decision is one activity driver for underwriting.

Step 3: Assign costs to each activity. Example: Managers track underwriting time and relevant costs for salaries, equipment, and supplies.

Step 4: Determine the unit cost of each activity for comparative purposes. Example: The insurer divides total underwriting costs by the number of applications underwritten. Therefore, $43,750 underwriting costs ÷ 350 applications underwritten = $125 unit cost.

Step 5: Identify all areas for which the costs of that activity apply. Example: The outputs for underwriting unit costs are individual life insurance policies for which a decision was made.

Step 6: Assign each activity cost to the smallest meaningful organizational unit for which it has meaning. Example: The insurer assigns the activity costs for underwriting individual life insurance to the individual life line of business.

Top-Down and Bottom-Up Budgeting

Top-down budgeting:

  1. Senior management builds budget based on strategic plan and prior year’s activities
  2. Lower-level management implements and adopts budget

Bottom-up budgeting:

  1. Lower-level management generates budgets per department with employee input and participation
  2. Senior management receives recommendations for department budgets and assembles a budget based on these recommendations

Figure 15.7

A Cash Budget

Table: A cash budget, divided into four quarters and a total for the year. The budget compares Total Cash Receipts to Total Cash Needed and Total Effects of Financing to arrive at its Ending Cash Balance.

The equation for Ending Cash Balance is Total Cash Receipts – Total Cash Needed + Total Effects of Financing. This balance is calculated for every quarter.

Note that some entries in the end-of-year Total column are not derived as a toal of the monetary amounts in Quarters 1 through 4.

  1. Beginning balance for the year is equal to the beginning balance of the first quarter.
  2. Ending cash balance for the year is equal to the fourth quarter’s ending cash balance.
  3. Minimum cash balance is constant throughout the year.
  4. Excess of cash is equal to end-of-year total cash available minus total cash needed.

Types of Controls

Administrative controls guide employees in day-to-day tasks; accounting controls authorize financial transactions, safeguard assets, and ensure reliable financial reporting.

Preventive controls are implemented before a transaction to prevent errors or problems from occurring; detective controls are undertaken after a transaction to detect an error or problem after it occurs.

Balancing Segregation of Duties and Employee Empowerment

Insurers have to balance segregation of duties with employee empowerment:

Figure 16.5

Principles of an Effective Internal Control System

Segregation of duties means that jobs are designed so that incompatible functions are assigned to different individuals.

Execution of transactions as authorized means that individuals are authorized to perform transactions of appropriate magnitude and importance.

Recording of transactions as executed means that transactions are recorded in the correct accounting period, in the correct accounts, and in the correct amounts.

Safeguarding of assets means that the company is protected against loss of items of value.

Physical comparison of recorded amounts means that actual assets owned are compared to recorded amounts.

Figure 16.6

Flowchart of Internal Audit Activities

  1. Selection: Choose area to be audited.
  2. Function: Identify job duties of all employees in the area and learn the nature of the area’s work.
  3. Process: Obtain description of the area’s system. Identify the flow of steps associated with each task.
  4. Controls: Verify the authorization level and the segregation of duties.
  5. Verification: Confirm audit trail of sample transaction and investigate any discrepancies.
  6. Evaluation: Propose recommendations that arise from audit findings.
  7. Communication: Convey audit results to appropriate management level.
  8. Closing the Control Loop: Follow up on audit findings and recommendations.

Figure 16.7

Flowchart of an External Audit

  1. Schedule the external audit.
  2. Learn the insurer’s internal control methods and the laws and regulations affecting its financial reporting requirements.
  3. Review the documents provided by the insurer’s internal auditors.
  4. Gather evidence through sampling, observations, calculations, and physical inspection.
  5. Test the audit trail of selected financial transactions.
  6. Issue the auditor’s report.

Types of Audits

An internal audit is performed by company employees for the company’s benefit and improvement.

An external audit is performed by external auditors, primarily for external stakeholders.

A financial audit examines company accounting records, policies, and procedures.

A compliance audit verifies adherence to financial and/or marketing laws and regulations.
