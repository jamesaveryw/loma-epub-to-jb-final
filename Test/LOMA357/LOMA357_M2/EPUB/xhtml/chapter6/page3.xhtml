Enterprise Risk Management

Insurance companies face many types of risks across multiple parts of their businesses. Examples of regulatory, operational, financial, and strategic risks include:

Regulatory risks: New laws and regulations, changes to the regulatory environment that affect the insurance industry

Operational risks: Mispricing of products, inappropriate underwriting decisions, claims fraud, privacy and cybersecurity breaches

Financial risks: Fluctuations in market values of investments, interest-rate risk, liquidity risk

Strategic risks: Loss of market share to competitors, disruption from new entrants to the insurance industry, geopolitical risk

To address all of the risks to their businesses, most insurers engage in enterprise risk management. Enterprise risk management (ERM) is a cross-functional system that (1) identifies and quantifies risks and (2) uses a coordinated approach to manage those risks. In addition to identifying risks, ERM involves prioritizing risks based on potential losses, planning responses to potential threats, and monitoring corporate progress on controlling risks.

ERM supports a company’s strategic objectives by providing a comprehensive framework for risk management. An ERM program shapes a company’s risk policy and risk management practices. All insurer functions, including the investment function, are affected by ERM decisions.

Many external parties assess an insurer’s ERM practices. For example, regulators scrutinize the risk management function of insurance companies. In addition to ensuring regulatory compliance, an effective ERM process promotes efficient operations and prudent financial management. Thus, ERM programs are impactful to customers, employees, and other company stakeholders.

 

Asset-Liability Management

ERM encompasses asset-liability management. As previously defined, ALM coordinates risks between an insurer’s assets and its liabilities. For instance, ALM focuses on the timing of cash inflows and outflows. The ALM function monitors an insurer’s liquidity and is responsible for ensuring that the company always has enough cash available to pay its obligations on time.

ALM also ensures the company remains solvent by monitoring cash-flow issues that could cause significant financial losses over time. Thus, ALM is an important component of an insurance company’s capital management practices.

ALM involves interaction between the functions within an insurance company. For instance, in performing ALM roles, investment and actuarial professionals attempt to achieve a pattern of investment cash inflows that will, along with other kinds of cash inflows, ensure that the company’s cash on hand always remains equal to or greater than its cash outflows.

EXAMPLE

Roles of Professionals in ALM

Product actuaries: Properly design products so that cash flows from assets are able to support the products

Investment professionals: Manage investments to meet current and future demands for cash

 

ERM and ALM Systems

Because risks can be complex and interrelated, insurance companies use a number of management systems, including ERM and ALM systems. For example, ALM professionals use ALM systems to identify mismatches between asset and liability cash flows.

ERM and ALM systems

  • Collect and manage information pertaining to a company’s KPIs and KRIs

  • Generate performance dashboards and reports that senior management can use to track and analyze key metrics

  • Generate a variety of reports that provide relevant information to regulators, internal auditors, and managers of specific business lines

Typically, an ALM system includes financial modeling capabilities. A financial modelis a computer-based mathematical model that approximates the operation of real-world financial processes. Financial models rely on complex calculations to produce likely outcomes for a given set of circumstances, called modeling assumptions. As you will see, ALM professionals use financial modeling to simulate a variety of situations that can affect asset and liability cash flows.
