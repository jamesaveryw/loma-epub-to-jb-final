Tools for ALM Analysis

ALM professionals at life insurance companies monitor anticipated cash flows and project the future values of their assets, liabilities, and capital. They also use asset adequacy analysis, which determines whether the company’s assets are sufficient to support certain reserves and other liabilities. In addition, asset adequacy analysis tests the liquidity of assets, which is important because insurers must be able to pay obligations in cash on short notice.

As part of ALM, insurers need to be able to measure their risk exposures and asset adequacy. Insurers use cash-flow testing and a variety of reports as tools for their ALM analysis.

 

Cash-Flow Testing

For insurance companies, the most popular method of asset adequacy analysis is cash-flow testing. Cash-flow testing uses simulation modeling to project the cash flows associated with an insurance company’s assets and liabilities as of a given valuation date in the future. Insurers can also use cash-flow testing to compare the timing and amounts of asset and liability cash flows at various times after the valuation date.

Insurance companies generally use cash-flow testing when the cash flows of assets, products, or other liabilities have the potential to vary under different economic scenarios, such as an increase in inflation or a decrease in market interest rates. Insurers use financial modeling software to project the future values of cash

  • Inflows from investments. Projection begins with asset values and applies mathematical processes to reflect the company’s assumptions for prevailing market interest rates, inflation or deflation rates, asset return rates, asset prepayment rates, asset default rates, and reinvestment rates.

  • Outflows to support products. Projection begins with data about liability reserve values and applies processes to reflect (1) assumptions about interest rates and other economic variables, (2) anticipated product cash outflows—including interest-crediting rates on certain products—and (3) policyowner behavior variables, such as withdrawal and surrender rates.

Modeling uses assumptions to generate probable outcomes, but a model’s output is only as good as its inputs. Thus, insurers must select appropriate assumptions for use in their models. Acceptable assumptions can be based on data from a company’s historical experience, data from industry studies, or random variables based on statistical distributions. Insurers conduct back-testing, which involves applying the models and their assumptions to known past data, to see if the models produce accurate projections of known data. By back-testing models and assumptions, insurers can identify areas for improvement.

To test the adequacy of cash flows, insurers analyze the projected net cash flow values for their assets and liabilities. Two common approaches to this analysis are sensitivity analysis and scenario testing.

 

Sensitivity Analysis

Insurers can run multiple simulations to understand the effects that increases or decreases in a given variable would have on their assets and liabilities portfolios. In other words, they can determine how sensitive their portfolios are to changes in that one variable through sensitivity analysis. Sensitivity analysisis a method of measuring the responsiveness of the outputs produced by a mathematical model to changes in the values of the model’s input variables.

Insurers can apply sensitivity analysis to an entire portfolio or to just a portfolio segment. For example, by changing the assumed surrender rate for its whole life insurance product, an insurer can see how various surrender rates affect the liabilities portfolio segment devoted to whole life policies.

An important type of sensitivity analysis for insurers is duration analysis, which evaluates the market-value sensitivity of assets and liabilities portfolios to changes in market interest rates. ALM professionals compare the two portfolios for differences in duration. A duration gap is a type of ALM mismatch in which the asset duration does not match the liability duration. Duration gaps can be

  • Positive, meaning asset duration is greater than liability duration. If interest rates change, asset values will move more than liability values.

  • Negative, meaning asset duration is less than liability duration. If interest rates change, asset values will move less than liability values.

Whether a duration gap is good or bad for an insurer depends on the type of gap and the direction of market interest rate changes. Regardless, the ALM function tries to avoid duration gaps. As you will see later in this chapter, investment professionals have a technique for narrowing duration gaps.

 

Scenario Testing

Using financial modeling simulations, insurance companies project multiple scenarios for their assets and liabilities portfolios. Scenario testing is a method of evaluating modeling results that involves entering different sets of data—which represent a given scenario—into a model and then determining how changes in the input data affect the model’s output.

Unlike sensitivity analysis, which changes the input of one variable between a series of simulations, scenario testing changes the inputs of multiple variables for a single simulation. As such, scenario testing seeks to identify the probable outcome given a particular set of circumstances. For example, one testable scenario is an economic recession, which would involve a contracting economic growth rate, falling market interest rates, and, generally, falling asset values.

For each scenario, ALM professionals assign values to their assumptions that reflect the scenario’s characteristics. Then they run their model to simulate the scenario, which generates outcomes for the assets portfolio and the liabilities portfolio. ALM professionals run multiple simulations, moderately changing input values for each scenario, to create a variety of outcomes that they can study.

Typically, insurers run multiple interest-rate scenarios to test cash flows for their assets and liabilities portfolios. Examples of other possible scenarios to test are different economic growth stages or financial market environments.

Insurers can include stress tests as part of their scenario testing protocol. A stress test is a form of scenario testing that uses an extreme scenario, such as a severe economic downturn, to test a portfolio’s expected performance during adverse conditions. Since the 2007–2008 financial crisis, the results of stress tests are of increasing interest to regulators around the world.

Sensitivity Analysis or Scenario Testing?

Sensitivity analysis and scenario testing both start with selecting all of the variable inputs for the first simulation, but differ in the number of variables changed in subsequent simulations. Thus, the number of variables changed depends on the purpose of the simulations.

If you are changing the input of one variable in multiple simulations to see how much a portfolio responds, you are conducting sensitivity analysis. For example, run multiple simulations with different values for the market interest rate if you want to determine how sensitive your portfolio is to market interest rate changes. By keeping the values of other variables constant, you can isolate the effects of market interest rate changes on your portfolio.

If you are changing the inputs of multiple variables in a single simulation to see how a portfolio responds to a particular set of inputs, you are conducting scenario testing. For example, if you want to see how your portfolio would respond during a recession, input values that reflect conditions during a recession—such as a low value for the economic growth rate and a high value for the unemployment rate. In addition, you could determine how your portfolio would respond if the recession worsens by testing another scenario with less favorable variable inputs than the first recession scenario—such as a lower economic growth rate value and a higher unemployment rate value.

 

Internal Reports

Insurance companies use a variety of internal reports for ALM. Some reports provide details concerning the company’s liabilities. Other reports provide details about investments in the assets portfolio. Some common investment reports are the investment activity report, investment portfolio performance review, duration gap report, risk positioning report, and hedge effectiveness report.

 

Investment Activity Report

An investment activity report specifies the details of all investment portfolio transactions, including all asset acquisitions and all dispositions of assets from the portfolio through sales, prepayments, or repayment at maturity. The report also specifies realized and unrealized capital gain and loss status, estimated investment returns, and the portfolio’s risk metrics in relation to the company’s risk policies.

Although narrow in scope, the investment activity report is usually the most detailed and most frequently produced of the investment reports used for ALM purposes. For instance, for each newly acquired debt security, the investment activity report typically provides the asset’s yield, term to maturity, duration, and quality ratings.

 

Investment Portfolio Performance Review

An investment portfolio performance review is a management report that presents a portfolio’s investment performance for presentation to the company’s oversight committees. This report is usually the broadest in scope of any ALM report, but is not the most detailed because it covers much of the information in summary form.

The investment portfolio performance review typically presents quarterly and year-to-date information about the following aspects of investment performance:

  • Details about overall growth trends in the portfolio’s monetary value, specified in terms of market value appreciation and income growth

  • Analysis of realized capital gains and losses

  • Analysis of changes in asset market values

  • Summary of purchases and sales of assets by asset type

  • Analysis of the effect of purchases and sales on asset yields

  • Discussion of the portfolio’s asset allocation, including the suitability of the current asset mix

  • Explanations of key events in the external environment that affect investments and the company’s strategic responses to those events

  • Summary reports on special topics such as problem assets, real estate investments, and international investments

 

Duration Gap Report

A duration gap report provides a snapshot of a company’s asset-liability match at the time of the report. Typically, the duration gap report is submitted quarterly to the company’s board of directors.

The report describes the results of duration analysis of the company’s assets and liabilities and discusses the insurer’s current exposure to cash-flow mismatches arising from changes in market interest rates. Even small changes in market interest rates can produce dramatic changes in the asset-liability duration gap, so information in the gap report is time-sensitive.

A duration gap report can include (1) duration statistics for assets and liabilities portfolios and (2) actual portfolio durations as compared to target portfolio durations.

 

Risk Positioning Report

For fixed-income assets, a risk positioning report typically shows an actual versus target comparison for security quality, duration, and performance relative to benchmarks. For assets portfolios, the report shows how far current asset allocations have drifted from the intended allocations.

 

Hedge Effectiveness Report

Many insurance companies use derivatives in their general accounts for hedging purposes. Each hedge position is intended to directly offset a specified financial risk. For a given hedge position, any change in the underlying risk exposure could prevent the hedge from fulfilling its intended purpose of risk reduction. Furthermore, these changes could cause derivative use to increase the financial risks to the company.

Typically, insurers apply statistical tests on at least a quarterly basis to establish the effectiveness of their hedging positions. Hedge effectiveness describes the extent to which a hedge position actually reduces risk in a portfolio. The hedge effectiveness report provides information on every position established for hedging purposes.

A particular hedge can lose its effectiveness when the insurance company

  • Purchases another derivative security that creates a better match

  • Sells the asset that formerly required a hedge

  • Reinsures a product liability that formerly required a hedge

  • Sells a product line that formerly required a hedge
