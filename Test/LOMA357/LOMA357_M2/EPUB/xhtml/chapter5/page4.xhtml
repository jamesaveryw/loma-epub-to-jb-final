Portfolio Objectives and Constraints

Institutional investors incorporate basic investment goals into their portfolios by designating a portfolio’s investment objectives. Investment objectives are statements that describe a portfolio’s purpose, typically by describing how the portfolio will meet an investor’s financial goals.

When constructing a portfolio, an investor may have to work around investment constraints, which are circumstances that limit the investment options that an investor has for a particular portfolio.

 

Investment Objectives

As part of portfolio planning, an investor needs to determine the portfolio’s objective(s). Typically, an objective is a brief sentence or two that describes the purpose of the portfolio or how the portfolio meets an investor’s needs.

EXAMPLE

Investment Objective

The objective of this investment portfolio is to meet the current income requirements of the Camion Industrial Corporation’s employee pension plan while protecting the value of the plan’s invested capital.

Accurately defining an investment objective is important because portfolio managers use objectives to guide their selection and management of portfolio investments. To arrive at an appropriate investment objective, a portfolio manager needs to understand the investor’s

  • Return requirements

  • Risk tolerance

Investment Constraints

A portfolio manager also has to be aware of a portfolio’s investment constraints, which typically include

  • Time horizon. The time horizon indicates the length of time the portfolio has for meeting its objectives, which are linked to the investor’s financial needs. The time horizon guides the portfolio manager’s selection of certain assets and asset classes. For example, a large percentage of volatile investments—such as equity securities—would be inappropriate for a portfolio with a short time horizon.

  • Liquidity. Investors may have specific cash needs that limit the suitability of certain types of assets. For example, a retail investor may be planning for a large cash outflow, such as a down payment for a house purchase. In this case, the investment portfolio should hold assets that can be easily converted to cash.

  • Tax considerations. In general, all individuals and institutions want to minimize taxes, but some investors have a strong desire to use their investments as part of a tax minimization strategy. For example, in a country where investment income is taxed at a higher rate than capital gains, an investor may restrict certain investments—such as dividend-paying stocks—that increase his taxable income.

  • Legal and regulatory constraints. These constraints usually apply to institutional investors. For example, life insurance regulators place limitations on the amount of certain types of assets—such as derivatives—that an insurer can hold.

  • Special circumstances. The investor may place restrictions on portfolio investments based on personal preferences. For example, a charitable foundation may specify industries to avoid investing in, such as the alcohol industry, because the charity’s founders believed that such industries were harmful to society.

 

Asset Allocation

Once a portfolio’s objectives and constraints are clearly described, a portfolio manager can develop a strategy to achieve the objectives. One of the first steps is deciding which asset classes to use and how to divide capital among those classes. In portfolio management, the asset allocation—also known as the asset mix—is the percentage distribution of all portfolio holdings across a specified array of asset classes, such as various subcategories of stocks and bonds.

EXAMPLE

The Algravita Fund Company’s VITA 60/40 Fund is designed to allocate 60% of the fund’s capital to stocks and 40% to bonds. The fund manager chose that asset allocation to help the fund achieve its objectives of moderately growing capital while generating some current income.

The asset allocation for any portfolio reflects the objectives and philosophy of the portfolio, while factoring in regulations and other constraints. Proper asset allocation diversifies a portfolio’s holdings among different asset classes to align the portfolio’s risks and expected returns with the investor’s (1) portfolio objectives, (2) risk tolerance, and (3) time horizon. For instance, an investor with a growth objective, a high tolerance for risk, and a long time horizon is likely to have a large percentage of equity securities in her portfolio.

In addition, asset allocation reflects return expectations. Portfolio managers assess financial market conditions and the economic environment when developing their long-term expectations for the performance of specific asset classes. They use their asset class return expectations to help them determine the best asset allocation to meet portfolio objectives.

For institutional investment portfolios, portfolio managers are responsible for maintaining appropriate asset allocations. When initially constructing a portfolio, the portfolio manager purchases assets in the proportions specified by the planned asset allocation. Over time, market values fluctuate and actual asset proportions usually drift from a portfolio’s intended asset proportions. The portfolio manager can return the portfolio to the intended asset allocation by rebalancing it. Rebalancing is the process of periodically adjusting the percentage of funds invested in a particular asset class in an investment portfolio to ensure that the portfolio continues to contain the desired mix of assets.

EXAMPLE

The VITA 60/40 Fund’s desired asset allocation is 60% stocks and 40% bonds. One year after the fund’s launch, its actual asset allocation is as follows:

Analysis: The 80% allocation to stocks is too high and the 20% allocation to bonds is too low. To return the fund to its proper allocation, the fund manager needs to rebalance the portfolio. The fund manager sells shares from the stock holdings and uses the proceeds to purchase more bonds for the portfolio. Once the fund manager sells enough stock shares and purchases enough bonds, the percentages will be back in line with the intended asset allocation, and the portfolio rebalancing will be complete.
