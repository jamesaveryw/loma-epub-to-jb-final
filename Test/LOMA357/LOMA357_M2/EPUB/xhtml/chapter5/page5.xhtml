Investment Policy Statements for Institutional Portfolios

For institutional investors, objectives and constraints form the backbone of an investment policy. Established by an institutional investor’s board of directors, an investment policy is a statement that outlines a set of formal rules and guidelines for investing.

Financial services companies establish investment policies at several levels. Figure 5.1 illustrates the typical investment policy hierarchy, which you will learn more about during the rest of this chapter.

At the corporate level, an investment policy specifies broad guidelines that apply to all company assets. For example, one such guideline might be that investment management should follow all applicable laws and regulations.

The corporate investment policy also identifies specific corporate investment constraints. For example, a company might prohibit investing in a socially controversial industry, such as the firearms industry.

In the corporate investment policy, the board of directors communicates to the company’s investment function its views on how to manage corporate assets. At the functional level, the investment policy provides more detailed guidelines for managing an investment portfolio or group of portfolios. For instance, the investment function’s investment policy may provide guidelines for using derivatives and other asset classes within a portfolio.

At the operational level, investment professionals implement plans for specific portfolios. Planning for institutional portfolio management involves creating a specific portfolio’s investment policy statement (IPS), which is a written document that describes the portfolio’s investment goals and strategies, the type and amount of risk the institution is willing and able to accept within the portfolio, and the investment costs the institution is willing to incur for the portfolio’s management. The IPS guides portfolio activities at the operational level.

An IPS describes the roles and responsibilities of the parties involved—such as the institution’s board of directors and the portfolio manager—with regard to a particular investment portfolio. The institutional investor decides the portfolio’s objectives and constraints. The portfolio manager then

  • Works with the investor to create the IPS

  • Implements the portfolio’s investment strategy

  • Monitors and evaluates the portfolio’s performance

  • Ensures the portfolio’s compliance with the IPS

Creating the IPS is an important step in developing a portfolio’s investment strategy because it provides parameters. In other words, the details in a portfolio’s IPS guide the decisions that portfolio managers make to reach specific investment goals. For instance, even if a portfolio manager strongly believes that growth-oriented equity securities will significantly outperform all other investments over the next couple of years, she cannot increase a portfolio’s equity allocation beyond the level outlined in its IPS.

In addition, an IPS forces discipline and accountability on investment professionals. All investors, even professionals, can be tempted to react emotionally to changes—actual and anticipated—in market conditions. A formalized plan can act as a check against an investment professional’s counterproductive impulses and behaviors because the portfolio management team is required to stick to the plan. Furthermore, an IPS expresses a portfolio’s goals and constraints in measureable terms—which enables the investor to evaluate how effective the portfolio management team is in executing the plan for a given portfolio.

The IPS should be reviewed periodically to ensure that it accurately reflects the investor’s circumstances and requirements. During an IPS review, investment professionals may also review a portfolio’s risk and return objectives to ensure alignment with corporate goals. After a review, the IPS can be adjusted as necessary.

No standard format exists for an IPS. Therefore, the elements of an IPS are customizable, depending on the needs and policies of an institutional investor. However, an institutional investor’s IPS typically includes the following elements:

  • Portfolio return and risk objectives

  • Asset allocation criteria, including authorized asset classes

  • Guidelines for portfolio management

  • Standards for monitoring and evaluating investment performance

  • Instructions for ensuring compliance with investment reporting standards, as well as with the IPS

The insight below is a simplified example of an IPS for a life insurance company’s general account.

INSIGHT

Sample General Account Investment Policy Statement

Investment Policy Statement

Primary Investment Objective

The portfolio’s primary investment objective is to ensure the company always has sufficient capital to support its general account obligations.

Secondary Investment Objectives

The portfolio’s secondary investment objectives are to (1) maximize long-term returns for the benefit of the company’s shareholders and (2) manage the company’s capital in accordance with industry regulations.

Authorized Asset Classes

Cash; bonds, including corporate and U.S. government bonds; stocks, including common and preferred stocks; commercial mortgages; REITs; structured securities, including asset-backed securities and mortgage-backed securities; alternative investments, including hedge funds and private equity funds; and derivatives.

Asset Allocation Guidelines

The portfolio should not exceed the following limits without approval from the company’s board of directors:

  • Cash: 1–5%
  • Bonds: 55–85%
  • Stocks: 0–15%
  • Commercial Mortgages: 0–10%
  • REITs: 0–5%
  • Structured Securities: 10–30%
  • Alternative Investments: 5–20%
  • Derivatives: 0–3%

Individual Asset Concentration Limitations

No more than 5% of the portfolio’s stock allocation may be held in any one issuer. No more than 1% of the portfolio’s total market value may be committed to any single bond issue.

Fixed-Income Credit Quality Limitations

If a debt security held by the portfolio is downgraded below a Baa3 (Moody’s) and BBB– (S&P) rating, it must be placed on a “watch list” for closer monitoring. Board approval must be received for any security that appears on the watch list for three consecutive years.

The portfolio cannot hold debt securities for which the Moody’s or S&P rating is less than B. If a debt security is downgraded below a B rating by both Moody’s and S&P, it must be sold at the best available price within three months.

Portfolio Reviews

The portfolio will be reviewed quarterly by the company’s asset-liability management committee. Each quarterly review will evaluate the portfolio’s investment performance and its adherence to this investment policy statement.

Performance Evaluation

Portfolio performance will be measured quarterly on an absolute basis and against a designated market index.

 

Portfolio Objectives

The IPS explicitly states the portfolio’s objective(s). Because a portfolio’s performance will be judged against the objectives outlined in the IPS, the objectives must be reasonable and measureable. In other words, an objective must be attainable through the responsible application of an investment strategy.

Although the portfolio objective is a brief statement in an IPS, a lot of consideration goes into shaping the statement. For example, the institutional investor must choose return and risk objectives for the portfolio.

Return objectives can be expressed as absolute or relative returns. An absolute return objective simply states a portfolio’s target return for a given period, while a relative return objective states a portfolio’s target return as compared to another metric—such as the rate of inflation or the performance of a market index—over a given period.

EXAMPLE

Absolute and Relative Return Objectives

  • Absolute return objective: The portfolio should earn an annualized rate of return of 8% over the next 10 years.
  • Relative return objective: The portfolio’s rate of return should outpace the rate of inflation over the next five years.

As you read in the previous chapter, investment returns can be measured in different ways, such as total return or rate of return. Similarly, portfolio return objectives are expressed using different return measurements. For instance, an important return objective for many life insurer portfolios is earning a specified book yield. For bond investments, the book yield is based on the investor’s actual purchase price instead of the bond’s current market price. Achieving the portfolio’s book yield return objective helps an insurer earn sufficient investment income, which it needs to meet its future liability obligations.

 

Risk Appetite and Risk Tolerance

In an IPS, portfolio risk objectives typically reflect the investor’s risk tolerance, which we previously defined as the willingness and ability to accept risk. However, companies with risk management functions can define the term “risk tolerance” differently. In the context of a company’s risk management framework,

  • Risk appetite is the amount and type of risk that a company is prepared to seek in the pursuit of profits or other strategic objectives

  • Risk tolerance describes the company’s capacity to bear a risk in order to achieve its objectives

Although the two terms appear to be interchangeable, there are some notable differences. Risk appetite broadly describes a company’s attitude toward risk—such as aggressive or risk-averse. Risk tolerance expands on a company’s risk appetite by defining the acceptable levels of variation from a company’s risk objectives. Essentially, risk appetite reflects a company’s willingness to bear risk, whereas risk tolerance reflects the company’s ability to bear risk.

A company clarifies its risk tolerance by establishing risk limits. A risk limit, also known as a risk criterion, is a quantitative statement that expresses how the company will assess and monitor its risk appetite and risk tolerance. A risk limit specifies the maximum variance that a company will tolerate. Risk limits translate a company’s risk tolerance into guidelines that the company can use on a day-to-day basis.

EXAMPLE

The investment professionals at Dexicos Life Insurance Company are developing an IPS for a new portfolio. They need Dexicos Life’s board of directors to clarify the company’s positions on investment risk. The board provides the following information to the investment function:

Risk appetite: Dexicos Life is generally conservative toward risk, but is willing to accept some investment risk to achieve long-term growth.

Risk tolerance: A portfolio’s bond investments should comprise investment-grade bonds. If a bond investment’s credit rating falls below investment grade, it will be added to a company watch list for monitoring.

Risk limits: A portfolio can have no more than 30% of its bond investments on the company watch list. If the portfolio exceeds this percentage, the portfolio manager must recommend steps to bring the credit quality of the bond investments back into alignment with the IPS. The recommendations must be approved by management before implementation.

For institutional investors, risk appetite, risk tolerance, and risk limits are established by a company’s risk policy. A risk policy is a corporate policy statement that explains the principles the company will follow for managing risk, outlines the process for managing risk, specifies the authority and responsibility for risk management, and establishes risk reporting and risk controls.

For operational guidance, the risk appetite and risk tolerance for an institutional investor’s investment function are often translated into quantifiable indicators, as follows:

  • A key performance indicator (KPI) is a quantitative measure that indicates success for a specified process or activity that is critical to the success of an organization. The attributes of a KPI include a target performance value, the currently achieved value, and identifiable trends. A KPI indirectly addresses risk appetite. For instance, a portfolio’s annual rate of return target is an investment KPI; setting a target of 10% signals that a company has a strong appetite for investment risk.

  • A key risk indicator (KRI) is a quantitative measure that indicates the level of potential adverse impact on a given activity. A KRI should be readily observable and should be closely correlated with the likelihood of a risk impact. A KRI directly addresses risk tolerances. For instance, a portfolio’s derivatives allocation is a KRI; the portfolio exceeds its risk tolerance if the percentage of assets made up of derivatives is greater than the stated limit of 5%.

KPIs and KRIs can be displayed on a performance dashboard, which is a computer-based graphical display of the current status of a company’s most important performance measures, presented in a readily understood format. Dashboards support decision making throughout all levels of an organization by showing a comparison of actual performance to performance standards. Figure 5.2 illustrates a sample dashboard containing some investment KPIs and KRIs.

In the investment function, a performance dashboard typically indicates relevant KPIs and KRIs. Through performance dashboards, portfolio managers have access to the current status of an array of important portfolio measures. For example, a performance dashboard can provide the following types of information to a portfolio manager:

  • Whether each monitored risk is (1) safely within risk limits, (2) approaching its limits, or (3) outside of its limits

  • How a portfolio is currently performing relative to the performance of an established standard, such as a market index

  • How well a portfolio’s actual asset allocations match its planned asset allocations

 

Asset Allocation Criteria

Another common element of an IPS is the asset allocation criteria—also known as diversification criteria or limitations on asset concentrations—which consist of rules stating limits on the portion of a portfolio that may be invested in a stated category.

EXAMPLE

Asset Allocation Criteria

Assets will be divided so that no single security will have a significant negative impact on the market value of the total portfolio. Assets will be diversified according to asset type, risk characteristics, and number of investments.

  • Investments in a single industry or a single company are limited to 20% and 5% of total assets, respectively.
  • Total international investments may not exceed 20% of the portfolio’s investments.
  • Total high-yield (speculative) bond investments may not exceed 5% of the portfolio’s bond investments.

A portfolio that becomes too concentrated in certain types of assets also becomes too exposed to the risks associated with those asset types. Therefore, asset allocation criteria serve to reduce portfolio risk by restricting the amount of each asset type to a level appropriate for the portfolio’s risk and return objectives.

 

Authorized Asset Classes

Typically, an IPS will include a list of authorized asset classes that have been deemed suitable for the portfolio. The authorized asset classes must have the potential to meet the portfolio’s risk and return objectives given the market conditions expected during the investor’s time horizon. Note that asset classes subject to risks that exceed established risk tolerances are not eligible for inclusion on the list of authorized asset classes.

The list may also specify a target percentage or acceptable percentage range that a portfolio can allocate to each asset class.

EXAMPLE

Authorized Asset Classes

  • Between 55 and 85% of total funds will be invested in fixed income investments.
  • Between 5 and 25% of total funds will be invested in equity investments.
  • Between 0 and 5% of total funds will be held in cash or cash investments.

Within the investment function, investment professionals evaluate each potential asset class in terms of the company’s established risk control framework. Institutional investors maintain lists of asset classes that have been approved by the company, and portfolio managers must justify and obtain approval for adding any asset class not already on the list.

 

Portfolio Management Guidelines

The IPS includes guidelines for managing the investment portfolio. Typical guidelines include instructions for periodic—usually quarterly or annual—rebalancing of the portfolio and criteria for asset trading practices. Two examples of trading instructions are to (1) seek the best execution price in all trades and (2) provide periodic reports on commissions paid for trade executions.

Some investors, including a growing number of institutional investors, have specific beliefs about suitable portfolio investments and may include portfolio management guidelines to reflect those beliefs. For example, an institution could restrict investing in tobacco companies because of the harmful effects of such products. According to this investment practice, called socially responsible investing (SRI), investors consider the nature of the business a company engages in before making a decision about whether to invest in the company.

The IPS stipulates that the institutional investor and portfolio manager must periodically review the statement and discuss potential changes. During a review, the IPS can be adjusted to account for changing needs or added constraints. An IPS may also need to be adjusted if the long-term economic environment shifts, prompting changes in asset allocation and other aspects of the portfolio’s investment strategy.

EXAMPLE

The global economy was in a recession but is entering a period of expansion. During the annual IPS review of the portfolio for the Camion Industrial Corporation’s employee pension plan, the portfolio manager suggests updating the portfolio’s future return expectations to reflect the new economic conditions.

 

Performance Evaluation

The IPS outlines standards for monitoring and measuring the performance of the portfolio’s investments. One of the most important factors in performance evaluation is choosing appropriate benchmarks, which are performance standards that represent portfolio performance goals. Investment professionals use objective benchmarks to set portfolio performance expectations and evaluate performance outcomes. Figure 5.3 shows a portfolio’s performance relative to its benchmark.

One common benchmark is a relevant market index. A market index allows investment professionals to compare the actual performance of a portfolio—or a given asset class within a portfolio—to the average performance for that market. If used as the benchmark for a specified asset class, a market index must closely reflect the long-term behavior of the asset class.

EXAMPLE

Standards for Monitoring Performance

The portfolio manager will monitor the performance of the portfolio’s equity securities in comparison to the S&P 500 Index. Performance should be monitored at least on a quarterly basis.

Portfolio managers continually monitor their portfolios against established benchmarks. Portfolios are guided by clear performance targets, which then form the basis for portfolio performance evaluation. For instance, if an IPS states that a portfolio’s expected rate of return is 8% per year, then the portfolio’s actual rate of return is judged against that target each year.

 

Reporting and Compliance

Investment professionals are responsible for regularly communicating the results of a portfolio’s investment activities. Portfolio performance reporting standards are practices accepted throughout the securities industry for proper reporting of portfolio values as of a specified date.

The CFA Institute’s Global Investment Performance Standards (GIPS®) are standards for consistent reporting of investment performance and are designed to ensure fair representation and full disclosure of investment performance across a global market. The GIPS provide a universal investment reporting methodology that allows a direct, apples-to-apples comparison of investment performance across different investment management firms, portfolios, portfolio managers, and jurisdictions.

In order to claim compliance with GIPS, institutional investors must follow the requirements for performance measurement and presentation included in GIPS. The CFA Institute also recommends independent third-party verification of investment professionals’ compliance with GIPS.

In addition to complying with GIPS, investment professionals must also follow applicable regulatory requirements related to the presentation of investment performance. For example, in the United States, SEC-sponsored financial reporting and control standards apply to companies that issue investment vehicles. In addition, insurance companies in the United States must follow state regulatory requirements for the valuation and reporting of securities held in their general account portfolios.

Besides compliance with performance reporting standards, investment professionals must follow the guidelines in each IPS. For instance, the investment function may fail to comply with an IPS by conducting portfolio transactions that are inconsistent with the portfolio’s stated risk appetite.

Institutional monitoring systems generate noncompliance reports if a portfolio manager exceeds risk limits. Other types of ongoing compliance checks ensure that a portfolio operates within specific return and risk objectives.
