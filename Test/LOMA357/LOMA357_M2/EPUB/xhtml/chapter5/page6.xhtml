Investment Objectives for Life Insurers

Life insurance companies manage general account and separate account portfolios. The varying needs of the different types of insurer portfolios result in differences in their respective portfolio objectives. Because the insurer bears the investment risk for general account assets but not for separate account assets, insurer investment policies focus heavily on general account portfolios. Therefore, the insurer portfolio objectives and constraints discussed in the remainder of this chapter apply mostly to general account portfolios.

A life insurance company’s general account supports many of the company’s product liabilities and other general obligations. In turn, the general account is affected by the company’s liabilities. A company’s general obligations have the following broad characteristics:

They can be lengthy, sometimes extending beyond 30 years.

They are typically illiquid and are not easily assigned a market value.

Their values can be sensitive to movements in market interest rates.

Some are specific in timing and amount—enabling the company to predict future payouts—but others have imprecise timing or unknown payouts.

They involve some risks that cannot be fully diversified or hedged.

Objectives for the general account portfolio typically require an insurance company to balance assets with liabilities so that the company

  • Always has ample capital to protect holders of the company’s general account obligations and meet regulatory requirements

  • Receives total cash inflows equal to or exceeding total cash outflows

  • Earns an optimal return for the company’s owners, while staying within regulatory constraints and the company’s performance objectives

  • Mitigates certain risk exposures between its assets and liabilities through diversification and hedging

  • Can actively manage its remaining risks after mitigating certain risk exposures

Because a life insurer needs to have cash on hand to cover product obligations and capital requirements, capital protection and current income are the main objectives for a general account portfolio. However, many contractual obligations extend years—or decades—into the future, so an insurer must also achieve appropriate rates of capital growth to cover future cash needs. By extension, insurers must accept some investment risk to achieve their long-term return objectives.

Separate account portfolios are different because the policyowners pick investment funds—called subaccounts—based on their personal investment goals. As you’ll see in Chapter 7, each subaccount has its own objective. Because policyowners pick the subaccounts, the investment function is not creating investment strategies for separate account portfolios. Instead, the insurer’s main responsibilities are to select a suitable range of fund options, perform due diligence on externally managed funds, and invest cash in selected funds on behalf of the policyowners.

 

Investment Income

When an insurance company designs a new product, it makes assumptions about the contractual obligations it can expect once the product is sold. The insurer also determines the crediting rate that policyowners will receive for an interest-bearing product.

A significant portion of an insurer’s general account profitability comes from the margin between invested asset returns and the costs to support insurer products and capital obligations. As a result, the insurer’s rate of return on the general account portfolio must, at a minimum, maintain a comfortable margin above the interest rate credited to its product liabilities.

To express the required rate of return on a general account portfolio, the insurer specifies a target yield for the portfolio and monitors actual performance relative to the target. If portfolio investments earn a yield that meets or exceeds the target, the insurer should be able to cover the crediting rate.

General account portfolios are also designed to generate sufficient investment income to meet the insurer’s cash needs. Therefore, as you will read in the next chapter, monitoring cash flows—including cash flows generated by investments—is part of an insurer’s asset-liability management (ALM) strategy.

 

Risk-Adjusted Returns

Riskier asset classes, such as stocks and real estate, have the potential to produce larger returns than safer asset classes, such as bonds. As you read earlier, to have the opportunity to earn larger returns, the insurer must generally accept more risk.

In general, investors do not consider investment returns without also considering risks. For instance, simply looking at the potential returns of stocks versus bonds would lead an investor to choose stocks every time to maximize returns. Obviously, though, not every investor is willing or able to accept the risk required for a chance to earn the potential returns that stocks offer. Therefore, investors need a way to compare investment returns across asset types while factoring in the different levels of risk associated with each type. Investors can make this comparison by calculating an asset’s risk-adjusted return, which is a measure that presents an investment return in the context of the amount of risk accepted to earn that return.

Investors use different metrics to express risk-adjusted returns. In Chapter 9, we cover specific ratios used by insurers to evaluate risk-adjusted returns. For a given portfolio, an insurer’s return objectives may designate target values for certain risk-adjusted return ratios.

 

Solvency and Liquidity

To maintain solvency, an insurance company must take prudent risks and earn sufficient returns to pay contractual obligations and cover company expenses. The investment function’s role is to take enough risk to earn adequate returns, but not so much risk that overwhelming losses occur.

Because life insurers must meet their obligations in cash, they also monitor their liquidity risk through ALM analysis. The investment function selects investments to facilitate liquidity.

In selecting investments that satisfy liquidity and solvency needs, insurers must perform a balancing act between portfolio objectives. Emphasizing capital protection will ensure that enough capital remains to satisfy liquidity needs. However, capital growth is necessary to ensure the company’s solvency.

In addition, capital growth is necessary to create and grow an insurer’s surplus, which is the cumulative amount of money that remains in an insurance company over time and is calculated as the insurer’s assets minus its liabilities and its capital. An associated return objective could be to maintain a stable level of surplus.

Insurers are subject to minimum capital and surplus requirements, which are solvency requirements that set specific minimum dollar amounts of capital and surplus for an insurer as a whole and for each of the company’s product lines. Minimum capital and surplus requirements, also known as statutory minimum capitalization requirements, are established by each jurisdiction’s insurance regulators. An insurer’s general account portfolio objectives should account for the minimum capital and surplus requirements designated by regulators.

 

Supporting Obligations

For life insurers, one of the main purposes of investing is to support product obligations. Because of the long-term nature of their liabilities and their need for steady income, insurer general account portfolios typically hold more debt securities than equity securities.

Portfolio return objectives may designate target values for debt security investments. For example, insurers can assess bond performance by measuring changes in market values, yields, and durations. By monitoring fluctuations in these values, investment professionals can determine whether a portfolio’s bond investments are adequately meeting its objectives.
