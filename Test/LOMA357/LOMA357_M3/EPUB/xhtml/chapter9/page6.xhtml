Evaluating Investment Performance

Institutional investors periodically evaluate the performance of their portfolios to assess their effectiveness in achieving their investment objectives. For example, life insurance companies evaluate their investment performance to ensure that they can (1) meet their contractual obligations to their customers, (2) maintain their solvency, and (3) increase their profitability.

The first step in evaluating a portfolio’s investment performance for a specific period is to gather data on the portfolio’s investments. Investment professionals usually want to know asset values for the beginning and end of the period being measured. In addition, they generally want records of portfolio transactions, such as asset sales or investment income received during the period.

Investment professionals use several types of systems to collect and record investment data. Such systems, as described in Chapter 1, contain information about portfolio holdings, transactions, and market values. Investment professionals use these systems to generate investment reports for review. At life insurance companies, such reports are reviewed by managers and oversight committees. For instance, an insurer’s risk committee reviews investment performance as part of its ALM oversight.

Evaluating an investment portfolio’s performance entails conducting internal reviews, external reviews, and attribution analysis. Investment professionals use the insights gained during the evaluation process to make adjustments to the portfolio’s ongoing management.

 

Internal Reviews

Internal reviews of investment performance focus on a portfolio’s returns as well as its risk exposure. The portfolio’s objectives specify internal standards by which performance is measured. For example, if the portfolio’s target for its annual rate of return is 7%, then each year’s rate of return will be judged against that standard. Likewise, a portfolio’s actual risk measurements are judged against its risk objectives.

EXAMPLE

The IPS for an insurance company’s investment portfolio includes the following risk objective: at least 90% of the portfolio’s debt securities must have an investment-grade rating (NAIC 1 or NAIC 2) from the NAIC’s Securities Valuation Office. If at least 90% of the portfolio’s debt securities are rated either NAIC 1 or NAIC 2, then the portfolio met this particular risk objective for the evaluation period.

Investment professionals also use metrics to evaluate their portfolio management efforts. Risk-adjusted return metrics, such as the Sharpe ratio or Jensen’s alpha, measure how effectively a portfolio used risk to generate its return. Thus, investment professionals use risk-adjusted return ratios as one way to evaluate their effectiveness in managing a particular portfolio.

 

External Reviews

In addition to internal standards, investment professionals evaluate a portfolio’s investment performance against the performances of its benchmark and other portfolios in its peer group.

 

Benchmark Review

Investment professionals typically compare the performance of a portfolio to the performance of its benchmark over the same period. Generally, investment professionals expect the performance of

  • An actively managed portfolio to outperform the benchmark’s performance

  • A passively managed portfolio to approximately match the benchmark’s performance

Remember that portfolios often use a market index to (1) signify the market’s return, and (2) act as a benchmark. Therefore, an actively managed portfolio that uses the S&P 500 Index as a benchmark would be expected to outperform the index’s return. If the portfolio underperforms the index, then it has not met its performance standard for the period.

 

Peer Review

Investment professionals also compare a portfolio’s performance to the performance of other portfolios with comparable return objectives and risk characteristics. In other words, they compare a particular portfolio’s returns to the returns earned by other portfolios in its peer group.

By comparing a portfolio’s return to the results of its peer group, investment professionals can evaluate the portfolio’s relative performance. When compared to its peers, a portfolio’s relative outperformance is another indicator of effective management. For instance, if a portfolio consistently outperforms its peers, then its portfolio managers are particularly effective at managing investments that are suitable for a particular group of investors.

Life insurance companies conduct peer reviews of their subaccount offerings. Investment professionals compare the performance of a separate account fund option to that of other funds in its peer group. In addition, some insurers—such as publicly traded insurers—are able to compare their general account investment performance to that of their peers.

 

Attribution Analysis

During performance evaluation, investment professionals want to know which portfolio management decisions were successful in adding value. Thus, they need a deeper understanding of the factors contributing to the portfolio’s returns. One method of determining how a portfolio manager achieved performance is attribution analysis, which is an analytical technique that involves assigning components of portfolio returns to specified portfolio management tactics. In this way, attribution analysis identifies the share of portfolio returns associated with each portfolio management tactic.

Investment professionals use attribution analysis to determine whether investment performance for a specific period was mainly due to portfolio management decisions or luck. Thus, attribution analysis helps explain why a portfolio performed the way that it did. The results of attribution analysis can confirm the effectiveness of a portfolio manager’s particular strategy or suggest that a fortunate set of circumstances led to positive performance results.

Attribution analysis compares a portfolio’s performance with that of its benchmark. Quantitative techniques are used to “decompose” the portfolio’s excess return into several attribution factors. Typical attribution factors that investment professionals analyze include

  • Asset allocation effects. This factor measures the effects that occur when a portfolio manager overweights or underweights an asset class relative to the benchmark’s asset allocations. Asset class weightings can be strategic or tactical decisions. If the asset weighting is a tactical decision, then the manager’s skill at market timing is tested. Typically, a portfolio manager’s asset allocation choices have a significant effect on performance.

  • Security selection effects. This factor measures the effects of a portfolio manager’s choices to include or exclude particular securities in the portfolio as compared to the benchmark’s composition. The effects of security selection may be less significant than an investment professional anticipates prior to attribution analysis. In fact, studies have shown that asset allocation decisions generally have a larger effect on portfolio performance than security selection decisions.

  • Residual effects. This factor generally measures the remainder of the portfolio’s excess return after subtracting the effects of asset allocation and security selection. This portion of the return is considered “unexplained” and may arise from estimation errors during the decomposition process. A high value for residual effects could indicate that a portfolio’s good performance was due to luck more than skill.

Some investment professionals include additional factors in their attribution analysis. For example, they might look at

  • Sector selection effects. The analysis would examine sectors or industries that a portfolio manager overweighted as compared to a benchmark.

  • Asset concentration effects. Attribution analysis would look at decisions to concentrate a portfolio in a few securities as opposed to the broader distribution that would be expected based on the strategic asset allocation.

  • Momentum effects. Momentum is sometimes included in attribution analysis because some portfolio managers successfully take advantage of asset price trends to add value to a portfolio. However, harnessing momentum is not a sustainable strategy for a portfolio manager.

For attribution of the fixed-income portion of a portfolio, investment professionals may also include factors such as decisions regarding duration, term to maturity, or credit rating. For instance, a portfolio manager could attempt to beat a benchmark by using a different approach to duration or credit risk than is used by the portfolio’s benchmark. Attribution analysis would seek to uncover the effect of the portfolio manager’s decision to use a separate approach.

 

Strategic and Tactical Adjustments

Using performance measurement and analysis, investment professionals identify the portfolio management practices that were effective and the ones that need improvement. For instance, they may decide to make changes to a portfolio’s asset holdings or make different tactical asset allocation decisions. If needed, they can also make adjustments to the portfolio’s long-term objectives, risk limits, and strategic asset allocations.

While determining the adjustments to make to the management of a particular portfolio, investment professionals also consider the types of factors that broadly influence investment returns. You read about a few of these factors in Chapter 4. For example, if an economy shifts from one phase of the business cycle to another during a given period, then the performance of multiple investments will be affected during that period. Therefore, investment professionals usually assess the market environment before implementing portfolio changes.
