Strategies for Improving Returns

When managing portfolios, investment professionals can employ a variety of investment strategies and approaches. For instance, investment professionals can use an active or passive management approach for a portfolio. They can also apply different approaches for different aspects of a given portfolio.

EXAMPLE

An investment team is managing a portfolio with the following asset allocations:

  • Bonds: 60%
  • Mortgage-backed securities: 25%
  • Alternative investments: 8%
  • Stocks: 5%
  • Cash: 2%

Because bonds and mortgage-backed securities account for 85% of the portfolio’s total holdings, the investment team wants to focus the majority of its efforts on those asset classes. Therefore, the team chooses to actively manage its bond and mortgage-backed security investments.

The stock allocation is a small percentage of the total portfolio, so the team believes that actively managing the stock holdings may be an inefficient use of its time. Thus, the team decides to passively manage the stock portion of the portfolio.

Because the investment team does not possess enough expertise in alternative investments, the team outsources the active management of that part of the portfolio to an asset manager who specializes in those types of assets.

Some investment strategies reflect a portfolio manager’s philosophy, such as with security selection strategies. Portfolio managers use other strategies, such as tactical asset allocation and market timing, to enhance a portfolio’s returns by taking advantage of market conditions.

As you know, insurance companies typically have large bond holdings. Therefore, investment professionals at these companies use different types of investment strategies to manage bond holdings for portfolios. Later in this chapter, we introduce some of these bond investment strategies.

 

Security Selection Strategies

Some portfolio managers select individual securities according to a particular investment style. As we introduced in the previous chapter, two popular investment styles are value investing and growth investing.

Portfolio managers can also select securities based on a preferred approach. Two examples of different selection approaches are bottom-up investing and top-down investing.

 

Value and Growth Investing

Value investors analyze securities and select those that are undervalued. In other words, they’re looking for bargains—instances in which a security’s current price falls below its intrinsic value. A security’s intrinsic value is an investor’s estimate of its true value; the estimate is typically based on the investor’s assessments of both tangible and intangible factors. (Intrinsic value has a different meaning for options contracts; we do not discuss intrinsic value in an options context.) Because investors can use different methods to calculate intrinsic value, they might disagree on the intrinsic value for a particular security. In addition, a security’s intrinsic value may be higher or lower than its current price.

To determine whether a security’s current price is reasonable, value investors often look at valuation ratios. They also study the security issuer’s financial statements, competitive positioning, and earnings prospects. These factors can indicate whether (1) a price drop is reasonable because the issuer’s long-term outlook has declined, or (2) the issuer is a strong performer, but its strength is not currently reflected in the price of its securities.

A security’s current price is more important to a value investor than a growth investor, who is more focused on a security’s future price. Growth investors are looking for securities that have the potential for strong capital appreciation. Therefore, they focus on company and industry growth trends. They typically look for (1) companies experiencing significant increases in earnings and profitability and (2) young companies in emerging or expanding industries.

For both value and growth investing, success relies on a portfolio manager’s skill in identifying the right securities and correctly anticipating the changes in their market values. The portfolio manager believes that other investors in the market have incorrectly evaluated those securities; by buying or selling them, the portfolio manager can outperform the market. Thus, both investment styles share elements of active management.

 

Bottom-Up and Top-Down Investing

Two approaches to investing are bottom-up investing and top-down investing. In bottom-up investing, investment professionals first select individual securities or other assets for a specific portfolio in accordance with portfolio risk guidelines; then they analyze the broader economic conditions. Bottom-up investing requires investment professionals to search for the best individual securities based on investment analysis. Then they narrow the potential investments according to which securities fit with a particular portfolio’s needs and constraints. Therefore, bottom-up investors pursue asset diversification by selecting one security or issuer at a time.

In contrast, in top-down investing, investment professionals first conduct a broad analysis of economic conditions, asset classes, risk exposures, and industries; then they select individual securities. In other words, top-down investing starts with assessing the big picture in financial markets and narrows the security search based on that analysis. Eventually, investment professionals select securities that represent the best opportunities in the type of market environment they’ve identified.

For both approaches, investment professionals analyze economic and market conditions, industry growth and competition, and the strength and quality of individual companies. The main difference is the order in which the factors are considered:

  • Bottom-up investors start by analyzing individual securities and work their way up to analyzing the broad economic and market conditions that affect the securities they’ve analyzed.

  • Top-down investors start by analyzing broad economic and market conditions and work their way down to analyzing the individual securities that will perform well, given current conditions.

Institutional investors generally use the top-down investment approach. First they assess the economic environment, which influences their asset allocation decisions. Once allocations are set, they select individual assets within each asset class. (This text’s presentation of portfolio management typically corresponds with the top-down approach.)

 

Tactical Asset Allocation

In the previous chapter, you read about how investment professionals create a portfolio’s strategic asset allocation (SAA). A portfolio’s SAA is structured to achieve its long-term objectives. In the short term, however, portfolio managers may identify market circumstances that favor particular asset classes. Thus, they may wish to temporarily adjust asset allocations to take advantage of such circumstances, thereby increasing portfolio returns.

Tactical asset allocation (TAA) is a strategy that involves making adjustments to asset-class weightings based on predictions of future market movements in the short term. TAA allows a portfolio manager to move away from the long-term SAA to take advantage of brief market opportunities.

EXAMPLE

Mirza Yusef is the portfolio manager for a simple portfolio with the following strategic asset allocation:

  • Bonds: 60%
  • Stocks: 35%
  • Cash: 5%

Based on his market analysis, Mirza concludes that the stock market is overvalued. In addition, he believes that stock prices are generally going to fall over the next year. To position the portfolio, Mirza temporarily decreases its stock allocation by 5%. He adds that 5% to the portfolio’s cash allocation. The portfolio’s new tactical asset allocations are:

  • Bonds: 60%
  • Stocks: 30%
  • Cash: 10%

Analysis: With the new tactical asset allocation, the portfolio will have extra cash for purchasing stocks after stock prices fall to more attractive levels. At that time, Mirza will lower the cash allocation to 5% and raise the stock allocation to 35%, which will bring the portfolio back to the SAA.

Tactically increasing the allocation of a particular asset class is called overweighting; tactically decreasing an allocation is called underweighting. When a portfolio manager overweights some asset classes in a portfolio, other classes are underweighted. Decisions to overweight or underweight asset classes can result in positive or negative investment results.

While tactical changes allow a portfolio manager to temporarily deviate from the SAA, the TAA must still adhere to the allocation guidelines in the portfolio’s investment policy statement (IPS). As this statement describes the portfolio’s SAA, the IPS also provides a range of permissible values around the target percentage for each asset class. The values at the low and high ends of the range—known as range limits—allow a portfolio’s actual allocations to deviate from the SAA up to a certain point. Portfolio managers can make tactical decisions to overweight or underweight asset classes as long as the new allocation of an asset class remains within the acceptable range limits.

EXAMPLE

According to a portfolio’s SAA, the target allocation to bonds is 60%, with acceptable range limits to allow for tactical decisions. The lower limit of the bond allocation is 50%, and the upper limit is 70%. The portfolio manager decides to make a tactical change to take advantage of a temporary market condition and increases the bond allocation to 65%.

Analysis: The portfolio manager’s tactical change keeps the bond allocation within the acceptable range of 50% to 70%. Thus, the portfolio’s TAA has a bond allocation of 65% until the temporary situation passes and the portfolio can be returned to the SAA.

Tactical asset allocations must fall within the acceptable range limits for each asset class. If asset class allocations exceed the acceptable limits, the portfolio needs to be rebalanced to return it to the SAA. Many institutional portfolios have policies requiring rebalancing at specified intervals.

Tactical adjustments for institutional investors can also involve using selected investment vehicles such as ETFs, which enable investment professionals to act quickly to implement their ideas. For example, insurance companies use ETFs in their general accounts for tactical reasons, such as holding fixed-income ETFs for short-term investment while researching individual fixed-income securities for long-term investment.

Market Timing Strategies

Market timing is an investment activity designed to anticipate the future direction of a market, typically through economic data or market indicators. Investment professionals use market timing strategies to position portfolios so that they will benefit from market trends. For example, based on a particular stock’s trading patterns, a portfolio manager anticipates that the stock’s price will rise considerably in the near future and purchases shares of the stock for a portfolio. If the stock’s price rises when anticipated, then the portfolio manager has successfully timed the purchase and the portfolio’s investment will increase in market value.

Market timing is challenging because an investment professional must both correctly identify a trend and its timing. Misjudging either of these aspects can result in investment losses.

In general, most investors are unsuccessful at timing the market. Even for investment professionals, consistently making accurate timing assessments is difficult. However, some investment professionals are effective at interpreting market conditions and making timing decisions that improve portfolio returns.

Tactical asset allocation includes an element of market timing because portfolio managers are making decisions about the optimum times to deviate from the SAA and overweight and underweight asset classes. In addition to TAA, other forms of market timing strategies include momentum investing and contrarian investing. While other market timing strategies exist, many involve trading behaviors that are considered risky. In addition, such strategies may be prohibited by an institutional investor’s IPS. Therefore, we focus on the timing strategies that institutional investors are more likely to employ.

 

Momentum Investing

In the context of investing, momentum is the tendency for a security that is gaining or falling in market value to continue moving in the same direction. Securities can experience upward or downward momentum, depending on the trend in price movements. A momentum investing strategyuses investments to benefit from securities with established upward or downward price trends. The technique for taking advantage of downward trends is beyond the scope of this book. Therefore, in the rest of this chapter, we focus on upward price trends when discussing momentum investing. A momentum investor takes advantage of an upward price trend by purchasing a security and allowing momentum to lift the security’s price higher, thus creating a potential gain.

A portfolio manager can take advantage of momentum to add returns to a portfolio. However, if the portfolio manager fails to identify the end of an upward price trend, the portfolio could get stuck holding a security with a falling market value. The portfolio manager also risks paying a higher price than warranted for a security with upward price momentum.

Momentum investing is a somewhat speculative activity. Remember from Chapter 1 that speculation involves making financial decisions based on short-term price movements. Momentum investors are speculating that a recent price trend will continue. In general, institutional investors do not consider a security’s momentum unless the security already meets other portfolio criteria.

 

Contrarian Investing

Contrarian investing is an investment strategy in which the investor seeks opportunities to buy or sell specific securities when the majority of investors appear to be doing the opposite. Contrarian investors believe that broad feelings of optimism or pessimism among investors can cause a security’s price to move away from its intrinsic value. By taking opposing investment actions, a contrarian investor can position a portfolio to benefit once the rest of the market moves security prices back toward their intrinsic values.

EXAMPLE

Mervin Gwynn is a contrarian investor who holds shares of Steady Railway Company stock. Even though most investors think that share prices will continue to rise, Mervin believes that investors are too optimistic about the stock and have driven the price well above its intrinsic value. Mervin sells his Steady shares before investors realize their mistake.

Analysis: If Mervin is correct, he has protected his portfolio from the security’s impending loss of value by selling his shares while most investors are still buying. However, if Mervin is wrong, he will have missed out on an opportunity to add value by continuing to own the shares.

Often, contrarian investors get their signals from market sentiment, also called investor sentiment, which is the general attitude of investors. Market sentiment can apply to a particular security, sector, asset class, or market. In the case of securities, contrarian investors see opportunities when market sentiment causes a security’s price to noticeably deviate from its appropriate value. Market timing is involved because contrarian investors need to act before market sentiment shifts and market values revert back to more reasonable levels.

Momentum investors and contrarian investors both use market sentiment to identify upward and downward price trends, but they react in different ways. Momentum investors intend to ride along with the market for as long as momentum will carry prices higher (or lower, depending on the price trend). In contrast, contrarian investors believe an upward or downward trend is unsustainable and prepare accordingly. Bond Investment Strategies

Many institutional investors, such as insurance companies, have significant bond holdings. Most life insurance companies prefer to manage their bond investments using the buy-and-hold approach, which typically entails purchasing bonds and holding them until maturity. Buy-and-hold bond investors periodically receive any applicable coupon payments and receive the full principal amount on the maturity date. Buy-and-hold bond investors prioritize the certainty of receiving steady coupon payments over the potential of profiting from selling bonds at favorable market values prior to maturity.

One advantage of the buy-and-hold approach is its simplicity. As long as bond issuers don’t default or call their bonds prior to maturity, investors know the rate of return they will receive from their bond investments.

By holding bonds until they mature, investors can mitigate the effects of market interest-rate movements on their bond returns. Although rate changes cause bond market values to fluctuate during the investment period, the investors who maintain their bond holdings avoid potentially selling at unfavorable market values.

Some buy-and-hold investors use specific strategies to structure the maturities of their bond investments. Two such strategies are the laddering and barbell strategies.

 

Laddering Strategy

Bond investors use a laddering strategy when they purchase multiple bonds, each of an approximately equal monetary amount but with differing maturity dates that occur at regular intervals. By staggering maturities, a bond ladder creates a steady stream of matured principal for spending or reinvestment. Watch Video 8.2 to see how a bond ladder works.

One benefit of bond laddering is greater liquidity for the investor. A long-term bond subjects an investor to liquidity risk because, while holding the bond, the investor cannot receive any portion of the principal. A laddering strategy reduces liquidity risk for bond investors—particularly those holding bonds with longer terms to maturity—by ensuring that some bonds are always close to maturity.

Fixed-rate bonds lock investors into steady coupon payments, which is not always beneficial. For example, if market interest rates rise during a fixed-rate bond’s term, the investor does not receive a higher coupon rate. However, an investor who buys a series of bonds with differing maturity dates over several years can reinvest the proceeds from each bond as it matures. In a rising interest-rate environment, the investor is reinvesting principal from bonds with lower rates into comparable bonds with higher rates, thus gradually earning more interest income from the bond ladder.

Although a laddering strategy reduces liquidity risk, it can increase reinvestment risk, depending on the interest-rate environment. Laddering pays off in a rising interest-rate environment; principal from each maturing bond is reinvested at a higher coupon rate. In a decreasing interest-rate environment, however, the investor is steadily reinvesting principal into bonds with progressively lower coupon rates. If interest rates fall far enough, the investor may be forced to reinvest in a bond with a coupon rate that is lower than the coupon rate received by his recently matured bond. If this situation continues, the investor will gradually earn less interest income from the bond ladder. In such a scenario, the investor would have been better off holding the initial bonds for longer terms to maturity, thus receiving more coupon payments at the higher rates.

 

Barbell Strategy

A barbell strategy is a bond investment strategy in which the investor divides the bonds in a portfolio between short-term and long-term maturities and avoids intermediate-term maturities. The strategy gets its name from the shape that appears when the bond holdings are represented on a timeline. Figure 8.2 illustrates a sample barbell-shaped bond portfolio.

The split between short-term and long-term maturities in a portfolio does not need to be exactly equal. Some bond investors using the barbell strategy may choose a slightly larger short-term group of bonds, while others may prefer a slightly larger long-term group. The main takeaway is that the barbell strategy excludes intermediate-term bonds to concentrate at the short and long ends of maturity terms.

The barbell’s combination of short-term and long-term maturities provides the following benefits:

  • Short-term bonds generally provide more liquidity by returning principal earlier than intermediate-term and long-term bonds

  • Long-term bonds generally provide higher yields than short-term and intermediate-term bonds

  • The investor can potentially earn higher returns because short-term bonds provide the opportunity to reinvest at higher rates in a rising interest-rate environment

  • Long-term bonds can reduce a portfolio’s overall interest-rate risk because, in the case of a falling interest-rate environment, they lock in higher coupon rates while gaining in market value

Like the laddering strategy, one drawback of the barbell strategy is that portfolio managers must work to maintain the strategy’s maturity structure. As the short-term bonds mature, portfolio managers using the barbell strategy need to reinvest principal by purchasing more short-term bonds. Otherwise, the portfolio will be left with a large group of long-term bonds and greater exposure to interest-rate risk.
