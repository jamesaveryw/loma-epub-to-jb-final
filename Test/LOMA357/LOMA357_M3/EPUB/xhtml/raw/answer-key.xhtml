  2. Consider a portfolio with two asset classes: Clas & bsp;A and Clas & bsp;B. Whenever the return on Clas & bsp;A increases by one percent, the return on Clas & bsp;B decreases by one percent. This information indicates that the strength of the correlation between Clas & bsp;A and Clas & bsp;B is

    1. modest positive correlation
    2. perfect positive correlation
    3. perfect negative correlation
    4. absent

    Two asset classes are correlated when a change in the market value of one asset class corresponds to a change in the market value of another asset class. If the correlation is positive, the values of the asset classes move in the same direction; if the correlation is negative, the values of the asset classes move in the opposite direction.

    Perfect positive correlation between a pair of asset classes exists if an exactly equal increase in the values of one asset class corresponds to an exactly equal increase in the values of the other asset class, or an exactly equal decrease in the values of one asset class corresponds to an exactly equal decrease in the values of the other asset class. Perfect negative correlation between a pair of asset classes is an inverse relationship that exists if an exactly equal increase in the values of one asset class corresponds to an exactly equal decrease in the values of the other asset class, or an exactly equal decrease in the values of one asset class corresponds to an exactly equal increase in the values of the other asset class.

    Perfect positive correlation or perfect negative correlation can occur in theoretical or virtual portfolios. However, in investment markets, finding a pair of asset classes with perfect positive correlation or perfect negative correlation is extremely unlikely. Investment professionals most likely encounter aspects of modest positive correlation or modest negative correlation.

  3. Noah Irby, a portfolio manager, is reviewing an asset-class correlation matrix to gain diversification benefits by adding a new asset class during construction of the Fruitful Portfolio. Mr. Irby noted the following four asset classes and their correlation coefficients from the asset-class correlation matrix:

    Asset Class Correlation Coefficient
    Class A –0.85
    Class B +0.12
    Class C +0.57
    Class D +0.94

    This information indicates that Mr. Irby most likely would achieve the greatest diversification benefits by adding to the Fruitful Portfolio the asset class called

    1. Class A
    2. Class B
    3. Class C

    The correlation coefficient indicates how closely movements in two asset classes track one another. Correlation coefficient values range from –1, which represents perfect negative correlation, to +1, which represents perfect positive correlation. The stronger the positive correlation, the more the investor could potentially lose; pairing asset classes with negative correlations reduces the volatility of the portfolio’s overall returns.

    Any two asset classes with a correlation coefficient lower than +1 will provide some diversification benefits to a portfolio. However, investors obtain the greatest diversification benefits by holding a pair of asset classes having some degree of negative correlation. In this situation, Class A has a correlation coefficient of –0.85; Mr. Irby most likely would achieve the greatest diversification benefits by adding Class A to the Fruitful Portfolio.

  11. Investment professionals evaluate a variety of factors—such as management team, portfolio turnover rate, and expense ratio—before selecting fund options for portfolios for life insurance subaccount menus. The following statement(s) can correctly be made about these factors:

    1. An active management strategy, which attempts to earn the average market return earned by fund peers, is characterized by a low portfolio turnover rate.
    2. Investment professionals, who often compare a particular fund’s expense ratio to those of other funds in its peer group, generally prefer funds with low expense ratios.
    1. Both A and B
    2. A only
    3. B only
    4. Neither A nor B

    Statement A is incorrect. In the context of fund investing, an active management strategy attempts to outperform the average market return earned by fund peers. A passive management strategy, also known as a buy-and-hold strategy, attempts to earn the average market return earned by fund peers. This strategy is characterized by a low portfolio turnover rate, which is the rate at which the fund buys or sells securities, expressed as a percentage of average total assets. For example, an active manager may trade a bond before maturity if the bond’s current price is above par value, whereas a passive manager usually intends to hold a bond to maturity.

    Statement B is correct. A fund’s expense ratio expresses the fund’s total annual operating expenses during a given period as a percentage of the fund’s average net assets during the same period. In general, actively managed funds have higher expense ratios than passively managed funds. Actively managed funds charge higher management fees to compensate for the additional resources used in pursuit of market outperformance. Compared to active management, passive management typically involves longer holding periods and less frequent trading, which results in lower transaction costs.

  8. An institutional investor placed the following order:

    Buy 25,000 shares of Peridot common stock at or below $47.75 a share

    With regard to types of orders, this institutional investor most likely placed a

    1. market order to buy at a limit price
    2. market order to sell that specifies an ask size
    3. limit order to buy at a limit price
    4. limit order to sell that specifies an ask size

    The two main types of orders for stock trades are market orders and limit orders. A market order, which is an order to buy or sell a certain number of shares of a stock immediately at the best available price, does not designate price constraints. A trader has no control over the price at which a market order is executed; therefore, institutional investors generally do not use market orders.

    A limit order is an order to buy a specified number of shares of a stock at or below a stated price—known as the limit price—or sell a specified number of shares of a stock at or above a stated price. A limit order that is not executed immediately remains in effect until it expires.

    The bid price is the price a buyer is willing to pay for a share, and the ask price is the price at which a seller is willing to sell a share. The bid size is the number of shares of stock the trader is seeking to buy, and the ask size is the number of shares of stock the trader is seeking to sell. The bid-ask spread is the difference between the highest bid price and the lowest ask price for a proposed trade. In this situation, the limit order will execute immediately if the current price of Peridot common stock is at or below $47.75 per share. Otherwise, the limit order remains valid until it fills, expires, or is cancelled by the investor.
