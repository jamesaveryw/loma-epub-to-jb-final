Planning for Institutional Investment Portfolios

The institutional investment process begins with planning. One important aspect of this planning is portfolio construction. Thoughtful portfolio construction lays the foundation for investment success. Without structure within the portfolio, investment activities can become unfocused, and the portfolio could fail to achieve its objectives.

How Institutional Investing Is Like Cooking a Meal

Common Elements

Cooking a Meal

Institutional Investing

Objective

Your cooking objective is to prepare a particular meal.

An institutional investor has specific risk and return objectives for an investment portfolio.

Guidelines

The meal’s recipe provides the instructions that you use to cook the meal.

Investment professionals use stated objectives and other guidelines from the investment policy statement (IPS) to manage the portfolio’s assets.

Ingredients

You need specific ingredients to carry out the recipe’s instructions.

Each asset in the portfolio is like an ingredient in a recipe—it serves a particular purpose in achieving the end result.

Marketplace

You can buy the ingredients you need at the grocery store.

Investment professionals purchase assets through financial markets.

Preparation

You prepare a grocery list, which helps you identify what you need for the recipe when you arrive at the store.

Portfolio construction is like putting together a grocery list. Investment professionals construct the portfolio to meet its objectives.

Shopping

At the store, you use your grocery list as a guide while you search for, select, and buy the ingredients you need.

In the markets, investment professionals use a portfolio’s strategic asset allocation to guide them as they search for, select, and buy assets for the portfolio.

Implementation

When cooking the meal, you incorporate the ingredients in the proportions specified by the recipe.

Once they have a portfolio’s assets in place in the correct proportions, investment professionals can begin implementing portfolio management strategies, similar to the chef cooking the meal.

Evaluation

After the meal is finished, you evaluate the results of your cooking. Did the meal come out as intended?

During periodic reviews, investment professionals evaluate how well the portfolio achieved its objectives.

Analysis: As the grocery store has its variety of products, the financial marketplace offers many investments from which to choose. Without their “grocery list,” investors may get distracted and wander around the marketplace, picking investments that are appealing but do not help with achieving portfolio objectives.

 

The portfolio construction process involves

  • Determining the portfolio’s objectives and constraints

  • Identifying asset classes that are appropriate for the portfolio

  • Structuring the asset allocation to align the portfolio’s risk and return characteristics with its objectives and constraints

  • Selecting appropriate benchmarks to measure the portfolio’s performance

  • Evaluating and purchasing individual assets within each asset class included in the portfolio

 

Determining Portfolio Objectives and Constraints

As you read in Chapter 5, institutional portfolio planning involves determining the portfolio’s purpose, objectives, and constraints. Institutions create investment policies that provide investment professionals with guidance on required returns, risk appetite, risk tolerance, risk limits, and restrictions on investment practices. Investment professionals periodically review a portfolio’s investment policy statement (IPS) to ensure that it accurately reflects the portfolio’s circumstances and requirements.

Insurance companies set investment policies at the corporate level, but particular portfolios—such as ones devoted to certain segments or product lines—can have additional policies or requirements that require attention during portfolio construction. For example, the investment professionals working on a portfolio dedicated to fixed annuity products tailor the portfolio to meet specific return requirements.

 

Identifying Appropriate Asset Classes

Once investment professionals know a portfolio’s objectives and constraints, they consider which asset classes to include in the portfolio. Remember that an IPS can include limitations on which asset classes are acceptable for a given portfolio.

In addition, the investment function may provide investment professionals with a list of approved asset classes and sub-asset classes from which to choose. An asset class is a broad categorization, while a sub-asset class is a more detailed subset within an asset class that groups assets by common characteristics. For instance, within the bond asset class, investors could group sub-asset classes by

  • Issuer—such as with corporate, municipal, and sovereign bonds

  • Credit quality—such as with investment-grade and non-investment-grade bonds

  • Location—such as with domestic and foreign bonds

  • Maturity—such as with short-term, intermediate-term, and long-term bonds

Sub-asset classes help investors diversify their holdings within an asset class. Therefore, investment professionals use sub-asset classes to further customize a given portfolio’s risk profile.

In general, investment professionals work with an institution to determine the asset classes and sub-asset classes that are appropriate for a given portfolio. Recall that investors expect assets within an asset class to behave similarly, but each class may behave differently than other classes depending on the market environment. Investment professionals select each asset class in a portfolio for the particular risk and return features that it contributes to the overall portfolio.

EXAMPLE

For a portfolio with a capital growth objective and a long time horizon, Kinnow Asset Management plans to allocate a significant portion to the equities asset class.

Analysis: The higher volatility associated with equities is not a problem for this portfolio because of its longer time horizon. In addition, equities are more likely to produce large investment gains over time, so this asset class will help the portfolio achieve its growth objective.

 

Setting the Strategic Asset Allocation

After investment professionals identify which asset classes they want to use for a given portfolio, they need to determine the weighting to give each class. The result of this determination is the portfolio’s strategic asset allocation (SAA), which is the set of asset-class weights designed to most effectively achieve a portfolio’s long-term objectives, while remaining consistent with the investor’s risk tolerance and investment constraints.

A portfolio’s SAA reflects the expert judgment of investment professionals on the most effective asset-class weightings. Professionals design the SAA to give a portfolio the proper exposures to systematic risks, as well as to balance those risks in the best proportions for the portfolio. In other words, the SAA assigns appropriate proportions to systematic risk exposures. For example, a portfolio’s bond allocation can mitigate its exposure to the systematic risks inherent in its equity investments.

Setting the SAA is a deliberate, analytical process. Besides addressing the portfolio’s investment objectives and constraints, the SAA should incorporate the expected returns and standard deviations of returns of each asset class. For instance, if a portfolio needs to earn at least a certain rate of return each year, investment professionals must assign an appropriate weighting to asset classes that are capable of generating sufficient returns and can be reasonably expected to do so.

Arriving at the best possible SAA is challenging, so investment professionals use financial modeling to evaluate potential allocation choices. To select the SAA, they

  • Study the relationships between asset classes by conducting asset class correlation analysis

  • Run modeling to find the optimal asset class weightings for a given portfolio

  • Run additional modeling to test optimized allocations against various economic scenarios

  • Choose the optimized allocation that best fits their portfolio’s needs

 

Asset Class Correlation Analysis

Because each asset class behaves differently, a portfolio can reduce risk by diversifying among asset classes. To assess the potential diversification benefits that can result from including various asset-class pairings in a portfolio, investment professionals conduct correlation analysis. In the context of investing, correlation analysis is a statistical technique that measures the strength of the relationship between a pair of asset classes.

Two asset classes are correlated when a change in the market value of one asset class corresponds to a change in the market value of another asset class. Depending on the nature of the relationship, a pair of asset classes can be positively or negatively correlated. If the correlation is

  • Positive, the values of the asset classes move in the same direction

  • Negative, the values of the asset classes move in opposite directions

Positive correlation does not mean that the change in values is always positive for both asset classes. The values for both classes could go down during a given period; if positively correlated, the values go down together. Investment professionals can evaluate the strength of the relationship between two asset classes by using the correlation coefficient. The correlation coefficientis a statistical measure that indicates how closely movements in two asset classes track one another. The correlation coefficient expresses the risk correlation for any pairing of assets.

Correlation coefficient values range from –1, which represents perfect negative correlation, to +1, which represents perfect positive correlation. For instance, if two asset classes have a correlation coefficient of +1, then an increase in the values of one asset class corresponds to an exactly equal increase in the values of the other asset class. The following table indicates the strength of the correlation associated with certain ranges of correlation coefficient values.

Correlation Coefficient of Asset-Class Pair

Strength of Correlation

Equal to –1

Perfect negative correlation

Between –1 and –0.5

Strong negative correlation

Between –0.5 and 0

Modest negative correlation

Equal to 0

No correlation

Between 0 and +0.5

Modest positive correlation

Between +0.5 and +1

Strong positive correlation

Equal to +1

Perfect positive correlation

If two asset classes in a given portfolio are positively correlated, the portfolio faces the heightened risk that a market event will negatively affect the asset holdings from both classes at the same time, potentially resulting in larger losses for the investor. The stronger the positive correlation, the more the investor could potentially lose. However, by holding two asset classes that are negatively correlated, the portfolio avoids having all the asset values moving together. Therefore, pairing asset classes with negative correlations reduces the volatility of the portfolio’s overall returns.

EXAMPLE

In general, stocks and bonds are negatively correlated. Assume that a portfolio holds assets from both asset classes. When the portfolio’s stock holdings are increasing in market value, its bond holdings are typically decreasing in value. Likewise, when its bond holdings are increasing in market value, its stock holdings are typically decreasing in value.

Analysis: In essence, holding both stocks and bonds helps the portfolio “smooth out” its returns. Traditionally, negative returns in one asset class are usually offset by positive returns in the other. Thus, pairing these two asset classes increases the likelihood that, at any point in time, the portfolio holds some assets that are gaining in value.

Theoretically, a portfolio receives the greatest diversification benefits by pairing two asset classes with perfect negative correlation. However, finding a pair of asset classes with perfect negative correlation is extremely unlikely in practice.

Investment professionals compare the correlation coefficient of one asset-class pairing to coefficients of other pairings to identify the best diversification possibilities. Investment professionals recognize that even asset classes with a strong positive correlation do not behave exactly alike. Thus, while negatively correlated asset classes are preferable, any two classes with a correlation coefficient of lower than +1 provide some diversification benefits to a portfolio.

To compare correlation coefficients, investment professionals can use an asset-class correlation matrix, which is a table showing the correlation coefficients for all potential pairs of asset classes. Figure 7.2 is an example of a possible correlation matrix for several asset classes.

EXAMPLE

Cora Friel, a portfolio manager, is constructing a new portfolio. She already knows the portfolio will have a significant allocation to U.S. large-cap stocks. Therefore, she wants to add another asset class that will offer diversification benefits to her portfolio. Using the correlation coefficient data from the first column of Figure 7.2’s asset-class correlation matrix, what asset classes should she consider selecting?

Analysis: Any asset class with a correlation coefficient lower than +1 will provide some diversification benefits when paired with U.S. large-cap stocks. However, Cora will maximize the portfolio’s diversification benefit by selecting the asset class with the largest negative correlation coefficient—the coefficient closest to –1. If none of the asset classes are negatively correlated with U.S. large-cap stocks, she should select the asset class with the smallest positive correlation coefficient—the coefficient closest to 0. Based on Figure 7.2’s data, Cora should pair U.S. large-cap stocks with U.S. TIPS bonds (correlation coefficient of –0.05). Pairing U.S. large-cap stocks with cash (–0.03) or non-U.S. bonds (+0.05) would also be good choices.

While an asset-class correlation matrix is a useful tool during portfolio construction, asset-class correlations can shift over time, undermining the assumptions that investment professionals made when selecting the asset classes. Therefore, investment professionals monitor changes in asset-class correlations and may need to adjust a portfolio’s strategic asset allocation during a periodic review.

 

Mean Variance Optimization

Investment professionals use the results from correlation analysis when they perform mean variance optimization. Mean variance optimization (MVO), also known as mean optimization or mean variance analysis, is a quantitative modeling technique used to identify portfolio asset allocations that represent the most efficient portfolios. In MVO modeling, each potential mix of asset classes is an asset allocation scenario. MVO attempts to identify asset allocation scenarios that are projected to produce the maximum portfolio return for a given risk level; different risk levels are indicated by different levels of variability in portfolio returns. (Remember that Chapter 4 described how investment professionals use the variability of an asset’s returns to signify its level of risk. Also recall that standard deviation is a statistical measure of return variability and is used to represent risk.)

Important data inputs to an MVO model are the

  • Expected return for each asset class

  • Risk—as measured by the standard deviation of returns—for each asset class

  • Correlation coefficient of each potential pair of asset classes under consideration

The output from MVO modeling is a selection of several optimal asset allocation scenarios, called efficient portfolios. An efficient portfolio, also called an optimum portfolio, is a hypothetical portfolio that earns the highest expected return for a given level of risk. In other words, an efficient portfolio represents a theoretically ideal asset allocation at a specified level of portfolio risk.

MVO modeling generates a group of efficient portfolios to represent ideal allocations for a range of risk levels. Each efficient portfolio consists of a list of asset classes and proportions, along with projected portfolio returns for each asset class under each of several economic scenarios. Investment professionals analyze MVO modeling results to search for the best possible asset allocation scenario within established risk and return constraints.

 
The Efficient Frontier

When evaluating MVO-generated asset allocations, investment professionals use the efficient frontier, which is the set of all hypothetical portfolios having the greatest expected return, each for a specified level of risk. An MVO model applies a mathematical formula to calculate an efficient frontier showing the positions of all efficient portfolios consistent with the investor’s modeling constraints.

The efficient frontier is an upward-sloping curve that incorporates every efficient portfolio. Video 7.2 illustrates a sample efficient frontier with several efficient portfolios from which investment professionals can choose. For comparison, several inefficient portfolios are also illustrated.

One way to interpret the efficient frontier is as the visual representation of the risk-return trade-off, in the context of portfolio asset allocations. For each efficient portfolio on the efficient frontier, an investor cannot

  • Add assets to the portfolio to obtain a higher expected return without increasing portfolio risk

  • Reduce portfolio risk without decreasing the expected return

During portfolio construction, investment professionals use their judgment to pick the efficient portfolio most aligned with the investor’s objectives and constraints. The efficient portfolio’s asset allocations become the template for the investment portfolio’s SAA.

 
Limitations of MVO Modeling

While MVO modeling provides a useful framework for developing a portfolio’s SAA, the method does have some limitations. For example, MVO modeling depends on risk and return data inputs. Thus, one major limitation of MVO modeling is that small differences in the forecasts of risk and return for the asset classes can significantly affect the optimal asset allocation.

In addition, MVO incorporates assumptions that may not always be realistic. For instance, investment professionals typically base MVO calculations on historical returns. However, past investment results are not guaranteed to repeat in the future. In some circumstances, historical returns may be poor predictors of future asset class performance.

As another example, the theory behind many MVO models assumes that investment results are normally distributed. As you read in Chapter 4, some assets have historical return distributions that are nonnormal. Although beyond the scope of this text, more sophisticated MVO models add complexity to optimization analysis by allowing for nonnormal distributions of asset class returns.

Despite the limitations, MVO modeling helps investment professionals evaluate the potential outcomes of their asset allocation decisions. Knowing that modeling cannot accurately predict future portfolio performance, investment professionals exercise judgment in applying the insights gained from modeling activities such as MVO.

 

Monte Carlo Modeling

Before finalizing the SAA, investment professionals confirm their impressions of the results of MVO analysis by conducting additional modeling. One popular method of testing a potential SAA is Monte Carlo modeling. Monte Carlo modeling, also known as probabilistic modeling, is a form of modeling in which an automatic process (1) randomly assigns values to specified input data—thereby creating a large number of scenarios, (2) conducts numerous process iterations as needed, and (3) produces output data that can be described in the form of a probability distribution.

In Monte Carlo modeling, investment professionals assign input values that represent assumptions for a set of asset classes. Examples of asset class assumptions include estimated returns, standard deviations of returns, and correlation coefficients. During processing, the model uses these inputs to run potentially thousands of computer simulations under various economic scenarios. For each simulation, the model also randomly changes certain variables—such as U.S. Treasury yields, corporate bond rates, the inflation rate, and the economic growth rate.

After generating a large number of scenarios, the Monte Carlo model projects probable investment returns for each set of inputs over various time frames. Investment professionals use the model’s output to evaluate potential asset allocation scenarios. Thus, Monte Carlo modeling helps investment professionals

  • Determine the probability that a particular asset allocation will achieve the investor’s desired risk and return objectives over a given period

  • Identify which asset allocation would perform best over several years under certain economic conditions, such as a period of high inflation

Monte Carlo modeling has applications beyond investment portfolio analysis. For instance, life insurance companies also use Monte Carlo simulations to model product liabilities and satisfy regulatory requirements.

Selecting Portfolio Benchmarks

Once investment professionals have selected the asset classes and the SAA for a given portfolio, they select the portfolio’s benchmark, which they will use to evaluate the portfolio’s performance. To select a portfolio benchmark, investment professionals

  • Identify the appropriate benchmark for each asset class used in the portfolio

  • Create a blended benchmark that assigns weightings to asset class benchmarks according to the planned asset class mix

Generally, each actively traded asset class has at least one associated market index. Recall that a market index tracks the performance of a group of similar investments. Investment professionals use the market index associated with a particular asset class as a benchmark for its performance.

Using the asset class benchmarks, investment professionals create a blended benchmark for the entire portfolio. A blended benchmark comprises the asset class benchmarks in the proportion that each asset class will be held in a given portfolio. In other words, the asset class benchmarks are weighted according to the portfolio’s strategic asset allocation.

EXAMPLE

Suppose a portfolio manager plans a 60% allocation to stocks and a 40% allocation to bonds for a particular investment portfolio. The portfolio manager selects the S&P 500 Index as the benchmark for the portfolio’s stock asset class and the Barclays US Aggregate Bond Index as the benchmark for the bond asset class.

Analysis: The blended benchmark is a weighted combination of the asset class benchmarks. In this case, the portfolio’s blended benchmark is calculated as:

Blended benchmark = (0.60 x S&P 500 Index) + (0.40 x Barclays US Aggregate Bond Index)

By creating a blended benchmark, investment professionals have a more effective performance standard by which to measure a portfolio that holds several asset classes.

 

Selecting Assets for Each Asset Class

The final step in constructing an investment portfolio is purchasing individual assets for each asset class. Investment professionals purchase assets in specified proportions, according to the planned SAA. In investing, purchasing assets to fit a portfolio’s asset allocation is called positioning the portfolio.

To position a portfolio, the investment team identifies appropriate assets to represent each SAA asset class. The team ranks available assets in terms of their fit with the portfolio’s needs for various risks, returns, and other characteristics. Then the team purchases the assets that best meet the needs within each asset class. This process continues until all the asset classes are filled and the portfolio contains its initial array of asset holdings.

Typically, the factors that investment professionals use to identify, evaluate, and select individual assets depend on the type of asset being purchased. Later in the chapter, we provide an extended example to demonstrate a few factors for a couple of selected asset types.

Some selection factors are common to multiple asset types, as described in Chapters 2 and 3. Usually, a portfolio’s IPS provides guidelines regarding these factors. Some common factors are the asset’s sector and industry, ratings, liquidity, transaction costs, and environmental, social, and governance (ESG) factors.

 

Sector and Industry

Investment professionals can group securities by the sector or industry of the issuer. For instance, investors could consider common stocks issued by companies in the energy sector or corporate bonds issued by health care sector companies.

Investors can also further categorize securities by grouping them according to sub-sector or sub-industry. For example, investors could group all corporate bonds issued by companies in the beverages industry. Within the beverages industry, sub-industries could include brewery companies, distillery companies, and soft drink companies.

Depending on an investment team’s research, it may decide to increase or decrease exposure to certain sectors or industries within an asset class. For instance, if a team is optimistic about the earnings prospects of the information technology sector, it might select more securities from that sector to include in the portfolio’s equity holdings.

 

Ratings

As previously discussed, rating agencies provide assessments on the quality of individual securities, security issuers, and funds. Investment professionals may use these ratings to supplement their own research. Some institutions, such as insurance companies, are subject to regulations concerning ratings that affect the securities they can own. For instance, in the United States, state regulators may restrict life insurers from investing in bonds that do not achieve a specific SVO designation.

 

Liquidity

Another factor influencing the selection process is asset liquidity. For investors with short time horizons, holding a large number of illiquid assets in their portfolios would be inappropriate. Investors with long time horizons are less concerned with asset liquidity because they do not need to convert their portfolio holdings into cash in the near future.

 

Transaction Costs

The transaction costs associated with a particular asset or asset type are an important consideration during asset selection. Because these costs reduce the amount of money that investment professionals can invest, they strive to minimize these costs so that they can earn the best possible returns. For example, alternative investments have relatively high transaction costs, which limits their attractiveness to some investors.

 

Environmental, Social, and Governance (ESG) Factors

In Chapter 5, we introduced the concept of socially responsible investing (SRI). For institutional investors, SRI generally involves excluding certain types of investments based on a particular institution’s beliefs.

In recent years, investors have started applying SRI concepts to the analysis of potential investments by considering how companies perform on measures of sustainability and ethical behaviors. Environmental, social, and governance (ESG) factors are a set of standards that screen potential investments based on a company’s practices regarding environmental, social, and governance issues. Figure 7.4 provides some examples of the kinds of criteria used in ESG screening.

Companies that score higher on ESG factors tend to be more conscientious about managing risks, increasing value for all stakeholders, and promoting long-term profitability. Academic research also suggests that these companies demonstrate stronger long-term financial performance than companies with lower scores on the same factors.

Institutional investors are placing a higher priority on investing in companies that reflect the values of an institution’s constituents. Therefore, more investment professionals are considering ESG factors—in addition to the traditional financial metrics—to identify the best investments for institutional portfolios.
