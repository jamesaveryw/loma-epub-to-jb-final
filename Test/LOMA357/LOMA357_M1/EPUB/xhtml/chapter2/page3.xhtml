Asset Classes

Institutional investors can choose to invest in many types of assets. Because of this diversity, investment professionals categorize invested assets in different ways. One popular way of categorizing assets is by asset class. An asset class is a group of similar investment vehicles linked by related risk and return features.

Investors expect the various assets belonging to an asset class to behave similarly in the marketplace. Furthermore, in a given market environment, a particular asset class will behave similarly to some asset classes and differently than others.

Typically, an investment portfolio includes assets that represent multiple asset classes. An important aspect of portfolio management is managing risks to the portfolio. As such, a portfolio manager chooses assets and asset classes to reduce investment risk. Two portfolio management strategies for reducing investment risk are

  • Diversification, which is a technique for spreading risk by investing in a variety of assets with different risk characteristics. One way of diversifying a portfolio is by holding investments from several asset classes.

  • Hedging, which is a risk management strategy that involves balancing one risk with a complementary risk that ideally offsets the original risk. An asset can act as a hedge against other assets, asset classes, or certain market conditions.

In Chapters 7 and 8, you will learn more about how portfolio managers use diversification and hedging to manage risk in investment portfolios.

Although no consensus exists over which groups of assets constitute asset classes, three of the most commonly recognized asset classes are cash (and cash equivalents), bonds, and stocks. Other types of assets that are important to insurers include real estate, mortgage loans, structured securities, derivatives, and alternative investments. You will learn about structured securities, derivatives, and alternative investments in Chapter 3.
