Bonds

Recall from Chapter 1 that one type of security—known as a debt security—represents an obligation of indebtedness owed by a business, a government, or an agency. When most people think of debt securities, they think of bonds. A bond is a security that represents a debt that the borrower—the issuer of the bond—owes to the bondholder—the person or company that buys and owns the bond. Essentially, the bondholder lends money to the issuer in exchange for regular interest payments and future repayment of the loan. Because interest payments generally provide a predictable stream of income to the bondholder, bonds are considered fixed-income investments.

Investors can compare the income received from different types of bonds by evaluating bond yields. Yield is a ratio measuring an asset’s rate of return on income paid to the asset holder. Yield is expressed as a percentage and is generally calculated by dividing an asset’s income payments during a specified period—such as one year—by the asset’s price. In addition to bonds, investors also calculate yields for other types of income-generating investments. You will read about bond yields and other return calculations in Chapter 4.

Many institutional investors hold bonds to earn regular income and steady investment returns. As the table earlier in this chapter shows, bonds are a significant portion of life insurers’ investment holdings. Bonds are particularly suitable for life insurers’ general account portfolios because insurers have long-term liabilities, need to protect capital, and experience regular cash outflows. In addition, bonds help with asset-liability management because insurers can match the cash flows of bonds with the cash flows of their liabilities. We explain how bonds meet insurance company needs in later chapters.

 

Bond Characteristics

A legally enforceable contract, called the bond indenture, specifies the promises of the bond issuer and the rights of the bondholder. A bond indenture establishes the bond’s features, such as

  • Par value. A bond’s par value—also known as principal, face value, or face amount—is the designated legal monetary value assigned to each bond—generally $1,000 or $10,000. For example, if a company issues (sells) twenty-five bonds at a par value of $1,000 per bond, it has raised $25,000 (25 x $1,000). In this case, $25,000 represents the amount the company borrowed from its bondholders.

  • Maturity date. The maturity date is the date on which the bond issuer must repay to the bondholder the amount originally borrowed (the par value). The length of time from a bond’s issue date to its maturity date is called the term to maturity. Bonds are available with a variety of stated terms to maturity—generally, from one year up to 30 years.

  • Coupon rate. The coupon rate is the interest rate that determines the amount of the periodic interest payments made to the bondholder. Until maturity, the bond issuer usually pays the bondholder interest on the par value. These coupon payments are made at specified intervals—typically semiannually, meaning every six months.

EXAMPLE

A bond with a par value of $1,000 pays a 3% coupon rate. Therefore, the bond issuer pays $30 in annual interest ($1,000 x 0.03) to the bondholder. Each semiannual payment to the bondholder is $15 ($30 / 2).

Most bonds are fixed-rate bonds, which means the coupon rate does not change during the bond’s term. However, some bonds specify coupon rates that fluctuate or “float” in specified ways. For example, a floating-rate coupon could be based on the variations in a certain index, which is a statistical measurement system that tracks the performance of a group of similar investments.

In addition, some bonds do not offer periodic coupon payments. A zero-coupon bonddoes not make coupon payments during the term of the bond; instead, the bond is sold at a discount to its par value and the principal is paid in full at the bond’s maturity date. Put differently, the interest that the bondholder should receive is factored into the principal payment at maturity.

In general, bonds are repaid on the maturity date. However, bond indentures also state conditions under which an issuer can retire—or repay—the bond before the maturity date. For instance, a callable bond allows the bond issuer to retire the bond prior to its maturity date at a specified price per bond, known as the call price. If market interest rates fall, a call provision benefits the bond issuer by enabling the issuer to repay a bond with a high coupon rate and subsequently issue a similar bond with a lower coupon rate.

The prospect of early repayment is a risk for the holder of a callable bond. As we explain in Chapter 4, investors require higher potential rates of return for riskier investments. Therefore, callable bonds typically have higher yields than noncallable bonds with comparable bond features.

 

Protections for Bondholders

Bond covenants are provisions in a bond indenture intended to protect the interest of the bondholder by mandating issuer behavior. Affirmative covenants require the issuer to take certain actions, while restrictive covenants—also known as negative covenants—restrict the issuer from taking certain actions that would be harmful to the bondholder. An example of a restrictive covenant is a financial covenant, which may stipulate that an issuer must not exceed particular financial measures—such as a maximum ratio of liabilities to assets.

Bond issuers that are having difficulties meeting a bond’s obligations often seek to alter the terms of the debt. Thus, a bond issuer may initiate debt restructuring, which is an agreement between a debt issuer and a lender, typically representing the lender’s concession to (1) extend the maturity date, (2) accept a lesser amount in repayment of the debt, or (3) otherwise alter the terms of the debt agreement. In debt restructuring for a bond, the issuer negotiates with the bondholders.

Despite bond covenants and debt restructuring options, bond issuers may default—fail to fulfill their financial obligations—on their bonds. In the event of a bond default, bondholders have a claim against the issuer’s assets. The claim priority of a bondholder depends on whether the bond is a senior bond or subordinated bond. In the event of the issuer’s default, a

  • Senior bond is a bond that has a higher claim priority than that of other bonds from the same issuer

  • Subordinated bond, also known as a junior bond, is a bond that has a lower claim priority than that of other bonds from the same issuer

In addition, bonds can be either secured or unsecured:

  • A secured bond is a bond in which the issuer pledges something of value—called collateral—to guarantee the safety of the bondholder’s investment.

  • An unsecured bond, also called a debenture, is a bond that is not backed by collateral but is backed only by the full faith and credit of the issuer.

Secured bondholders typically have first priority in satisfying their claims against a defaulted issuer’s collateral. Thus, secured bonds are often senior bonds. If the bond issuer does not have any outstanding secured bonds, senior unsecured bondholders have a higher claim priority on the issuer’s assets than subordinated unsecured bondholders. However, in a default, holders of unsecured bonds rarely receive the full value of their investments. Figure 2.1 illustrates bond types classified by claim priority and security.

 

Bond Issuers

The major bond issuers are corporations, local governments, national governments, and supranational entities.

 

Corporations

Corporations are the primary nongovernmental issuers of bonds. Corporations typically issue bonds to raise capital, which they use to expand their business activities. Bonds issued by corporations, called corporate bonds, are classified by the industry of the issuing company. For example, utility bonds are issued by providers of utilities, such as telephone and electric companies.

As you saw from the table earlier in this chapter, corporate bonds are one of the largest holdings in life insurers’ general accounts. Corporate bonds are also significant holdings in many other institutional investors’ portfolios.

 

Local Governments

Local governments and local government agencies issue bonds to raise funds for public works—such as schools, parks, bridges, highways, and hospitals. The bonds issued by city, county, and state governments are called municipal bonds, or munis. Outside of the United States, investors might refer to municipal bonds as sub-sovereign bonds. For example, Canadian provinces issue sub-sovereign bonds to borrow money for public works.

The two main types of municipal bonds are

  • General obligation bonds, which are backed by the full faith and credit of the issuer—which includes the taxing and further borrowing power of the issuer

  • Revenue bonds, which are supported by a specific income-producing project—such as a toll road

The interest income from municipal bonds is typically exempt from federal, state, and local income taxes if the bondholder resides in the state that issued the bond. However, life insurance companies do not benefit from these income tax exemptions and generally hold municipal bonds that are fully taxable.

Municipal bonds have some potential disadvantages. For example, many munis are relatively illiquid and have call provisions.

 

National Governments

To cover the cost of public spending that is not fully supported by tax revenues, many national governments issue federal government bonds—also called sovereign bonds—in either their domestic currency or a foreign currency. The U.S. government, including the U.S. Treasury and various federal agencies, is the world’s single largest borrower. Government bonds issued by the United States are backed by the full faith and credit of the federal government. Because default by the United States is highly unlikely, investors consider Treasuries to be essentially risk free, thus making them one of the safest investments in the world. For this reason, Treasuries are popular with life insurers and other institutional investors.

In general, investors consider sovereign bonds to be among the least risky investments because governments are able to collect taxes to service their debts. In addition, governments are motivated to fulfill their debt obligations, because they don’t want to jeopardize their ability to borrow at relatively low interest rates.

 

Supranational Entities

Some bonds are issued by multinational or supranational entities—such as regional development banks—which operate in more than one country. For example, the World Bank issues bonds to promote economic development projects in member countries. Bonds issued by supranational entities are commonly referred to as supranational bonds. Some life insurance companies invest in supranational bonds.

 

Credit Ratings

Bond investors expect to receive regular interest payments and a return of principal upon maturity. To evaluate how likely bondholders are to receive the interest payments and principal they are owed, investors look at credit ratings. Credit ratings are grades or scores that assess the creditworthiness of specific bonds and bond issuers. Issuers that are more likely to fulfill their debt obligations and avoid defaults are less risky and earn higher ratings.

For companies and governments, credit ratings affect the coupon rates that they have to pay on the bonds they issue:

  • Issuers with high ratings can generally offer lower coupon rates because their bonds are reasonably safe investments.

  • Issuers with lower ratings generally must offer higher coupon rates to entice investors because their bonds are riskier.

Credit ratings are grouped by quality. Bonds that are in the highest rating classifications are known as investment-grade bonds. Some institutional investors investing in bonds are restricted—by internal controls, regulators, or both—to investment-grade bonds.

Bonds that are not investment-grade bonds are known as high-yield bonds. High-yield bonds are also called junk bonds or speculative bonds. Investment-grade bonds pose lower levels of risk to bondholders, while high-yield bonds pose higher levels of risk. Thus, new issues of high-yield bonds generally offer higher coupon rates than those of investment-grade bonds that are issued at the same time.

Some institutional investors employ credit analysts to evaluate bond issuers and assign a proprietary rating. In addition, most investors look at ratings from external credit rating agencies. A credit rating agency is an independently owned, private organization that evaluates the financial condition of companies and governments and provides information about bonds and bond issuers to investors and potential customers.

Although insurance companies use popular credit analysis services to evaluate bonds, insurance regulators increasingly emphasize that companies must perform analysis independently of the rating agencies. Therefore, life insurers cannot rely entirely on external services to assess the credit quality of bonds.

The National Association of Insurance Commissioners (NAIC) also provides ratings for bonds. These ratings are intended to provide support for solvency monitoring of insurance company investments. You will learn more about the application of the NAIC’s bond ratings in Chapters 7 and 10.
