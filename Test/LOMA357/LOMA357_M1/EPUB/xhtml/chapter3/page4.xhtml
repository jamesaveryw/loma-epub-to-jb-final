Derivatives

A derivative is a financial security that derives its investment value from the value of another security. Because a derivative’s value is based on the underlying security’s value, the derivative can be used to hedge against that security’s adverse price movements. As such, investors use derivatives for risk management in portfolios. You will read more in Chapter 8 about how portfolio managers use derivatives.

In addition, derivatives can be used to speculate on an asset’s price movements or to increase leverage. In investing, leverage is the use of borrowing to enhance the potential return of an investment, at the risk of enhancing the potential loss on the investment.

Because derivatives can magnify losses, insurance companies are subject to regulatory restrictions on their investments in derivatives. Derivatives used by insurers and other institutional investors include options, swaps, futures, and forwards.

 

Options

An option is a type of derivative that gives the holder the right to either buy or sell a specified security—such as a stock or bond—at a stated price on or before a specified expiration date. Options give investors an opportunity to (1) reduce the risk of holding the underlying asset or (2) profit from an expected change in the underlying asset’s market value.

One party, known as the option writer, sells an option to an investor, known as the option holder. The option holder purchases the right to buy or sell the underlying security at the strike price. If the holder executes the option, the option writer is contractually obligated to fulfill the transaction at the strike price, regardless of the underlying security’s current market price.

Options contracts are written to last for a certain period of time, such as one year. If the holder does not exercise the option by the expiration date, the contract is closed and has no further value to the holder.

Options are either calls or puts, depending on the rights of the holder:

  • A call option, or call, gives the holder the right—but not the obligation—to buy an underlying security at a specified strike price, on or before the expiration date. To the holder, a call option becomes profitable once the underlying security’s market price rises above the strike price, because the holder can buy the security below its market value.

  • A put option, or put, gives the holder the right—but not the obligation—to sell the underlying security at the strike price on or before the option’s expiration date. To the holder, a put option becomes profitable once the underlying security’s market price drops below the strike price, because the holder can sell the security above its market value.

When an option holder will realize a profit from exercising the option, the option is in the money. Option writers profit if an option remains out of the money and expires without being exercised.

 

Swaps

A swap, or swap contract, is a derivatives contract in which two parties agree to exchange one asset, liability, security, or cash flow for another. Swap contracts are not standardized; the parties to a swap often negotiate the exact terms of the swap.

Institutional investors commonly use

  • Credit default swaps (CDSs), in which the contract writer promises to pay a benefit to the purchaser if the purchaser experiences the credit default defined in the agreement. In effect, a CDS acts as a form of insurance that protects the purchaser against the risk that the issuer of a security—such as a bond or mortgage-backed security—will default.

  • Interest-rate swaps, which consist of an agreement between two parties to exchange a set of cash flows—typically with one cash flow based on a fixed interest rate and the other based on a variable, or floating, interest rate. Interest-rate swaps generally expire after a specified period of time. Because their assets and liabilities are sensitive to interest rates, life insurers are significant investors in interest-rate swaps.

  • Currency swaps, which are agreements to exchange principal and interest payments on a particular debt instrument payable in one currency for principal and interest payments on a similar debt instrument payable in another currency.

Although institutional investors can reduce certain types of risk by using swaps, they must carefully consider the level of counterparty risk involved. Counterparty riskis the potential that one party to a contract will fail to fulfill its obligations to the other party. Investors face counterparty risk in all financial contracts, but counterparty risk is an especially important concern in swap contracts due to the direct negotiation between the parties.

 

Futures and Forwards

Life insurance companies also use futures and forwards. Futures contracts, or futures, are limited-time agreements that give the owner of the agreement the right to buy or sell a certain asset at a specified future date and price.

Futures were originally established to trade contracts involving commodities, which are products comprising (1) natural resources that have been mined or extracted—such as copper, gold, coal, and oil—and (2) agricultural products and livestock—such as grains, coffee, sugar, and pork. Besides commodities, other assets commonly underlying futures contracts include currencies, market indexes, and interest rates.

Unlike options, which do not impose on the holder the obligation to buy or sell an underlying asset, futures impose on both parties the obligation to complete the transaction involving the underlying asset. However, futures contracts rarely result in the actual delivery of an asset. Investors typically purchase an offsetting futures contract that closes out the initial contract before the initial contract’s delivery date.

A futures contract can be considered a type of bet between two parties and is often used for speculation. Recall the Chapter 1 example of the commodities speculator. The contract that the speculator, Adam, uses to bet on the movement of corn prices is a futures contract. If Adam is wrong about the direction of corn prices, he will incur financial losses.

Similar to futures, forward contracts, or forwards, are limited-time agreements in which a seller promises to deliver a specified asset to a buyer sometime in the future for a price specified in the agreement.

Like swaps, forward contracts are negotiated between parties. Thus, like swaps, forwards expose each party to more counterparty risk than other types of investments.

Futures and forwards are similar, but there are some notable differences:

  • Futures contracts are standardized, while forward contracts are customized.

  • Futures contracts are more regulated than forward contracts.

  • Like options, futures are typically traded on securities exchanges. Like swaps, forwards are typically traded in OTC markets.
