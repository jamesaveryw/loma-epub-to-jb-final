Capital Market Transactions

Capital market transactions between buyers and sellers occur in primary markets and secondary markets. Many transactions occur via exchanges and involve intermediaries.

 

Primary Market

The primary market is the market in which new issues of securities—including stocks and bonds—are sold to investors for the first time. Issuers sell new issues of debt and equity securities directly to investors—mostly institutional investors and financial intermediaries—through the primary market.

The two methods of issuing securities in the primary market are public offerings and private placements.

 

Public Offerings

A public offering is a method of issuing securities in which the security issuer makes a new security available for sale to the public, usually through the services of an investment banking firm. For stocks, the first time a corporation’s stock is offered for sale to the public is called an initial public offering (IPO). Stocks, bonds, and other securities issued by public offering are often called public securities or publicly traded securities.

Many new securities issues require the services of an investment bank. Investment banks provide two broad types of services to issuers:

  • An investment bank underwrites an issue by buying the bonds or stocks from the issuer at a specified price, and then selling the securities to the public at a somewhat higher price. In a firm commitment contract, the bank enters into an agreement to buy all of the newly issued securities from the issuer. By doing so, the bank accepts the risk that it may sell the issue at a price below the price that it paid.

  • An investment bank sells issues of securities on a best efforts basis, in which the bank attempts to sell the new issue on behalf of the issuer on the best terms obtainable, but does not first purchase the bonds or shares from the issuer. The best efforts basis protects the bank in the event that it cannot sell all of the issue. Any unsold shares or bonds are returned to the issuer.

Typically, an issuer and an investment bank agree to either a firm commitment contract or a best efforts contract to complete a new issue. However, some issuers complete new issues without help from an investment bank. For example, the U.S. Treasury Department issues bonds and sells them at regularly scheduled public auctions.

Issuers also use public offerings to issue additional shares of publicly traded securities. Newly issued shares of a security increase the total number of shares outstanding of that security.

 

Private Placements

A private placement is a method of issuing securities in which the issuer sells the security directly to a limited number of qualified investors, typically institutional investors. Stocks, bonds, and other securities issued by private placement are often called private placement securities.

Private placement securities typically face fewer regulatory requirements because regulators assume buyers are capable of evaluating the securities and their corresponding investment risks. For instance, regulators do not require a prospectus for private placements.

Private placements offer the following benefits:

  • Lower transaction costs for issuers because of less extensive regulatory filings.

  • The potential for immediate funding for issuers because transactions are often conducted quickly between highly sophisticated investors.

  • Greater diversification of issuers because a larger quantity and wider variety of potential issuers are available than in the public markets.

  • Institutional investors can potentially earn higher rates of return than would be available from investing only in public securities.

  • Private placement bond investors are protected by covenants that tend to place more requirements on the borrower than do covenants in otherwise comparable publicly offered bonds.

  • Private placement stocks may offer investors a greater share of a company’s equity and higher dividend payments.

Banks, insurance companies, and pension funds are notable investors in private placement securities. For example, private placement bonds make up a significant percentage of the assets in the portfolios of insurance companies.

 

Secondary Markets

A secondary market is a market in which issues of securities are subsequently traded after the initial sale. The bulk of securities trading takes place in secondary markets.

While security prices in the primary market are often set in advance of sales to investors, secondary market prices fluctuate regularly due to market forces, such as changes in overall economic conditions. Prices also move when investor perceptions of a particular security’s value change due to current events or newly available information.

Secondary markets give securities their liquidity because of the large number of participants. When a market has more participants, more opportunities exist for transactions between participants. In addition, more transaction volume in a security means the transaction prices more closely approximate the current market value for the security.

Institutional investors occasionally sell securities in the secondary market by means of a secondary offering. A secondary offering—also known as a secondary distribution—is a sale of a large block of registered securities by a private investor through an investment bank, typically at a set price. Generally, the securities that institutional investors sell in a secondary offering were previously acquired in a private placement.

 

Exchanges

A security that is traded on a particular exchange is said to be listed on that exchange. Issuers must meet the listing requirements of a particular exchange in order for their security to be traded on the exchange.

Different types of stock exchanges have certain methods for matching buyers with sellers:

  • Auction-based markets. A trade occurs in an auction-based market if the highest bid price equals the lowest ask price. The bid price is the price a buyer is willing to pay for a share, and the ask price is the price at which a seller is willing to sell a share. If the highest bid price is less than the lowest ask price, no trade occurs. Watch Video 3.2 to see an example of a trade in an auction-based market. In the United States, the New York Stock Exchange (NYSE) is primarily an auction-based network.

  • Dealer-based markets. In a dealer-based market, dealers act as market makers. A market maker is a securities dealer that holds an inventory of a particular stock and stands ready to buy and sell shares of that stock continuously at the published bid and ask prices. Dealers publicly post bid and ask prices for the securities in their inventory. In a dealer-based market, buyers and sellers can conduct trades immediately because market makers agree to trade at published bid and ask prices. In the United States, the National Association of Securities Dealers Automated Quotations (Nasdaq) is an example of a dealer-based market.

  • Alternative electronic markets. Alternative electronic markets allow buyers and sellers to conduct trades directly with each other. Traders place orders in an electronic trading network, and the network automatically matches buy and sell orders whenever possible. Large institutions can use alternative electronic markets to resell private placement securities to other qualified institutions.
