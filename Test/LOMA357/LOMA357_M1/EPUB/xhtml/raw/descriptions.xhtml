Figure 3.1

The Basic Securitization Process

We list the steps in the securitization process and the activities of the participants. Before the securitization process begins, borrowers enter into loans from a loan originator.

The loan originator originates loans, creates a special purpose entity, appoints a trustee, and sells the original loans to the special purpose entity. In addition, the loan originator typically services loans and distributes payments to investors.

The special purpose entity purchases the original loans, structures a portfolio of loans, provides credit enhancements, and issues and sells structured securities.

The investors in the structured securities purchase packaged securities and collect investment income.

Comparison of Tranches in a Structured Security

Let’s compare the AAA debt tranche, AA debt tranche, A debt tranche, and equity tranche. In terms of payment priority, the AAA debt tranche is the first to collect and make payments. Second is the AA debt tranche and third is the A debt tranche. The equity tranche is the last to collect and make payments.

In terms of creditworthiness, the AAA debt tranche is the most creditworthy. Second is the AA debt tranche and third is the A debt tranche. The equity tranche is the least creditworthy.

In terms of riskiness, the AAA debt tranche has the lowest risk. Second lowest is the AA debt tranche and third is the A debt tranche. The equity tranche has the highest risk.

In terms of interest rates, the AAA debt tranche has the lowest interest rate. Second lowest is the AA debt tranche and third is the A debt tranche. The equity tranche does not have a set interest rate, but has the potential to earn the highest rate of return.

Video 3.2

Action-Based Market Trade Example

The stock of Yupair Corporation trades in an auction-based market.

[Three buyers and three sellers appear on screen.]

Potential buyers enter bid prices and potential sellers enter ask prices for one share of Yupair stock.

[Buyer 1 offers $48.00. Buyer 2 offers $50.00. Buyer 3 offers $49.50. Seller 1 asks $51.00. Seller 2 asks $52.00. Seller 3 asks $51.50.]

The highest bid price, $50.00, is lower than the lowest ask price, $51.00. Therefore, no trade occurs.

One of the sellers enters a new ask price...

[Seller 1 asks $50.50.]

The lowest ask price decreased to $50.50, but the highest bid price is still lower, so no trade occurs.

The buyers and sellers adjust their prices…

[Buyer 1 offers $47.75. Buyer 2 offers $49.50. Buyer 3 offers $50.00. Seller 1 asks $50.00. Seller 2 asks $51.75. Seller 3 asks $52.25.]

We have a trade! Because the highest bid price equals the lowest ask price.

The seller sells one share of Yupair Corporation stock to the buyer at a price of $50.00.

Figure 4.3

Sample Normal and Nonnormal Data Distributions

There are three graphs. Each graph has a vertical line in the center that represents the mean. Each graph also has a curve that moves upward from the left, peaks, and moves downward toward the right. These curves show data distribution in relation to the mean.

One curve shows a normal distribution, in which the peak occurs on the vertical mean line. Therefore, the number of values to the left, or lower than the mean, is equal to the number of values to the right, or higher than the mean.

The other two curves show nonnormal distributions, in which the peak occurs to the left or the right of the mean. If the peak is to the left of the mean, then the number of values lower than the mean is greater than the number of values higher than the mean.

If the peak is to the right of the mean, then the number of values higher than the mean is greater than the number of values lower than the mean.

Figure 4.4

Standard Deviations and Confidence Intervals for Normal Distribution Curves

There are two graphs, each with a vertical line in the center that signifies the expected annual return, or the mean. The curve for each graph indicates that historical annual return values that are normally distributed. Within each graph, several other vertical lines represent standard deviations. The lines mark one, two, and three standard deviations both to the left and to the right of the mean.

The area ranging from one standard deviation to the left of the mean to one standard deviation to the right of the mean corresponds to a confidence interval of 67%. The wider range of values covered by two standard deviations to the left and two to the right of the mean correspond to a confidence interval of 95%. The widest range, which consists of three standard deviations to the left and three to the right, corresponds to a confidence interval of 99%. Beyond three standard deviations, at the far left and far right of each graph, are areas marked as the tails.

The curve for Investment A is wider than the curve for Investment B. Therefore, Investment A has a wider range in its values relative to its mean. In addition, Investment A’s standard deviations are further apart than Investment B’s standard deviations.

Interest-Rate Changes and Bond Prices

A central bank influences market interest rates. The prices of existing bonds react to changes in market interest rates.

[A seesaw with a person representing a Central Bank to the left. The left side of the seesaw is labeled “Market Interest Rates.” The right side is labeled “Prices of Existing Bonds.” The seesaw is level.]

When market interest rates rise, prices of existing bonds generally fall.

[Person lifts up on seesaw so the left side moves up and the right side moves down.]

Prices fall because existing bonds have become less appealing than similar new issues paying higher coupon rates.

[Seesaw is level again.]

When market interest rates fall, prices of existing bonds generally rise.

[Person pushes down on seesaw so the left side moves down and the right side moves up.]

Prices rise because existing bonds have become more appealing than similar new issues paying lower coupon rates.

Example of Margin Compression as Market Interest Rates Rise

[A block labeled Investment Earnings appears.]

An insurer credits a share of its investment earnings to policyowners. The insurer retains a share, called the interest margin.

[The Investment Earnings lock splits in two. The top half is labeled “Interest margin.” The bottom half is labeled “Share credited to policy holders.”]

Over time, market interest rates rise. As they rise, the insurer increases the crediting rate for its policyowners.

[A graph shows the Interest Margin narrows over time, while the Share credited to policyholders increases over time.]

Because the crediting rate is increasing faster than the rate the insurer earns on its investments, the insurer’s interest margin narrows.
