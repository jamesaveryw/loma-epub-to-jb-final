Measuring Investment Returns

Investors can measure investment returns for individual investments and investment portfolios. For the sake of simplicity, this chapter focuses on measuring returns of individual investments.

An investment return for a specific period can be expressed as a monetary amount or a percentage. To find the rate of return—which is expressed as a percentage—the investor divides the monetary amount of the return by the principal amount of the investment, as represented in the following formula:

Rate of return = Amount of return × 100
Principal amount

EXAMPLE

Eliza Dawson invested a principal amount of $10,000 in a mutual fund. After one year, the fund earned a return in the amount of $750. The rate of return on Eliza’s investment is 7.5%, calculated as follows:

Rate of return = Amount of return × 100
Principal amount
 
  = $750 × 100
$10,000
  = (0.075) x 100 = 7.5%

Many investors express investment returns using rates of return. By using a percentage rate to represent their returns, investors can easily compare rates of return from a variety of investments—regardless of the differences in the type of asset, amount invested, or location of the investment.

Often, the expressed rate of return is the nominal rate of return. However, in periods of high inflation, the real rate of return is a better indicator of an investment’s growth in value. To find the real rate of return for a given period, investors need to adjust for the rate of inflation in that period.

EXAMPLE

In 2018, Algravita Fund Company earned a nominal rate of return of 8.5% on its investment portfolio. If the rate of inflation for 2018 was 2.0%, then Algravita’s real rate of return in 2018 was 6.5%, found as 8.5% – 2.0%.

 

Total Return

For a single investment or an investment portfolio, the total return is the sum of all returns—including all income as well as all realized and unrealized gains or losses—from the investment over a given period, typically one year. For performance measurement, total return is usually presented as a percentage. The formulas to express total return as an amount and a percentage are

Amount of total return = Income received + Realized gain/loss + Unrealized gain/loss
Rate of total return = Amount of total return x 100
Principal amount

EXAMPLE

Laila Gregovich purchased $10,000 worth of shares of Lauticoal Company stock. Laila did not sell any shares for one year. After one year, her shares were worth $11,000. During the year, she also received $200 in dividend payments on her Lauticoal stock. Laila wants to calculate the rate of total return she received on her Lauticoal shares over the course of the year.

Analysis: First, Laila calculates her unrealized gain of $1,000, found as $11,000 – $10,000. Because she did not sell any shares, she does not have a realized gain.

Next, she calculates the amount of the total return for the year:

Amount of total return = Income received + Realized gain/loss + Unrealized gain/loss
  = $200 + $0 + $1,000
  = $1,200

Using the amount of her total return, Laila calculates her rate of total return:

Rate of total return = Amount of total return x 100
Principal amount
  = $1,200 x 100
$10,000
  = (0.12) x 100 = 12%

In addition to measuring individual asset returns, institutional investors measure investment portfolio returns. For a portfolio, total return encompasses all sources of return for all assets in a portfolio. Calculating total return for a diversified portfolio can be complex and may not be the best tool for measuring the performance of institutional portfolios. We discuss other measures of portfolio returns in Chapter 9.

 

Required Return

Investors use historical returns to estimate future returns for assets. A historical return, also known as an actual return, is a known investment performance result for a given period. A historical return can be expressed as a monetary amount or a percentage rate of return.

Using an asset’s historical returns, an investor estimates the probability that the asset’s future return will match its historical return. The investor also assigns probabilities based on a study of market and economic factors affecting the investment. Using these probabilities, the investor calculates the expected return, which is the estimated future return—expressed as a monetary amount or a percentage rate of return—that an investor anticipates earning from an investment.

Theoretically, the expected rate of return of an asset must meet or exceed an investor’s required rate of return before the investor will invest in the asset. The required rate of return is the annual rate of investment return an investor requires as reasonable compensation for a given level of risk and to satisfy the investor’s objectives. The formula for required rate of return is

Required rate of return = Inflation rate + Risk - free rate of return + Risk premium

The required rate of return is calculated as the sum of the inflation rate and an investment’s

  • Risk-free rate of return, which is the rate of return on a “risk-free” investment—that is, the least risky investment opportunity available in the market. In the United States, investors commonly use the current interest rate on a three-month U.S. Treasury bill to represent the risk-free rate because they consider it unlikely that the government would default on its payments. In other countries, the current interest rates of short-term sovereign bonds that are highly stable and have almost no risk of default can serve as the risk-free rate of return.

and

  • Risk premium, which is the portion of the rate of return in excess of the risk-free rate of return. The risk premium is required to compensate an investor for taking the extra risk associated with a specific type of investment. Typically, the risk premium for an equity security is higher than the risk premium for a debt security.

EXAMPLE

Bernard Westhaus, an investment analyst, is assessing a potential purchase of Lauticoal Company stock for his company’s investment portfolio. He wants to determine whether buying Lauticoal shares is appropriate for his company by comparing the stock’s expected rate of return to his company’s required rate of return for stocks.

Bernard estimates that the expected rate of return on Lauticoal’s shares is 10% annually. The current annual inflation rate is 1% and the risk-free rate is 2%. Bernard’s company has calculated the risk premium on stocks to be 5%.

Analysis:

Required rate of return = Inflation rate + Risk - free rate + Risk premium
  = 1% + 2% + 5%
  = 8%

The 10% expected rate of return on Lauticoal shares is greater than the company’s 8% required rate of return on stocks. Therefore, Bernard can consider recommending a purchase of Lauticoal Company shares to his portfolio manager.

Based on their objectives, investors can have different values for required rates of return. For an institutional investor, an organizational value for the required rate of return takes into account the organization’s growth goals and the minimum rate of return needed to sustain the business.

 

Varying Time Periods

Investment returns can be measured over the entire time an investment is held, or measured over a uniform time period—typically one year. Three types of return calculations that depend on the time period used are holding period return, arithmetic mean annual return, and annualized return.

 

Holding Period Return

For an individual investment, an investor’s holding period is the length of time that the investor owns the investment. The holding period return, also known as holding period rate of return, is the rate of return on an investment over the entire period of owning the investment. Typically, investors calculate the holding period return for investments that they held for less than a full year. Expressed as a percentage, the holding period rate of return formula is

Holding period rate of return = Amount of total return for the period x 100
Principal amount

EXAMPLE

Sabrina invested $5,000 into a mutual fund. One month later, Karl invested $1,000 into the same mutual fund. Three months later, Sabrina and Karl sold their investments.

Over four months, Sabrina received a total return amount of $175. Her holding period rate of return is 3.5%, calculated as follows:

Holding period rate of return = Amount of total return for the period x 100
Principal amount
  = $175 x 100
$5,000
  = (0.035) x 100 = 3.5%

Over three months, Karl received a total return amount of $25. His holding period rate of return is 2.5%, calculated as follows:

Holding period rate of return = Amount of total return for the period x 100
Principal amount
  = $25 x 100
$1,000
  = (0.025) x 100 =2.5%  
 

Arithmetic Mean Annual Return

The arithmetic mean annual return is the average return—usually expressed as a percentage rate of return—of a series of annual investment returns during a multiyear holding period. The formula for calculating arithmetic mean annual return for an investment is

Arithmetic mean annual return = Year 1 return + Year 2 return + . . . Final year return
Total number of years

EXAMPLE

Algravita Fund Company held an investment for four years. The investment earned the following annual rates of return:

Year 1

Year 2

Year 3

Year 4

4%

1%

7%

12%

Over four years, the arithmetic mean annual return—expressed as a rate of return—of Algravita’s investment was 6%, calculated as follows:

Arithmetic mean annual return = Year 1 return + Year 2 return + Year 3 return + Year 4 return
Total number of years
  = (4% + 1% + 7% + 12%)
4
  = 24%
4
  = 6%

The arithmetic mean annual return can be misleading because the calculated value implies a steady rate of return for each year. In reality, the rates in some years may be close to the calculated average, while rates in other years may deviate significantly from the average.

The arithmetic mean annual return is a simple calculation that does not indicate the variability of returns in relation to the calculated average. Thus, the measure is not commonly used in investment performance calculations. Later in this chapter, we introduce a measure that uses the arithmetic mean to indicate the variability of returns.

 

Annualized Return

An annualized return, also known as a yearly rate of return or an annual rate of return, is a rate of return calculation that expresses a return for a period longer than a year as a rate of return for one whole year. The formula for annualized return is beyond the scope of this text, but it involves accounting for the effect of compounding returns.

Annualized returns are not the same as annual returns, which are measured over a period of one year. Annualization is an adjustment to transform a rate of return for a longer period into a one-year rate of return. Annualizing returns helps investors compare the returns of investments held for different lengths of time.

Technically, one could annualize a return for a period of less than one year. However, certain international investment performance reporting standards—which we introduce in Chapter 5—specify that returns for periods of less than one year should not be annualized.
