Glossary

ABS
See asset-backed security. See Module 1, Chapter 3.
absolute return fund
A type of investment fund that seeks to achieve positive returns in all market conditions by investing in a variety of asset classes—including alternative investments and derivatives—and employing alternative investment management techniques. See Module 3, Chapter 7.
active management strategy
In fund investing, a fund management strategy that attempts to outperform the average market return earned by fund peers. See Module 3, Chapter 7.
actuary
An expert in financial risk management and the mathematics and modeling of insurance and annuity products, as well as other financial instruments. See Module 1, Chapter 1.
admitted asset
A type of asset in which the asset’s full value can be reported on the Assets page of an insurance company’s Annual Statement. Return to text.
agency MBS
A security based on portfolios of residential mortgages that meet the guidelines as to credit quality, underwriting standards, and other risk factors set by one of several U.S. government agencies. Also known as an agency mortgage-backed security. See Module 1, Chapter 3.
AI
See artificial intelligence. See Module 1, Chapter 1.
ALM
See asset-liability management. See Module 1, Chapter 1.
alpha
A measure of the difference between an investment portfolio’s actual returns and its expected returns. See Module 3, Chapter 9.
alternative investment
Any investment that is not considered a traditional investment; most institutional investors consider hedge funds and private equity funds to be the primary types of alternative investments. See Module 1, Chapter 3.
amortization
The reduction of a debt by regular payments of principal and interest that result in full payment of the debt by the maturity date. See Module 1, Chapter 2.
amortizing loan
A type of loan for which each repayment reflects both principal and interest. See Module 1, Chapter 3.
annualized return
A rate of return calculation that expresses a return for a period longer than a year as a rate of return for one whole year. Also known as a yearly rate of return or an annual rate of return. See Module 1, Chapter 4.
Annual Statement
A document that presents information about an insurer’s operations and financial performance, with an emphasis on demonstrating the insurer’s solvency. Return to text.
appraisal
An estimate of a property’s value as of a given date. See Module 1, Chapter 2.
arithmetic mean annual return
The average return—usually expressed as a percentage rate of return—of a series of annual investment returns during a multiyear holding period. See Module 1, Chapter 4.
artificial intelligence
Intelligent machines that develop to work, learn, and react like humans. See Module 1, Chapter 1.
asset
An item of value that an entity owns. See Module 1, Chapter 1.
asset adequacy analysis
In asset-liability management, a type of analysis which determines whether the company’s assets are sufficient to support certain reserves and other liabilities. See Module 2, Chapter 6.
asset allocation
In investing, the percentage distribution of all portfolio holdings across a specified array of asset classes, such as various subcategories of stocks and bonds. Also known as the asset mix. See Module 2, Chapter 5.
asset allocation criteria
Rules stating limits on the portion of an investment portfolio that may be invested in a stated category. Also known as diversification criteria or limitations on asset concentrations. See Module 2, Chapter 5.
asset-backed security
A structured security backed by debt instruments other than real estate mortgages. See Module 1, Chapter 3.
asset bubble
A phenomenon in which the market values of an entire asset class rise rapidly, so that prices of assets in the class come to greatly exceed their estimated values. Also known as a speculative bubble or price bubble. See Module 1, Chapter 4.
asset class
A group of similar investment vehicles linked by related risk and return features. See Module 1, Chapter 2.
asset-class correlation matrix
a table showing the correlation coefficients for all potential pairs of asset classes. See Module 3, Chapter 7.
asset concentration risk
The risk that results when investments in a portfolio are concentrated excessively in any single category. See Module 3, Chapter 8.
asset-liability management (ALM)
The practice of coordinating the risks that simultaneously affect the company’s investments and products. For life insurers, this entails coordinating the administration of an insurer’s asset portfolio—its investments—with the administration of its liability portfolio—its obligations to customers—so as to manage risk and still earn an adequate level of return. See Module 1, Chapter 1.
asset-liability management risk
The risk that a company’s capital could be depleted due to a narrowing of the difference between returns earned on assets and liabilities or a mismatch in cash flows for assets and liabilities. See Module 1, Chapter 1.
asset-liability manager
An investment risk management professional who monitors a company’s assets and investments supporting a specified product line, such as annuities. See Module 1, Chapter 1.
assets under management
The amount of money that an advisor or company manages on behalf of a client. See Module 1, Chapter 3.
asset valuation
The process of calculating the monetary values for assets; conducted for financial reporting purposes. See Module 3, Chapter 9.
Asset Valuation Reserve
A type of investment reserve that minimizes the effects of both realized and unrealized gains and losses on an insurer’s invested assets when those gains and losses are related to the credit quality of the assets or fluctuations in the prices of equity securities. Return to text.
attribution analysis
An analytical technique that involves assigning components of investment portfolio returns to specified portfolio management tactics. See Module 3, Chapter 9.
audit
An examination of a company’s records and operations to evaluate the accuracy of the records and the effectiveness of the company’s operational policies and procedures. Return to text.
audit committee
A committee that assists a company’s board of directors in overseeing the internal audit function, independent external auditors, financial reporting practices, and internal controls. Return to text.
audit policy
A corporate policy that states a company’s process for systematically examining, testing, and evaluating the company’s compliance with one or more specified sets of standards. Return to text.
AUM
See assets under management. See Module 1, Chapter 3.
authentication
The technological process of ensuring that someone requesting or sending information is who he claims to be. See Module 1, Chapter 1.
authorization limit
The maximum monetary amount that a company employee has official power to approve for disbursement without prior approval by another company employee with higher authority. See Module 1, Chapter 1.
AVR
See Asset Valuation Reserve. Return to text.
balloon payment
A lump-sum payment of the final amount of the mortgage. See Module 1, Chapter 2.
barbell strategy
A bond investment strategy in which the investor divides the bonds in a portfolio between short-term and long-term maturities and avoids intermediate-term maturities. See Module 3, Chapter 8.
benchmark
In investing, a performance standard that represents portfolio performance goals. See Module 2, Chapter 5.
best efforts basis
A type of service in which an investment bank attempts to sell a new issue on behalf of the issuer on the best terms obtainable, but does not first purchase the bonds or shares from the issuer. See Module 1, Chapter 3.
beta
A measure of the volatility of an asset or investment portfolio in relation to the volatility of a market. See Module 3, Chapter 9.
bid-ask spread
The difference between the highest bid price and the lowest ask price for a proposed trade. See Module 3, Chapter 8.
blended benchmark
A type of investment performance benchmark that comprises the asset class benchmarks in the proportion that each asset class will be held in a given portfolio. See Module 3, Chapter 7.
bond
A security that represents a debt that the borrower—the issuer of the bond—owes to the bondholder—the person or company that buys and owns the bond. See Module 1, Chapter 2.
bond covenant
A provision in a bond indenture intended to protect the interest of the bondholder by mandating issuer behavior. See Module 1, Chapter 2.
bottom-up investing
An investment approach in which investment professionals first select individual securities or other assets for a specific portfolio in accordance with portfolio risk guidelines; then they analyze the broader economic conditions. See Module 3, Chapter 8.
broker-dealer
A financial institution that (1) buys and sells securities either for itself or for its customers and (2) provides information and advice to customers regarding the purchase and sale of securities. See Module 1, Chapter 1.
business cycle
A recurring pattern of fluctuations in economic activity that is characterized as expansion, peak, contraction, and trough. See Module 1, Chapter 4.
callable bond
A bond that allows the bond issuer to retire the bond prior to its maturity date at a specified price per bond, known as the call price. See Module 1, Chapter 2.
call option
A type of option that gives the holder the right—but not the obligation—to buy an underlying security at a specified strike price, on or before the expiration date. Also known as a call. See Module 1, Chapter 3.
capital
In investing, a financial resource available to initiate an investment. See Module 1, Chapter 1.
capital gain
The amount by which the selling price of an investment is more than its purchase price. See Module 1, Chapter 2.
capital growth
An investment goal which prioritizes investing in assets with the greatest potential for gains. Also known as capital appreciation or wealth accumulation. See Module 2, Chapter 5.
capital market
The aggregate of all marketplaces, participants, and activities that move funding in a given economic system from net suppliers of capital to net users of capital. See Module 1, Chapter 3.
capital protection
An investment goal which prioritizes investing in stable assets that are unlikely to incur significant losses. Also known as preservation of capital or preservation of principal. See Module 2, Chapter 5.
cash dividend
A cash payment to a shareholder for each share of stock the shareholder owns. See Module 1, Chapter 2.
cash equivalent
An investment that is not cash but that can be converted to cash within 90 days with little or no risk of losing value. See Module 1, Chapter 2.
cash-flow matching
In asset-liability management, an investment management technique which involves identifying the patterns of cash outflows for insurance products and matching those cash outflows with a selection of assets that will produce a similar pattern of cash inflows. See Module 2, Chapter 6.
cash-flow testing
A method of asset adequacy analysis that uses simulation modeling to project the cash flows associated with an insurance company’s assets and liabilities as of a given valuation date in the future. See Module 2, Chapter 6.
CDS
See credit default swap. See Module 1, Chapter 3.
CFTC
See Commodity Futures Trading Commission. Return to text.
chief investment officer
A senior-level executive who manages a company’s investment function. See Module 1, Chapter 1.
CIO
See chief investment officer. See Module 1, Chapter 1.
CLO
See collateralized loan obligation. See Module 1, Chapter 3.
closed-end mutual fund
A type of mutual fund that establishes a portfolio of securities with a fixed number of outstanding shares that are traded on securities exchanges or in the over-the-counter market. See Module 1, Chapter 3.
CMBS
See commercial mortgage-backed security. See Module 1, Chapter 3.
CMO
See collateralized mortgage obligation. See Module 1, Chapter 3.
code of conduct
A formal statement of a company’s values and its expectations for how its employees should behave in the course of business. Also known as a code of business conduct or a code of ethics. Return to text.
collateralized loan obligation
A type of asset-backed security that securitizes a pool of corporate debt, such as leveraged loans and other business loans. See Module 1, Chapter 3.
collateralized mortgage obligation
A type of mortgage-backed security that is backed by a pool of residential mortgage loans, which are organized into classes based on their risk profile. See Module 1, Chapter 3.
collusion
An agreement between two or more people to defraud another person of his rights. Return to text.
commercial mortgage
A mortgage loan secured by commercial real estate. See Module 1, Chapter 2.
commercial mortgage-backed security
A type of structured security that is secured by a pool of various commercial mortgages. See Module 1, Chapter 3.
commoditization
The process by which a product reaches a point in its development where it has no features that differentiate it from competitive products other than price. Return to text.
commodity
A product comprising (1) natural resources that have been mined or extracted—such as copper, gold, coal, and oil—or (2) agricultural products and livestock—such as grains, coffee, sugar, and pork. See Module 1, Chapter 3.
Commodity Futures Trading Commission
An independent federal agency that regulates the futures and options markets in the United States. Return to text.
common stock
A type of stock that usually entitles the owner to (1) vote on the selection of the board of directors and on other important company matters and (2) receive dividends on the stock, if they are declared. See Module 1, Chapter 2.
comparables method
A method a real estate appraiser uses to estimate the market value of a property by referring to recent selling prices for other properties having similar characteristics. See Module 1, Chapter 2.
compliance audit
A type of audit that verifies that a company’s actions adhere to applicable laws and regulatory requirements and to the company’s own policies and procedures. Return to text.
compliance committee
A committee that may handle a variety of corporate issues such as corporate privacy policies, data security and usage policies, market conduct, and general compliance. Return to text.
conflict of interest
A situation in which the interests or actions of one entity are incompatible with the interests or actions of another entity. Return to text.
contraction
A phase in the business cycle in which unemployment rises and the pace of economic activity slows down. See Module 1, Chapter 4.
contractionary monetary policy
A series of actions by a central bank which decrease the growth of the money supply and the amount of credit available in the economy. See Module 1, Chapter 4.
contrarian investing
An investment strategy in which the investor seeks opportunities to buy or sell specific securities when the majority of investors appear to be doing the opposite. See Module 3, Chapter 8.
conventional mortgage loan
A mortgage loan made on the basis of the borrower’s credit rating and the value of the collateral that is the basis for the mortgage. See Module 1, Chapter 2.
core fund
A type of investment fund that invests in growth and value stocks. Also known as a blend fund. See Module 3, Chapter 7.
corporate bond
Bonds issued by corporations; they are classified by the industry of the issuing company. See Module 1, Chapter 2.
corporate culture
The attitudes, values, perceptions, beliefs, and experiences shared by a company’s employees and instilled in new employees when they join the company. Also known as an organizational culture. Return to text.
corporate governance
The responsibility and authority of a company’s board of directors to direct the organization to properly fulfill its missions on behalf of the company’s legitimate stakeholders in a legal and fiscally responsible manner. Return to text.
correlation analysis
In investing, a statistical technique that measures the strength of the relationship between a pair of asset classes. See Module 3, Chapter 7.
correlation coefficient
In investing, a statistical measure that indicates how closely movements in two asset classes track one another. See Module 3, Chapter 7.
counterparty risk
The potential that one party to a contract will fail to fulfill its obligations to the other party. See Module 1, Chapter 3.
coupon rate
The interest rate that determines the amount of the periodic interest payments made to the bondholder. See Module 1, Chapter 2.
credit default swap
A derivatives contract in which the contract writer promises to pay a benefit to the purchaser if the purchaser experiences the credit default defined in the agreement. See Module 1, Chapter 3.
credit rating
A grade or score that assesses the creditworthiness of a specific bond and bond issuer. See Module 1, Chapter 2.
credit rating agency
An independently owned, private organization that evaluates the financial condition of companies and governments and provides information about bonds and bond issuers to investors and potential customers. See Module 1, Chapter 2.
credit risk
The risk that (1) a borrower defaults on debt obligations by failing to make payments of principal or interest on time or (2) a lender sustains a financial loss based on an adverse change in a borrower’s creditworthiness. See Module 1, Chapter 4.
currency risk
A risk arising from changes in currency exchange rates. Also known as foreign exchange risk. See Module 1, Chapter 4.
currency swap
A derivatives contract in which the parties agree to exchange principal and interest payments on a particular debt instrument payable in one currency for principal and interest payments on a similar debt instrument payable in another currency. See Module 1, Chapter 3.
current income
An investment goal which prioritizes investing in assets that generate regular income. See Module 2, Chapter 5.
current yield
The amount of annual interest received from a bond stated as a percentage of the current market price. See Module 1, Chapter 4.
custodial task
In a financial context, a task which includes the physical or electronic handling of assets. See Module 1, Chapter 1.
cybersecurity
A type of information security which encompasses measures undertaken to protect a company’s computer networks, systems, and data from unauthorized access. See Module 1, Chapter 1.
data analysis
A process of applying a variety of analytical techniques to collected data, and using the results of the analysis to formulate recommendations concerning a research objective. See Module 1, Chapter 1.
data governance
The process of establishing organizational ownership and accountability for data so that business processes are optimized and data are secure and protected. See Module 1, Chapter 1.
data mining
A type of analysis in which numerical data are examined to uncover trends, patterns, and relationships. See Module 1, Chapter 1.
data visualization tool
A technology-driven graphical display that conveys an intuitive understanding of data. See Module 1, Chapter 1.
debt restructuring
An agreement between a debt issuer and a lender, typically representing the lender’s concession to (1) extend the maturity date, (2) accept a lesser amount in repayment of the debt, or (3) otherwise alter the terms of the debt agreement. See Module 1, Chapter 2.
debt security
A type of security that represents an obligation of indebtedness owed by a business, a government, or an agency. See Module 1, Chapter 2.
debt service coverage ratio
The ratio of a borrower’s monthly earnings—before interest and taxes—to its monthly mortgage payments owed. See Module 1, Chapter 4.
default
In bond investing, an event that occurs when a bond issuer fails to fulfill its financial obligations. See Module 1, Chapter 2.
default risk
In investing, the risk that an investor will not receive the cash flow payments to which it is entitled because a borrower fails to fulfill its debt obligations. See Module 1, Chapter 4.
deflation
The decline in the general level of prices in an economy over a period of time. See Module 1, Chapter 4.
derivative
A financial security that derives its investment value from the value of another security. See Module 1, Chapter 3.
disinflation
A decrease in the rate of inflation over a period of time. See Module 1, Chapter 4.
distressed debt fund
A type of private equity fund that invests in bonds and other debt securities issued by target companies in financial distress. Also known as a vulture fund. See Module 1, Chapter 3.
diversification
A technique for spreading risk by investing in a variety of assets with different risk characteristics. See Module 1, Chapter 2.
dividend
A distribution of a company’s profits payable to owners of the company’s stock. See Module 1, Chapter 2.
dividend yield
In stock investing, the ratio of a company’s annual dividend payments to its current market price. See Module 3, Chapter 7.
Dodd-Frank Wall Street Reform and Consumer Protection Act
A federal law designed to (1) promote the financial stability of the United States, (2) improve accountability and transparency in the financial system, and (3) protect consumers from abusive financial services practices. Also known as the Dodd-Frank Act. Return to text.
downgrade risk
The risk of deterioration in a credit rating assigned to an issuer of a debt security. See Module 1, Chapter 4.
due diligence
In investing, the careful and thorough investigation of a potential investment. See Module 1, Chapter 3.
duration
A statistic that measures the interest-rate sensitivity of an interest-bearing security. See Module 1, Chapter 4.
duration gap report
A report that provides a snapshot of a company’s asset-liability match at the time of the report. See Module 2, Chapter 6.
duration matching
In asset-liability management, an investment management technique that involves matching the duration statistics for fixed-income assets—such as bonds—with the duration statistics for the liabilities that the assets support. See Module 2, Chapter 6.
duration policy
A policy, set by the asset-liability management function, that specifies a numerical target for a company’s duration gap value and a numerical range of acceptable values around the target value. Return to text.
earnings per share
A profitability ratio that reflects the portion of a company’s net profit after taxes that is attributable to each outstanding share of the company’s common stock. See Module 3, Chapter 7.
economies of scale
Unit cost savings that increase as the size of a business operation grows. See Module 1, Chapter 3.
efficient frontier
The set of all hypothetical investment portfolios having the greatest expected return, each for a specified level of risk. See Module 3, Chapter 7.
efficient portfolio
A hypothetical investment portfolio that earns the highest expected return for a given level of risk. Also known as an optimum portfolio. See Module 3, Chapter 7.
emerging market fund
A type of investment fund that invests in companies located in developing countries, which are typically countries with low levels of income per citizen and little industrialization. See Module 3, Chapter 7.
encryption
A type of technology that encodes data so that only an authorized person possessing the required hardware and/or software can decode that data. See Module 1, Chapter 1.
enterprise risk management
In corporate risk management, a cross-functional system that (1) identifies and quantifies risks and (2) uses a coordinated approach to manage those risks. See Module 2, Chapter 6.
environmental, social, and governance (ESG) factors
A set of standards that screen potential investments based on a company’s practices regarding environmental, social, and governance issues. See Module 3, Chapter 7.
EPS
See earnings per share. See Module 3, Chapter 7.
equity fund
A type of investment fund that typically consists of common stocks, but may also include preferred stocks or real estate. Also known as a stock fund. See Module 3, Chapter 7.
equity security
A security that represents an ownership interest in the issuing company. See Module 1, Chapter 2.
ERM
See enterprise risk management. See Module 2, Chapter 6.
ETF
See exchange-traded fund. See Module 1, Chapter 3.
ethics
A system of accepted standards of conduct and moral judgment that combines the elements of honesty, integrity, and fair treatment. Return to text.
EU
See European Union. Return to text.
European Union
An influential governing and lawmaking confederation of member nations with provision for oversight of the European Union financial system. Return to text.
excess return
The amount of an investment portfolio’s total return that exceeds the benchmark return. See Module 3, Chapter 9.
exchange-traded fund
A type of security that usually tracks a market index and trades continuously during the trading day on an exchange. See Module 1, Chapter 3.
expansion
The phase in the business cycle in which unemployment is low and the pace of economic growth is accelerating. See Module 1, Chapter 4.
expansionary monetary policy
A series of actions by a central bank which increase the growth of the money supply and the amount of credit available in the economy. See Module 1, Chapter 4.
expected return
The estimated future return—expressed as a monetary amount or a percentage rate of return—that an investor anticipates earning from an investment. See Module 1, Chapter 4.
expense ratio
In fund investing, a ratio that expresses the fund’s total annual operating expenses during a given period as a percentage of the fund’s average net assets during the same period. See Module 3, Chapter 7.
federal funds rate
Set by the Federal Reserve, the (1) rate banks pay when borrowing from other banks and (2) the benchmark interest rate in the United States. Also known as the fed funds rate. See Module 1, Chapter 4.
federal government bond
A bond issued by a national government to cover the cost of public spending that is not fully supported by tax revenues. Also known as a sovereign bond. See Module 1, Chapter 2.
Federal Reserve
The central bank responsible for conducting monetary policy in the United States. See Module 1, Chapter 4.
fiduciary
An individual or entity that holds a special position of trust or confidence when handling the affairs of another and that must put the other’s interest above the fiduciary’s own interests. Return to text.
fiduciary duty
The obligation to act in the best interest of a particular party. Return to text.
financial audit
A type of audit that evaluates whether a company’s financial information, financial statements, and source documents comply with accounting standards and are a fair and consistent depiction of the company’s financial condition and performance. Return to text.
financial holding company
A company which conducts activities that are financial in nature or incidental to financial activities—such as insurance activities, securities activities, banking, and investment and advisory services. See Module 1, Chapter 1.
Financial Industry Regulatory Authority
A self-regulatory organization in the United States which sets rules stipulating how member companies and registered individuals must conduct securities transaction business. Return to text.
financial model
A computer-based mathematical model that approximates the operation of real-world financial processes. See Module 2, Chapter 6.
FINRA
See Financial Industry Regulatory Authority. Return to text.

In the United States, a Financial Industry Regulatory Authority rule that requires members and registered individuals to have reasonable grounds for recommending investment products to customers based on an understanding of certain facts about the customer, including his financial situation, tax status, and investment objectives. Return to text.
firewall
A combination of hardware and software that creates electronic barriers between the public and private areas of an insurer’s systems. See Module 1, Chapter 1.
fixed fund option
A separate account fund option which guarantees a fixed rate of interest for a specified period of time. See Module 1, Chapter 1.
fixed-income fund
A type of investment fund that typically consists of specified debt instruments and is categorized by the specified type of debt instrument. See Module 3, Chapter 7.
fixed-income investment
An investment, such as a bond, that generally provides a predictable stream of income. See Module 1, Chapter 2.
flat yield curve
A type of yield curve that shows a horizontal straight line, which indicates that yields on debt securities are not responsive to varying terms to maturity. See Module 1, Chapter 4.
foreign exchange (FX) market
A market which converts currencies used by buyers into currencies acceptable to sellers. See Module 1, Chapter 2.
forward contract
A limited-time agreement in which a seller promises to deliver a specified asset to a buyer sometime in the future for a price specified in the agreement. Also known as a forward. See Module 1, Chapter 3.
fraternal insurer
An insurance company that is formed to provide social and insurance benefits to its members. Also known as a fraternal benefit society. Return to text.
front running
A specific type of insider trading that involves the unethical trade of a security by an investment professional based on advance knowledge of large pending client orders of the same security. Return to text.
functional regulation
The principle that a single regulatory body should oversee similar financial activities, regardless of which type of financial institution engages in the activity. Return to text.
futures contract
A limited-time agreement that gives the owner of the agreement the right to buy or sell a certain asset at a specified future date and price. Also known as a future. See Module 1, Chapter 3.
GAAP
See generally accepted accounting principles. Return to text.
gambling
The act of wagering something of value—such as money—on the outcome of an event involving chance. See Module 1, Chapter 1.
general account
A life insurance company investment account in which the insurer maintains assets that support all of the insurer’s contractual obligations for guaranteed insurance products, such as whole life insurance or fixed annuity products. Also known as a general account portfolio. See Module 1, Chapter 1.
generally accepted accounting principles
In the United States, a set of financial accounting standards, conventions, and rules that all publicly traded companies must follow when summarizing transactions and preparing their financial statements. Return to text.
general obligation bond
A type of municipal bond that is backed by the full faith and credit of the issuer—which includes the taxing and further borrowing power of the issuer. See Module 1, Chapter 2.
GIPS®

Global Investment Performance Standards
Standards for consistent reporting of investment performance that are designed to ensure fair representation and full disclosure of investment performance across a global market. See Module 2, Chapter 5.
growth fund
A type of investment fund that invests in stocks of companies with the potential for above-average growth, resulting in capital appreciation. See Module 3, Chapter 7.
hedge effectiveness report
An asset-liability management report that provides information on every position established for hedging purposes. See Module 2, Chapter 6.
hedge fund
An alternative investment vehicle that (1) is often structured as a limited partnership, (2) pools capital from a limited number of sophisticated investors, and (3) is virtually unlimited in the investment strategies it can pursue. See Module 1, Chapter 3.
hedging
A risk management strategy that involves balancing one risk with a complementary risk that ideally offsets the original risk. See Module 1, Chapter 2.
high-yield bond
A bond that is not an investment-grade bond. Also known as a junk bond or speculative bond. See Module 1, Chapter 2.
historical return
A known investment performance result for a given period. Also known as an actual return. See Module 1, Chapter 4.
holding period return
The rate of return on an investment over the entire period of owning the investment. Also known as holding period rate of return. See Module 1, Chapter 4.
hybrid security
A security which has characteristics of both equity and debt securities. See Module 1, Chapter 2.
hyperinflation
A rapid and unsustainable increase in prices in an economy over a period of time. See Module 1, Chapter 4.
IMR
See Interest Maintenance Reserve. Return to text.
income fund
A type of investment fund that is dedicated to earning a reliable income stream from investments that provide interest, dividend payments, or rent. See Module 3, Chapter 7.
incompatible functions
Job duties in the normal course of employment that, when combined, place an employee in a position where he could commit an illegal act or could conceal errors or irregularities. See Module 1, Chapter 1.
index
A statistical measurement system that tracks the performance of a group of similar investments. See Module 1, Chapter 2.
index fund
A type of investment fund that seeks to produce a pattern of performance similar to that of a specified market index. See Module 3, Chapter 7.
inflation
A prolonged rise in the average level of prices in an economy that decreases the purchasing power of the economy’s currency. See Module 1, Chapter 2.
information ratio
The ratio of the excess return for an investment portfolio divided by the portfolio’s tracking error; the result is usually expressed as a percentage. See Module 3, Chapter 9.
information security
The physical, technical, and procedural steps a company takes to prevent the loss, wrongful disclosure (accidental or intentional), or theft of information. See Module 1, Chapter 1.
infrastructure
The basic facilities and systems that provide the services necessary for societies and economies to function. See Module 1, Chapter 3.
initial public offering
For stocks, the first time a corporation’s stock is offered for sale to the public. See Module 1, Chapter 3.
insider trading
The prohibited act of buying or selling a company’s securities based on knowledge of material nonpublic information. Return to text.
institutional investor
An organization that holds financial assets and trades securities in large volume. See Module 1, Chapter 1.
insurance value appraisal
A real estate appraisal approach in which the appraiser estimates the amount an insurance company would potentially cover under property insurance if all buildings and fixtures on a property were destroyed. Also known as a replacement value appraisal. See Module 1, Chapter 2.
intangible asset
An asset that represents ownership of a legal right or another nonphysical resource. See Module 1, Chapter 1.
Interest Maintenance Reserve
A type of investment reserve that helps absorb realized gains and losses on an insurer’s interest-bearing assets when those gains or losses are caused by changes in market interest rates rather than changes in the credit quality of the assets. Return to text.
interest margin
The share of investment earnings that an insurer retains after crediting a share of investment earnings to policyowners. Also known as the interest-rate margin or the interest spread. See Module 1, Chapter 4.
interest-rate anticipation strategy
An investment strategy in which trades of fixed-income securities are conducted in anticipation of estimated future changes in market interest rates. See Module 2, Chapter 6.
interest-rate risk
The risk that the market value of an investment will change due to a change in nominal market interest rates. See Module 1, Chapter 4.
interest-rate swap
A derivatives contract which consists of an agreement between two parties to exchange a set of cash flows—typically with one cash flow based on a fixed interest rate and the other based on a variable, or floating, interest rate. See Module 1, Chapter 3.
International Organisation of Securities Commissions
An international self-regulatory organization for the securities industry that develops international standards for securities regulation. Return to text.
intrinsic value
In security analysis, an investor’s estimate of a security’s true value; the estimate is typically based on the investor’s assessments of both tangible and intangible factors. See Module 3, Chapter 8.
inverted yield curve
A type of yield curve that shows a downward slope from left to right, indicating that yields on short-term debt securities are higher than yields on long-term debt securities. See Module 1, Chapter 4.
investment
Any use of an investor’s resources that is intended to generate a positive return of some kind. See Module 1, Chapter 1.
investment activity report
A report that specifies the details of all investment portfolio transactions, including all asset acquisitions and all dispositions of assets from the portfolio through sales, prepayments, or repayment at maturity. See Module 2, Chapter 6.
Investment Advisers Act of 1940

strike price
In an options contract, the price at which the option holder can buy or sell the underlying security. See Module 1, Chapter 3.
structured security
An investment vehicle that repackages pools of existing debt obligations into a new form of financial security that is tradeable and generally promises investors a stream of payments. Also known as structured debt or a structured debt security. See Module 1, Chapter 3.
style drift
The divergence of an investment portfolio from its stated investment objective or style. See Module 3, Chapter 9.
subaccount
An investment fund option within a separate account. See Module 1, Chapter 1.
subordinated bond
A bond that has a lower claim priority than that of other bonds from the same issuer. Also known as a junior bond. See Module 1, Chapter 2.
subsidiary
A company that is owned or controlled by a holding company. See Module 1, Chapter 1.
suitability requirement
A requirement that imposes a duty on financial professionals or financial services companies to have reasonable grounds on which to believe that a specific product is suitable for a customer’s needs. Return to text.
surplus
In insurance, the cumulative amount of money that remains in an insurance company over time and is calculated as the insurer’s assets minus its liabilities and its capital. See Module 2, Chapter 5.
SVO
See Securities Valuation Office. See Module 2, Chapter 5.
swap
A derivatives contract in which two parties agree to exchange one asset, liability, security, or cash flow for another. Also known as a swap contract. See Module 1, Chapter 3.
systematic risk
A type of risk that has a broad, similar effect on all elements in an economic system and cannot be eliminated through diversification. Also known as a nondiversifiable risk or market risk. See Module 1, Chapter 4.
systemic crisis
A series of adverse events occurring in a significant number of financial institutions or markets that has a catastrophic effect. See Module 1, Chapter 4.
systemic risk
A risk that (1) affects securities markets, banking systems, and currencies, and (2) can result in a systemic crisis. See Module 1, Chapter 4.
TAA
See tactical asset allocation. See Module 3, Chapter 8.
tactical asset allocation
An investment strategy that involves making adjustments to asset-class weightings based on predictions of future market movements in the short term. See Module 3, Chapter 8.
tail risk
A risk of a low-probability but high-impact occurrence. See Module 1, Chapter 4.
tangible asset
An asset that has a physical form. See Module 1, Chapter 1.
target-date fund
A type of investment fund that invests in various asset classes and automatically changes the asset allocation over a period of time. Also known as a lifecycle fund. See Module 3, Chapter 7.
taxable event
An investment transaction that results in taxable income. See Module 1, Chapter 4.
tender offer
An offer made by a prospective buyer to all current shareholders of a particular publicly traded security to purchase their shares at a specified price during a specified time. Return to text.
term structure of interest rates
The relationship between interest rates on bonds of the same credit quality but with different maturities. See Module 1, Chapter 4.
time horizon
The length of time that an investor plans to invest in order to reach an investment goal. See Module 2, Chapter 5.
top-down investing
An investment approach in which investment professionals first conduct a broad analysis of economic conditions, asset classes, risk exposures, and industries; then they select individual securities. See Module 3, Chapter 8.
total return
The sum of all returns—including all income as well as all realized and unrealized gains or losses—from an investment over a given period, typically one year. See Module 1, Chapter 4.
tracking error
A measure that indicates the closeness of fit between an investment portfolio’s returns and the returns of its benchmark. Also known as active risk. See Module 3, Chapter 9.
trader
An investment professional responsible for executing purchases and sales of publicly traded securities under instructions from a portfolio manager. See Module 1, Chapter 1.
trading ahead of research reports
An unethical activity that occurs when, prior to the release of a research report, the trading operation of an investment management firm uses nonpublic information in a research report as a basis for increasing or decreasing its inventory position in a particular security. Return to text.
treasury operations
In insurance companies, the function that manages and maintains records and reports for all of an insurer’s cash transactions, specifically money deposited or withdrawn from accounts at a bank or other financial institution. See Module 1, Chapter 1.
trough
A point in the business cycle when conditions stabilize after the end of a significant decline in economic activity; the phase after contraction and before expansion. See Module 1, Chapter 4.
trust
A legal arrangement that is (1) created at the direction of an individual or entity and (2) designed to allow one party, the trustee, to hold specified property belonging to the trust’s creator for the benefit of trust beneficiaries. Return to text.
trust indenture
A formal agreement that establishes a trust and forms the relationship between a trustee and a trust beneficiary. Also known as a trust deed. Return to text.
Trust Indenture Act of 1939
A U.S. federal securities law that applies to bonds and other debt securities and imposes certain requirements on the issuer before the securities can be sold publicly. Return to text.
UIT
See unit investment trust. See Module 1, Chapter 3.
Uniform Law Commission
An organization that provides U.S. states with nonpartisan, model legislation that brings clarity and stability to critical areas of state statutory law. Return to text.
Uniform Securities Act
A long-standing model law designed to provide a pattern for U.S. state legislators to follow in crafting state securities laws. Return to text.
unit investment trust
A portfolio of securities assembled for a specific investment purpose and held for a designated amount of time. See Module 1, Chapter 3.
unsecured bond
A bond that is not backed by collateral but is backed only by the full faith and credit of the issuer. Also known as a debenture. See Module 1, Chapter 2.
valuation ratio
In stock investing, a type of financial ratio that compares a share’s market price to another measure of a company’s ability to generate value for shareholders. See Module 3, Chapter 7.
Value at Risk
A measure that (1) estimates the maximum amount that a group of investments could potentially lose in a specified time period and (2) assigns a probability to indicate the level of confidence in that estimate. See Module 3, Chapter 9.
value fund
A type of investment fund that invests in stocks of companies that are undervalued in relation to the overall market and that often pay significant dividends. See Module 3, Chapter 7.
value-in-use appraisal
A real estate appraisal approach in which the appraiser estimates a property’s value to a particular user. Also known as a use value appraisal. See Module 1, Chapter 2.
VaR
See Value at Risk. See Module 3, Chapter 9.
vendor
A third party that a company hires to perform specific tasks or provide specific services. Also known as a service provider or a solution provider. See Module 1, Chapter 1.
venture capital fund
A type of private equity fund that provides capital and management expertise to target companies, such as start-up companies with high growth potential. See Module 1, Chapter 3.
volatility
In the context of investment returns, a measure of the tendency for an investment’s rate of return to fluctuate from year to year, as indicated by the investment’s historical rates of return over a specified time period. See Module 1, Chapter 4.
watch list
In fund investing, a list of funds that require heightened monitoring and must subsequently meet specified performance requirements. See Module 3, Chapter 9.
yield
A ratio measuring an asset’s rate of return on income paid to the asset holder. See Module 1, Chapter 2.
yield curve
A line graph that illustrates the relationship between the yields of debt securities and the maturities of those securities. Also known as a debt curve or a bond curve. See Module 1, Chapter 4.
yield spread
The difference in yields between two debt instruments. See Module 3, Chapter 8.
yield to maturity
The rate of return a bondholder will earn if he holds a bond until maturity. See Module 1, Chapter 4.
YTM
See yield to maturity. See Module 1, Chapter 4.
zero-coupon bond
A bond that does not make coupon payments during the term of the bond; instead, the bond is sold at a discount to its par value and the principal is paid in full at the bond’s maturity date. See Module 1, Chapter 2.
