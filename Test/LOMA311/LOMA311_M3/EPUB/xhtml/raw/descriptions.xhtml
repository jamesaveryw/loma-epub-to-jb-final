Figure 7.2

IRIS Ratios and Financial Analysis

Flowchart.

Stage One: Statistical phase of IRIS — NAIC compares insurer’s ratios with IRIS financial ratios

If Insurer “passes”: no further action.

If insurer does not pass, move to Stage Two, analytical phase of IRIS.

Stage Two: Analytical phase of IRIS — NAIC examiners apply qualitative and quantitative standards to further analyze insurer’s Annual Statement data

If no unusual results: no further action.

If there are unusual results, NAIC reports findings first to insurer’s state of domicile and then to the other states where the insurer operates. Move to Stage Three, regulatory action.

Regulatory action: State regulators have the authority to act based on their judgment that an insurer’s financial condition is hazardous.

Activities that Might Indicate Money Laundering

[Customer talking to agent.]

Customer: “So how much money will I be get if I cancel the policy next year?”

If an applicant for a single premium universal life insurance policy asks how much money he will receive if he cancels the policy in a year…

he might be a money launderer.

[Agent talking to customer.]

Agent: “If you’ll give me your bank information, we can arrange a funds transfer.”

[Customer puts a huge bag of money onto a table.

Customer: “There’s no need for bank information.”

If an applicant for a single premium universal life insurance policy wants to pay the premium in cash…

he might be a money launderer.

[Agent and customer having a conversation. Agent looks at a piece of paper.]

Agent: “So you’re 20 years old and your annual income is $25,000, correct?”

Customer: “Yes.”

Agent: “And you’re applying for a $1,000,000 cash value policy on your life?”

Customer: “Yes.”

If an applicant applies for a cash value policy that seems beyond his means…

she might be a money launderer.

[Agent points to piece of paper.]

Agent: “It looks like you forgot to complete this section.”

Customer: “Why do you need to know that?”

If an applicant is unwilling to provide identifying information…

she might be a money launderer.

Video 7.2

China Banking and Insurance Regulatory Commission

For years, the China Banking Regulatory Commission (CBRC) and the China Insurance Regulatory Commission (CIRC) operated independently.

In 2018, as part of a broad reorganization of government agencies, China merged its banking regulator and insurance regulator.

The goals of the newly created China Banking and Insurance Regulatory Commission (CBIRC) are to (1) clarify and better coordinate regulatory responsibilities in China’s financial services industry, (2) institute regulatory reforms, and (3) more closely monitor financial risk in banks and insurance companies.

Figure 8.2

Filing Submissions

Checklist with icons.

A typical policy form filing submission includes:

A transmittal (submission) letter; each state specifies the information that this letter must include.

The required number of copies of the policy form (when sent as hardcopy).

A statement of variability to indicate specific information in the policy form that the insurer might adjust when issuing each policy.

Documents that will be issued with the policy form, if applicable, such as the application for insurance and any endorsements or riders.

The policy form’s score on a readability test, if required.

Premium rates and an actuarial memorandum, if required, which shows the form’s compliance with applicable state laws and regulations.

Any required certification forms, such as a company officer’s certification that—based on her best knowledge, information, and belief—the form meets all of the state’s.

Filing fees, which are required by some states and vary in amount by state and type of form.

Figure 8.3

Complaint Handling Process

Step-by-step infographic.

Top box: Complaint to insurance department typically follows this process:

Step one: Complaint is given file number and assigned to complaint examiner

Step two: In most states, examiner completes NAIC standard complaint form to collect information about complaint

Step three: NAIC compiles information from forms into NAIC Complaints Database System (CDS) which provides regulators with aggregated complaint data on insurers

Step four: Examiner sends copy of complaint to insurer and asks insurer to respond in writing

Step five: If insurer responds by stating complaint is not valid, it must provide examiner with supporting documentation

Step six: If complaint is valid, insurer responds to examiner and explains how it will correct situation

Figure 8.4

Insurance Company Antifraud Plans

Checklist infographic.

An antifraud plan usually sets forth procedures that enable the insurer to:

  • Prevent fraud
  • Review claims to detect fraud and investigate claims where fraud is suspected
  • Report fraud to appropriate law enforcement authorities and cooperate with authorities in prosecuting cases of fraud
  • Pursue civil actions against persons who commit fraud
  • Train employees and insurance agents to detect and prevent fraud

Figure 9.2

ERISA Plan Documents and Summary Plan Descriptions

Checklist infographic.

Plan documents and summary plan descriptions must contain the following information:

  • Plan administrator: Name and address of plan administrator
  • Plan trustees: Names and addresses of all plan trustees, if applicable
  • Named fiduciary: Plan’s named fiduciary
  • Eligibility requirements: Plan’s requirements relating to eligibility for participation and benefits
  • Nonforfeitable retirement benefits: Description of provisions regarding nonforfeitable retirement benefits, if applicable
  • Disqualification: Description of what may result in disqualification, ineligibility, or denial or loss of benefits
  • Financing: Source of financing of the plan
  • Organization providing benefits: Identity of any organization through which plan benefits are provided
  • Plan year: Date of the end of the plan year and whether the plan’s records are kept on a calendar-year, policy-year, or fiscal-year basis
  • Claims: Procedures to be followed in presenting claims for benefits under the plan
  • Remedies: Remedies available for the redress of claims that are denied in whole or in part

Examples of Defined Contribution Plans

Grid infographic; each column lists the characteristics of a different type of defined contribution plan.

Column One: Traditional Profit Sharing Plan

  • Plan Sponsor Contributions: Voluntary; amounts usually based on and payable from employer’s profits
  • Plan Participant Contributions: Usually not allowed
  • Benefits: Lump-sum or monthly annuity

Column Two: Stock Bonus Plan:

  • Plan Sponsor Contributions: Voluntary; amounts usually based on company profits
  • Plan Participant Contributions: None
  • Benefits: Paid in company stock

Column Three: 401(k), 403(b), or 457(b) Plan:

  • Plan Sponsor Contributions: Voluntary or mandatory, depending on plan structure; may match participant contributions up to specified percent of salary; contributions limited by tax law
  • Plan Participant Contributions: Voluntary; usually a fixed percentage of annual salary; contributions limited by tax law
  • Benefits: Lump-sum, installments, or monthly annuity

Video 9.2

How a Group Insured’s Enrollment Works

Rosa Fuentes recently started a new job.

She is eligible for coverage under her employer’s group life insurance contract.

The contract has a 30-day probationary period and a 31-day enrollment period.

Her coverage is contributory.

[Timeline showing Rosa’s first day of work, followed by a marker for 30 days, showing a 30-day probationary period.]

Rosa has no coverage during the probationary period.

[Timeline extends to 61 days, adding a 31-day enrollment period.]

After the probationary period, Rosa may elect to enroll during the 31-day enrollment period. Because coverage is contributory, Rosa is not automatically enrolled in the plan. If Rosa enrolls during the enrollment period, coverage begins the day she enrolls.

[Timeline extends ending with an arrow to indicate ongoing time passing.]

If Rosa enrolls after the enrollment period ends, she is a late entrant. To enroll, she must provide satisfactory evidence of insurability to the insurer.

If the group contract includes an open enrollment period, then Rosa may enroll during the next open enrollment period without submitting evidence of insurability.

Rosa also may enroll during a special enrollment period at the time of a specified life event, such as marriage or the birth of a child.
