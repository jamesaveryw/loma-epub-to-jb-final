    1. In the United States, insurance companies are subject to dual regulation.
    2. The federal government has the power to regulate interstate commerce.
    3. The federal McCarran-Ferguson Act gives the states primary authority to regulate insurance as long as Congress finds such state regulation to be adequate.
    1. A, B, and C
    2. A and B only
    3. A and C only
    4. B and C only
    5. B only

    All the statements are correct. The states and the federal government each play a role in regulating insurance. One of the powers the Constitution gives to the federal government is the power to regulate interstate commerce, which is commerce across state lines. The states, on the other hand, have the power to regulate intrastate commerce, which is commerce within a state. Because insurers typically conduct business across state lines, the federal government has authority to regulate insurance activities. According to the McCarran-Ferguson Act, the regulation of insurance by the states is in the public interest. Congress retains the right to enact insurance legislation if it decides that state regulation is inadequate or not in the public interest.
